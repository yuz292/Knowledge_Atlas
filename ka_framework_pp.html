Predictive Processing

The brain as a prediction machine: how anticipation, surprise, and precision-weighted error together build what we perceive, attend to, and learn.

At a glance

In plain English. Predictive Processing is the idea that perception is not the passive registration of sensory signals but an ongoing inferential act — the brain constantly generates predictions about what it is about to encounter and processes mainly the residual "prediction error" its predictions failed to anticipate.

The physiology is now partially mapped. Deep cortical layers 5 and 6 send top-down predictions via slow alpha and beta-band oscillations through NMDA-receptor-dependent pyramidal projections; superficial layers 2 and 3 return prediction errors via fast gamma-band signals through AMPA-receptor-dependent feedforward connections. Cholinergic input from the basal forebrain and noradrenergic input from the locus coeruleus set the gain on these error signals — acetylcholine tracks sensory reliability, noradrenaline tracks environmental volatility. Dopaminergic signals from VTA and substantia nigra carry a specific kind of prediction error — reward-related. Attention, expectation, surprise, and learning are all interpretable as precision-weighted operations on this hierarchical message-passing architecture.

Predictive Processing is the most theoretically developed of the eleven T1 frameworks but also among the most contested. Clear physiological support exists for hierarchical message passing in sensory cortex, with microcircuit evidence for distinct prediction and error populations. Contested territory includes whether the free-energy principle adds testable content, whether prediction error in higher cognitive regions resembles its sensory-cortex cousin, and what the neural code actually looks like at the single-neuron level. Apply the framework with care beyond early perception.

Environments that contain ambiguous or subtly aberrant signals — slightly off-true geometry, inconsistent acoustic reflections, irregular lighting transitions — generate persistent, low-level prediction error and impose an invisible cognitive cost. The most restorative spaces are not the most predictable; they are those where predictions match reality at a comfortable rate, with occasional mild novelty that engages curiosity without leaving unresolved error behind. For practitioners this means aligning visual, acoustic, and thermal cues so they are either coherently consistent or obviously varied — never almost-correct.

Deep dive — the framework in full

Origins and conceptual roots

Predictive Processing has roots that run back at least a century and a half, to Hermann von Helmholtz's argument (1867) that perception is a form of unconscious inference — the brain combines incoming sensory data with prior expectations to arrive at a best-guess interpretation of what lies in front of it. Helmholtz's insight lay fallow in experimental psychology for most of the twentieth century before Richard Gregory (1980) revived it through his work on perceptual illusions, framing perception as a process of hypothesis-testing rather than passive reception. The modern computational formulation crystallised in the 1990s. David Mumford (1992) proposed that cortico-cortical feedback loops implement a generative model: higher regions predict lower-region activity, and lower regions report back the residual error. Rao and Ballard (1999) elaborated this as predictive coding in the primary visual cortex, showing that a number of extra-classical receptive-field effects that had resisted simpler explanations fall out naturally from a hierarchy that exchanges predictions and errors. Karl Friston's subsequent work (2005) generalised the scheme to a free-energy principle that frames the brain as a Bayesian inference engine minimising long-run surprise. The framework now runs from molecular mechanisms through computational neuroscience into philosophy of mind.

The core mechanism in three claims

Predictive Processing rests on three inter-locking claims, each with its own evidentiary status.

First, the cortex contains hierarchies that exchange two kinds of signal. Top-down connections carry predictions — the higher region's best guess about what the lower region is about to register. Bottom-up connections carry prediction error — the portion of the lower region's actual activity the top-down prediction failed to explain away. Anatomically, top-down predictions originate in infragranular layers 5 and 6 and terminate in layer 1 of the target region; bottom-up errors originate in supragranular layers 2 and 3 and terminate in layer 4 of their targets. The cortical microcircuit Felleman and Van Essen mapped in the early 1990s is, on this reading, a prediction-and-error transmission network. Frequency-band analyses have added further specificity: top-down predictions carry alpha and beta rhythms (roughly 8–30 Hz) supported by NMDA-receptor-dependent recurrent activity, while feedforward errors carry gamma rhythms (30–80 Hz) dominated by AMPA-receptor-dependent feedforward synapses — a dissociation observed in macaque (Bastos et al., 2015) and human cortex. Only the error is passed forward for further processing, which means the brain's message traffic is dominated by surprise rather than by the sensory signal itself. The routing claim has strong support in visual cortex and moderate support in auditory cortex.

Second, prediction error is precision-weighted. Not every mismatch between prediction and reality matters equally. The brain assigns a precision — effectively a confidence weight — to each prediction error, amplifying errors it judges reliable and suppressing errors that might be sensory noise. Precision weighting is how attention operates in the Predictive-Processing scheme: attending to a stimulus means raising the precision of prediction errors in the attended region, thereby privileging those signals in downstream inference. The neuromodulators acetylcholine and noradrenaline are thought to set precision, with acetylcholine tracking sensory reliability and noradrenaline tracking environmental volatility.

Third, predictions at every level are learned from prior experience. Statistical regularities in an organism's environment become baked into the generative model that produces top-down predictions, which is why a trained musician hears a missing note, a radiologist spots a tumour an untrained observer misses, and a long-time resident of a building navigates it without conscious effort. Learning in this scheme is the adjustment of the generative model to reduce future prediction error — a single principle that connects perceptual learning, motor learning, and the acquisition of conceptual knowledge.

The three claims do not stand or fall together. Evidence for hierarchical message passing is strongest; evidence for precision weighting is reasonably good; the claim that every cortical computation reduces to prediction-error minimisation is the most contested.

Evidence: what is well-established

Several empirical findings have accumulated enough weight that they are no longer seriously disputed. Laminar fMRI and high-density electrophysiology in macaque and human visual cortex show that deep cortical layers (layers 5 and 6) carry signals that behave like top-down predictions while superficial layers (layers 2 and 3) carry signals that behave like bottom-up errors, with distinct temporal profiles and distinct responses to expectation violations (Keller & Mrsic-Flogel, 2018). The layered-circuit prediction of the theory has been directly tested and held up. Unexpected stimuli — those that violate an established prediction — reliably produce larger neural responses than expected stimuli of matched physical energy; this is the clearest behavioural signature of prediction error, and it has been replicated across sensory modalities and species. Attention modulates early sensory processing in a manner consistent with precision weighting: attended stimuli produce enhanced responses specifically in the error-signal populations identified by the theory, and pharmacological manipulations of cholinergic tone produce the predicted changes in perceptual precision. Finally, learning curves in perception, motor control, and language acquisition all follow profiles that match what a prediction-error-minimising system would produce: initial rapid improvement, then slow asymptotic approach to perfect prediction, with learning rates modulated by surprise (Walsh et al., 2020; Heilbron & Chait, 2018). A more recent wave of optogenetic experiments in mouse V1 has directly manipulated superficial-layer error populations and observed the predicted effects on behavioural sensitivity: stimulating the error population during matched visual input produces perceptual reports consistent with surprise, even when the stimulus itself is unchanged. These causal manipulations are harder to square with alternative accounts that place Predictive-Processing-like phenomena downstream of sensory processing rather than within it.

Where it is contested

The framework faces three live critiques. The first concerns falsifiability. The free-energy formulation is mathematically elegant but flexible: if any behaviour can be cast as minimising some free-energy functional, the theory risks explaining everything and predicting nothing. Careful experimental work has narrowed this critique but not dissolved it; contemporary proponents draw a sharp line between the implementation-level claim (specific circuits carry prediction and error signals) and the meta-theoretical claim (all cognition minimises free energy). The second concerns scope. Evidence for predictive coding in sensory cortex is strong. Evidence that the same mechanism operates in prefrontal or parietal cortex — where cognition proper takes place — is more tentative. The "perceptual prediction paradox" (Press, Kok & Yon, 2020) names a specific tension: expected stimuli sometimes produce larger responses rather than smaller ones, which is difficult to reconcile with a strict error-minimisation reading. The third concerns the neural code. Even granting that cortex exchanges predictions and errors, the question of how these signals are represented at the single-neuron level remains unsettled (Millidge, Seth & Buckley, 2022). Taken together, the framework is well-supported in early sensory cortex, reasonably supported as a general organising principle, and speculative as a complete theory of cognition. Research and design practice should track that gradient rather than treat the framework as uniformly established.

How this connects to the other T1 frameworks

Predictive Processing sits at the centre of the Tier-1 network. Salience Networks (SN) are, in this reading, the machinery that detects high-precision prediction errors and triggers re-allocation of processing resources — making salience a special case of precision weighting applied to surprise. Default-mode and Place (DP) systems maintain the long-term generative models top-down predictions draw upon. Neuromodulatory Systems (NM) supply the precision signals — acetylcholine for sensory reliability, noradrenaline for volatility, dopamine for reward-prediction error — that gate the whole scheme. Embodied Cognition (EC) enters through the motor side: under active inference, actions themselves are generated to reduce prediction error about the organism's proprioceptive state. Interoceptive-Constructionist Affect (IC) is the Predictive-Processing treatment of emotion: affect is the brain's inference about bodily state, continuously updated by interoceptive prediction error. The superordinate Implicit-Explicit Dual-Process configurer (IE_DPT) decides when prediction-error minimisation runs in fast automatic mode versus slow deliberate mode.

Implications for environmental cognition

For architects, landscape designers, and building scientists, Predictive Processing matters because the built environment is a long-duration source of either satisfied or violated predictions. A well-designed space is one whose structural cues — geometry, acoustic reflection, thermal gradient, light-source location — are mutually consistent and match the statistics of the environments our perceptual systems evolved to inhabit. Small coherence violations (walls almost-but-not-quite perpendicular, speakers that place voices slightly in the wrong apparent location, sunlight arriving from a direction inconsistent with the visible window) generate persistent low-level prediction error the occupant cannot easily resolve and cannot easily ignore. The cognitive cost of such spaces shows up not in reported discomfort — occupants typically cannot articulate what is wrong — but in sustained-attention breakdowns, elevated cortisol, and earlier onset of fatigue on tasks performed in the space. Conversely, environments with too little prediction error are sub-optimal: they fail to engage the exploratory machinery that thrives on mild, resolvable novelty. The goal is not maximum predictability but calibrated surprise — coherent where coherence matters, varied where variation invites engagement.

Instrumenting calibrated surprise in practice is at the frontier of environmental-cognition research. Behavioural measures — reading comprehension over extended sessions, error rates on continuous performance tasks, accuracy on sustained-attention probes — are already tractable. Physiological measures — pupil dilation as a precision-weighting proxy, heart-rate variability as autonomic load, skin conductance as rapid surprise signal — are moving from the lab into the field as wearable sensors improve. The design implication is empirical rather than prescriptive: for any proposed environmental intervention, whether a new ceiling geometry, a shift in acoustic treatment, a change of glazing spectrum, the relevant question is whether it changes the behavioural and physiological signatures of prediction-error resolution in the occupants it actually serves. Predictive Processing, in other words, sets an agenda for how environmental-cognition research can be made mechanistic without requiring brain imaging in the building.
