Step-by-step (draft)

The walk-through below is a first-pass draft. It will be replaced once the PNU panel writes the canonical common-sense explanation.

Step 1 · Input

Daylight reaches the eye

The relevant feature is not just brightness. It is the spectral content of daylight: a broad, blue-shifted spectrum that peaks near 480 nm. Electric lighting can match daylight on visible brightness but typically delivers a different spectrum, with much less power at 480 nm.

Well established
Step 2 · Receptor

Melanopsin-bearing retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs)

A small population of retinal ganglion cells contains melanopsin, a photopigment that responds maximally near 480 nm. Unlike the rods and cones that drive image-forming vision, ipRGCs project directly to non-visual structures and signal "ambient brightness in the blue band". Berson et al. (2002) was the first clean demonstration.

Well established
Step 3 · Master clock

Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)

ipRGCs project to the SCN, a small hypothalamic nucleus that maintains the body's roughly-24-hour circadian rhythm. Light input here resets the clock — morning daylight advances the phase, late-evening light delays it. This is the link that makes "office daylight" matter for sleep at night.

Well established
Step 4 · Effectors

Locus coeruleus and pineal gland

The SCN modulates two downstream systems relevant to alertness. The locus coeruleus is the brain's main source of noradrenaline and is a principal driver of cortical arousal. The pineal gland's melatonin output is suppressed by daytime light via the SCN. Both effects feed forward into how alert and task-engaged a person feels during the day.

Established, with quantitative gaps
Step 5 · Cortex

Cortical arousal and circadian phase

The combined effect of higher noradrenergic tone and suppressed daytime melatonin is a small but reliable lift in cortical arousal — visible in EEG indices and self-reported alertness. The phase effects are slower and only visible across days.

Mechanism plausible; effect-size variable
Step 6 · Outcomes

Alertness, mood, and sleep

The end-of-chain occupant outcomes — alertness during the workday, lower depressive symptomatology over weeks, better sleep at night — are the outcomes the Boyce et al. review tabulates. The chain above is the proposed mechanism that links those outcomes to the architectural input (a window).

Supported in aggregate; modest effect sizes
