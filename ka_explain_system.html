Chapter 1

What K-ATLAS Is

K-ATLAS — the Knowledge Architecture for Translating Life-science and Affective-environment Science — is a reasoning machine built around a single conviction: that a scientific claim is only as strong as the evidence and argumentation that support it. Where a conventional database stores facts, K-ATLAS stores structured credences: beliefs tagged with how well-supported they are, where the support comes from, and why the system holds each belief with the confidence it does.

The closest popular analogy is Google's PageRank. PageRank scores web pages not by counting how many links point to them, but by weighing the authority of those linking pages, recursively. K-ATLAS does something analogous for scientific beliefs: a claim is not simply "in the literature" — it receives a credence value that reflects the strength, independence, and theoretical warrant of the evidence behind it. When new evidence arrives, credences propagate through the network, and the whole system re-equilibrates, much as PageRank scores shift when new pages are indexed.

Example — PageRank Analogy

Suppose three independent studies show that forest environments reduce cortisol. Each study is a "link" pointing to the belief Forest exposure lowers cortisol. K-ATLAS asks: Are these studies truly independent? Does a theoretical framework (Stress Recovery Theory, say) provide additional warrant? How well-replicated is the effect across populations? The credence assigned to the belief is the answer to all three questions simultaneously — not a simple vote count.

Open Questions

Three design questions remain open. First, would a sixth layer — a dedicated explanation layer whose job is to generate natural-language accounts of the system's own reasoning — improve the architecture, or should that function remain embedded in QA? The current L5 Application layer is a thin wrapper; a principled argument for or against a sixth layer has not been made. Second, should the PageRank analogy be pressed further? PageRank uses the iterative power method; the EN uses Noisy-OR propagation. A formal comparison of convergence properties might reveal whether Noisy-OR produces the right fixed-point for heterogeneous evidence networks. Third, how well does the five-layer architecture generalise beyond built environments? The three-tier theory structure is probably general; the specific warrant vocabulary and the δ population-transfer function are domain-specific and would need renegotiation for clinical decision support, nutritional epidemiology, or educational psychology.

How We Use It

Chapter 1 is the entry point for every new student and collaborator. Read it before any other chapter to understand why each component exists. The five-layer diagram is the most concise single-image summary of K-ATLAS; the PageRank analogy has proven effective with students who have no formal Bayesian background — it conveys that authority propagates rather than merely vote-counts, without requiring probabilistic notation. When explaining the system to a research collaborator, this diagram and that analogy are the right starting point.

Chapter 2

The Evidence Network

The Evidence Network (EN) — also called the Web of Belief — is K-ATLAS's central epistemic object. Every belief in the system is a node; every piece of support or opposition between beliefs is a typed, weighted edge. The EN is a foundherentist structure in the tradition of Susan Haack (1993): it is neither purely foundationalist (no claim is certain by definition) nor purely coherentist (empirical connections to evidence matter). Support flows through the network; nothing is assumed without a warrant chain reaching back to observations.

Quine's Web of Belief: The Intellectual Ancestor

The name "Web of Belief" is not incidental. It acknowledges an explicit intellectual debt to W. V. O. Quine's account of scientific knowledge, developed first in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (1951) and worked out in detail — with J.S. Ullian — in the 1970 book of the same name. Quine's web of belief is one of the most consequential ideas in twentieth-century epistemology, and understanding it is essential to understanding why K-ATLAS is structured as it is.

Quine's central argument against the then-dominant logical empiricist picture was that individual statements — even scientific hypotheses — cannot be tested in isolation against experience. They face experience only in groups, as a corporate body. The beliefs we hold form a network, and what impinges on us from the world impinges on the network as a whole. The same sensory experience could, in principle, be accommodated by revising any of a large number of beliefs in the web, not just the directly implicated ones. This is the thesis of confirmation holism.

Three features of Quine's web are structurally significant for K-ATLAS:

Centrality and revision cost. Beliefs at the centre of the web — those with the most connections to other beliefs — are more costly to revise, because revising them requires revising many other things. Beliefs at the periphery — close to the "sensory" surface, with fewer internal connections — can be revised with minimal ripple effects. Quine called this the conservatism principle of rational belief revision: when experience forces revision, we revise minimally, choosing the path of least disturbance. K-ATLAS's entrenchment score T_ent is a direct computational implementation of Quine's notion of centrality. A T1 framework like Circadian Biology carries T_ent = 0.93 not because it is foundationally certain, but because it is so densely connected to the rest of the web that revising it would require revising an enormous amount else.

No immunity from revision. Quine insisted — controversially — that no belief is immune from revision, not even logical truths. If experience were strange enough, we might even revise the law of excluded middle. This anti-foundationalist commitment is built into K-ATLAS: no belief has credence 1.0. The T1 frameworks have the highest entrenchment scores, but even Circadian Biology (T_ent = 0.93) is in principle revisable; it merely has an extremely high revision cost. The system refuses to treat any claim as certain.

Holism and coherence. Quine's web is held together by coherence — the mutual support of connected beliefs. A new belief that coheres well with the existing web is easier to accept; one that requires extensive revision elsewhere is harder. K-ATLAS implements this through coherence warrant scaling: a belief gains a credence bonus when multiple independent lines of evidence converge on it, and loses credence when contesting beliefs are present. The coherence formula (base 0.55 + 0.05√n_coherent − 0.05√n_contradicting) is a quantitative version of Quinean coherentism.

Quine vs. Haack: Why K-ATLAS is foundherentist, not purely Quinean

Quine's web is flat: beliefs differ only in how centrally connected they are, not in kind. There is no principled distinction between observational and theoretical beliefs — both are simply nodes in the web, distinguished only by their number of connections.

Susan Haack's foundherentism (1993) introduces a distinction Quine resists: between the experiential dimension of justification (how directly a belief is anchored to observation) and the coherential dimension (how well it fits with other beliefs). Both dimensions matter, and neither suffices alone. This is the K-ATLAS architecture: T2 empirical claims are warranted partly by direct observational support from studies (Haack's experiential dimension, implemented as CONSTITUTIVE and EMPIRICAL_ASSOCIATION warrants with high d values) and partly by their coherence with T1 theoretical frameworks (Haack's coherential dimension, implemented as THEORY_DERIVED and MECHANISM warrants). The two dimensions trade off: a well-replicated T2 claim can survive even if its theoretical backing is thin; a theoretically central claim can survive even limited direct evidence, but not indefinitely. This is what the warrant type taxonomy and the Noisy-OR combination rule together implement.

Warrant Types and Discount Factors

Edges in the EN are not all equal. Each edge carries a warrant type — a formal category indicating the logical relationship between the source evidence and the target belief — together with a discount factor d that controls how much credence flows across that edge. The discount factors are not arbitrary: they reflect the epistemic distance between the type of evidence and the type of claim.

FUNCTIONAL0.65Functional analogy holds across conditionsCross-condition or cross-population replications

THEORY_DERIVED0.25Derived from theoretical prediction only, no direct testModel predictions, simulation outputs

Credence Combination: The Noisy-OR Rule

When multiple independent pieces of evidence support the same belief, K-ATLAS combines them using the Noisy-OR operator — not simple averaging. Noisy-OR rewards the accumulation of independent evidence; two independent studies each with credence 0.70 together produce a combined credence of 0.91, not 0.70.

P(belief) = 1 − ∏ᵢ (1 − credenceᵢ) Example (3 independent studies: 0.70, 0.65, 0.60): P = 1 − (1−0.70)(1−0.65)(1−0.60) = 1 − (0.30)(0.35)(0.40) = 1 − 0.042 = 0.958

Entrenchment (T_ent)

Entrenchment is a Quinean concept: some beliefs are more central to our theoretical web and therefore harder to revise. K-ATLAS tracks entrenchment as a continuous score from 0 to 1. High-entrenchment beliefs sit at the core of well-confirmed theoretical frameworks. Low-entrenchment beliefs are peripheral, speculative, or sparsely supported.

Open Questions

Three questions about the EN's core design remain active. First, is Noisy-OR the right combination rule? Its independence assumption — that evidence pieces are conditionally independent given the hypothesis — is violated when studies share methods, populations, or theoretical frameworks. A learned combination function might do better, but the corpus is too small and too heterogeneous for reliable learning. Second, should warrant confidence decay over time as theoretical frameworks evolve? A belief warranted by a 1990 study under a framework that has since been substantially revised arguably deserves reduced credence, but no temporal-decay mechanism is yet specified. Third, should entrenchment scores be computed algorithmically from graph-topological properties of the EN, or remain assigned by expert judgment? Both approaches have formal precedent — Spohn's (2012) ranking functions on one side, Quine's (1951) web-of-belief metaphor on the other — and comparing the two computationally might be illuminating.

How We Use It

When a QA answer cites a credence value, the EN is what produced it. Understanding ClaimV2 structure and warrant types allows you to interpret any answer intelligently: a credence of 0.63 with interval [0.45, 0.78] tells a very different story than 0.78 with interval [0.71, 0.84], even though both represent moderate confidence. The warrant-type table is the most pedagogically valuable single table in the system — it makes the implicit hierarchy of evidence quality explicit and quantitative, and is worth a full class session.

Chapter 3

The Warrant System

Warrants are the logical connective tissue of the Evidence Network. A warrant is not simply a reference — it is a specification of why one piece of evidence should raise or lower confidence in a claim. K-ATLAS distinguishes between three families of warrant: bridge warrants (connecting lab findings to population-level claims), evidence evaluation warrants (assessing quality, coherence, and contest), and the internal credence propagation produced by those warrants.

Bridge Warrants

The most mathematically precise warrant in the system is the bridge warrant — a formal rule for translating evidence from one context to another. Its core formula is:

logit(p_target) = d(τ) · ω · δ(pop, pop_target) · logit(p_lab) where: d(τ) = discount factor for warrant type τ (Table, Ch.2) ω = baseline weight (default 1.0) δ(pop, pop_target)= population similarity scalar (0–1) p_lab = observed probability in source study p_target = inferred probability in target context
Example — Worked Bridge Warrant

Source finding: In a Japanese cohort (n=280, healthy adults), shinrin-yoku reduced salivary cortisol by 23% (p<0.001). Estimated p_lab = 0.85.

Target claim: Does urban green-space access reduce cortisol in US adults?

Warrant type: FUNCTIONAL (d = 0.65) — functional analogy between forest bathing and green-space access.

Population similarity: δ = 0.72 (healthy adults, similar age range; different cultural context, different green-space format).

Calculation: logit(p_target) = 0.65 × 1.0 × 0.72 × logit(0.85) = 0.65 × 0.72 × 1.735 ≈ 0.812

Result: p_target ≈ 0.693 — moderate-to-good support for the target claim, substantially discounted from the source.

Coherence Warrant Scaling

When multiple independent lines of evidence converge on the same belief from different directions, K-ATLAS adds a coherence bonus. The scaling is sub-linear (square-root) to prevent artificial inflation from redundant citations.

coherence_warrant = base(0.55) + 0.05 × √(n_coherent_links) − 0.05 × √(n_contradicting_links) ceiling: 0.75 Example: 9 coherent links, 1 contradiction: = 0.55 + 0.05 × 3 − 0.05 × 1 = 0.55 + 0.15 − 0.05 = 0.65

Evidence Evaluation Warrants

Three further warrant types assess evidence quality rather than evidence content. They modify the credence that flows through the associated edges.

WarrantFunction
METHODOLOGICAL_QUALITYAdjusts for study design (RCT > quasi-experimental > observational)
REPLICATIONBoosts credence when independent replication exists; penalizes replication failures
CONTESTIntroduces a countervailing pressure: a contested edge reduces the credence of the target belief, but also flags it as requiring explicit justification in QA answers
Open Questions

Three unresolved problems lie within the bridge warrant formula. First, the δ(pop, pop_target) population-similarity scalar is a lookup table with five binary dimensions — operationally useful but theoretically unsatisfying. A principled derivation, perhaps from information-theoretic distributional distance or Bayesian model comparison, would put the bridge warrant on more rigorous ground. Second, the threshold for promoting an ANALOGICAL warrant to a FUNCTIONAL warrant is not formally specified — when does cross-context replication become strong enough to move up the type hierarchy? This is currently assessed by expert judgment at sprint boundaries. Third, the coherence warrant base value (0.55) and ceiling (0.75) were set by expert elicitation; a formal calibration study using held-out corpus subsets as ground truth would either validate or force revision of these values.

How We Use It

Bridge warrants are the primary mechanism for translating Japanese forest-bathing studies into design guidance for offices in San Diego. Every design recommendation that crosses a population or context boundary relies on a bridge warrant, and the formula makes that translation auditable rather than implicit. In the course, Chapters 3 and 5 work together: Chapter 3 explains how evidence is translated across contexts; Chapter 5 explains how translated credences enter the projection model. A student who understands both can trace a design recommendation from the original study all the way to the final probability projection.

Theory Tiers: T1, T1.5, and T2

K-ATLAS distinguishes three epistemic tiers for theoretical content. The distinction matters because theories at different tiers warrant different kinds of inference, carry different entrenchment values, and play different roles in answer generation.

T1 — Core Neural Frameworks

T1 frameworks are well-confirmed neuroscientific and psychological frameworks with canonical experimental paradigms, established neural substrates, and high entrenchment. T1 status is reserved for frameworks with direct mechanistic accounts at the neural or physiological level. A T1 framework distributes theoretical warrants downward to empirical claims.

T1.5 — Domain Theories That Reduce to T1

T1.5 frameworks are domain-level theories — typically from environmental psychology — whose explanatory claims can be formally traced to one or more T1 mechanisms. They are less entrenched than T1 frameworks because their specific claims (e.g., "fascination" in Attention Restoration Theory) have not been fully operationalized at the neural level. Their warrant value derives partly from their reduction to T1.

T2 — Empirical Claims

T2 claims are the working claims of the EN: specific, testable propositions about observable phenomena that are supported by (or in tension with) empirical evidence. Every T2 claim has a credence, a credence interval, a population scope, and at minimum one warrant chain reaching to evidence. T2 claims may be SUPPORTED, CONTESTED, or UNKNOWN.

Example — T2 Claim with CONTESTED Edge

Claim (T2): "Exposure to fractal patterns (D ≈ 1.3–1.5) reduces physiological stress markers."

Credence: 0.63 | Interval: [0.48, 0.76]

Support: Taylor et al. (2006) — MECHANISM warrant (d=0.80); Hägerhäll et al. (2008) — EMPIRICAL_ASSOCIATION (d=0.80).

Contest: Spehar et al. (2016) — CONTEST edge — stimulus confound concern. This contest reduces credence by 0.07 and flags the claim for explicit uncertainty disclosure in QA answers.

Open Questions

Two deep questions about the tier architecture have not been answered. First, what is the principled basis for the T1 → T1.5 reduction relation? The system requires that T1.5 constructs be traceable to T1 mechanisms, but the reduction relation is assessed by expert judgment rather than formalised. A more precise account, drawing on Nagel's (1961) bridge-law model of theory reduction or on mechanistic explanation (Machamer, Darden, & Craver, 2000), is needed before the tier architecture can be presented as more than a useful heuristic. Second, what happens when a T1 framework is itself contested? Predictive processing (PP) — the highest-entrenchment T1 framework in K-ATLAS — has been challenged on empirical testability grounds (Colombo & Seriès, 2012). If PP's entrenchment should be revised downward, the revision propagates through all T1.5 theories that reduce to PP, potentially requiring a system-wide credence recalculation.

How We Use It

Every extraction assigns each claim to a tier, and this assignment determines which warrant types are permissible and how credences propagate. In practice, the tier table is the first reference when reading an unfamiliar paper: it tells you how much theoretical weight to give a paper's claims and what kind of warrant connects its findings to K-ATLAS beliefs. For course purposes, the T1.5 table — which lists domain theories with their reductions to T1 mechanisms — is the most useful teaching tool: it shows students that concepts like "fascination" in Attention Restoration Theory are not theoretical primitives but derived constructs that inherit their credence from predictive-processing and directed-attention research.

Chapter 5

Bayesian Network & Projection Calculus

The Evidence Network answers questions about what the literature says. The Bayesian Network (BN) answers a different class of question: given what we know about an environment or an intervention, what outcomes should we expect for a particular person? The BN is a Pearl-compliant Directed Acyclic Graph whose nodes represent measurable or latent variables and whose edges represent conditional dependencies calibrated from the EN's credence values.

Four Node Types in the BN

Node TypeExamplesRole
Observable (T3)Fractal dimension D, mEDI (lux), cortisol levelDirect measurement inputs
Latent MechanismDirected-attention fatigue, HPA activationUnobserved mediating variable; informed by T1 literature
OutcomeStress recovery, attentional restorationTarget quantity for projection
ModeratorChronotype, prior stress load, ageIndividual-difference conditioning variable

The Epistemic Causal Bridge (ECB)

The connection between the EN and the BN is mediated by the Epistemic Causal Bridge (ECB) — a π function that translates EN credence values into BN conditional probability table entries. A high-credence EN claim maps to a tight conditional probability distribution in the BN; a low-credence or contested claim maps to a diffuse distribution with high variance.

Example — Projection Calculus: Hospital Oncology Unit

Intervention query: "We are fitting a dayroom with biophilic elements: mEDI 250 lux at 9am–11am, fractal art (D=1.4), nature soundscape. Patient: F/52, high prior stress (SAM-9), morning chronotype. What is the projected stress-recovery probability?"

Step 1 — Observable inputs (T3): mEDI=250, D=1.4, soundscape complexity=moderate.

Step 2 — Activate BN paths: mEDI → Circadian alignment (CB, T_ent=0.93) → stress reduction. D → fractal response (T2, credence=0.63) → attention restoration (DT). Soundscape → autonomic regulation (IC, T_ent=0.78).

Step 3 — Apply moderators: High prior stress load increases SN pathway sensitivity (+0.12). Morning chronotype aligns with mEDI timing (+0.08).

Step 4 — Projection result: P(stress recovery within 40 min) = 0.74 [0.61, 0.84]. Dominant pathway: CB→SN (49% contribution). Contested pathway: fractal response flagged.

Dual-BN Diagnostic

K-ATLAS maintains two parallel BN configurations for every projection query. The empirically-grounded BN uses only conditional probabilities calibrated from actual study data. The theory-scaffolded BN supplements data with theoretical priors from T1 frameworks where direct evidence is sparse. Answers report both and flag the divergence when it exceeds a threshold — signalling to the user that theoretical inference is carrying significant weight in the projection.

Open Questions

Three questions about the BN design remain active. First, structure learning versus expert elicitation: the current BN topology was specified by expert judgment, not inferred from data. Structure learning algorithms (PC, GES) could in principle infer the DAG from the corpus, but they are sensitive to unobserved confounders and require sample sizes the corpus cannot yet supply; expert elicitation is probably correct for Phase α. Second, CPT calibration: conditional probability tables are currently populated via the π function from EN credence values, which is a rough approximation. Bayesian elicitation studies — eliciting CPT entries from domain experts and checking against observed co-occurrence frequencies — would strengthen the BN considerably. Third, when the dual-BN diagnostic reveals high divergence between the empirical and theory-scaffolded configurations, which should the QA answer trust? A principled divergence-resolution policy is needed.

How We Use It

The BN answers "what-if" design questions that the EN cannot. While the EN tells you what the literature says about forest exposure and cortisol, the BN tells you what outcome to expect for a specific person in a specific room with specific design parameters. For the VR stimulus design work, this is the key projection tool: the BN predicts which parameter combinations — fractal dimension, mEDI, soundscape complexity — should produce the largest stress-recovery effect for a given patient population. The dual-BN diagnostic is worth inspecting whenever a projection seems surprising: high divergence signals that theoretical inference is doing most of the work.

Chapter 6

The QA System

The QA layer is the user-facing entry point to everything K-ATLAS knows. A user's natural-language question is classified into one of 23 question types across five families, routed to the appropriate handler, and enriched through a nine-step pipeline before it is returned as an answer. The enrichment pipeline is where K-ATLAS distinguishes itself from a simple retrieval system: every answer is annotated with its epistemic status, its warrant chain, its population qualifications, and its genuine uncertainties.

The Evidence / Interpretation / Prediction Distinction

K-ATLAS enforces a strict three-way distinction in every answer. Evidence statements report what studies found. Interpretation statements report what K-ATLAS infers from that evidence using its warrant chains. Prediction statements report BN projections for specific contexts. These are never blurred — each is labelled explicitly in the answer surface, so the user always knows whether they are reading a finding, an inference, or a forecast.

Open Questions

Three questions about the QA design remain. First, which question types are missing from the 23 classified types? The current five-family structure covers research questions well, but design-practice questions — "How do I achieve a target cortisol-reduction effect within a given cost envelope?" — do not map cleanly to any existing family; a sixth Design Specification family may be needed. Second, how should the system handle genuinely contradictory warrant chains, where MECHANISM warrants and ANALOGICAL warrants support opposite conclusions? The current system falls back to net credence, but an adversarial presentation of competing chains would be more informative. Third, should the QA cache be indexed by question text, by belief cluster, or by warrant chain? Text-based indexing misses semantically equivalent questions; cluster-based indexing may invalidate too aggressively.

How We Use It

The QA system is where K-ATLAS becomes a research tool rather than a database. For students in the spring course, QA sessions are the primary interaction mode. The most important habit is to read the Population Qualifier (enrichment step 4) and the Gap Disclosure (step 7) before using any answer in a design argument — these are where the system tells you what it does not know and why. The Related Questions panel (step 9), generated by the Interpretation Space, often surfaces the question the student should have asked but did not know to ask.

Architecture & Contracts

K-ATLAS's architecture is specified through a contract system — a set of formal schema and interface definitions that every component must honour. Contracts are the authoritative specification of data structure and service behaviour. They are checked at runtime and enforced in tests. When code and contracts diverge, the contract wins.

K-ATLAS maintains epistemic integrity through three interlocking systems: a governance layer (the Overseer), a test suite that enforces contracts and success conditions, and a self-repair capability triggered automatically when the Overseer detects violations. Self-Repair Principle

The governing principle for self-repair is: a system that knows what it believes should also know when those beliefs are inconsistent — and should fix that inconsistency without waiting for human intervention. When the IntegrityChecker detects a violation (e.g., a high-credence belief with no warrant chain, or a population scope mismatch), the MaintenanceEngine selects the appropriate remediation playbook and executes it. The repair is logged to docs/overseer_reports/ for human review.

What's Missing

No honest description of K-ATLAS ends without an account of its gaps. What follows is a frank inventory of the intellectual capital that does not yet exist in systematic form, the components that are specified but not yet built, and the empirical coverage that the system still lacks. This chapter exists to be acted on: each gap listed here is an invitation to work.

Intellectual Capital Gaps

Missing: Defense of the Five Evaluative Criteria

The five criteria used to evaluate evidence quality (mechanistic integration, replication, population specificity, measurement precision, theoretical grounding) exist as heuristics in the codebase but have never been formally argued for. A dedicated document making the philosophical case — with references to Haack, Pollock, and contemporary philosophy of science — is needed before these criteria can be presented as principled rather than pragmatic.

Missing: T1/T1.5/T2 Stratification Argument

The three-tier stratification is used throughout the codebase but nowhere fully argued for as an epistemological framework. Why are these the right tiers? What is the principled basis for the T1 → T1.5 reduction relation? What happens when a T1 framework is itself contested? A 15–20 page argument needs to be written, drawing on Nagel's (1961) account of theoretical reduction and more recent accounts of mechanistic explanation.

Missing: ACh/NE to Architectural Pre-information Note

There is a strong theoretical connection between neuromodulator dynamics (acetylcholine and noradrenaline in particular) and what David Kirsh has called "architectural pre-information" — the way environments prime cognitive states before task engagement. This connection appears in scattered working notes but has never been assembled into a focused argument. A 3–5 page theoretical note would add significant depth to both the IC and NM framework entries.

Missing: δ Function First-Principles Account

The population similarity scalar δ(pop, pop_target) in the bridge warrant formula is currently implemented as a lookup table with five binary dimensions (age match, health status, cultural context, measurement context, species). This is adequate for operational use but theoretically unsatisfying. A first-principles derivation — perhaps from Bayesian model comparison or from information-theoretic measures of distributional distance — would put the bridge warrant formula on more rigorous ground.

Chapter 10

The Four Service Layers

Layer 3 of the K-ATLAS architecture — Core Services — contains four subsystems that together provide the reasoning, interpretation, question-answering, and knowledge-annotation capabilities of the system. Each subsystem is architecturally distinct: it has its own contract, its own data models, and its own relationship to the Evidence Network. Understanding each layer separately is essential for understanding both what K-ATLAS can do and where it is currently incomplete.

1 — The Argumentation Layer

The Argumentation Layer sits between the raw evidence network and the QA system. Its function is to structure the logical relationships between claims, papers, and findings — not merely to note that evidence exists, but to represent how pieces of evidence support, critique, contest, or hierarchically organise one another. Where the Evidence Network asks "What do we believe and how strongly?", the Argumentation Layer asks "Why should we believe it, and what are the strongest objections?"

Walton Argument Schemes

The vocabulary of argument types used in the Argumentation Layer draws on Douglas Walton's taxonomy of argumentation schemes — a systematic classification of the logical patterns that appear in natural-language reasoning. K-ATLAS uses a subset of Walton's schemes adapted for scientific reasoning, stored in contracts/vocab/argument_schemes.json. The most frequently occurring schemes in the biophilic environment literature are: argument from analogy (cross-species transfer), argument from expert opinion (theoretical authority), argument from sign (biomarker as indicator), and argument from consequences (design implications of a finding).

Current Status: Partial

The ArgumentationEngine is built and tested. However, argument graphs are currently in-memory only (not persisted to the database), so they must be rebuilt on every session. The full Walton scheme implementation is incomplete: only 8 of the 25 planned schemes are classified. Orchestrator-level tests are missing. See Chapter 9 for the full gap analysis.

Contested Views

The argumentation schemes approach adopted in K-ATLAS derives primarily from Douglas Walton and colleagues (Walton, Reed, & Macagno, 2008; Walton, 1996). Walton's contribution was to systematise the informal logic tradition into a taxonomy of roughly 25 canonical presumptive argument patterns, each with associated critical questions that defeat the argument when unanswered — the scheme approach. This is the framework implemented in contracts/vocab/argument_schemes.json.

The competing research tradition is pragma-dialectics, developed by Frans van Eemeren and Rob Grootendorst in their 1992 book Argumentation, Communication, and Fallacies. Where Walton focuses on classifying individual argument moves and their defeating conditions, van Eemeren and Grootendorst focus on the dialectical procedure for rationally resolving a dispute — an idealised "critical discussion" governed by ten procedural rules (the freedom rule, the burden-of-proof rule, the standpoint rule, the relevance rule, and so on). On the pragma-dialectical account, a fallacy is not a misused scheme but a violation of procedural rules for rational discussion.

The practical difference for K-ATLAS is significant. Walton's scheme approach is computationally tractable: you tag argument types and check critical questions. Van Eemeren's dialectical approach requires modelling the full dialogue structure — confrontation, opening, argumentation, and concluding stages — which is much harder to implement for a static literature corpus. However, the pragma-dialectical framework is arguably better suited to explaining why a scientific disagreement exists and what conditions would resolve it — which matters for the Dialectical (R₄) operator in the Interpretation Space. The two frameworks are not necessarily in conflict: Walton's schemes could be used to classify individual argument moves within a pragma-dialectical dispute structure, combining the tractability of the one with the diagnostic power of the other.

Open Questions

Three questions are unresolved. First, should argument scheme classification be automatic (ML-based, applied during extraction) or manual (expert review at sprint boundaries)? Automatic classification would scale, but requires training data and may produce false positives for domain-specific schemes not in Walton's canonical list. Second, argument graphs are currently in-memory only — they must be rebuilt each session. A JSON graph file (fast to write, inspectable) and a SQLite adjacency table (queryable, harder to visualise) are both viable persistence options; the choice has implications for how the Overseer monitors graph health. Third, should the Argumentation Layer classify the global argument pattern of an entire paper — "this paper makes a convergent argument from three independent lines of evidence" — or only classify local moves between individual claims? Global classification would be more informative for systematic review but is substantially harder to automate.

How We Use It

The Argumentation Engine is currently invoked primarily through engine.vulnerability_report(), which surfaces in QA answers as the "Alternative Explanations" enrichment step (step 8). For a student asking "Is biophilia well-supported?", the vulnerability report is the most informative output — it tells them not just that the credence is 0.43 but specifically why: WEIRD sampling bias, an unreplicated fractal-dimension claim, and two direct theoretical challenges. The PaperRelation component is most useful for students doing systematic review work — it identifies which papers in the corpus replicate, extend, or directly contest any other paper, saving manual cross-referencing time.

2 — The Interpretation Space Layer

The Interpretation Space is the most philosophically distinctive component of K-ATLAS. Its purpose is to make explicit the conceptual space of possible questions that a given body of evidence opens up — not just to answer questions that users ask, but to generate the questions that users might not know to ask. It is, in this sense, a question-generation engine rather than a question-answering engine.

The theoretical basis for the Interpretation Space is the concept of closure operators — mathematical operations on a set that produce, from any element, all of the elements that can be derived from it by a fixed rule. K-ATLAS defines four closure operators, R₁ through R₄, that generate four types of question from any T2 mechanism template:

OperatorQuestion typeExample (Attention Restoration)
R₁Definitional"What exactly is directed-attention fatigue? How is it operationalised in the laboratory?"
R₂Evidential"What is the warrant chain behind the claim that natural environments restore directed attention? How strong is each link?"
R₃Applied"What are the design implications of the directed-attention restoration mechanism for workplace environments?"
R₄Dialectical"What are the strongest objections to ART as the primary account of nature's restorative effect? How does ART compare with Stress Recovery Theory?"
Example — Interpretation Space in Operation

A graduate student uses K-ATLAS to research biophilic design. She asks about "fractal patterns." The QA system returns an evidence summary. The Interpretation Space Engine simultaneously generates 16 questions from the fractal-dimension template (4 × R₁–R₄). Three of these questions appear in the "You might also want to know" panel at the bottom of the QA answer:

R₂: "How strong is the evidence that the fractal stress-reduction effect generalises beyond laboratory settings?"

R₃: "Which architectural surfaces are most amenable to fractal content at the stress-reducing D range (1.3–1.5)?"

R₄: "Does the fractal dimension effect compete with or complement the colour and luminance effects identified in the same paradigm?"

These are questions the student would not have thought to ask, but each opens a substantive direction of inquiry. This is the Interpretation Space doing its job.

Question Storage

Generated questions are stored in data/interpretation_space/questions.json as a searchable registry of QuestionRecord objects. Each record carries: a unique ID (template_id::question_type::index), the question text, the operator type (definitional/evidential/applied/dialectical), the source template, the mechanism name, and a confidence score (0–1). The registry is regenerated when templates change.

Current Status: Partially Built

The InterpretationSpaceEngine is implemented and generates questions from templates. The InterpretationSpaceSuggestionsManager tracks which suggestions have been acted on. However, the live wiring to the QA answer surface (Step 9 of the enrichment pipeline) is not yet complete — generated questions are stored but not yet reliably surfaced in QA answers. The suggestion-tracking loop (monitoring whether generated questions lead to article discovery) is registered in the Overseer but not yet fully operational.

Open Questions

Three questions about the Interpretation Space remain active. First, is the four-operator (R₁–R₄) taxonomy complete? A fifth operator (R₅) that generates questions about future experiments — "What study would most efficiently fill the largest credence gap adjacent to this mechanism?" — is arguably missing; generating it requires value-of-information analysis rather than template closure. Second, should generated questions be ranked by epistemic value, i.e., by the expected credence gain if the question were answered? This would require a VOI calculation for each question using the BN — feasible but computationally expensive at scale. Third, the suggestion-tracking loop — does a generated question eventually lead to article discovery? — is registered in the Overseer but not yet operational. Without this feedback signal, it is impossible to evaluate whether the Interpretation Space is generating useful questions or merely plausible-sounding ones.

How We Use It

The Interpretation Space currently feeds the "Related Questions" panel (QA enrichment step 9), though this wiring is not yet complete. In the intended design, every QA answer surfaces three to five generated questions from the relevant mechanism templates: the R₁ (definitional) question for students new to the topic, the R₂ (evidential) question for those who want to interrogate the evidence base, and the R₄ (dialectical) question for those who want to understand the contested terrain. For the fall course, the Interpretation Space is the most promising tool for guided independent research: rather than assigning a reading list, the course could assign three R₄ questions per topic and ask students to find papers that answer them — turning the Interpretation Space into a research curriculum generator.

The QA Answer Norms Contract

The QA layer is governed by a formal norms contract (contracts/QA_ANSWER_NORMS.md) that specifies how the nine enrichment outputs are translated into prose. The norms synthesize the science communication practices of eight scholars whose work bears directly on how epistemic uncertainty should be communicated to different audiences: Daniel Kahneman (confidence calibration), Philip Tetlock (probabilistic forecasting communication), Naomi Oreskes (communicating scientific consensus and disagreement), Steven Pinker (curse of knowledge), Carl Sagan (honest uncertainty), Andrew Gelman (statistical communication), Brian Nosek (reproducibility and publication bias), and Deborah Mayo (severe testing).

Three norms from this contract are worth highlighting because they are most frequently violated in conventional research synthesis:

NormSourceWhat it requires in K-ATLAS answers
Calibrated confidenceTetlock (2005, 2015)Express credence as a numerical range (e.g., 0.65–0.75), not as verbal hedges ("the evidence suggests"). Numerical expressions are more honest and more useful to decision-makers.
Publication bias disclosureNosek et al. (2012, 2022)All QA answers involving positive findings must flag that the K-ATLAS corpus is biased toward published positive results. Null results are systematically underrepresented.
Evidence / interpretation / prediction labellingSagan (1996); Mayo (1996)Every sentence in a QA answer is labelled as a finding (what studies observed), an inference (what K-ATLAS concludes from the evidence using warrants), or a projection (a BN-derived prediction for a specific context). These are never mixed.

QA Caching

Answers are cached by the QACacheManager to avoid redundant enrichment computation. The cache is invalidated on every new paper integration (Step 14 of the cascade) — because any new paper might change the credence of a belief that an existing answer depends on. Cache invalidation is tracked by the Overseer as an invariant: a stale cache is an integrity violation.

Open Questions

Three norms-contract questions remain. First, should the contract be extended to cover domain-specific communication conventions? An architect's question ("What D-value should I specify for my fractal façade?") calls for a different answer format than a neuroscientist's question ("What is the neural pathway from fractal perception to stress reduction?"). Domain-specific templates would improve usability but require user profiling. Second, the three-way evidence / interpretation / prediction labelling is the hardest norm to enforce automatically, because the boundary between inference and projection is context-sensitive. A formal decision procedure specifying when a sentence should be labelled INFERENCE versus PROJECTION is needed before this labelling can be automated reliably. Third, should the QA cache be indexed by question text, by belief cluster, or by warrant chain? Text-based indexing misses semantically equivalent questions; cluster-based indexing may invalidate too aggressively when a new paper touches a large belief cluster.

How We Use It

The QA system is the point where K-ATLAS becomes a research tool rather than an infrastructure layer. For students in the spring course, QA sessions are the primary interaction mode, and the most important reading habit is to check the Population Qualifier (step 4) and Gap Disclosure (step 7) before using any answer in a design argument. The evidence / interpretation / prediction labelling (step 2) is worth examining in class: it makes explicit the epistemic status of each sentence in an answer, practising a form of epistemic vigilance that students rarely develop from reading papers alone.

4 — The Annotation Layer

The Annotation Layer is the system's memory for metadata that is not extractable from papers themselves. Every belief in the Evidence Network has a fixed factual content — the claim that was extracted from a source study. The Annotation Layer allows the system (and named human experts) to attach additional structured knowledge to any belief, edge, or card, without modifying the source record. Annotations are append-only and immutable after creation; they are superseded, not overwritten, when they become outdated.

Authorship and Lifecycle

Annotations are authored by either the system (automated annotation from QA and Overseer processes) or named human experts. The authorship model is flat: David Kirsh's annotations have the same data structure as system-generated annotations, but are distinguishable by the author field. No crowdsourcing is planned in Phase α. All annotations are stored in SQLite (a separate table from the L2 JSON templates) and are applied at query time rather than baked into the belief record — ensuring that the raw extraction data is never contaminated by annotation metadata.

Open Questions

Three design questions remain. First, should annotations be crowd-sourced beyond the current single-expert model? Adding multiple annotators would increase coverage but introduces inter-annotator agreement as a new data-quality problem. A trust-weighted model — where expert annotations carry higher weight in QA answers than student annotations — is a natural solution, but specifying trust weights is itself an expert judgment problem that has not been solved. Second, four new annotation types have been proposed but not yet implemented: HISTORICAL_PRIORITY (flags claims where priority of discovery is contested), REPLICATION_DIVERGENCE (marks cases where replication produced a significantly different effect size), THEORETICAL_COMMITMENT (marks claims that presuppose a contested theoretical framework), and DESIGN_THRESHOLD (annotates the specific parameter value at which a design implication becomes actionable). Third, the append-only immutability constraint means incorrect annotations accumulate — they can be superseded but not removed. A formal supersession policy, specifying when and how superseded annotations affect credence computation, is needed.

How We Use It

The Annotation Layer is the primary channel for injecting domain expertise into K-ATLAS without modifying the source extraction data. A new annotation requires three fields — the belief ID, the annotation type, and the content — added through annotation_service.add_annotation(). In practice, the most common annotation types are DESIGN_IMPLICATION (attaching an architectural recommendation to a belief) and OPEN_QUESTION (flagging an unresolved question from a QA session). For the fall course, a student team with annotation privileges could collectively annotate the corpus over the semester, building a structured layer of expert commentary on top of the extracted evidence — a form of peer learning that would benefit subsequent cohorts.
