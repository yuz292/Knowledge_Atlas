◎Goalwhat this deliverable teaches

You will run a short moderated usability study with five participants drawn from at least two of the three user archetypes (public visitor, COGS 160 student, administrator). Each participant works through three scripted scenarios from T4.b while thinking aloud. You will record what actually happened, time each task, code moments of friction, and produce a structured findings file that feeds directly into T4.d (severity triage) and T4.f (redesign proposal).

This is the hardest deliverable in Track 4 because the craft is not in running the sessions — the protocol is short — but in resisting the temptation to explain, defend, or rescue the interface while a participant is stuck. The Nielsen & Landauer (1993) result that five users surface roughly 85% of usability issues assumes an observer who reports what was observed rather than what the observer wishes had been observed.

›What you'll dothe steps

  1. Recruit five participants across at least two archetypes. Friends and classmates are fine; do not recruit anyone who has already audited the site for you (that contaminates the pilot).
  2. Write a one-page moderator script covering consent ("I'm testing the site, not you"), the think-aloud instruction, and the three scenarios. The scenarios come from your T4.b file — do not invent new ones here.
  3. Run each session for no more than 40 minutes. Screen-record if the participant consents. Otherwise take timestamped notes.
  4. For each task, record: completion (yes / no / gave-up), time-on-task, errors observed, verbal friction moments, and your post-hoc severity estimate.
  5. After all five sessions, code the notes into structured finding records (one per distinct problem). A problem observed in ≥ 2 sessions gets a "recurrence" tag; this is the filter for band-3 credit.
  6. Submit one pilot_log.md per session plus one pilot_findings.md synthesis. Both go under 160sp/tracks/t4/{your_id}/pilot/.

✗Example of a naive approachso you can see what scores low

The most common failure mode is a pilot log that reads like an auto-reassuring monologue rather than a trace of what the participant did. The example below is a lightly-edited composite of real band-0 submissions from prior cohorts. Read it and note everything the moderator did for the participant.

band 0–1 example — do NOT write pilot logs like this

Pilot session 2 · 2026-04-28

Today I tested my roommate. She loves the site! I asked her to find a paper on lighting and she clicked around for a bit and then I showed her where the topic page was and she said oh that's really clear. Then we did the student-setup page. I told her to pick a track and she picked T1 because it's the most colourful. Overall she found the site very intuitive. Success on all three tasks.

Why this scores band 0 or 1. The log contradicts its own protocol at every turn. The moderator disclosed affect ("she loves the site") before the task even began, framing consent negotiation as flattery rather than informed choice. When the participant could not find the topic page, the moderator showed her where it was — which is the single action the think-aloud protocol most explicitly forbids (Ericsson & Simon 1993). The track selection is attributed to colour preference without evidence the participant said that; attribution of cognition to the observer's guess is the classic think-aloud error. No timings are recorded, no verbal trace is quoted, and the "success" judgment is the moderator's opinion, not the participant's behaviour. A participant who is guided to the answer by the moderator has not completed the task; they have completed a tour. For band 3 you need: verbatim quotes, wall-clock timings, a literal record of navigation, and your own hands off the keyboard.

✓Verification

how your work is graded
  • Automated: the AI grader counts session-log files (≥ 5 expected), checks the template is complete, and validates that every scenario has a start/end timestamp and at least one verbatim quote.
  • Automated: a recurrence pass groups findings that appear in ≥ 2 session logs; recurrence count enters the Quality band.
  • TA audit: 30% of submissions are re-read end-to-end by a TA; moderator-intervention leaks (the failure mode in the naive example above) are the most common deduction.
  • Feeds-forward: your pilot_findings.md is the input to T4.d. If your severity ratings in T4.d disagree with what your own pilot notes imply, T4.d loses a band.

Scoring bands (0–3 per criterion, rescaled to the 15-point value):
