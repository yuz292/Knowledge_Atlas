Week 3

Question for the week: What is the smallest end-to-end transformation that proves the pipeline is real?

Use AI well

Before you ask AI for help, make sure you can state what you are trying to learn this week and what evidence would count as a useful answer.

Class meetings

Meeting A — Build one complete proof of concept
Start here: Name the smallest successful transformation you could demonstrate by the end of the week.

In class

  • Choose one room and one operator.
  • Trace the whole path: room selection, parameter choice, room edit, validation, export, and evidence capture.
  • Do not expand scope until the class has one modest but complete example.
  • Ask AI for debugging help only after you can state where the pipeline is failing.

Home

  • Complete one end-to-end demonstration outside class.
  • Record the exact sequence of steps, including failed attempts and the final successful path.
  • Prepare evidence that another team could reproduce the same result.
Meeting B — Review failure modes and stabilize the first pipeline
Start here: List the three places your pipeline is most likely to break.

In class

  • Show every team's proof of concept, including what broke.
  • As a class, identify recurring failure categories: asset problems, operator ambiguity, toolchain mismatch, export problems, scene-structure breakage, validation gaps.
  • Decide which failures are worth fixing now and which ones should be documented as limitations.
  • Create a common validation checklist for the next stage.

Home

  • Refine the pipeline on a second room or second parameter setting.
  • Write short notes on which parts are robust and which parts still depend on luck or manual intervention.
  • Update your operator sheets if the implementation exposed hidden assumptions.

Week deliverables

D5 First end-to-end proof of concept

Rationale
The studio needs an early concrete success. A modest but complete transformation is worth more than a large but unfinished architecture diagram.

What to submit
One room, one controlled transformation, one exported result, and a short reproducibility note explaining exactly how the result was obtained.

What good work looks like
The transformation is small enough to be real and complete. The export can be opened and inspected. The team can explain what was changed and what remains unstable.

Example
A room with a reliably increased ceiling height, updated lighting positions, preserved navigation, and a short video showing before, after, and validation checks.

D6 Validation checklist v1

Rationale
Once the first transformations exist, the class needs a shared way to judge whether a result is acceptable.

What to submit
A one-page checklist covering geometry sanity, openings, collision, material continuity, hierarchy, exportability, and transformation logging.

What good work looks like
The checklist is specific enough to use during testing and short enough that teams will actually use it every week.

Example
The checklist asks whether doors and windows remain aligned to walls, whether walkable paths still exist, and whether the changed parameters were recorded in a metadata note.
