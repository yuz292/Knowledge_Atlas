Track ONE: Image Tagging

Develop annotation tools that label architectural and environmental images with evidence-based tags aligned with K-ATLAS propositions. Learn data verification and pipeline architecture.

Start Track 1 Setup → Learn about Track 1

What the Image Tagger Is

At its simplest, the Image Tagger is an image database that lets scientists see real-life examples of the stimuli their experiments are supposed to tell us about. You can use it to locate ecologically valid photographs of the kinds of rooms, corridors, lobbies, cafés, or outdoor spaces a hypothesis turns on, rather than relying on stylised lab renderings or hand-picked exemplars.

At a deeper level, by running tagging procedures that extract what we believe are the abstract features of an environment — visual complexity, biophilic content, regional fractal dimension, shape, size, openness, material composition, and so on — we can look for commonalities among environments that look superficially very different. Equally important, we can then query images using the properties of the stimuli used in published experiments, rather than searching by everyday folk terms such as "dining room," "corridor," or "wood floors." This is what makes the tagger a bridge between the experimental literature and the visual world the literature is supposed to describe.

To understand more about this part of the system, read the page describing the image-tagging repo — its function and structure — here: Image Tagger Guide →. To learn what is implemented, what remains, and where student effort is most needed, see the Image Tagger Status Report (PDF) →.

Sprint 1 (Weeks 3–5): Integrate Latent-Variable Tags into Tagging_Contractor

Before you can detect a latent variable such as Sociopetal Seating Topology (L44) or Prospect (L17), the system has to know about it in a machine-readable, quality-controlled way. That is the job of the Tagging_Contractor registry. Your first sprint is to move the L01–L58 latent variables documented in the Environment–Cognition Taxonomy V2.6 into the registry with the full semantic contract that production extraction requires. This is the single most leveraged activity in the track: every downstream extractor, every BN node, every piece of HITL training data inherits its meaning from what you write here.

Why this is not copy-and-paste

A taxonomy row in the V2.6 spreadsheet is roughly five fields: ID, name, short definition, a handful of 2D cues, a tier. A production tag entry in Tagging_Contractor is roughly fifty fields across seven categories — identity, extractability, extraction method, Bayesian-network role, value type, semantics (aliases, positive/negative examples, scope_includes / scope_excludes, extraction_notes for 2D and 3D, factor_associations), and literature. The registry's semantic gate (./bin/tc doctor --prod) refuses to release a tag until every required field is filled with non-placeholder content. The work is therefore epistemic, not clerical: you are fixing the meaning of each construct well enough that two different annotators, or an LLM and a human, will agree on what counts as a positive case (cf. Krippendorff, 2018, on construct specification as a prerequisite to reliable coding).

Do These Fields Require Panels? (Short Answer: Yes, Four of Them.)

A "panel" in this course is not a bureaucratic ceremony; it is the deliberative mechanism we use whenever a decision involves genuine disagreement among reasonable experts. The style we recommend follows the Delphi / modified-nominal-group tradition (Dalkey & Helmer, 1963; Murphy et al., 1998): each panellist writes an independent first pass, the group surfaces disagreements explicitly, and the final wording is the minimum formulation that all panellists can defend in writing. Four of the seven field groups meet this bar and therefore require a panel, even if the panel is only three students plus the TA:

  1. Extractability. Whether Prospect is recoverable from a single 2D photo is a contested empirical claim (Stamps, 2014, argues yes with caveats; Dosen & Ostwald, 2016, argue only with floor-plan augmentation). Without panel deliberation the field becomes a guess.
  2. Extraction method. Choice of method_family commits downstream students to months of implementation work. Whether L44 (Sociopetal Seating) is best computed from object-segmentation + pairwise-angle geometry or from a VLM prompt is itself a research question.
  3. BN role. Whether a latent is a true hidden node or an aggregated observation determines whether it can be learned, clamped, or merely reported. Spohn-style ranking-theoretic concerns arise here (Spohn, 2012).
  4. Semantics (scope_includes / scope_excludes / examples). This is where construct validity lives or dies (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955; Flake & Fried, 2020, on the "validity crisis" in psychology caused by under-specified constructs). Two students who write independent scope boundaries for Interactional Visibility (L42) will produce non-overlapping sets of positive examples unless they deliberate.

Value type is only partially panel-worthy — the type (boolean / ordinal / continuous) is often dictated by the instrument, but the choice of Likert anchors is a semantic decision. Identity and literature fields do not require panels; they are research tasks one student can do alone.

Team Size & Roles (Recommended)

This is a multi-person task. A single student working alone will either (a) shortcut the semantic contract, producing entries the tc doctor --prod gate rejects, or (b) spend the entire quarter on three tags. We recommend teams of three, with the following role division, rotating weekly so everyone touches every role:

  • Driver (semantics & examples). Writes definition_short, definition_long, scope_includes, scope_excludes, examples_positive (≥ 6), examples_negative (≥ 4). Consults the original empirical papers for each latent.
  • Engineer (extractability, method, BN). Writes from_2d / from_3d_vr / monocular_3d_approx, compute_from, method_family, demand_state, evidence_role. Must be able to sketch the detection pipeline on paper before committing.
  • Auditor (literature, gate, integration). Fills APA literature, runs ./bin/tc doctor --prod, runs ./bin/tc audit-semantics and ./bin/tc audit-extraction-plan, writes the change_log, drafts the handoff phase bundle.

A class-sized cohort of nine Image-Tagger students therefore yields three panel-teams. Each team owns roughly six latent variables in Sprint 1 (18 social-interaction latents L41–L58 divided three ways), then expands to cognition/affect latents from L01–L40 in Sprint 2. This matches the empirical team-size evidence for knowledge-elicitation tasks of this kind: three-person teams outperform both pairs and larger groups on conceptual-definition work (Hackman & Vidmar, 1970; Laughlin et al., 2006).

7-Step Scaffold for Sprint 1

Each step maps onto the course's AI-directed development method:

  1. Panel. Read registry_v0.2.8.json for two existing tags (e.g., affect.cozy and affordance.isovist_area) to see what a finished entry looks like. Convene your three-person panel. For each of your assigned latents, produce an independent first pass of the four panel-critical field groups (extractability, method, BN role, semantics). Meet to reconcile; minute the disagreements.
  2. Spec. Write a one-page tag-specification document per latent, covering all seven field groups. Use the same schema as registry_v0.2.8.json; write it in Markdown first, not JSON, so your AI assistant can read and critique it.
  3. Tests. Draft positive and negative image sets (6 + 4 minimum) for each latent. These become the gold-set for downstream extractor testing. Record why each image is a positive or negative — this is the construct-validity trail.
  4. Build. Translate the Markdown spec to a JSON patch against registry_v0.2.8.json. Open a feature branch track1/latents/L4x-L5x-your-team.
  5. Verify. Run the registry's validation chain: ./bin/tc audit-tags → ./bin/tc audit-semantics → ./bin/tc audit-extraction-plan → ./bin/tc doctor --prod. Fix every gap the gate identifies; do not ship with warnings suppressed.
  6. Diagnose. Have a second panel-team do a blind read of your positive / negative examples without seeing your definition — can they reverse-engineer the intended construct? This is the adversarial-collaboration test (Clark & Tetlock, 2022) and it catches ambiguity that your own team cannot see.
  7. Iterate. Revise based on blind-read failures. Re-run tc doctor --prod. When green, hand off the phase bundle to the Article-Eater track so its HITL reviewers can use the examples as ground truth.
  8. Stub. For every latent you defined, add a one-function extractor stub (def detect_L44(image, segments, depth) -> float: raise NotImplementedError) in tagging_pipeline/extractors/latents/. The stub guarantees that Sprint 2 begins with a code hook per latent, not a fresh scaffolding problem, and it makes the contract between registry entry and production code syntactically enforceable.
Deliverable for Week 5: a merged PR against Tagging_Contractor adding at minimum the L41–L58 social-interaction latents to registry_v0.2.8.json, passing ./bin/tc doctor --prod with zero errors and zero suppressed warnings, with an accompanying LATENT_INTEGRATION_NOTES_2026-05-04.md that records every panel-level disagreement and its resolution. This document is itself a research artefact — it is the construct-validity provenance for the extractors that follow in Sprints 2–4.

Sprint 2 (Weeks 5–6): Rule-Based Extractors — One Per Latent

With the registry written and the stubs in place, Sprint 2 gets each latent producing a value on a real image. The rule is: implement the cheapest viable detector consistent with the method_family your team committed to in Sprint 1. Cheap does not mean sloppy; it means making the computation as transparent as possible so that a failure mode is diagnosable by reading the code, not by probing a neural network. Most of L41–L58 reduce to one of three computations over the tagger's existing intermediate representations:

  • Furniture geometry. L44 Sociopetal Seating, L48 Dyadic Intimacy, L49 Small-Group Support, and L53 Shared Attention Anchor are recoverable from pairwise angles, distances, and cluster counts over the COCO-133 segment masks already produced by the tagger. The classical F-formation geometry (Kendon, 1990; Marquardt et al., 2012) gives the predicate set.
  • Sightlines & isovists. L42 Interactional Visibility, L54 Boundary Permeability, and L17 Prospect (once Sprint 2 expands into cognition/affect latents) are recoverable from isovist area, maximum viewshed depth, and partition-height ratios computed over the existing monocular depth map (Benedikt, 1979; Wiener et al., 2007).
  • Circulation & apertures. L41 Chance-Encounter Potential, L47 Turn-Taking Support, and L57 Disengagement Ease reduce to path counts, aperture widths, and dead-end detection over a skeletonised floor mask (Hillier & Hanson, 1984).

7-Step Scaffold for Sprint 2

  1. Panel. For each of your six latents, write a one-page detector design note that identifies (a) the upstream tags the detector consumes, (b) the geometric / logical predicate that encodes the latent, (c) the failure modes you expect. Panel-critique your teammates' notes before coding begins.
  2. Spec. Convert the design note into a typed function signature and a docstring written in the same vocabulary as the registry's definition_long. The docstring is the contract; if it drifts from the registry, the semantic gate will catch it.
  3. Tests. Write pytest cases against the Sprint 1 gold-set before implementing the detector. At minimum: six passing positives, four passing negatives. Add regression fixtures for every edge case the panel surfaced.
  4. Build. Implement the detector. Keep each detector under 150 lines; if it grows beyond that, the predicate is probably wrong. Commit frequently to track1/extractors/L4x-your-team.
  5. Verify. Wire the detector into the Image-Tagger pipeline as a new extractor stage. Run the full pipeline on a 50-image diagnostic batch. Confirm that output columns match the registry's value_type and range fields.
  6. Diagnose. Plot the per-latent value distribution across the 50-image batch. Distributions that are constant, degenerate, or bimodal in unexpected ways flag a predicate bug.
  7. Iterate. Fix predicates that failed gold-set tests. If a latent cannot be made to pass the gold-set with a defensible rule, this is a scientific finding — downgrade the registry entry's from_2d to false and defer to the VLM backstop in Sprint 3.
Deliverable for Week 6: six working rule-based detectors per team, merged into the Image-Tagger pipeline, each passing its pytest gold-set and each producing a non-degenerate value distribution over a 50-image diagnostic batch.
Mid-Sprint Checkpoint — End of Week 5 (Friday, no timeline slip)

Sprint 2 and Sprint 3 sit back-to-back, and a team that loses Week 5 to one unworkable predicate will carry that pain into Sprint 3 when the VLM was meant to be the backstop, not the lifeline. To prevent that without pushing any downstream deliverable, every team holds a 30-minute TA-run checkpoint by end of Week 5 (Friday) in which each of the team's six latents is classified into exactly one of three buckets:

  • On track. Rule-based detector is drafted, at least four of the ten gold-set cases pass, and the team expects to close the remaining cases in Week 6. No action required.
  • Demote to VLM-only for Sprint 3. The rule-based predicate is defeated by the construct (typical for circulation-graph latents where floor-mask skeletonisation is under-determined from a 2D photo). The team files a one-paragraph Extractability Finding that amends the latent's from_2d field in the registry to false and commits to relying on the VLM backstop in Sprint 3. This is a scientific result, not a concession, and must be logged as such in the team's decision log.
  • Escalate for panel reassignment. If three or more of a team's six latents land in the "demote" bucket, the TA convenes a short cross-team panel to swap one or two latents with a team that is over-performing. Load is rebalanced rather than deferred.

Why this preserves the timeline. The checkpoint replaces quiet slippage with an explicit, 30-minute triage. Week 6 ends with six detectors per team either working or formally flagged as VLM-dependent — in both cases the Sprint 2 Week-6 deliverable is met. The Sprint 3 bake-off then runs on known terrain rather than discovering the demotion in the middle of its own sprint (Tuckman & Jensen, 1977, on the cost of unacknowledged storming; Edmondson, 1999, on psychological safety as a precondition for surfacing failure early in team work).

Sprint 3 (Weeks 7–8): VLM Backstop & Rule-vs-VLM Bake-Off

Rule-based detectors are transparent but brittle; vision-language models are opaque but robust to variation the rules never anticipated. Sprint 3 builds the second extractor and pits the two methods against each other on a 200-image held-out batch. The bake-off is not a horse race; it is a construct-validity probe. Where the two methods agree, confidence is high; where they disagree, the registry's semantic contract is probably ambiguous and must be rewritten. This is the Cronbach & Meehl (1955) multitrait-multimethod matrix applied in miniature (see also Campbell & Fiske, 1959).

7-Step Scaffold for Sprint 3

  1. Panel. Draft VLM prompts by copying the Sprint 1 definition_long + scope_includes + scope_excludes fields verbatim. The panel's job is to audit that the prompt says nothing the registry does not already commit to — if the prompt "improves" on the definition, the definition is at fault.
  2. Spec. Choose the VLM (GPT-4o-mini or Claude Haiku 4.5 for throughput; Claude Sonnet 4.6 for reliability) and write a thin wrapper that returns the same value type as the rule-based detector for each latent.
  3. Tests. Run the VLM on the Sprint 1 gold-set. Expect ≥ 90% accuracy on positives and ≥ 80% on negatives; a VLM that fails the gold-set is signalling definition ambiguity, not model weakness.
  4. Build. Pull a 200-image held-out batch from the Image-Tagger pool (stratified across the 13 room types so no team's latents are evaluated only on living rooms).
  5. Verify. Run both extractors on the full batch. Compute per-latent precision, recall, and method agreement (Cohen's κ for boolean latents, Krippendorff's α for ordinal / continuous). Log runtime and cost.
  6. Diagnose. For each latent with κ < 0.6 or α < 0.6, pull the disagreement cases and panel-review them. The typical finding is that the definition is silent on some scene configuration the rule and the VLM resolve oppositely.
  7. Iterate. Rewrite the registry's scope_includes / scope_excludes to resolve each disagreement, re-run both extractors, and push the registry update. This is the feedback loop Flake & Fried (2020) call "continuous validity monitoring" — contracts that cannot survive operational contact are revised, not sanctified.
Deliverable for Week 8: a Bake-Off Report per team — one page per latent with the rule vs. VLM agreement statistics, runtime / cost accounting, and a short prose section titled "Definition Revisions This Bake-Off Forced" that documents every registry change the disagreement cases induced.

Sprint 4 (Weeks 9–10): HITL Reliability & Production Handoff

Inter-method agreement (Sprint 3) is necessary but not sufficient. Two extractors can agree with each other and both be wrong against human judgement. Sprint 4 closes the loop by running extractor outputs through the HITL review page that Track 2 will have built by Week 6 (see Task A on the Track 2 page). Each latent earns — or loses — its status: production flag on the strength of its agreement with human reviewers.

7-Step Scaffold for Sprint 4

  1. Panel. Define the HITL rating rubric per latent. Five-point Likert anchors, written in the same vocabulary as the registry. Pilot-test the rubric on two images per latent within the team before releasing it to reviewers.
  2. Spec. Prepare the review queue — for each latent, stratify 40 images into four bins: rule-positive / VLM-positive, rule-positive / VLM-negative, rule-negative / VLM-positive, rule-negative / VLM-negative (10 each). This stratification means reviewer time is spent on informative cases, not redundant ones.
  3. Tests. Recruit three independent reviewers per latent (Track 4 students and TAs). Three is the minimum for Krippendorff's α on ordinal data to be meaningful (Hayes & Krippendorff, 2007).
  4. Build. Run the review. Collect ratings through the Track 2 HITL page; all ratings land in the Article-Eater HITL database table.
  5. Verify. Compute inter-reviewer α (to confirm the rubric is usable) and extractor-vs-consensus α (to confirm the extractor is usable). Both must exceed 0.6 for the tag to graduate to status: production.
  6. Diagnose. For latents that fail either α threshold, panel-review the failure mode. Inter-reviewer failure means the rubric is ambiguous; extractor-vs-consensus failure means the extractor is wrong but the construct is measurable. These are very different problems.
  7. Iterate. Revise rubric or extractor; re-run the review on a smaller 20-image batch; update status in the registry. Write the per-latent Extraction Evidence Report that goes into the registry's literature field as the empirical warrant for the tag's production status.
Final deliverable for Week 10: a merged registry where every tag carries an explicit status grounded in measured α, an Extraction Evidence Report per latent, and a fall-2026 handoff document listing which latents graduated to production, which remain skeletal, and which were downgraded in scope after the bake-off forced revision.

Restoration & Attention
RESTORATIVE
Restorativeness, fascination, being-away, coherence, compatibility. Theoretical lineage: Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) Attention Restoration Theory; Ulrich (1984). Visual cues include biophilic content, complexity, and softly fascinating patterning.
Privacy, Crowding & Control
SOCIAL REGULATION
Privacy, crowding, personal space, control/autonomy, territoriality. Theoretical lineage: Altman (1975); Baum & Valins (1977). Visual cues include density, partitioning, personal-space geometry, and the presence or absence of user-modifiable elements.

AFFORDANCE
