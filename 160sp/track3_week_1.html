Week 1

Question for the week: What makes a room model a good starting point for controlled transformation?

Use AI well

Before you ask AI for help, make sure you can state what you are trying to learn this week and what evidence would count as a useful answer.

Class meetings

Meeting A — Start from the problem, not the tools
Start here: Write, in plain language, what the studio is trying to build and what it is refusing to build.

In class

  • Individually write a three-sentence answer to the studio problem.
  • In pairs, compare answers and produce a shared version that distinguishes generation from transformation.
  • As a group, make a board with three columns: what must stay stable after an edit, what can change, and what must be measured or logged.
  • Ask an AI assistant to define three unfamiliar terms you encounter during discussion. Then rewrite the definitions in your own words with direct relevance to this studio.
  • End by producing a studio problem statement that everyone can support for the next seven weeks.

Home

  • Read enough about scene hierarchy, collision, pivots, scale, materials, and editability to explain why each matters for room transformation.
  • Use AI to help you gather short explanations, but keep only points you can connect to an actual room model.
  • Bring one annotated screenshot of a room model and label at least five features that matter for later edits.
Meeting B — Learn how to inspect a room asset
Start here: Bring one candidate room and be ready to argue why it is or is not a good benchmark.

In class

  • Review candidate room models in small teams.
  • Use a shared checklist to inspect scale, mesh organization, openings, materials, pivots, naming, and likely editability.
  • Discuss which room types are likely to be easy or hard for ceiling, window, curvature, and enclosure changes.
  • Make a short list of benchmark rooms that are strong enough to become the class corpus.

Home

  • Finalize the benchmark shortlist.
  • Write one room sheet per candidate room: what the room is, what it seems to support, what is risky, and why it belongs or does not belong in the benchmark set.
  • Use AI to help you name likely transformation opportunities, but verify them against the actual asset.

Week deliverables

D1 Team working charter

Rationale
In a compressed studio, confusion about scope, roles, and handoffs wastes more time than almost anything else. The charter is a practical agreement about how your team will work.

What to submit
A one-page document stating the shared goal, the names and initial roles of team members, what is in scope and out of scope, how files and scenes will be named, what counts as a stable build, and how your team will resolve blocked work.

What good work looks like
The document is concrete enough that a new person could join the team and know how work is organized. It distinguishes this studio from a generic VR or AI project. It states realistic norms rather than slogans.

Example
A strong charter says that the team is building a controlled room-transformation workflow, not a general text-to-3D tool; it names who owns corpus curation, factor sheets, operator tests, and weekly integration; and it states exactly where benchmark assets and logs will live.

D2 Benchmark room corpus

Rationale
The corpus becomes the shared testing ground for the rest of the course. Without it, teams work on incompatible examples and cannot compare results.

What to submit
A manageable set of 6 to 8 room models with one sheet per room. Each room sheet should include room type, source, rough dimensions if known, current asset condition, likely supported transformations, likely breakage risks, and why the room belongs in the shared set.

What good work looks like
The set is varied enough to test meaningful edits but small enough that the class can reuse it all semester. Each room sheet names both opportunities and constraints. Another team could take the corpus and start testing immediately.

Example
A strong room sheet notes that a certain office supports window and lighting changes well, has clean hierarchy and materials, but would make wall-curvature edits difficult because openings and trim are tightly coupled to the walls.
