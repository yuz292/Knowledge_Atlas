Why this week matters

The final phase closes the loop. You began by building an evaluation instrument (the persona panel), used it to diagnose problems (journey maps and heuristic audits), implemented fixes (PRs), and now you measure whether the fixes worked by re-running the same instrument on the same pages. This is the empirical evaluation of your own work.

Before/after with the same instrument

Run the exact same panel prompt on the exact same pages you audited in Week 5, now with your fixes applied. Compare friction inventory counts, severity distributions, and panel satisfaction ratings. The difference is your measured impact.

Re-evaluation protocol

Step 1: Re-run the panel

Use your revised panel prompt (the same one from Week 3.5, updated if needed) on at least 4 pages from your cluster — including the pages where you implemented fixes.

Step 2: Compare friction inventories

Count: how many friction points existed before? How many remain? Which heuristics improved? Which are unchanged? Did any new friction points appear?

Step 3: Measure severity shift

Did catastrophic violations drop to major? Did major drop to minor? A fix that moves a violation from "catastrophic" to "cosmetic" is a significant improvement even if the violation still exists.

Step 4: Document what remains

Not everything can be fixed in one sprint. The remaining issues, with their severity ratings and suggested approaches, become the roadmap for the summer development sprint.

Presentation format

Each student presents a 10-minute assertion-evidence slide deck. Every slide has a sentence headline (the assertion) supported by visual evidence (screenshot, data, diagram) — no bullet-point slides.

Slide 1: Persona portrait

Who is your persona? What do they want from K-ATLAS? What is their time budget, device, and knowledge state?

Slides 2–3: Journey map with friction

The key journey from Week 4, annotated with the friction points your panel found. Visual: a step-by-step diagram with red flags at friction points.

Slides 4–5: Top audit findings

Your 3 most important findings from the Week 5 heuristic audit. For each: the heuristic violated, the evidence, and why it matters for your persona.

Slides 6–7: Fixes implemented

Before/after screenshots of your PRs. For each: what changed, which heuristic was addressed, and the cross-persona review verdict.

Slide 8: Measured improvement

Friction inventory comparison: before vs. after. How many violations resolved? How did severity distribution change?

Slides 9–10: Remaining issues & recommendations

What should the summer sprint focus on? Rank by severity × frequency. Which user types are best served now? Which are worst served?

Week deliverables

D8 Re-evaluation report

Rationale
The re-evaluation closes the empirical loop. It proves your fixes worked (or didn't) using the same instrument you used for diagnosis.

What to submit
Before/after friction inventories for at least 4 pages. Summary metrics: violations resolved, severity shifts, new issues introduced.

What good work looks like
Honest about what improved and what did not. New issues introduced by fixes are documented, not hidden.

D9 Assertion-evidence slide deck

Rationale
The deck is the final communication artefact. It synthesises your entire quarter's work into a coherent 10-minute narrative.

What to submit
10-slide deck in assertion-evidence format. No bullet-point slides. Every claim supported by visual evidence.

What good work looks like
A viewer who missed every class could understand: what the persona needed, what was broken, what was fixed, and what remains.

D10 Summer sprint recommendations

Rationale
The quarter ends but the site doesn't. Your remaining-issues document becomes the input for summer development.

What to submit
1-page document ranking remaining issues by severity × frequency, with suggested approaches and which persona each issue most affects.

What good work looks like
Actionable, prioritised, and grounded in evidence from the quarter. A developer who reads only this document knows where to start.
