Track Overview

Knowledge Atlas is a working research instrument that will be used by very different kinds of people: a nineteen-year-old undergraduate meeting this literature for the first time, a postdoc hunting for a null finding, an architect with ten minutes between client meetings, a high-school teacher building a lesson plan, a science journalist looking for a defensible quote, a collaborating scientist uploading evidence, and the principal investigator watching the whole epistemic graph. Each of these users wants different things from the same pages, and each of them gets frustrated in different ways. Your job this quarter is to make the site work for all of them, page by page, evidence in hand — not in the abstract and not by personal taste. The rule of the track, which you will hear from me repeatedly, is the one Jakob Nielsen has spent thirty years repeating: you are not the user (Nielsen, 1993).

The shape of the quarter. Tuesday of Week 3 you set up the site and backend on your own machine and are assigned one user persona you will own for the rest of the quarter. Thursday of Week 3 you work with the conjectured journeys I have drafted for that persona and begin refining them through a structured panel with real exemplars. From Week 4 onward, you work through the site's interactive pages one at a time — an argumentation page, an interpretation page, a question-and-answer page, topic cards, PDF-linked cards, the PI's own page, and so on — and for each page the team that owns it produces a short slide deck that documents the current behaviour, the use cases that test it, and the changes the team proposes. I review those decks with you in person. That is the rhythm of the track.

§2. Choose a Persona

On Tuesday the TA assigns each of you one persona below. That persona is yours for the rest of the quarter. Every page review you contribute to, every critique you write, every journey you map — all of it is done from your persona's point of view. The point of owning a persona is not to role-play; it is to ensure that the pages, workflows, and journeys you propose actually serve someone whose goals, tools, literacy, and time budget you can describe in concrete terms (Cooper, 1999/2014; Pruitt & Adlin, 2006).

Read through all eight personas before Tuesday so you know the landscape you are working in. Then, once the TA assigns you yours, your job for the rest of the quarter is to ensure that the site gives your persona the journeys that meet their user scenarios — not journeys that are plausible in the abstract, and not journeys that fit your own habits.

§3. Thursday Panel — Refining the Personas & Surfacing Better Journeys

I have given you a first shot; your Thursday task is to test it against reality and revise it until it is articulate. A persona that has not been tested against real exemplars is a stereotype. You will convene, for your assigned persona, a panel — not a focus group and not a friend interview, but a small structured conversation with people who actually belong to the user type. The technique is Flanagan's critical-incident interview (Flanagan, 1954), the "show-me, don't tell-me" stance of Beyer & Holtzblatt's contextual inquiry (1997), and the open-ended-first discipline of Portigal (2013). Read those three short pieces before Thursday.

The right panel

  • Three exemplars, not one. A single interviewee produces a biography, not a persona. Three lets you see what is shared and what is idiosyncratic (Nielsen & Landauer, 1993, on why small samples already reveal most of the usability signal).
  • Real exemplars, not classmates. If your persona is "AP Psychology Teacher," email three AP Psychology teachers. The TA will help with introductions where needed.
  • Diverse within the persona. For Maya's panel, recruit a COGS student, a Communication student, and a non-major fulfilling a GE — same persona class, different ways of being it.
  • 45 minutes, one at a time. Group interviews flatten the signal; sequence them.

The right questions — open first, specific second

Begin with general questions that let the interviewee narrate rather than react. Close-ended questions early in an interview produce the answer the interviewee thinks you want. The purpose of the open phase is to surface scenarios of use — the concrete situations in which your persona comes to a site like K-Atlas, and the questions they arrive with in their head. Those scenarios are what the design/usability/science-writing agents (§8) need in order to have informed biases when they later critique pages for you. Scenario-surfacing matters more than journey-mapping at this stage: you cannot design a journey to satisfy a need you have not yet articulated.

Tier 1 — fully open. Start here.

  • "Walk me through the last time you went looking for a research finding — what were you trying to do, and what did you actually do?" (Flanagan's critical-incident technique, 1954.)
  • "Show me the three tabs you had open when you came in today." (Beyer & Holtzblatt's contextual inquiry, 1997 — surfaces the ecology the user works inside.)
  • "If you had thirty seconds to tell me what makes a website trustworthy for your work, what would you say?" (Values surfacing.)
  • "When a page frustrates you, what's the first thing you do?" (Failure repertoire.)
  • "What would make this site something you come back to once a week without being asked?" (Loyalty driver.)

Tier 2 — scenario-seeding. Ask these after Tier 1 has loosened the interview.

These questions probe the scenarios that actually bring people to a literature-organising site. They are the questions I — as a thirty-five-year research professor, P3's class — would expect a Researcher panellist to recognise immediately. Do not ask all of them; ask the three or four that are plausible entry scenarios for your persona class and let the interviewee keep going.

  • (Researcher) "Is there a topic in this field where you think you could make a name for yourself? How would you go looking for it?"
  • (Researcher) "When you want to understand the big issues that currently drive this field, where do you turn?"
  • (Researcher) "How do you get a sense of how big and how diverse this field is — how many research fronts it has, how they relate?"
  • (Researcher — Neuro) "When you want to know the plausible neural underpinnings for a finding, how do you assemble that picture?"
  • (Researcher) "How do you decide whether the main findings in this field are robust and well confirmed, or whether you should hold them at arm's length?"
  • (Researcher / Theory Explorer) "What are the main theories and mechanisms in this field, and how well substantiated is each? Where would you tell me the real theoretical divide lies?"
  • (Researcher) "You have written the first draft of a paper. What are the best papers you would want to cite that support your view? Which ones contradict or challenge it?"
  • (Researcher) "You want to fatten your list of references. How do you do it? What makes a reference worth adding?"
  • (Researcher) "You have to write the review section of your paper, where you discuss the relevance and implications of other articles. How do you go about that?"
  • (Student Explorer) "You have been assigned to write a 1500-word paper on an environmental-cognition topic. What would you do in the first ten minutes?"
  • (Contributor) "When you have a paper or dataset you think the field should pay attention to, what is your usual route for getting it seen?"
  • (Instructor) "In the middle of a term, what do you actually need to know about what your students are producing, and how often?"
  • (Practitioner) "A client asks a question you are not sure about. What is the shortest honest path from that question to an answer you would be willing to sign?"
  • (Theory Explorer) "When you evaluate whether a theory is really doing explanatory work, what do you ask it to show you?"

These are scenario questions, not journey questions. A scenario answer looks like "I want to find papers that challenge my view" — a journey answer looks like "I go to Google Scholar, search, copy DOIs into Zotero, then…". Let the scenario crystallise before you ask how the interviewee currently walks through it.

Tier 3 — task-on-site. Only in the last third of the interview.

Have the interviewee open K-Atlas on their own machine and think aloud (Van Someren, Barnard & Sandberg, 1994) while attempting a concrete task drawn from their scenarios — not from your goal list. Do not lead. Do not defend the site. Write what you see, not what you wish you were seeing. Plan an hour and expect twenty minutes of useful signal — the well-documented field-UX ratio.

Deliverable from the panel (end of Week 3)

A two-page, per-persona revised persona sheet committed to docs/personas/<persona-id>_<your-name>.md with: (a) what my first shot got right, (b) what it got wrong, (c) three verbatim quotes from panellists, (d) a revised goal list with three to five ranked goals, and (e) three critical incidents in the panellist's own words. If a choice helps Maya finish A0 in three clicks but degrades Elena's ability to trace a warrant, Elena wins.

§4. Scenarios of Use & Journeys (My First Shot)

Three distinct objects get conflated in UX conversation, so a vocabulary note first. A scenario of use is where and why a persona is coming to the site — the situation in their working life that produced the impulse to open a browser tab. A scenario is expressed as a question or a task in the persona's own language ("I've written a first draft and I want to find the papers that challenge my view"). A journey is the page-by-page path the persona takes once they arrive, in pursuit of a scenario. A journey is expressed as a sequence of URLs and actions. A use case is a concrete instantiation of a scenario with specific content ("I want papers that challenge the claim that warm colours increase arousal"). One scenario supports many use cases; one use case is satisfied by one or more journeys.

The five-stage Universal Journey Template in docs/USER_TYPE_HOME_PAGES_AND_JOURNEY_ARCHITECTURE_2026-04-03.md already defines the generic arc through which all scenarios resolve: Orient → Inspect → Explain → Evaluate → Act. Your redesigns should stay within that arc unless you have a principled reason to depart from it — and if you do, write that reason in your persona brief.

§5. How to Imitate Your User Type Honestly

Between panel sessions you will stand in for your persona. This is the hardest part of the track: you cannot simulate Maya's confusion if you already know what a latent variable is, and you cannot simulate Jamal's hurry if you have two hours free. Method acting is a useful metaphor, but the discipline is Kelley & Kelley's (2013) "persona empathy" — you inhabit the constraints, you do not invent the feelings. Concrete rules:

  • Use their tools, not yours. If your persona uses Chrome on a 2019 Dell, do your critique on Chrome and, if possible, throttle to a 2019 spec. If they are on a phone, test on a phone.
  • Match their time budget. If Jamal has eight seconds to judge a page, set a timer and bail when it goes off. Do not let yourself read past the deadline.
  • Match their vocabulary. If Maya does not know "coherence" as a Haack-style technical term, you do not know it either for the duration of the session. The moment you find yourself thinking "well, of course they'd click the warrant tab," you are out of character.
  • Stay in character for at least one task. Do not break character to take notes; record the session on Zoom to your local drive and watch it back.
  • Triangulate weekly. Once a week, ask the real exemplar whether your imitation of their behaviour would ring true. If they say no, recalibrate.

The failure mode to avoid is the one Nielsen calls "designing for your mother": a persona sketched once, then quietly replaced by the designer's own intuitions. Guard against it by keeping the verbatim panel quotes (§3) visible as you work.

§7. Page-by-Page Protocol — Starting Week 4

From Week 4 on, the track's rhythm is weekly: one interactive page, one team, one slide deck. The team for a page is every student whose persona cares about that page. (A page may have four people on it if four personas depend on it, or one person if only one does.)

The weekly cycle

  1. Monday — Individual exploration. Each team member loads the page on their own machine, in their own persona, and completes the two or three use cases tied to that persona. They record the session (screen capture + think-aloud audio) and take notes on what works, what breaks, what is missing, and what is confusing. Notes land in docs/page_reviews/<page>_<persona>_<name>.md.
  2. Tuesday — Individual proposals. Each member drafts a persona-specific proposal for the page: two to four concrete changes, each tied to a panel quote or a use-case failure. Proposal lives in the member's own git branch; the original is kept untouched.
  3. Wednesday — Zoom silent review. The whole team meets on Zoom with microphones muted and shares each member's variant one at a time, for five minutes each, no commentary. This is a deliberate design choice borrowed from the Nominal Group Technique (Delbecq & Van de Ven, 1971) and the "silent critique" practice of studio architecture reviews. It prevents the loudest voice from anchoring the room. Members mark up each variant in a shared doc as they watch.
  4. Wednesday, second half — Sound on, reconciliation. After the silent round, talk. Decide which features from which variants are keepers and why. Capture the reasoning, not just the decision. Write a one-paragraph reconciliation note.
  5. Thursday — Collective slide deck. The team jointly produces a .pptx for the page with four obligatory sections:
    1. The current page. Screenshot + a sentence on what it does.
    2. The use cases we tested. One slide per use case, per persona, with the session recording linked.
    3. Proposed changes. Annotated mockups — what changes and why, one slide per change.
    4. Justification. Panel quotes and use-case failures that motivated each change, plus any disagreements the team could not reconcile (honest minority reports welcomed).
  6. Friday — In-person review with Kirsh. The team walks me through the deck in 20 minutes. I ask questions, we decide what ships to the main branch and what needs another round.
Why "Zoom with sound off" matters. Anchoring is one of the best-documented failures in group design work (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974; Stasser & Titus, 1985, on hidden-profile tasks). Silent review forces every proposal to stand on its own merits before discussion compresses the space. The technique is standard in architecture studios precisely because three-dimensional critiques dominated by the most articulate voice produce worse buildings.

§8. Using the Lab's Design, Usability, and Science-Writing Agents

Three AI agents are available to you and you should use all of them — not to replace your judgement but to multiply it. Each has a specialty and a known failure mode. Treat them as competent junior collaborators: always read their output, always verify.

Prime every agent with the persona + scenarios before use. An agent asked "critique this page" gives generic output. An agent asked "critique this page from the point of view of P2 Nia, who is entering with scenario S2.5 (draft-defending) and whose governing question is does the cumulative evidence support my hypothesis" gives useful output. Paste the persona card from §2 and the relevant scenarios from §4a into every agent prompt. The scenarios are where the agent's bias comes from — without them it has none.
  • Design agent. Given a page's current HTML and a persona card + scenario set, proposes layout, CTA, and affordance changes with rationale. Good for surfacing options you did not consider. Failure mode: over-designs — you do not have to accept all its changes. Use it early in the cycle (Monday/Tuesday) to expand your option set.
  • Usability testing agent. Simulates a persona's interaction with a page, flags friction points, and produces a short usability report. Good for a fast first pass before you run real panellists. Failure mode: confabulates frustration a real user would not have. Use it as a sanity check, not as evidence on its own.
  • Science writing agent. Rewrites prose for a target reading level and a target rhetorical stance (e.g., "Maya's Grade-11-accessible summary of a warrant"). Good for testing whether a page's copy works for a non-expert persona. Failure mode: occasionally smooths over technical precision — read its output as if you were P2 Nia or P3 Elena before shipping.

Document every agent run in your page-review notes: the prompt you used (including the pasted persona + scenarios), the output you got, and what you changed in response. This matters for replicability and for the end-of-quarter retrospective on which agents are actually useful.
