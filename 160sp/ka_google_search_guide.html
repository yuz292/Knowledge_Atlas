Writing Google AI Citation Queries

A complete guide to formulating natural-language search queries that retrieve peer-reviewed evidence about environment–behavior relationships — the foundation of your K-ATLAS article submission.

Why Google AI Citation?

Academic search has changed fundamentally. Traditional Boolean keyword search — stringing together terms with AND, OR, and quotation marks — was designed for index-based retrieval systems that matched exact strings. Google AI Citation (the AI Overview with source citations that appears at the top of Google results) works differently: it reads your query as a natural-language question, reasons about what you are asking, and returns papers whose arguments and findings answer that question, even when the papers use different terminology than you do.

For environmental psychology research — a field where the same phenomenon might be called "restorative experience," "psychological restoration," "cognitive recovery," or "directed attention fatigue relief" in different labs — this semantic understanding is transformative. A well-formed natural-language query finds papers across terminological traditions. A keyword query finds only papers using your exact phrasing.

Key Insight

Google AI Citation treats your query as a research question, not a vocabulary list. The better your question is as a scientific question — specifying mechanism, outcome, population, and theoretical frame — the more precisely it retrieves. Your goal is to write something a careful researcher would actually ask.

How Google AI Citation Works

When you type a query into Google and the AI Overview box appears at the top, Google has used a large language model to synthesize an answer from multiple sources and, crucially, to link those sources with numbered citations. Each cited source is a real paper or article that the model judged relevant to your specific question.

The model is not doing keyword matching. It performs semantic retrieval: it encodes your question and each candidate paper into a shared semantic space and returns papers whose meaning is closest to yours. The practical implications are as follows. Synonyms are handled automatically: "stress recovery" and "physiological restoration" and "autonomic recovery" are understood as related concepts. Implicit relationships are caught: asking about "why green spaces reduce cortisol" retrieves papers about HPA axis regulation and nature exposure even if they never use the word "cortisol" in their titles. Specificity is rewarded: vague queries return generic results, while a precise, well-framed question about a specific mechanism, population, and outcome returns targeted, high-quality papers. Full sentences consistently outperform fragments: "restorative environments attention" is a fragment, whereas "What evidence shows that exposure to natural environments restores directed-attention capacity in adults with high cognitive load?" is a research question — and it retrieves better papers.

Complementary Tools

Google AI Citation works best for initial discovery. Once you have candidate papers, use Elicit (elicit.org) to extract structured data from PDFs, Consensus (consensus.app) for evidence summaries across many papers, and Connected Papers (connectedpapers.com) to trace citation networks. This module focuses on Google AI Citation because it is the primary discovery tool in K-ATLAS.

Query Anatomy

The most effective Google AI Citation queries follow a consistent sentence architecture. Every component contributes to precision. Below is a fully annotated example drawn from the K-ATLAS question bank (SQ-037):

What do neuroimaging studies using fMRI reveal about amygdala reactivity when healthy adults view natural landscapes versus urban scenes, and does reduced amygdala activation explain the stress-buffering effect of nature exposure?
Method signal — specifies the evidence type (fMRI, RCT, survey…)
Neural / physiological target — the specific mechanism or measure
Population — who the participants are
Experimental contrast — the comparison that drives the effect
Explanatory bridge — the mechanism to be explained
Theoretical anchor — the broader outcome or theory

Not every query needs all six elements. The minimum viable query contains a specific outcome and a theoretical or empirical anchor. Adding method signals, population specifiers, and mechanistic bridges progressively sharpens precision.

The Five Components of a Strong Query

Here is a systematic framework for constructing any K-ATLAS query. You do not need all five every time, but knowing them allows you to diagnose why a weak query is weak.

Component Status What it specifies Example phrase
1. Evidence type Optional but powerful The method or study design you want. Signals to the AI that you want empirical papers, not reviews or opinion pieces. "What do peer-reviewed studies show…" / "What experimental evidence demonstrates…" / "What do RCT findings indicate…"
2. Mechanism or measure Required The specific neural, physiological, cognitive, or behavioral process you want explained. This is the heart of the query. "…about cortisol reduction…" / "…about directed attention restoration…" / "…amygdala reactivity…"
3. Environmental condition Required The built or natural environment feature being manipulated or measured. Specific is better than general. "…when exposed to green roofs…" / "…in response to ceiling height variation…" / "…viewing fractal patterns at D=1.3–1.5…"
4. Population or context Optional but sharpening Who the study population is, or in what setting. Prevents retrieval of irrelevant subfields such as animal studies when you want human data. "…in healthy adults…" / "…among office workers…" / "…in children with ADHD…" / "…in post-surgical patients…"
5. Theoretical anchor Required The theory, outcome, or explanatory goal that frames the query. This anchors retrieval to papers that matter to K-ATLAS's knowledge base. "…consistent with Attention Restoration Theory?" / "…as predicted by Stress Recovery Theory?" / "…to explain biophilic design benefits?"

Boolean vs. Google AI Citation: Side-by-Side

The most common student mistake is writing Boolean-style fragments and pasting them into Google. The comparisons below show why this produces poor results — and what the correct version looks like.

Example 1: Attention Restoration Theory

Wrong — Boolean
"attention restoration" AND "built environment" AND (nature OR green) -review
Correct — Google AI Citation
What peer-reviewed evidence shows that exposure to natural elements in built environments — such as indoor plants, water features, or views of greenery — restores directed attention and reduces mental fatigue in office workers?

Example 2: Biophilic design and stress

Wrong — Boolean
biophilic design stress cortisol reduction workplace
Correct — Google AI Citation
What experimental studies using cortisol measurement or skin conductance have found that biophilic design features — natural light, living walls, or natural materials — reduce physiological stress responses in workplace settings?

Example 3: Neural mechanisms of nature exposure

Wrong — Boolean
fMRI nature urban amygdala activation difference brain
Correct — Google AI Citation
What do neuroimaging studies reveal about differences in amygdala and prefrontal cortex activation when participants view natural landscapes versus urban environments, and do these neural differences explain the well-being benefits attributed to nature exposure?

Example 4: Social interaction in built environments

Wrong — Boolean
common space design social interaction residential buildings
Correct — Google AI Citation
What evidence shows that the design of common spaces — courtyards, shared kitchens, lobby seating — in residential buildings increases the frequency and quality of social interactions among residents, and does this effect depend on spatial enclosure or biophilic elements?

Sentence Patterns That Work

The following sentence starters are strongly associated with high-quality AI Citation retrieval because they signal to the model that you want empirical evidence rather than background essays.

Evidence-seeking starters (highest retrieval precision)

Pattern A
What peer-reviewed evidence shows that [environmental feature] [affects / improves / reduces] [outcome] in [population]?
Pattern B
What do experimental studies measuring [physiological / cognitive measure] find when [population] is exposed to [environmental condition] compared to [control condition]?
Pattern C
What neuroimaging / EEG / physiological evidence supports the claim that [theory's central mechanism] is responsible for [outcome] in [built environment context]?

Mechanism-explanation starters (for IC→DC queries)

Pattern D
How does [mechanism — e.g., amygdala down-regulation / HPA axis suppression / default mode network engagement] explain why [environmental feature] produces [benefit] in [population]?
Pattern E
Through what neural or physiological pathway does [environmental stimulus] reduce [negative outcome — stress / fatigue / anxiety] according to [theory name] predictions?

Comparative and critical starters (for theory-testing queries)

Pattern F
When ART and SRT make conflicting predictions about [specific phenomenon], which theory is better supported by [type of evidence]?
Pattern G
What studies have attempted to replicate [specific finding about environmental feature and outcome], and do the replication results support or undermine the original claim?

Evidential Framing: Signaling the Evidence Type You Need

Google AI Citation responds to explicit signals about evidence type. By specifying the methodology you are looking for, you narrow retrieval to papers that provide that kind of evidence — not merely papers that discuss the topic in general.

Signals for experimental studies

Use phrases like "experimental studies," "randomized controlled trial," "controlled lab experiment," "within-subjects design," or "quasi-experimental field study" when you want studies that manipulated the environment and measured outcomes.

Strong example
What do controlled experimental studies using skin conductance measurement find about the acute stress-buffering effects of brief nature walks compared to urban walks in adults with elevated baseline stress?

Signals for neuroimaging and physiological studies

Use "fMRI studies," "EEG studies," "neuroimaging evidence," "cortisol measurement," "heart rate variability," "salivary alpha-amylase," etc. when you need papers that measured biological or neural responses.

Strong example
What do fMRI and EEG studies reveal about default mode network activation and alpha-wave synchrony during passive exposure to natural landscape images versus urban architectural images, and are these neural signatures consistent with Attention Restoration Theory's "soft fascination" construct?

Signals for observational and survey studies

Use "post-occupancy evaluation," "occupant survey," "longitudinal study," "cross-sectional survey," "self-reported wellbeing" when you want field studies conducted in real buildings.

Strong example
What do post-occupancy evaluation studies of LEED-certified office buildings find about self-reported cognitive performance, mood, and absenteeism among occupants, and do biophilic design features independently predict positive outcomes?

Signals for systematic reviews and meta-analyses

When you want aggregated evidence, use "systematic review," "meta-analysis," "pooled effect sizes," "evidence synthesis." These are especially useful for core relationships where you need to show the weight of evidence, not just one paper.

Strong example
What do systematic reviews and meta-analyses of the restorative effects of natural environments conclude about effect sizes, moderating variables, and the relative strength of Attention Restoration Theory versus Stress Recovery Theory as explanatory frameworks?

Advanced: Neural Mechanism Queries (IC to DC)

K-ATLAS uses a Quinean coherentist epistemology in which beliefs are assigned initial credence values (IC) that are revised upward to default credences (DC) when converging evidence strengthens them. Neural mechanism queries are particularly valuable because they target the explanatory layer: not just that an effect occurs, but why it occurs at the level of neural or physiological process. Papers that identify specific neural pathways provide the strongest IC-to-DC transitions in the system.

Mechanism queries require two additional components beyond the standard five: explicit reference to a neural or physiological pathway, and an explanatory framing that asks how or through what mechanism.

Why Mechanism Queries Matter for K-ATLAS

When K-ATLAS ingests a paper that shows "nature reduces amygdala activation, and this explains stress reduction," it can link that claim to the warrant supporting SRT's safety-signalling mechanism. This raises DC from approximately 0.62 to 0.78 — a significant update. Papers that only report "nature reduces stress" without a mechanism provide weaker warrant support and produce smaller DC updates.

The mechanism query template

Template
Through what [specific neural / physiological / cognitive] mechanism does [environmental feature] produce [outcome], and what [method — fMRI / EEG / physiological measure] evidence supports this mechanistic account as predicted by [theory]?

Three Fully Worked Examples

The following examples take a research topic from initial idea to polished query, showing each step of the reasoning process.

Worked Example 1: Ceiling height and cognition

1

Start with the phenomenon

I want to know whether high ceilings make people think more abstractly.

2

Identify mechanism and measure

The mechanism is embodied cognition or spatial-conceptual metaphor. The measure would be performance on abstract versus concrete cognitive tasks.

3

Specify the environmental contrast

High ceilings versus low ceilings, or variations in ceiling height within the same room.

4

Add the theoretical anchor

This is relevant to embodied cognition theories and the "cathedral effect" literature.

Final Query
What experimental studies have measured the effect of ceiling height variation on abstract versus concrete cognitive task performance in human participants, and do higher ceilings reliably promote abstract thinking consistent with the embodied cognition account of the "cathedral effect"?

Worked Example 2: Acoustic environment and healthcare

1

Start with the phenomenon

Hospital noise seems to harm patient recovery. I want papers about this.

2

Identify outcomes and mechanism

Outcomes: length of stay, pain medication use, sleep quality, cortisol. Mechanism: sleep disruption and HPA axis activation from noise.

3

Add evidence type and population

Clinical studies in hospital settings, post-surgical patients.

4

Anchor in theory

SRT and evidence-based design literature predict that noise-free environments should accelerate physiological recovery.

Final Query
What clinical studies measuring sleep quality, cortisol levels, and length of hospital stay have found that reducing ambient noise in post-surgical wards improves patient recovery, and do these findings support the Stress Recovery Theory prediction that physiologically safe environments accelerate healing?

Worked Example 3: Writing your own question

If you choose to propose your own question to K-ATLAS rather than claiming one from the bank, it must meet the same standards. Use this checklist before finalizing your custom query:

  • Is it a complete question sentence with subject, verb, and object?
  • Does it specify at least one environmental feature?
  • Does it specify at least one measurable outcome — behavioral, physiological, or neural?
  • Does it have a theoretical anchor — a named theory or named phenomenon?
  • Could a cognitive scientist evaluate whether a paper answers it with a clear yes or no?
  • Is it not already answered by a commonly cited textbook or review — that is, is there genuine empirical uncertainty?

Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Mistake 1: Using Boolean operators

Wrong
"biophilic design" AND stress AND (office OR workplace) -animal
Fix: Write a complete sentence
What evidence shows that biophilic design features in workplace settings reduce self-reported stress and improve mood among office workers?

Mistake 2: Too vague — no mechanism or measure

Too vague
Does nature help people feel better in buildings?
Fix: Specify mechanism and measure
What peer-reviewed studies measuring heart rate variability or cortisol find that incorporating natural elements — plants, water features, natural light — into building interiors reduces physiological stress responses in occupants?

Mistake 3: Asking for information, not evidence

Information-seeking
What is Stress Recovery Theory and who developed it?
Fix: Ask for evidence
What experimental studies testing Ulrich's Stress Recovery Theory have measured autonomic nervous system recovery — using heart rate, blood pressure, or skin conductance — and found faster physiological restoration in natural versus urban visual environments?

Mistake 4: Missing the theoretical anchor

No anchor
Do plants in offices help workers concentrate better?
Fix: Add theoretical anchor
What experimental evidence supports the claim that the presence of indoor plants in office environments improves sustained attention and working memory performance, consistent with Attention Restoration Theory's prediction that involuntary fascination with natural elements replenishes directed attention?

Mistake 5: Too narrowly historical

Too narrow
What did the 2019 Kaplan study find about attention restoration in Chicago parks?
Fix: Make it generalisable
What do post-occupancy studies of urban parks find about directed attention restoration in city dwellers, and do these findings replicate Kaplan and Kaplan's ART predictions across different cultural contexts?

The Search Workflow

Once you have claimed your K-ATLAS question, follow these steps to find ten qualifying articles.

1

Copy your assigned query exactly

From your student assignment page, copy the question text. Do not paraphrase it — the query was written to maximise AI Citation retrieval precision.

2

Paste into Google and look for the AI Overview box

The AI Overview box appears at the top of results for research questions. It shows a synthesised answer with numbered citations. Each citation is a potential article.

3

Expand citations — click "Show all sources"

Google shows 3–5 citations initially. Clicking the citation numbers or "Show all sources" reveals the full list. Aim for 8–12 candidate papers.

4

Verify each paper against the K-ATLAS corpus

Before logging a paper, search its DOI in K-ATLAS (use the duplicate-check step in your assignment page). Papers already in K-ATLAS do not count toward your ten.

5

Cross-check with Elicit for structured data

For each candidate paper, use Elicit (elicit.org) to extract sample size, methods, key findings, and limitations. This data strengthens your CSV submission.

6

Run variant queries for remaining slots

If Google AI Citation returns fewer than ten new papers, vary your query: try a different sentence pattern, add a method signal, or change the population specifier. Each variant probes a different corner of the literature.

7

Compile your CSV and upload PDFs

Fill in all required CSV fields (see format below) and locate PDFs via Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, or your library proxy. Upload both to complete your submission.

Evaluating Search Results

Not every paper returned by Google AI Citation qualifies for K-ATLAS. Apply these criteria before logging a paper to your CSV.

Criterion What counts Status
Peer-reviewed — published in a journal with editorial review, or a conference with blind review Journal article, conference paper, or book chapter in academic press Required
Empirical — reports original data collection and analysis Study with participants, measurements, and statistical results. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses also qualify. Required
Addresses the query's mechanism or outcome — not just tangentially related Abstract explicitly mentions the key outcome the query specifies Required
Not already in K-ATLAS corpus — confirmed by DOI check DOI search returns no match in K-ATLAS Required
Published 1999 or later unless the paper is canonical Year ≥ 1999, or paper is a landmark (e.g., Ulrich 1984, Kaplan & Kaplan 1989) Preferred
Full text accessible — PDF retrievable for upload Available via library proxy, Unpaywall, or author's personal page Required for PDF upload
DOI available — permanent identifier present doi.org prefix or CrossRef record Strongly preferred
When AI Citation returns reviews and meta-analyses

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses count as qualifying papers if they synthesise empirical data and report effect sizes or pooled estimates. Do include them. Do not include narrative review articles that discuss findings without reporting original data or statistical synthesis.

CSV Submission Format

Your article list must be submitted as a CSV with the following columns. Field names are case-sensitive. Use the template download button in your assignment page to get a correctly formatted starter file.

Column Status Format / Notes
title Required Full paper title, no truncation
authors Required Surname, Initials format; multiple authors separated by " ; "
year Required Four-digit year of publication
doi Required Full DOI URL, e.g. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxx
abstract Required Full abstract text, copied from the paper or database
source Required Journal name or conference name
notes Optional One sentence explaining the paper's relevance to your search question
Avoiding CSV Formatting Errors

Abstracts frequently contain commas. Wrap every abstract field in double quotes. If the abstract itself contains double quotes, escape them as two consecutive double quotes. The CSV template downloaded from K-ATLAS handles this automatically when you use spreadsheet software such as Excel or Google Sheets.

Pre-Submission Checklist

Confirm every item before submitting.

  • My assigned query (or my custom query) is written as a complete natural-language question in Google AI Citation format — not Boolean keywords.
  • I have found at least ten peer-reviewed, empirical articles that address the query's mechanism or outcome.
  • I have checked every DOI against the K-ATLAS corpus and none of my ten papers are already in the database.
  • My CSV has all six required columns — title, authors, year, doi, abstract, source — with no empty required cells.
  • I have uploaded PDFs for all ten papers, or noted which are behind paywalls and provided full abstracts instead.
  • Each paper's abstract explicitly addresses the key outcome or mechanism specified in the query — not merely the general topic area.
  • I understand that K-ATLAS will use my submitted papers to update credence values in the Web of Belief engine — papers that address neural or physiological mechanisms carry higher evidential weight than those reporting only behavioral outcomes.
