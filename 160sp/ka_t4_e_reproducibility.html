Reproducibility check: paired-replication of UX findings

Span Week 7 Hardness Hard Points 13 / 75 Method Direct-replication design (after Brandt et al. 2014)
Full rubric →
Track 4 deliverables T4.a T4.b T4.b.2 T4.c T4.d T4.e T4.f T4.g

◎Goalwhat this deliverable teaches

You will pair with a classmate and each attempt to reproduce five of the other person's findings — without access to their screenshots or commentary, working only from the finding schema (heuristic, scenario, page, expected vs. observed). Each reproduction produces a binary judgement (reproduces / does not reproduce / partial) and a short narrative note. The deliverable is a replication_report.md that commits to a reproducibility rate and reflects on which pieces of the finding schema were most load-bearing.

Usability findings have the same epistemic status as behavioural experiment results: a finding that reproduces on a second observer's machine is evidence; one that does not is either poorly specified or poorly grounded. This deliverable is hard because it forces you to audit your own T4.a/T4.c schema quality through a colleague's failure to reproduce it, which is less comfortable than writing new findings.

›What you'll dothe steps

  1. Pair up. Your instructor will assign pairs by track-cohort; you can swap if both parties agree.
  2. Exchange five findings each (ten reproductions per student total). Send only the schema fields — not your screenshots, your pilot quotes, or your severity rating. The other party should not know which finding you thought was most important.
  3. For each finding you receive, open the stated page, perform the stated scenario, and attempt to produce the stated observed behaviour. Record what actually happened on your machine, in your browser, at the timestamp you attempted it.
  4. Rate each attempted reproduction: FULL (observed behaviour matches), PARTIAL (something similar but not identical), NONE (behaviour could not be reproduced), or UNDERSPECIFIED (the schema did not give you enough to try).
  5. Write one short note per attempted reproduction: what browser, what exact steps, what you saw. If NONE, propose the most charitable hypothesis for why (the page changed between observations, your viewport differs, a feature-flag differs, etc.).
  6. Submit replication_report.md with a summary table of ten rows plus a reflection paragraph. The reflection paragraph is graded separately.

✗Example of a naive approachso you can see what scores low

The most common naive failure is to conclude that "the site changed" for every non-reproduction, which converts the exercise into a self-exculpatory ritual. The block below illustrates.

band 0–1 example — do NOT write a replication report like this

Replication report — summary

F-01  NONE    — could not find the page they described, probably changed
F-02  NONE    — site updated
F-03  NONE    — maybe a CSS change
F-04  NONE    — the site works fine now
F-05  FULL    — yes reproduced (no notes)
F-06  NONE    — didn't load
F-07  NONE    — couldn't tell
F-08  NONE    — site probably fixed
F-09  NONE    — different browser
F-10  NONE    — n/a

Reflection: I reproduced 1/10 findings. This shows the site has improved a lot since my partner's audit, so most findings are no longer valid. I think T4.a is a pretty unreliable method.

Why this scores band 0 or 1. Every non-reproduction is attributed to a change in the target rather than a failure of the schema or a failure of the observer. No browser is named, no timestamp is given, no exact steps are traced. The "F-05 FULL (no notes)" row is worse than a NONE row — a reproduction without evidence is not a reproduction, it is an affirmation. The reflection inverts the logic of the deliverable: when a partner's finding fails to reproduce on your machine, the most honest hypothesis is usually that the original schema was underspecified, not that the world conveniently fixed itself. A band-3 report forces the student to assign failure-modes: page-absent / schema-incomplete / viewport-specific / interaction-missed / genuine-regression, and to distinguish between them using evidence. Concluding "T4.a is unreliable" on a 1/10 sample with no diagnostic work is exactly the kind of conclusion Brandt et al. (2014) warn against when they insist that a single replication is evidence, not proof.

The report-row template. One row per attempted reproduction:

## F-{partner_id} — {their title}

**Verdict**: FULL | PARTIAL | NONE | UNDERSPECIFIED
**Browser / viewport**: {e.g. Safari 17.3, 1440×900}
**Timestamp**: {YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM}
**Steps taken** (numbered, verbatim if possible):
  1. {navigated to URL}
  2. {did action}
**What I actually observed**: {literal, 30–60 words}
**If NONE or PARTIAL, my hypothesis for why**:
  schema-incomplete | page-absent | viewport-specific
  | interaction-missed | genuine-regression
**Evidence for that hypothesis** (≤ 30 words): {one sentence}

✓Verification

how your work is graded
  • Automated: the AI grader checks that replication_report.md has exactly 10 row entries each with a verdict, a browser/viewport, a timestamp, and a steps-taken list.
  • Automated: NONE or PARTIAL verdicts without a hypothesis column cap Rigor at band 1.
  • Automated: cross-check — the partner's original severity rating is looked up in their backlog CSV; large disagreements (you say NONE on a severity-5 finding, or FULL on a severity-1) trigger a human review.
  • TA audit: a TA attempts one of your reproductions on a third machine; disagreement between your verdict and the TA's is rare but instructive.

Scoring bands (0–3 per criterion, rescaled to the 13-point value):

CriterionBand 0Band 1Band 2Band 3
Rigor < 5 attempts or no evidence Attempts thin, hypotheses missing 10 attempts, most with evidence 10 attempts, every verdict with verbatim steps + hypothesis
Calibration All verdicts the same Verdicts span 2 categories Verdicts span 3 categories Verdicts span all 4 categories; distribution defended
Reflection No reflection Generic reflection Names a specific schema weakness Names schema weakness, proposes concrete schema revision, and cites ≥ 1 replication-literature source
