## 1. What this deliverable is

The student takes a single, structured research query on their declared Track-2 sub-topic, sends it to each of several AI research-assistant services (the RAGs DK subscribes to — see §Services below), harvests each service's paper list and evidence claims, runs each returned paper through the project's article classifier and topic classifier, and produces an audit document that reports where the RAG services agree with our classifiers and where they systematically disagree. The deliverable is simultaneously (a) a training-data enrichment pass for our own classifiers and (b) a documented audit of the external services' quality on environment-cognition literature.

The deliverable is deliberately epistemically honest: it treats external RAGs as **hypotheses about what the evidence base contains**, not as ground truth. Our classifiers are the check; the disagreements are the signal.

## 2. Why this deliverable exists

The current T2 deliverables (T2.a through T2.g) all ask students to find articles but are agnostic about *how*. Students can use any tool they want: Google Scholar, Consensus, Elicit, SciSpace, Perplexity, ChatGPT Deep Research, Gemini, PubMed, or a plain library catalogue. The rubric cannot distinguish a student who read one review paper and followed its references from a student who pasted the assignment into Elicit and submitted whatever came back. That is a weakness: students who use AI-retrieval services without reflection are being graded the same as students who read primary literature, and the course has no mechanism to assess either the students' use of those services or the services themselves.

T2.d.2 corrects this. It forces three things into the workflow that were previously implicit or absent: (i) the student uses the RAG services deliberately and documents the queries, (ii) the student runs the returned papers through our classifiers rather than trusting the RAG's own relevance claims, and (iii) the student writes a disagreement analysis that is only possible if they have read enough of the primary literature to adjudicate between the RAG's claim and our classifier's claim.

The empirical basis for this design is the last two years of work on AI-assisted systematic review and the documented failure modes of commercial research RAGs. Thomas et al. (2023) report that Elicit's precision on tightly-specified clinical queries drops below 0.6 once the query moves outside the biomedical training distribution. Khraisha et al. (2024) find GPT-4 screening of systematic-review candidates has sensitivity ≈ 0.90 but specificity ≈ 0.55, meaning it sweeps in many false positives. Wang, Scells, Koopman, and Zuccon (2024) show LLM-generated Boolean queries retrieve the target reference set for only 40–60 % of well-defined questions. These are not edge cases; they are the modal performance of AI retrieval tools on scholarly questions. The pedagogical point is that students who will rely on these tools after graduation should learn to audit them, not trust them.

## 3. What the student produces

A **single harvest-and-audit package** at `160sp/tracks/t2/{student_id}/T2.d.2/` containing:

1. `query.md` — the structured query the student submitted to every service, with a paragraph of rationale for the query's wording (~ 200 words).
2. `harvest/` — one subdirectory per RAG service, each containing the raw returned payload from that service (JSON or HTML, as appropriate) and a normalised-to-common-schema JSON blob. The normalisation is done by `scripts/rag_harvest.py` (to be built), which the student invokes; the student does not hand-normalise.
3. `classifier_check.csv` — for every unique paper across the union of all service results, one row with: `doi`, `title`, `services_returning_it` (comma-separated), `service_claimed_relevance` (JSON of per-service claims), `our_article_classifier_prediction` (T1 framework + T1.5 theory), `our_article_classifier_confidence`, `our_topic_classifier_prediction`, `agreement_flag` (one of `agree`, `our_disagreement`, `service_disagreement`, `mixed`). Produced by `scripts/rag_classify_check.py` joining the harvest with our classifier columns from `pipeline_registry_unified.db`.
4. `disagreement_report.md` — the student's written analysis (~ 800–1200 words) of the disagreement set. Structure: summary of per-service precision against our classifier; three specific disagreements where the student read the primary paper and formed a view on who was right; one hypothesis about why a given service systematically miscalled a particular sub-topic; one actionable recommendation for our classifier based on what the RAG services caught that we missed.
5. `reflection.md` — standard ~ 300-word reflection, with the specificity bar raised to **≥ 4 referents** (specific paper titles, specific service names, specific classifier confusion patterns).

## 5. Scoring bands (three criteria, 0–3, rescaled to 10 points)

### Completeness

- **0** — Query document missing, no harvest directories present, or classifier_check.csv has fewer than 10 rows.
- **1** — At least one RAG service queried, classifier_check present but partial, writeup thin.
- **2** — ≥ 3 RAG services queried with normalised output, classifier_check.csv has ≥ 30 unique papers, disagreement report ≥ 700 words with all four required sections.
- **3** — All services in the canonical services list queried, ≥ 50 unique papers in classifier_check.csv, disagreement report ≥ 1,000 words.

### Quality

- **0** — Harvest output is not from the actual services (fabricated or trivially copy-pasted from a single tool), classifier_check columns are empty or wrong, disagreement report is generic.
- **1** — Harvest is real but normalisation is broken, classifier_check partially populated, disagreement report restates RAG output without reading primary papers.
- **2** — Harvest is real and normalised, classifier_check is populated end-to-end, disagreement report cites three specific disagreements of which ≥ 2 are supported by verifiable primary-paper reading.
- **3** — Same as band 2, plus all 3 disagreements are verifiable AND the classifier-improvement recommendation names a specific concrete change (a training-data gap, a feature to add, a misclassified sub-topic) rather than a platitude.

### Reflection

Standard reflection bands from `rubrics/README.md § 3`, with the specificity threshold raised to 5 referents for band 3. Consistency check: every RAG-service and paper-title named in the reflection must be traceable to the harvest directory or the CSV. Provenance check against A0/A1 baseline as normal.

## 7. Point allocation — proposed change to T2 budget (DK to approve)

T2 currently totals 75 points across seven deliverables. Adding T2.d.2 without removing budget from elsewhere overruns the track cap. Three options, with my recommendation:

| Option | Change | Net effect |
|---|---|---|
| **A** (recommended) | Absorb T2.e (Near-miss triage, 10 pts) into T2.d.2. Near-miss triage is cognitively the same act: "does this paper's claimed relevance survive our check?" — T2.e does it against our classifier's near-miss flags, T2.d.2 does it against external RAG output. Merging is more coherent than duplicating. | 75 → 75, deliverable count 7 → 7 (T2.d.2 replaces T2.e) |
| **B** | Shrink T2.f from 15 → 10 pts and give 5 to T2.d.2 as a 5-point deliverable | 75 → 75, deliverable count 7 → 8 |
| **C** | Raise the track cap to 80 pts (and shrink A0 or F160 correspondingly to keep the class cap at 100) | Changes DK's 5/5/75/15 allocation; requires rethinking at the class level |

Option A is the most honest: T2.e's near-miss triage is a degenerate case of the T2.d.2 audit (our classifier is the only "service"). Combining them teaches the same skill in one deliverable with explicit multi-service breadth.

## 11. Addendum (2026-04-18) — what the actual classifier check-side looks like

After this rubric was drafted, CW ran `scripts/audit_classifiers.py` against `Article_Eater_PostQuinean_v1_recovery/data/pipeline_registry_unified.db` (the real classifier DB) to verify the check-side of T2.d.2 was sound. Findings are documented in full in `docs/CLASSIFIER_AUDIT_FINDINGS_2026-04-18.md`. The implications for this rubric:

**Classifier surface is a 9 × 18 modality × outcome matrix, not a fine-grained topic hierarchy.** The cross-check axes available to the student are:

1. `canonical_classifications.canonical_article_type` — 14+ values (empirical_research, narrative_review, theoretical, qualitative_research, systematic_review, meta_analysis, etc.). The strongest cross-check axis. Well populated.
2. `papers.topic_category` (modality) — 9 values: luminous, spatial, acoustic, natural, material, control, thermal, social_spatial, multisensory. Coarse but psychologically sensible. ~50% populated.
3. `papers.topic_subcategory` (outcome) — 18 values: cog.performance, affect.wellbeing, physio, neural, affect.negative.stress, cog.attention, social, affect.preference, affect.comfort, cog.memory, affect.restoration, behav.navigation, affect.soundscape, health, affect.satisfaction, cog.creativity, cog.cognitive_load, mechanism. ~49% populated.
4. `papers.classification_confidence` — 0–1 numeric (TEXT-typed; some "high" string values), heavy skew toward 0.95 but not flat.

**T2.d.2's `classifier_check.csv` columns are now anchored to these.** `scripts/rag_classify_check.py` joins each unique RAG-returned paper against the four columns above by DOI lookup. The student's disagreement report compares (a) RAG-claimed article type vs `canonical_article_type`, (b) RAG-claimed topical relevance vs `(topic_category, topic_subcategory)` cell, (c) RAG-claimed confidence vs `classification_confidence`.

**The rubric does NOT require finer topic resolution than the classifier provides.** Earlier drafts implicitly assumed a free-form topic taxonomy ("prospect-refuge in office design"); the actual classifier resolves to "spatial × affect.preference" or similar. Students writing the disagreement report should be told this explicitly so they don't penalise the classifier for failing to produce a label it was never trained to produce.

**One papers cell is unpopulated and should not be used.** `primary_topic_candidate` is 0% populated; `canonical_triage_decision` is empty across the corpus. Earlier drafts of the rubric referenced these — they should be removed before this rubric goes canonical (CW to land in a follow-on commit if DK approves).

**This addendum supersedes any contradiction with §1–§10 above.** The substantive design is unchanged: the deliverable is still a RAG-audit + classifier cross-check + disagreement writeup. Only the specific column references and the topic-taxonomy granularity assumption are updated.
