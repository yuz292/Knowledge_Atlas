Studio framing

Current AI tools can generate useful parts of 3D environments, but they do not reliably produce coherent, editable, Unreal-ready experimental rooms from research-style specifications. Your job in this studio is to work on the missing layer: controlled transformation of existing built-environment models.

That means the class is not mainly about text-to-3D generation. It is about taking strong source models and learning how to alter them in ways that are geometrically plausible, experimentally meaningful, and clearly documented.

Some requests will not arrive in everyday design language. A researcher may ask for a room that increases refuge while preserving visibility control, or increases awe without making the room feel cold. You must learn how to translate that language into room edits, and how to explain those edits back to a user.

What success looks like

You can start from an existing room model and identify which transformations it can support.

You can map a research-style request onto architectural factors such as ceiling height, wall curvature, enclosure, light, and prospect/refuge.

You can implement or specify controlled edits without breaking navigation, openings, materials, or scene structure.

You can show what changed, why it changed, and which latent variables the change was meant to influence.

You can package variants so another researcher could use them in an experiment.

How to work with AI in this track

You are encouraged to ask AI for definitions, comparisons of toolchains, code sketches, debugging suggestions, and summaries of relevant papers. But you must convert that advice into concrete tests and artifacts.

  1. You are encouraged to ask AI for definitions, comparisons of toolchains, examples of data structures, code sketches, debugging suggestions, and summaries of relevant papers. But every time you ask AI something important, you should also ask yourself four questions:
  2. What would I need to know to tell whether the answer is wrong or too vague?
  3. What concrete evidence or test would show that the advice actually works on a room model?
  4. What assumptions is the answer making about the toolchain, asset quality, or scene structure?
  5. What part of the answer needs to be translated into a deliverable for this studio?

How the studio is organized

You will work as a small production studio rather than as isolated individual projects. The most sensible structure for a 10-student group is four functional teams plus one rotating integrator.
