Overview

How Week 2 Works

Tuesday (Day 3): Prof Kirsh demos Exercise 0 live — the full seven-step cycle on a real task, start to finish. You watch the method in action: convene panel, write spec, implement with AI, verify, iterate when it fails.

Thursday (Day 4): You do one exercise yourself (assigned by team). Same method, different task. Submit everything: all prompts (including failures), the AI’s output at each step, verification results, and a two-paragraph reflection.

Procedure

Your Step by Step

This is what you actually do on Thursday. Follow these steps in order.

1. Choose your exercise. Scroll down to the exercise assigned to your team. Read the problem description so you understand what you’re building.

2. Give your AI the context file. Each exercise has a .md file that explains the task, the data structures, and the relevant parts of the codebase. Save the file to your computer, then drag it into your AI chat window as the very first message. If your AI doesn’t support drag-and-drop, open the file, select all, copy, and paste the entire contents. This works with ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Codex, Claude Code — all of them.

3. Ask the AI to explain the task back to you. Before doing anything else, make it tell you what it thinks the task is about. What are the key data structures? What’s the hardest part? What could go wrong?

4. Compare its explanation to your own reading. You read the context file yourself in step 2. Does the AI’s understanding match yours? If it misunderstands the data model, correct it now. Everything downstream depends on this.

5. Give it the panel prompt. Each exercise below includes a panel prompt written for you. Copy it and paste it into your AI. Normally you would write this prompt yourself — crafting the right panel is a skill — but for this assignment the prompts are provided so you can focus on the method.

6. Read the panel output carefully. The AI will simulate 2–3 experts arguing about the design. Read what they say. Notice where they disagree. Their disagreements are the interesting part — they surface requirements you would not have thought of on your own.

7. Ask the AI to write the specification. The current assignment says you should write the spec. That is silly. The AI is better at exhaustive enumeration than you are. Instead, give it the problem and tell it: “Write a specification for this component. Define inputs, outputs, success conditions, and edge cases. This must be a testable contract — every requirement must be something I can verify with a concrete test.” Review the seven-step method table above for what a good spec covers, then check the AI’s spec against those criteria.

8. Tell it to write test cases. Say: “Now write 3–5 concrete test cases before any code. For each test, state the input, the expected output, and how to verify it.” Then add: “Before writing the tests, explain your plan — why these tests and not others? What are you trying to catch?” Forcing the AI to justify its choices makes it think harder and produce better tests.

9. Build, verify, diagnose, iterate. You know the remaining steps from the seven-step method. Tell the AI to build it against the spec. Run the tests. When something fails — and something will — figure out whether the spec was wrong or the implementation was wrong. Fix it and re-test. Add guidance to your prompts the way Prof Kirsh did in the demo: be specific about what you want, anticipate problems, and tell the AI what not to do.

10. But you cannot verify unless you give the AI the data. Your component needs real data to run. Each exercise uses one or both of two JSON files: evidence.json (~1,900 evidence records) and argumentation.json (~1,900 claim nodes with edges). Save the file to your computer and drop it into the AI chat, just like you did with the context file. If the file is too large to upload, tell the AI to embed 20–30 realistic sample records directly in the code.

↓ evidence.json ↓ argumentation.json

11. Complete all the steps and submit your work. See the pre-submission checklist at the bottom of this page.

The Rule
You must complete steps 1–8 before building anything. If you skip the panel and spec, the AI will produce plausible-looking code that fails in ways you can’t diagnose. That’s Failure Mode #1.

Exercise 0: Mechanism Pathway Tracer
Track 4 (Theory/Mechanism) · ~40 min live · D3.js visualization
📄 Context file: context_ex0_mechanism_pathway.md

The Problem

Knowledge Atlas has ~1,900 evidence claims and ~4,810 edges connecting them. Among these, 34 are explicitly tagged as mechanism_claim — they describe how one thing causes another, not just that it does. But there’s no way to visualize the chain. A researcher asking “how does natural light affect performance?” has to manually click through dozens of evidence cards. We need a component that traces the pathway automatically and shows confidence at each step.

Step 1 — Convene Panel

Panel Prompt You are running a design panel for a mechanism pathway visualization component. The panelists are: - Judea Pearl (causal inference): What makes a mechanism chain valid vs. merely plausible? - Edward Tufte (information design): How should confidence be encoded visually without clutter? - A cognitive science graduate student (target user): What would actually help me understand the pathway? The component takes an IV and DV and displays the mechanistic chain connecting them, with confidence ratings per step. The data source is argumentation.json (~1,900 claim nodes with typed support/attack edges and strength scores 0-1). Each panelist: state your top 3 requirements and one thing that would make it misleading or useless.

Step 2 — Write Spec (Example)

COMPONENT: MechanismPathwayTracer INPUT: iv_term (string), dv_term (string) OUTPUT: SVG/HTML showing directed graph from IV to DV DATA SOURCE: argumentation.json - claim nodes with belief_id, content_preview, warrant_status - edges with source, target, scheme_hint, strength (0-1), defeat_type REQUIREMENTS: 1. Find mechanism_claim nodes matching IV and DV terms 2. Trace shortest path via support edges 3. Display each node with: claim text, confidence, warrant type 4. Color edges by strength: green (>0.7), amber (0.4-0.7), red (<0.4) 5. Show attack edges as dashed red lines with defeat_type label 6. If no path exists, show "No mechanism chain found" + closest partial EDGE CASES: - Multiple paths → show highest-confidence, note alternatives - Circular references → detect and break cycles - IV or DV not found → suggest closest matches

Step 3 — Acceptance Tests

TEST 1: Input "natural light", "task performance" EXPECT: Directed graph with ≥2 intermediate nodes VERIFY: Each node's content_preview mentions plausible mechanism TEST 2: Input "noise", "stress" EXPECT: Pathway through at least one mechanism_claim VERIFY: Edge strengths match argumentation.json exactly TEST 3: Input "unicorns", "happiness" EXPECT: "No mechanism chain found" message VERIFY: No graph rendered, no crash TEST 4: Visual verification EXPECT: High-strength edges visibly different from low-strength VERIFY: Screenshot comparison

Steps 4–7: Implementation, Verification, Iteration

This is where the live demo happens. The audience watches the AI build the D3.js visualization, Prof Kirsh runs the acceptance tests, something fails (it always does), the diagnosis happens, and the fix is applied.

Exercise A: “Why Trust This?” Evidence Panel
60–80 min · UI panel component
📄 Context file: context_exA_trust_panel.md

The Problem

Every evidence card shows a credence score (0–1) and a warrant type. But a student looking at “credence: 0.68, warrant: empirical_association” has no idea whether to trust it. Is 0.68 good? Is empirical association strong evidence? How many studies support it? The numbers are in the data but not assembled into a readable trust judgment.

Your Data

FieldWhat It Tells YouExample
credenceOverall confidence (0–1)0.68
warrant_classType of evidenceempirical_association
warrant_discountHow much to trust this type (0.25–1.0)0.80
support_countClaims backing this one up3
attack_countClaims contradicting it1
warrant_statusWARRANTED or UNGROUNDEDWARRANTED
defeat_typerebutting / undercutting / BOTH / emptyrebutting
qualifierstable / supported_but_contested / underspecifiedstable

Step 1 — Convene Panel

Panel Prompt You are running a design panel for a "Why Trust This?" evidence panel. The panelists are: - Deborah Mayo (philosophy of science, severe testing): When is evidence actually strong vs. merely present? - A first-year graduate student (target user): What would help me decide whether to cite this claim in my thesis? - Ben Shneiderman (information visualization): How do you present trust information without overwhelming the user? The panel appears alongside evidence cards. It must answer two questions: "Why trust this?" and "What could be wrong?" using only the fields available in the data. Each panelist: state your top 3 requirements and one design mistake that would make the panel misleading.

Steps 2–7: Spec, Tests, Build, Verify, Diagnose, Iterate

Follow the same structure as the demo. Write your spec from the panel discussion. Define at least 4 acceptance tests (two starters are in the full exercise doc). Implement, test on real claims from evidence.json, diagnose failures, iterate.

Exercise B: Debate Cluster Visualizer
60–80 min · D3.js argument map
📄 Context file: context_exB_debate_visualizer.md

The Problem

argumentation.json contains 38 debate clusters. Each represents a real scientific disagreement — e.g., Attention Restoration Theory vs. Stress Reduction Theory, with 9 papers and multiple claims on each side with support/attack edges. This is extraordinary data, but there’s no way to see it. We need a component that renders one debate cluster as a visual argument map.

Sample Data

{ "cluster_id": "C8", "paper_count": 9, "theories": ["Attention Restoration Theory", "Stress Reduction Theory"] } // claim nodes reference these via paper_id // edges carry: scheme_hint, defeat_type, strength (0-1) // scheme_hints: mechanistic_support, empirical_support, // theoretical_rebuttal, methodological_undercut

Step 1 — Convene Panel

Panel Prompt You are running a design panel for a scientific debate visualizer. The panelists are: - Stephen Toulmin (argumentation theory): What makes an argument map truthful vs. decorative? - Mike Bostock (D3.js creator): What graph layout makes support/attack structure readable at 20-50 nodes? - An undergraduate who has never seen an argument map (target user): What would help me understand which side is winning and why? The component renders one debate cluster: two competing theories, claims on each side, and support/attack edges between them. Data includes edge strength (0-1), defeat type, and scheme hints. Each panelist: state your top 3 requirements and one thing that would make the visualization misleading.

Steps 2–7: Spec, Tests, Build, Verify, Diagnose, Iterate

Write your spec from the panel discussion. Key decisions: two-column layout? Force-directed? How to show which side is “winning”? Define tests, build, verify against real clusters from the data.

Exercise C: Warrant Strength Calculator
60–80 min · JavaScript function + receipt UI
📄 Context file: context_exC_warrant_calculator.md

The Problem

The credence field is a single number (e.g., 0.68). Nobody knows how it was computed. The underlying components exist — warrant_discount, support_count, attack_count, studyType, signal — but they aren’t combined transparently. We need a function that recomputes trust from first principles and shows its work, like a receipt where every step is visible.

What the Output Looks Like

Trust Score: 0.68 ——————————————— Warrant type (mechanism): 0.80 ← causal pathway evidence Replication (3 supporting): +0.12 ← multiple confirmations Contestation (1 attack): -0.08 ← one rebutting defeat Study type (Empirical): +0.05 ← controlled measurement Signal (Direct Measured): +0.03 ← not speculative Defeat adjustment (rebutting): -0.04 ← direct contradiction ——————————————— Computed trust: 0.68

Step 1 — Convene Panel

Panel Prompt You are running a design panel for a transparent trust score calculator. The panelists are: - Wolfgang Spohn (ranking theory): How should warrant discount and support/attack counts combine mathematically? - Susan Haack (foundherentism): When is a claim well-grounded vs. merely coherent? How should the score reflect it? - A data scientist (implementation): What's the simplest formula that captures key distinctions without overfitting? The calculator takes one evidence claim and produces a trust score (0-1) with step-by-step breakdown. Available fields: warrant_discount, support_count, attack_count, studyType, signal, warrant_status, defeat_type, qualifier. Each panelist: propose a formula, justify it, and identify when it would give a misleading result.

Steps 2–7: Spec, Tests, Build, Verify, Diagnose, Iterate

Your panel will argue about the formula. The key question: multiplicative (like conditional probabilities) or additive (like weighted scoring)? Define tests with real claims from evidence.json — pick high-trust, low-trust, and edge cases.

Exercise D: Evidence Search Filter
60–80 min · Filter bar UI component
📄 Context file: context_exD_search_filter.md

The Problem

ka_evidence.html currently shows all ~1,900 claims in a scrollable list. There’s no way to say “show me only mechanism claims about lighting with credence above 0.6 that haven’t been defeated.” The data fields for filtering exist, but no UI exposes them.

Available Filter Fields

FieldTypeValues
warrant_classenum (7)constitutive, mechanism, empirical_association, functional, capacity, analogical, theory_derived
credencefloat0.0–1.0
constructtext~200 unique (Lighting, Biophilia, Acoustics, etc.)
qualifierenum (3)stable, supported_but_contested, underspecified
warrant_statusenum (2)WARRANTED, UNGROUNDED
studyTypeenum (4)Empirical Research, Review Article, Theoretical, Policy/Opinion
findingtextKeyword search in claim text

Step 1 — Convene Panel

Panel Prompt You are running a design panel for a search and filter bar on the Evidence Explorer page. The panelists are: - Jakob Nielsen (usability): What are the most common filtering mistakes? Which filters visible by default vs. "advanced"? - A practitioner (architect) trying to answer: "What does the evidence say about biophilic design and stress reduction?" - A philosophy student trying to find: "All contested claims with undercutting defeaters." The data has ~1,900 claims. Available filter fields: warrant_class (7 types), credence (0-1), construct (text), qualifier (3 values), warrant_status (2 values), studyType (4 values), signal (4 values), support_count and attack_count (integers). Each panelist: which 3 filters matter most? What filter would be misleading or useless?

Steps 2–7: Spec, Tests, Build, Verify, Diagnose, Iterate

Your panel will argue about which filters to show by default. A filter bar with 10 dropdowns is overwhelming. Think about progressive disclosure: primary filters visible, advanced behind a toggle. Test with real queries against the 1,900 claims.
