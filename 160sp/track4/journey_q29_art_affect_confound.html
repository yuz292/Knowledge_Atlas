Does Attention Restoration Theory survive the affective confound?

A worked evidential journey · Toulmin shape · researcher persona · Q-29 in the canonical question corpus.
⌕

The reader has typed the question above. The Atlas will now build a Toulmin argument around it, surface the principal defeater inline, and ask the reader which Chinn & Brewer (1993) response they would take with the rebuttal before they leave.

Step 1 · Data

Re-analyses controlling for affect attenuate the restoration effect by roughly 40 to 60 per cent.

The empirical core of the affect-confound critique is a set of re-analyses of attention-restoration studies in which the analyst statistically partials out the post-exposure improvement in mood. The restoration effect — operationalised either as improved performance on attention-demanding tasks or as self-reported restorativeness — does not vanish under this treatment, but it shrinks substantially. Pooled across the studies that report enough detail to be re-analysed, the shrinkage falls in the 40 to 60 per cent range.

~40–60% attenuation of the restoration effect when affect is statistically controlled. Pooled across roughly a dozen re-analysed studies (Joye & van den Berg, 2011; Stevenson et al., 2018); per-study Ns range from ≈30 to ≈400. The range reflects real heterogeneity across studies rather than a single confidence interval — this is why the journey's claim is "partly survives" rather than a point estimate.

What the data does not show is that the residual effect is zero. It also does not show whether the attenuated effect is what is left of "real" restoration after a confound is removed, or whether it is what is left of restoration after a mediator is removed. The two interpretations are statistically indistinguishable at this level of analysis.

Sources. Joye & van den Berg (2011), Urban Forestry & Urban Greening; subsequent re-analyses summarised in Stevenson, Schilhab, & Bentsen (2018), Health & Place.

Step 2 · Warrant and Backing

If two candidate mechanisms are always present together, no analysis that fails to separate them can determine which is doing the work.

The argument from data to claim is licensed by a general principle in the philosophy of science: a putative causal mechanism is only established by evidence that distinguishes it from alternative mechanisms operating in the same conditions. Where two candidate mechanisms — restored attention and improved affect — are always present together (because exposure to restorative environments produces both), neither is established until an analysis is run that holds one constant and varies the other.

The backing for this warrant lives in the methodological tradition that distinguishes confounders (variables that independently produce the outcome and are correlated with the putative cause) from mediators (variables that lie on the causal pathway between cause and outcome). The distinction is consequential here: a confounder defeats the original mechanistic claim; a mediator preserves the mechanism while specifying its pathway.

Backing. MacKinnon (2008), Introduction to Statistical Mediation Analysis; the confounder-vs-mediator distinction is itself drawn from Hill (1965) and developed in modern causal inference (Pearl, 2009).

Step 3 · Qualified Claim

Attention Restoration Theory partly survives the affective-confound critique; the mechanism it posits remains underdetermined.

Reading the data through the warrant produces the claim above with two attached qualifiers. The first qualifier is partly: the effect itself is not eliminated by the affect-controlled re-analyses, only attenuated. The second qualifier is underdetermined: the residual effect could reflect genuine restoration with a smaller true magnitude than originally claimed, or it could reflect a mood-improvement story that the affect controls did not fully capture, or it could reflect a mixed mechanism in which restoration and affect both contribute through partly overlapping pathways. The data available in 2026 cannot distinguish these.

Data

Affect-controlled re-analyses attenuate the effect by ~40–60%.
→

Claim (probably)

ART partly survives; mechanism uncertain.

This is the claim a journey can defensibly leave a reader with in 2026. It is weaker than the canonical Kaplan claim from 1989 and 1995; it is stronger than the rejection claim a hostile reading would propose.

Step 4 · Rebuttal

The principal threat to the claim: affect may be a mediator rather than a confounder.

The claim above treats the affect-controlled attenuation as evidence that the original ART mechanism is not as well established as previously believed. A coherent rebuttal exists. If affect is a mediator rather than a confounder — that is, if the causal pathway runs from environment through affective change to restored attention, rather than from environment to restored attention with affect riding along independently — then statistically partialling out affect would attenuate the restoration estimate even though ART's mechanism is intact. Under the mediation reading, the original ART claim is preserved; only its description of the pathway changes.

Whether affect is a confounder or a mediator cannot be settled by the existing pooled re-analyses. It would take dose-response and temporal-order designs that the field has only begun to run. A Bayesian reader who began with high credence in ART will reasonably take the data as compatible with the mediation reading and update only modestly; a reader who began sceptical will reasonably take the data as evidence of confounding and update more sharply.

Defeater source. The mediator-vs-confounder distinction as it applies here is articulated in MacKinnon (2008) §1.3; its direct application to ART has been raised in commentary on Joye & van den Berg (2011) and in Hartig et al. (2014), Annual Review of Public Health.

Step 5 · How readers respond to a defeater

The seven responses to anomalous data, applied to the affect-confound critique.

Chinn & Brewer (1993) catalogue seven responses that readers actually take when presented with evidence that conflicts with a theory they hold. The responses are not equally defensible; some preserve the original theory at the cost of epistemic honesty, others revise it at the cost of accumulated commitment.
