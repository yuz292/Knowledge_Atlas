# Researcher Fitting Catalog — Task 2 Worked Exemplar

*CW, 2026-04-27. The Task-2 deliverable for the researcher persona,
shown as the canonical worked example for Track 4 students.
Companion files: `question_corpus_researcher_v1.md` (the Task-1
deliverable this is built on) and
`journey_q29_art_affect_confound.html` (the Task-3 deliverable that
descends from Q-29 below).*

A reader who has finished Task 1 has a tagged corpus of forty to
sixty questions. Task 2 turns that pile into a working corpus: it
runs the four-stage winnowing protocol to cut roughly a third of the
items, then fits each surviving question to one of the five canonical
answer shapes and names at least one defeater that the journey for
that question must surface. This file shows what that operation looks
like for the researcher persona, end-to-end.

## §2. Shape-fits and defeater catalogues

Ten representative items from the working corpus, covering all five
canonical answer shapes. The other twenty items are fitted in the
team's spreadsheet using the same template; only the ten with the
most pedagogical interest are reproduced here in full.

The template for each entry is: question (verbatim from the corpus);
the five-axis tags carried forward from Task 1; the chosen answer
shape; a one-paragraph shape sketch describing how the shape would
be filled in for *this* question; and at least one named defeater
the journey must surface.

### Q-29 — Toulmin shape (the showpiece)

**Question.** *"Does Kaplan's Attention Restoration Theory survive
once you control for the affective confound — i.e., is restoration
anything more than a mood improvement plus a mediation analysis?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: inquiry · demand: causal-with-mechanism
· persona: P2-theory-prober · commitment: ART-sceptical.

**Chosen shape.** Toulmin diagram.

**Shape sketch.** *Data:* re-analyses controlling for affect attenuate
the restoration effect by roughly 40 to 60 per cent (Joye & van den
Berg, 2011; Stevenson et al., 2018). *Warrant:* a putative mechanism
that cannot be statistically separated from a co-occurring candidate
mechanism is not yet established. *Backing:* the
confounder-vs-mediator distinction in mediation-analysis methodology
(MacKinnon, 2008; Pearl, 2009). *Qualifier:* probably; in adult,
healthy samples; office and indoor-plant studies. *Claim:* ART partly
survives the affect control; the mechanism it posits remains
underdetermined. *Rebuttal:* affect may sit on the causal pathway as
a mediator rather than as a confounder, in which case the original
ART claim is preserved under a more specific pathway description.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The mediator-vs-confounder distinction
itself — if it can be shown that affect lies on the restoration
pathway, the rebuttal becomes the operative reading and the original
ART claim is re-licensed. (ii) Forthcoming dose-response and
temporal-order designs would settle the question and would make this
journey's claim either too weak or too strong.

**Worked journey:** the prototype in
`journey_q29_art_affect_confound.html` realises this fit as a
working artefact.

### Q-30 — Toulmin shape (theory-prober variant)

**Question.** *"Is biophilia a single construct, a family of
constructs, or a folk category that breaks under measurement
scrutiny?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: inquiry · demand:
converging-with-mechanism · persona: P2-theory-prober · commitment:
construct-validity-prioritising.

**Chosen shape.** Toulmin diagram (with a nested contrast pair in
the data position).

**Shape sketch.** *Data:* factor-analytic studies of biophilic-design
inventories produce inconsistent factor solutions across populations
and instruments — sometimes one general factor, sometimes a
three-factor structure (nature contact, fractal exposure, biomorphic
form), sometimes more. *Warrant:* a construct whose factor structure
fails to replicate across populations and instruments has weak
construct validity in the Cronbach-Meehl sense; it may be a folk
category rather than a unified scientific construct. *Backing:*
construct-validity tradition (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955; Borsboom et
al., 2004). *Qualifier:* probably; conditional on the validation
samples available in 2026. *Claim:* "biophilia" is best read as a
family of partially related constructs with shared affective
overtones, not as a single measurable construct. *Rebuttal:* the
inconsistent factor solutions may reflect measurement artefacts (bad
items, sample contamination) rather than the construct's
non-existence; a clean second-generation instrument might recover the
unified factor.

**Named defeaters.** (i) Kellert & Wilson's (1993) original
formulation as a unified construct, which would re-license the
unified reading if a clean instrument were developed; (ii) the
specific-pathway accounts (Hartmann & Apaolaza, 2010) which would
re-license a *family* reading rather than a folk-category reading.

### Q-07 — Field-map shape

**Question.** *"What's an honest summary of where attention-
restoration theory stands in 2026?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand:
converging-with-mechanism · persona: P2-general · commitment:
agnostic.

**Chosen shape.** Field map (concept-map-style with time axis).

**Shape sketch.** Vertical time axis from 1989 to 2025. Five named
positions: Kaplan & Kaplan as the foundation; the PRS scale family
(Hartig et al., 1997; Berto, 2005) as one operationalisation;
cognitive-task batteries (Berman et al., 2008; Bratman et al., 2015)
as the second operationalisation; the affect-confound critique
(Joye & van den Berg, 2011) as the central transit point; two
descendants — biophilia integration and predictive-processing
re-readings. Edges labelled per Novak convention: *operationalises*,
*construct challenged*, *effect re-analysed*, *motivates*, *informs*.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The diagram could give the misleading
impression that the field has settled into the modern reinterpretations;
in fact a substantial Kaplan-loyalist position persists, particularly
in clinical and design-practice circles (Ulrich-tradition work
continues to be cited approvingly). (ii) The five-camp simplification
omits the Stress Reduction Theory tradition (Ulrich, 1991) which sits
adjacent to ART but is not derived from it; a journey reader who
arrives from the Ulrich tradition will find the diagram
underspecified.

**Worked diagram:** Figure 2 in
`answer_shape_catalogue.html`.

### Q-21 — Procedure shape

**Question.** *"How do I instrument a residential apartment to
measure indoor luminance, spectrum, and occupancy in a way an IRB
will approve and tenants will tolerate?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: practical
with-ethical-and-regulatory-gates · persona: P2-sensors-seeker ·
commitment: deployment-feasibility primacy.

**Chosen shape.** Procedure (five steps with provenance column).

**Shape sketch.** Five numbered steps: (1) spectroradiometers at
1.2 m height oriented vertically toward dominant window wall (CIE S
026/E:2018); (2) sampling rate 1 Hz illuminance / 1/60 Hz spectrum
(Brown et al., 2022); (3) PIR occupancy sensors instead of cameras
(IRB requirement, UCSD HRPP §III.C.4); (4) tenant negotiation with
a "blackout" toggle (Williams et al., 2020 compliance audit);
(5) seven-day pilot before formal data collection (lab norm).

**Named defeaters.** (i) IRB requirements vary by institution — a
team using a different institution's HRPP may face different
constraints around occupancy sensing; the procedure must be flagged
as IRB-conditional. (ii) Spectroradiometer placement at 1.2 m
assumes seated workers; for studies of standing or sleeping
occupants, the placement must be re-justified rather than copied.

**Worked diagram:** Figure 3 in `answer_shape_catalogue.html`.

### Q-22 — Procedure shape (acoustic variant)

**Question.** *"What's the lowest-cost validated way to capture
room-level acoustics for a 4-week study without continuous audio
recording (which IRBs increasingly veto)?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: measurement-
grade with privacy compliance · persona: P2-sensors-seeker.

**Chosen shape.** Procedure (with regulatory rebuttal column).

**Shape sketch.** Four numbered steps: (1) deploy SPL meters logging
A-weighted equivalent levels at 1-second intervals (no waveform
storage); (2) supplement with octave-band loggers if speech-band
analysis is needed (still no waveform); (3) document the audio-
absent design in the IRB protocol with reference to the privacy
literature; (4) cross-validate with a 30-minute attended audio
sample at week one to confirm SPL meter calibration. Each step
carries a provenance column.

**Named defeaters.** (i) SPL meters do not capture spectral content
or speech intelligibility — for studies whose hypotheses concern
those, the procedure is inadequate and a different design is needed.
(ii) Some IRBs treat SPL meters as acceptable; others require explicit
opt-in from any potentially-recorded individual; the procedure must
be checked against the local HRPP.

### Q-19 — Contrast pair

**Question.** *"For continuous skin-conductance during a museum
visit, what's the wear-vs-quality tradeoff between EmotiBit, BIOPAC
dry-electrode, and a research-grade cuff?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: measurement-
grade · persona: P2-sensors-seeker.

**Chosen shape.** Contrast pair (three-column).

**Shape sketch.** Seven differentia rows: wearability, signal
quality, sampling rate, movement artefact, cost, IRB friction, and
suitability for the museum-walking use case. Cell colour encodes a
verdict per cell. EmotiBit is best on cost, multi-sensor coverage,
and IRB friction; BIOPAC is best on signal quality and sampling rate
but has chest-lead friction; the cuff is the reference standard but
cannot be worn walking.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The verdict on EmotiBit's "substantial
movement artefact" is partial — IMU-based correction substantially
mitigates it for arousal-trend hypotheses but not for fast SCR
morphology. (ii) The cost column ignores software-stack costs;
EmotiBit's open-source pipeline is cheap to *acquire* but expensive
to *integrate* if the lab has no streaming-data infrastructure.

**Worked diagram:** Figure 4 in `answer_shape_catalogue.html`.

### Q-10 — Contrast pair (measures-shopping variant)

**Question.** *"Which of the standard creativity measures (TTCT,
RAT, AUT, CAT, divergent-thinking fluency) is least sensitive to
environmental confounds and most sensitive to environmental change?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: measurement-
grade · persona: P2-measures-seeker · commitment: assumes
commensurability.

**Chosen shape.** Contrast pair (five-column).

**Shape sketch.** Five differentia rows: construct breadth, scoring
reliability, sensitivity to environmental manipulation, time to
administer, and ceiling-effect risk in healthy young adults. AUT
and divergent-thinking fluency rate well on environmental
sensitivity; CAT (Consensual Assessment Technique) rates highest on
construct validity but worst on time-to-administer; TTCT is
historical reference standard but ceiling effects in adults are
substantial.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The Said-Metwaly et al. (2017) systematic
review showed creativity measures are *not* fully commensurable —
they tap related but partially separable constructs; the contrast
pair's framing assumes more commensurability than the literature
supports. (ii) "Sensitivity to environmental change" varies
dramatically by environmental dimension — AUT is sensitive to noise
manipulation but indifferent to lighting; the single column hides
this.

### Q-09 — Ranked brief

**Question.** *"What's the best validated instrument for perceived
restorativeness in office settings, suitable for repeated measures
within a workday?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: measurement-
grade with brevity constraint · persona: P2-measures-seeker.

**Chosen shape.** Ranked brief (five rows × six columns).

**Shape sketch.** Six columns: rank, instrument, why-this-ranking,
item count, administration time, evidence quality. Five rows:
PRS-11 (top), ROS-6, SBE single-item probe, POMS-SF as proxy,
custom VAS. The administration-time column is decision-relevant
because the use case constrains to within-workday repeated
administration; instruments above five minutes are de-ranked
regardless of psychometric provenance.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The PRS-11's validation samples are biased
toward college-student populations; measurement invariance across
cultures has been challenged (Pasini et al., 2014). For an office
study with a culturally diverse sample, the instrument may behave
differently than its validation suggests. (ii) All instruments
measure perceived restorativeness, not actual restoration outcome;
the affect-confound critique (Q-29's terrain) operates at the
instrument level, not just the theoretical level. A reader who is
sceptical of ART will be sceptical of every instrument here.

**Worked diagram:** Figure 5 in `answer_shape_catalogue.html`.

### Q-16 — Ranked brief (psychometric-shopping variant)

**Question.** *"For psychological wellbeing as a DV, do I want
Ryff's six-factor model, the WHO-5, the SWLS, or a flourishing
measure — and what makes the difference?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: deliberation · demand: measurement-
grade · persona: P2-measures-seeker · commitment: assumes
hedonic/eudaimonic distinction.

**Chosen shape.** Ranked brief (four rows × six columns).

**Shape sketch.** Columns: rank, instrument, construct coverage,
item count, administration time, evidence quality. The Ryff
six-factor instrument is ranked first for studies whose hypotheses
concern eudaimonic wellbeing; WHO-5 is ranked first for studies
whose hypotheses concern hedonic wellbeing; SWLS sits between them
as a global life-satisfaction probe; a flourishing measure (Diener's
or Keyes's) is ranked third or fourth depending on whether the
positive-functioning dimension matters for the hypothesis.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The hedonic/eudaimonic distinction is
itself contested (Kashdan et al., 2008); a reviewer who rejects the
distinction will reject the brief's framing. (ii) Ryff's six-factor
model has been challenged on factor-structure grounds (Springer et
al., 2006); the brief's first ranking is conditional on accepting
the original factor structure.

### Q-36 — Field-map shape (cross-product high/high)

**Question.** *"What does the field currently know about how spatial
environments shape cognition?"*

**Tags from Task 1.** purpose: discovery · demand: converging ·
persona: P2-general (entry question), P7-newcomer · commitment:
agnostic.

**Chosen shape.** Field map (functions as a *portal* — a map of
sub-fields, each leading to its own journey).

**Shape sketch.** A field map whose nodes are not theories but
sub-literatures: spatial-navigation neuroscience (place cells, grid
cells, Doeller-Burgess), embodied-cognition spatial work (Hommel,
Goschke), environmental-psychology spatial-affect work (Ulrich,
Kaplan), HCI/architectural cognition (Kirsh, Hutchins), and
predictive-coding-of-architecture. Edges labelled with intellectual
inheritance and methodological cross-pollination.

**Named defeaters.** (i) The diagram cannot deliver an *answer* — it
can only deliver an *orientation*. A reader who arrives expecting a
single state-of-the-field paragraph will be disappointed. The
journey must reset expectations on the landing page. (ii) Different
sub-literatures use the same words to mean different things ("space"
in spatial-navigation neuroscience is metric; "space" in
environmental-psychology is largely affective/affordance-based); the
diagram must surface these definitional drifts or it will produce
false impressions of agreement.

## §3. What the catalog is meant to teach

Three things become visible when the ten worked entries are read
together that are not visible when any single entry is read alone.

The first is that **shape fitting is a non-trivial design choice
made under real ambiguity.** Q-30 (the biophilia construct
question) could plausibly be fitted as a Toulmin or as a field map;
the team chose Toulmin with a nested contrast pair on the data side.
That choice is defensible but not unique, and a different team could
defensibly choose differently. The catalog records the choice so it
can be argued with rather than treated as given.

The second is that **defeater identification is the place the
journey designer's intellectual honesty is most visible.** Every
entry above carries at least two named defeaters drawn from the
literature — not invented for the exercise. Where the team could not
find a real defeater in the literature, the entry would have been
flagged for further mining rather than dressed up with a hypothetical
one. The catalog reads as careful precisely because the defeater
column reads as careful.

The third is that **the cross-product cells of the IV × DV hierarchy
produce questions whose answers cannot be packaged in a single
shape.** Q-36 is a high/high cross-product question and its only
honest fitting is as a *portal* field map — a map whose nodes are
sub-journeys rather than answers. Recognising this saves the team
from designing a single answer journey for a question that does not
have one, which is one of the easier ways to fail at journey design.

## §4. What the team submits at end of week 6

For Task 2 the deliverable is, per student:

(i) the working corpus (the Task-1 spreadsheet with the three new
fields populated for every surviving row: chosen shape, shape sketch,
named defeaters);

(ii) the winnowing log (counts at each sieve stage; backlog
populated; gaps acknowledged in writing);

(iii) the team-collective fitting critique notes — for at least one
fitting from each teammate's catalog, a written critique signed by
the reviewer.

The reviewer should ask: is the chosen shape the right one for the
question's purpose and adequacy demand? Is the shape sketch specific
enough to constrain a journey design, or is it generic? Are the
named defeaters real (citable in the literature) or invented?

The Curator's codebook-custody role pivots in this task to *fitting
consistency*: the same kind of question across two students'
catalogs should have been fitted to the same shape, or the difference
should be explained.
