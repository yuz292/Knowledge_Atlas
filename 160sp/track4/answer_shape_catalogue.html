Five Answer Shapes

Every question in the corpus is fitted to one of five canonical answer shapes before it becomes a journey. The shapes are not arbitrary decorations — each one is a different commitment about what would satisfy a reader. A theory-prober wants a Toulmin; a newcomer wants a field map; a sensors-seeker wants a procedure; a measures-shopper wants a contrast pair; a busy PI wants a ranked brief. This page visualises each shape with a worked example from the researcher corpus.

1. Toulmin diagram 2. Field map 3. Procedure 4. Contrast pair 5. Ranked brief

1.Toulmin diagram

A horizontal inference from data to claim, tagged with a qualifier on the inference arrow itself (e.g., "probably", "usually", "in adult office workers"). The warrant hangs vertically from the inference arrow and states the rule that licenses the move from data to claim. The backing hangs below the warrant and justifies the warrant itself — typically a theoretical framework, a body of prior literature, or a methodological norm. The rebuttal hangs vertically from the claim and names the conditions under which the claim would fail. The geometry is not decoration: reading top-to-bottom on the left tells you why the inference is licensed; reading top-to-bottom on the right tells you what could undercut it. (After Toulmin, 1958/2003; the canonical layout is the one used in most logic and rhetoric textbooks.)

Use when: the question is theory-prober, the cognitive purpose is inquiry or persuasion, and the evidential demand is causal-with-mechanism. The default shape for any answer where the reader will press on the warrant.

Worked example: Q-29 — Does Kaplan's Attention Restoration Theory survive once you control for the affective confound?

Figure 1. Attention Restoration Theory partly survives the affective-confound critique, but its mechanism remains underdetermined.

The thesis that natural and biophilic environments help people recover from stress by reducing attention fatigue (Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989; Kaplan, 1995) was challenged on the grounds that it did not control for the improvement in mood and emotion that nature exposure also produces — so better affect, not restored attention, might be the real de-stressor (Joye & van den Berg, 2011). The warrant for that challenge is a general one in the philosophy of science: when two candidate mechanisms are always present together, no analysis that fails to separate them can determine which is doing the work. The rebuttal is that the jury is still out on whether affect is in fact a confound or whether it sits downstream of restoration as a mediator — in which case ART's mechanism is preserved and merely re-described. On balance the probable conclusion in 2026 is that the restoration effect itself remains, weakened by the affect-controlled re-analyses, while the mechanism that produces it is not yet settled.

How a journey fills it in

One landing page for each of the six positions. The rebuttal is surfaced inline with the claim, not buried at the bottom — the reader sees the threat to the inference at the same moment they see the inference. The journey ends on a page that asks the reader which Chinn-Brewer response they want to take.

2.Field map (concept-map style)

A directed map of a literature, drawn after Novak & Gowin's (1984) concept-map convention. Nodes are positions, theories, or camps. Edges are labelled and directional — the label names the relationship: extends, operationalises, rebuts, narrows, informs, motivates, reinterprets. A vertical time axis on the left lets the reader see how the field moved from a foundational position to its present state. Two adjacent exemplars are worth knowing: Wardley's (2018) value-chain map adds an explicit evolution axis (genesis → custom-built → product → commodity) for questions about where a field is heading; Kunz & Rittel's (1970) IBIS, popularised as Conklin's (2005) dialogue mapping, makes pro/con relationships explicit by typing edges as supports, objects-to, or responds-to. All three share the rule that edges carry typed direction; that rule is what makes the diagram a map rather than a cluster.

Use when: the cognitive purpose is discovery, the question sits at a high/high cell of the IV × DV cross-product, or the asker wants orientation in a literature they do not yet know. The default shape for a newcomer-style researcher question.

Worked example: Q-07 — What's an honest summary of where attention-restoration theory stands in 2026?

Figure 2. The attention-restoration literature, 1989–2025: a foundation, two operationalisations, a critique that ties them together, and two reinterpretations that descend from the critique.

The Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) thesis — that natural environments restore depleted directed attention through "soft fascination" that does not require effortful focus — was operationalised in two complementary ways during the late 1990s and 2000s. The Perceived Restorativeness Scale (Hartig et al., 1997; Berto, 2005) captured what people report about an environment's restorative qualities; cognitive-task batteries (Berman et al., 2008; Bratman et al., 2015) measured whether attention-demanding performance actually improved after nature exposure. Both lines of work were challenged together by Joye & van den Berg's (2011) argument that the effect they appeared to demonstrate was not separable from a simpler mood-improvement story. That single critique is the central transit point of the modern field: the predictive-processing reinterpretations of the early 2020s and the biophilia-integration accounts both descend from it, each preserving some of Kaplan's original architecture while reframing the mechanism. The original thesis has not been displaced; it has been operationalised, challenged, and partially preserved by reinterpretation. A reader who arrives in 2026 looking for a single "state of the literature" will not find one — what they will find is a field that has split into a foundational position, two operationalisation traditions, a critique, and two heirs that descend from the critique rather than from the foundation.

How a journey fills it in

Each node is a click target leading to a sub-page that briefly describes the position, its central paper, its operational definition of restoration, and its place in the field's sequence. Each edge is also a click target — clicking on operationalises opens a page that shows how the foundational claim was rendered measurable; clicking on construct challenged opens a contrast-pair view of the original PRS treatment versus the affect-controlled re-analysis. A reader's journey is a path through the graph; the journey designer chooses an entry node by persona (Kaplan loyalists enter at the foundation, sceptics enter at the critique) and a stopping rule (a fixed depth, or until a question's adequacy condition is met).

3.Procedure

A numbered sequence of steps with provenance for each step. The provenance column says why this step is here — a citation, a regulatory requirement, an empirical justification. A procedure is not a recipe: a reader needs to know which steps are negotiable and which are non-negotiable, and the provenance column makes that visible.

Use when: the question is sensors-seeker or tests-seeker and the adequacy demand is practical with regulatory or ethical gates. Also the default shape for many contributor (P3) questions in later passes.

Worked example: Q-21 — How do I instrument a residential apartment to measure indoor luminance, spectrum, and occupancy in a way an IRB will approve and tenants will tolerate?

Figure 3. A five-step deployment protocol for a residential lighting and occupancy study, with each step's authorising source named.

  1. Define spatial sampling. Place spectroradiometers at 1.2 m height in living room, kitchen, and one bedroom; orient receivers vertically toward the dominant window wall.
    Provenance: CIE S 026/E:2018 §4.3 on receiver placement; pilot data showed bedroom lux differs ≥10× between window and far wall.
  2. Choose sampling rate. 1 Hz for photopic illuminance, 1/60 Hz for spectrum (full SPD captures every 60 s).
    Provenance: Brown et al. (2022) recommend ≥1 Hz to capture transient cloud effects; SPD storage cost dominates above 1/60 Hz.
  3. Layer occupancy. Battery-powered PIR (passive infrared) sensors in each instrumented room; no cameras, no microphones.
    Provenance: IRB will reject cameras in residential interiors absent extraordinary justification (UCSD HRPP §III.C.4).
  4. Tenant negotiation. Walk-through with tenant prior to deployment; offer a "blackout" toggle the tenant can flip for ≥4 h to suspend logging.
    Provenance: Williams et al. (2020) compliance audit found ≥30% drop-out without an opt-out affordance; tenant agency is also an IRB norm.
  5. Pilot the deployment for 7 days. Inspect data daily; do not begin formal data collection until sensors have logged ≥6 of 7 days without dropout.
    Provenance: internal lab norm; sensor-failure modes (battery, occlusion) typically appear in the first week.

A residential lighting study has to satisfy three constituencies at once, and the protocol's shape is determined by their simultaneous demands. The first is the measurement standard — CIE S 026/E:2018 — which fixes receiver placement at 1.2 m and orientation toward the dominant window wall, because a spectroradiometer measuring at chest height in a kitchen tells you about the kitchen and not about the worker. The second is the Institutional Review Board, which will reject any residential deployment that records identifiable behaviour; cameras and microphones are out, and passive infrared occupancy detection is the rare instrument that captures the variable an occupancy researcher needs (someone is in the room) without capturing the variable an IRB will not let cross the apartment threshold (who they are or what they are saying). The third is the tenant, who will withdraw if the instrumentation feels surveillant; Williams et al. (2020) found a thirty per cent drop-out rate in residential studies that lacked a tenant- controlled "blackout" toggle, and that finding now drives the design rather than the data analysis. The seven-day pilot is the lab's hard-learned rule: sensor failures (battery, occlusion, network drop-out) almost always appear in the first week, and a deployment that does not catch them produces a month of unusable data. A team that departs from this protocol in some particular respect must say which of the three constituencies it is choosing to relax — the protocol does not authorise quiet omissions.

How a journey fills it in

Each step is a page; the provenance column is rendered as a sidebar that the reader can collapse. The journey exposes a "negotiability indicator" on each step: which steps can be adapted for context (most) and which are hard regulatory or ethical gates (the IRB ones). A defeater panel at the end asks the reader to consider what specifically about their building or population would call for departing from the procedure.

4.Contrast pair (n columns)

Two or more options laid out side-by-side, with rows of differentia down the left. The rows are themselves curated: the journey designer chooses which dimensions matter, and the reader can see the choice. A contrast pair is not a winner-takes-all comparison — its job is to make the trade-offs explicit so the reader's decision is informed.

Use when: the question is a measures-seeker or sensors-seeker choice and the cognitive purpose is deliberation. Two- and three-way contrasts are the most common; beyond four columns, switch to a ranked brief.

The choice among continuous skin-conductance instruments for an ambulatory study is not a choice among "better" and "worse" devices but a choice about which compromise the study can absorb. The research-grade cuff is the field's reference standard for signal quality, but it tethers the participant and so cannot be used in a museum at all. The BIOPAC dry-electrode system delivers lab-grade SCR resolution at a sampling rate above 125 Hz, which preserves the fast morphology of individual skin-conductance responses, but the chest leads draw IRB scrutiny in any setting where the participant has to be ambulant in public space. The EmotiBit produces a lower sampling-rate EDA signal at 25 Hz and so cannot resolve the fastest SCR features, but it is open-source, costs about $700 per unit, and arrives with a multi-sensor stack — PPG, IMU, and skin temperature — whose value for an ambulatory study is not cosmetic: the IMU stream supports motion-artefact correction that is otherwise impossible at the wrist. For a museum-walking study the trade-off resolves toward EmotiBit because the alternatives cannot be deployed in this use case at all; the study accepts the loss of about one order of magnitude of EDA temporal resolution because its hypotheses concern arousal trends across exhibits rather than the morphology of individual responses. A study that needs that morphology must change its use case rather than its instrument.

How a journey fills it in

Rows can be re-weighted by the reader (a "what matters most to me" slider on each row). A change in row weights re-colours the cells and produces a recommended option on the right edge of the diagram. A defeater panel at the end asks: under what conditions would the recommendation be wrong?

5.Ranked brief

A ranked list of options with three columns: the option, the criteria-based reason, and the evidence quality (strong / moderate / weak). The brief is short — typically five to seven rows — and assumes a reader who is choosing under time pressure. The evidence column is non-negotiable: every row carries an honest evidence rating.

Use when: the cognitive purpose is deliberation under brevity constraint. The default shape for measures-shopping under time pressure and for senior-PI strategic questions in P8's forthcoming pass.

Choosing a perceived-restorativeness instrument for within- workday repeated measures is, before it is anything else, a choice about how much of an office worker's day the measurement can absorb. The brief PRS-11 is ranked first not because it is the most validated instrument overall — the older PRS-26 has a longer validation history — but because it is the briefest validated form that still covers all four ART dimensions (being-away, fascination, extent, compatibility), and at two to three minutes per administration it can be delivered three or four times across a workday without participant fatigue collapsing the data. The ROS-6 is ranked second because it measures the outcome of restoration rather than the perceived restorativeness of the environment; this makes it complementary to PRS-11 rather than a substitute, and a study that wants both axes of the construct should plan to administer the two together. The SBE single-item probe is third on the strength of its ten-second administration time — it is the only instrument here that can be administered as a momentary intrusion rather than a measurement event — but it sacrifices dimensional coverage entirely. POMS-SF appears as a proxy because the literature uses it that way, but it does not measure restoration directly and at five to seven minutes per administration it is barely workable for repeated within-day use. Custom VAS scales are quick but their psychometric properties are site-specific and do not transport across studies. A team choosing for a different use case — a single end-of-day measurement in a longitudinal study, say — would rank these instruments differently: the PRS-26 would likely move to the top, and POMS-SF's administration time would no longer rule it out.

How a journey fills it in

Each row is a click target leading to a one-page brief on that instrument: psychometric provenance, validation samples, administration cost, known sensitivities. The brief ends with a "rebuttal" row that names the most common objection the recommended instrument faces, and points at the alternative most likely to address it.
