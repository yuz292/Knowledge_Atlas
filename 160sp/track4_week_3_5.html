Why this week matters

Walking the site as yourself teaches you nothing you do not already know. The discipline of persona adoption forces you to encounter the site the way a stranger does — someone with different goals, different knowledge, and a different threshold for giving up.

Nielsen's first law applies here

You are not the user. You know where the navigation is because you built it. You know what "warrant strength" means because you read about it in class. Your persona does not. The persona panel is how you simulate the encounter you cannot have directly.

What you need to understand before building

A persona is not a label

"Undergraduate student" is a label. Maya Chen — 19, second-year, iPhone, ChatGPT daily, does not know what a latent variable is, will bail in 5 seconds if she hits jargon — is a persona. The difference is generative power: Maya predicts specific reactions; "undergraduate student" predicts nothing (Cooper, 2014).

Three panellists, not one

A single AI persona gives you one viewpoint. Three deliberately-different panellists — a sceptic, an enthusiast, a pragmatist — generate productive disagreement. When all three agree on a friction point, you have strong evidence. When they disagree, you have a research question.

The falsifier is the key

A panel prompt that cannot disconfirm itself is generating narrative, not evidence. Desideratum D6 in the exercise requires that your prompt include at least one prediction that the panel could falsify.

Anchor to a specific URL

A panel asked to evaluate "the site" will hallucinate reactions to features that do not exist. Name the exact page URL in your prompt. The panel walks that page, not an abstraction.

The 8 desiderata for a strong panel-creation prompt (D1–D8)

Your panel prompt will be graded against these 8 criteria. A strong prompt meets all of them.

D1 · Specificity over generality

Each panellist has a named context, not a category. "A 160 student in week 4 who has never touched FastAPI" beats "a CogSci undergrad".

D2 · Three deliberately-different angles

The three panellists should disagree productively. One sceptic, one enthusiast, one pragmatist — or one focused on speed, one on depth, one on social proof.

D3 · Goals and constraints made explicit

Each panellist has a goal they came to the site to accomplish, a time budget, a device, and a competing tab they could switch to.

D4 · Knowledge state declared

What does this panellist already know about cognitive science, evidence weighting, or the topic? Vagueness lets the AI smooth over confusing parts.

D5 · Output protocol specified

Tell the panel the exact form their reaction should take: a click trace, a list of friction points, a one-sentence verdict. Without this, you get prose that cannot be compared.

D6 · A falsifier built in

The prompt should make at least one prediction the panel could falsify — e.g., "If useful, list why; if not, list three things you wanted instead."

D7 · No flattery instructions

Avoid "be helpful", "be encouraging", or anything that biases the panel toward agreement. Negative findings are the main asset.

D8 · Anchored to a specific URL or page

The panel walks a real page. Name the URL in the prompt. A panel asked to tour "the site" will hallucinate features that do not exist.

How to use AI this week

Ask AI for

  • help drafting your three-panellist prompt against the 8 desiderata (see below)
  • a critique of your draft prompt — "which desiderata does this prompt fail?"
  • page-specific reactions — paste the page HTML and ask the panel to walk it
  • severity ratings for friction points using Nielsen's 4-level scale

Do not ask AI to

  • generate a persona from scratch without grounding it in the 8 provided personas
  • evaluate a page you have not actually opened yourself
  • flatten all panel disagreements into a consensus — the disagreements are data

Week deliverables

D1 Revised panel-creation prompt

Rationale
The prompt is the instrument that generates all your usability data for the rest of the quarter. A vague prompt produces generic observations; a well-constructed prompt produces findings you can act on.

What to submit
The final revised prompt (post-critique), submitted via the panel exercise form.

What good work looks like
Meets all 8 desiderata. Each panellist has a named context, explicit goals, a declared knowledge state, and an output protocol. Includes a falsifier.

Example
Three panellists for Maya Chen: (1) Maya in Week 3, first visit, on her phone during a bus ride; (2) Maya in Week 6, revisiting to finish A1, on her laptop; (3) Maya's roommate who overheard about the site but has no CogSci background.

D2 Panel transcript + journey note

Rationale
The transcript is your raw data. The journey note is your first analysis. Together they demonstrate that you can extract actionable findings from a persona simulation.

What to submit
Full panel transcript (verbatim) plus a one-page journey note with ≥3 friction points, each tagged to a heuristic.

What good work looks like
Friction points are specific ("the breadcrumb says 'Home' but links to the 160sp schedule, violating H2 — match between system and real world") rather than vague ("navigation is confusing").

Example
Filed as a PR to docs/ux/journeys/ with the journey note as a Markdown file.

D3 One GitHub Issue

Rationale
Issues turn findings into actionable work items. The ux-journey label makes them findable by anyone working on site improvements.

What good work looks like
Title names the page and the problem. Body includes the heuristic violated, the severity, a screenshot or quote from the panel, and a proposed fix.
