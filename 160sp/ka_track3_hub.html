Studio framing

Track 3 is about controlled built-environment experimentation. You will inspect and clean room assets in Blender, learn how to alter them in experimentally meaningful ways, and package those results so they can be used in browser-runnable A-Frame scenes.

This means the class is not mainly about text-to-3D generation and not mainly about Unreal engineering. It is about taking strong source models and altering them in ways that are geometrically plausible, academically legible, and easy for other students and researchers to review.

Some requests will not arrive in everyday design language. A researcher may ask for a room that increases refuge while preserving visibility control, or increases awe without making the room feel cold. You must learn how to translate that language into room edits, and how to explain those edits back to a user.

What success looks like

You can start from an existing room model and identify which transformations it can support.

You can map a research-style request onto architectural factors such as ceiling height, wall curvature, enclosure, light, and prospect/refuge.

You can implement or specify controlled edits without breaking hierarchy, openings, materials, or scene structure.

You can build one browser-runnable A-Frame scene that preserves the logic of a real experiment rather than merely looking impressive.

You can package variants, logs, and explanations so another researcher could understand and reuse the work.

Why the course uses A-Frame

Advantages

A-Frame scenes run in the browser, are easy to inspect, easy to submit to Git, and much easier for TAs and future students to review. That matters in a course where experimental scenes must be shared, criticized, and reused quickly.

Limits

A-Frame is not the best choice for maximal photorealism, heavy physics, or the most ambitious headset-specific interactions. Those are real limits, and students should understand them rather than pretend they do not exist.

Course decision

For this course, the official deliverable path is Blender for asset preparation plus A-Frame for final scenes. Unity and Unreal remain acceptable prototype environments, but they are not the required student baseline and not the submission contract.

How to work with AI in this track

You are encouraged to ask AI for definitions, comparisons of toolchains, code sketches, debugging suggestions, and summaries of relevant papers. But AI is a copilot here, not the source of truth. You must convert its advice into concrete tests and artifacts.

  1. You are encouraged to ask AI for definitions, comparisons of toolchains, examples of data structures, code sketches, debugging suggestions, and summaries of relevant papers. But every time you ask AI something important, you should also ask yourself four questions:
  2. What would I need to know to tell whether the answer is wrong or too vague?
  3. What concrete evidence or test would show that the advice actually works on a room model or scene?
  4. What assumptions is the answer making about the toolchain, asset quality, browser limits, or scene structure?
  5. What part of the answer needs to be translated into a deliverable for this studio?

How the studio is organized

You will work as a small production studio rather than as isolated individual projects. With a compact team, everyone contributes across roles rather than specialising in one lane. The areas of responsibility below rotate or overlap as the quarter progresses.

Benchmark Rooms & Asset Integrity

Curate, inspect, and maintain the shared room corpus. Everyone does this in Week 1; afterwards one person owns corpus health.

Factor Ontology & Research Mapping

Define and refine the six core factors. Connect research language to measurable room parameters.

Transformation Operators

Build and test the Blender-based edits that change geometry, materials, and light without breaking the asset.

Scene Assembly & Instrumentation

Move the cleaned assets into one browser-runnable A-Frame scene with explicit condition labels and event hooks.

Integration & Packaging

Keep the pieces consistent and make the final bundle intelligible to someone outside the team.

Standing weekly packet

  • one primary artifact for the week
  • one short memo on what was learned, what broke, and what remains uncertain
  • evidence of completion: screenshots, export notes, browser checks, scene files, or test notes
  • a self-assessment against that week's criteria
