Article Finder Assignment

Find ten peer-reviewed empirical papers that address your assigned K-ATLAS search question, build a relevance filter that automates the screening decision, validate it against your own manual judgments, and contribute both the papers and the filter to the K-ATLAS corpus.

What Is This Assignment?

K-ATLAS's knowledge base grows by ingesting peer-reviewed papers about environment–behavior relationships. Each paper that enters the system adds evidence that can update the credence values the system assigns to theoretical claims — for example, raising the default credence (DC) of the claim that natural environments reduce amygdala activation, or strengthening the warrant connecting fractal complexity to stress recovery. But the system needs a way to decide, quickly and reliably, whether a given paper is relevant to a particular search question. That is where you come in.

Your assignment has two interlocking parts. The first is a systematic article search: using Google AI Citation, Elicit, Consensus, and related tools, you will find ten peer-reviewed, empirical papers not currently in K-ATLAS that directly address your assigned search question. The second is a technical contribution: you will build a relevance filter — a Python program that takes a search question and a paper's abstract and outputs a relevance score and binary judgment. You will calibrate this filter against your own manual labels, validate it on a held-out set, and integrate it into K-ATLAS as a callable function.

Both parts depend on each other. The filter is calibrated on papers you find during your search. The filter, in turn, helps you screen candidates quickly during extended searches. Understanding what the filter gets right and wrong will deepen your understanding of what relevance means in this domain.

Individual work

The article finder assignment is completed individually — unlike the image tagger, which is a group project. You claim one question from the K-ATLAS question bank (questions marked available), and that question becomes yours for the duration of the assignment. No two students work on the same question.

The Intellectual Challenge

Relevance is not a binary property of papers — it is a relational judgment that depends on the specificity of the question, the evidence type sought, and the theoretical frame being used. A paper that reports cortisol reduction after a 20-minute walk in a forest is relevant to a question about physiological stress recovery in natural environments, but may or may not be relevant to a question specifically about Stress Recovery Theory, depending on whether the paper engages SRT as a theoretical frame or merely reports the finding. A paper on aquatic soundscapes and relaxation might be relevant to a question about water feature design, but irrelevant to one specifically asking about visual features.

This is precisely why building a relevance filter is intellectually interesting: you must make these distinctions explicit. The act of defining what counts as relevant — precisely enough that a program can reproduce your judgment — forces clarity about the question's scope and the theoretical claims it is trying to support. You will discover that some of your initial intuitions about relevance are inconsistent, and resolving those inconsistencies is part of the work.

How the Question Queue Works

K-ATLAS begins with 130 search questions. As students complete assignments — each submission represents approximately ten new papers for a given question — the marginal value of additional submissions for that question diminishes. Most of the high-profile, easily-discoverable papers for a well-trodden question will already be in the corpus after the first two or three submissions. A fourth student working on the same question faces a harder search task and produces less new value for the system.

For this reason, the question bank is a priority queue: questions with zero completed submissions are listed first and are flagged as high priority. Questions with one or two completed submissions are listed next. Questions with three or more completed submissions appear at the end of the queue and are flagged as lower priority — not because the question is unimportant, but because the corpus coverage for that question is already substantial.

When you log in to the student portal and browse available questions, you will see a priority badge next to each question. Claiming a high-priority question is better for K-ATLAS and, practically, means a more open search landscape — fewer papers to rule out as already-in-corpus. The queue updates in real time as other students submit, so check it before finalising your choice.

Queue priority badges

🟢 HIGH — 0 completed submissions. Open territory; most good papers are available.
🟡 MEDIUM — 1–2 completed submissions. Still worthwhile; expect ~20–30% corpus overlap.
🔴 LOW — 3+ completed submissions. Hard mode; most accessible papers already taken.
✅ COMPLETE — 10+ completed submissions. Only available for extra-credit bonus work.

Two Tools K-ATLAS Provides

Rather than building all search and filtering infrastructure from scratch, K-ATLAS provides two scripts that handle the heaviest parts of the pipeline. You are expected to use both, understand what each does, and report on their output in your reflection report. The relevance filter you build sits inside this infrastructure as the question-specific scoring layer.

1. auto_search.py — Automatic paper discovery

Given your question ID, this script queries the Semantic Scholar and OpenAlex academic APIs and returns a ranked CSV of candidate papers. It performs lightweight TF-IDF relevance scoring, checks each DOI against the K-ATLAS corpus, and flags open-access papers with downloadable PDFs. Use it at the start of your search to get an initial candidate pool quickly.

python3 scripts/auto_search.py \\ --question-id SQ-031 \\ --db ./ae.db \\ --output ./search_results/SQ-031_candidates.csv

The output CSV is sorted by relevance score (new-to-corpus papers first). Open-access papers include a direct PDF URL in the open_access_url column — download those PDFs for processing in the next step. The script is a starting point, not a complete solution. It typically returns 15–25 candidates per run; you should also search manually using Google AI Citation and Elicit to find papers that APIs miss.

2. process_submitted_pdfs.py — PDF drop and review table

This is the core workflow tool. Once you have a folder of PDF candidates — from auto-search downloads, from Google AI Citation, from Elicit, or from your library — drop them all into a folder and run this script. It reads each PDF, extracts the title, DOI, and abstract automatically, checks the DOI against the K-ATLAS corpus, runs a three-stage relevance assessment, and outputs a review table with a recommendation for each paper.

python3 scripts/process_submitted_pdfs.py \\ --folder ~/Downloads/my_candidate_pdfs/ \\ --question-id SQ-031 \\ --db ./ae.db

The output table has one row per PDF and the following key columns: in_corpus (yes/no — papers already in K-ATLAS cannot count toward your ten), question_score (cosine similarity of the PDF's abstract and title to your assigned question), domain_score (maximum cosine similarity across all 130 K-ATLAS questions — indicates whether the paper is in the right field even if not your specific question), is_empirical (heuristic detection of methods-section language), and recommendation (SUBMIT / REVIEW / SKIP).

The script uses both the abstract and the full PDF text. The abstract is used for relevance scoring (it is the most concentrated statement of the paper's contribution). The full text is used for the empirical detector — methods-section language (sample sizes, statistical terms, data collection descriptions) appears in the body, not the abstract. This dual-source approach catches cases where a paper has a relevant abstract but is actually a commentary or theoretical essay without data.

What you build vs. what K-ATLAS provides

The provided scripts handle domain filtering (Pass 1: is this paper in our field?) and empirical detection (Pass 3: does this paper report data?). The relevance filter you build is Pass 2: the question-specific scoring layer calibrated to your assigned question. Your filter is what makes the pipeline precise for your question specifically — it is also what gets integrated into K-ATLAS's infrastructure permanently.

Log in to K-ATLAS and claim a question from the available question bank, choosing from the high-priority end of the queue. Read your question carefully several times before beginning any search. Ask yourself: what exactly is being asked? What kind of evidence would answer it most directly — an RCT, an fMRI study, a post-occupancy survey, a meta-analysis? What theory does it anchor to, and what would a paper need to say to be genuinely relevant rather than merely topically adjacent?

You have two search modes, and you should use both during the week.

Automatic mode: run auto_search.py with your question ID. This queries Semantic Scholar and OpenAlex and returns a ranked CSV of 15–25 candidates within a minute. Download the open-access PDFs listed in the output for processing in Week 2. Record the session in your search log with query_variant = original and tool = SemanticScholar or OpenAlex.

Manual mode: read the Google AI Citation teaching module in full, then run your assigned query verbatim in Google and examine the AI Overview citations. Open each cited paper in a new tab, read the abstract, and make a quick relevance judgment. Download the PDFs of candidate papers. Run additional searches in Elicit and Consensus. Record every session in your search log — including rejected papers.

By the end of Week 1, your PDF candidate folder should contain at least 20 PDFs and you should have a search log covering at least three sessions across at least two tools. These PDFs feed directly into the Week 2 workflow.

Collect PDFs, not just titles

The PDF drop workflow in Week 2 requires actual PDF files. During Week 1, whenever you find a candidate paper, download the PDF immediately — via the open-access URL, your library proxy, Unpaywall, or the author's page. It is much harder to track down PDFs later than to grab them when you first encounter the paper.

Week 2 PDF Drop, Review Table, and Build Your Filter Days 8–14

At the start of Week 2, run the PDF processor on your full candidate folder:

python3 scripts/process_submitted_pdfs.py \\ --folder ~/my_candidate_pdfs/ \\ --question-id SQ-031 \\ --db ./ae.db

The script produces a review table — a CSV with one row per PDF, showing whether each paper is already in the corpus, its relevance scores (question-specific and domain-wide), whether it appears to be empirical, and a recommendation of SUBMIT, REVIEW, or SKIP. Open this table in Excel or Numbers and work through it: confirm SUBMIT papers by reading their abstracts, investigate REVIEW papers to resolve the ambiguity, and spot-check a few SKIP papers to verify the recommendation is correct.

The review table is your ground truth data for building your relevance filter. Add a column my_label and fill it in as you review each paper: 1 (relevant), 0 (not relevant), 0.5 (uncertain). These labels — your own considered judgments — are what your filter will be trained and validated against.

Then implement your chosen filter approach (see the section below on the three options). Run it on all your labeled papers and examine the score distribution. Where does it correctly rank relevant papers above non-relevant ones? Where does it fail? By end of Week 2: working Python filter, complete manual-label CSV, and a first calibration plot.

Week 3 Validate, Calibrate, and Complete Your Paper Set Days 15–21

This week has two concurrent workstreams. The first is filter validation: hold out 10–15 papers from your calibration set, run the filter on them, and compute precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC comparing filter predictions to your manual labels. Examine false positives and false negatives. Write a brief analysis of what failure modes reveal about the filter's limitations and about the scope of your search question.

The second workstream is completing your paper set. If you have ten qualifying papers already, verify them: check each DOI against the K-ATLAS corpus, confirm the papers are empirical and peer-reviewed, confirm each abstract directly addresses the mechanism or outcome specified in your question, and collect full-text PDFs. If you do not yet have ten qualifying papers, run additional search sessions using the query variant strategy described below.

Apply your calibrated filter to any new candidate papers found this week — this is the practical payoff of building it. Papers that score below your calibrated threshold are likely not qualifying; examine them briefly before discarding to confirm the filter's recommendation is correct.

The Relevance Filter

Why Build a Filter?

A relevance filter serves two purposes. Practically, it speeds up your search: once calibrated, you can run it on a batch of candidate abstracts in seconds and get a ranked list of likely-relevant papers to prioritise for closer reading. Intellectually, it makes your relevance criteria explicit and testable. Any judgment that can be operationalised can be scrutinised, refined, and improved. A filter that performs poorly is not a failure — it is evidence that your relevance criteria are underspecified or that the question is ambiguous in ways that matter for K-ATLAS's evidence base.

More importantly: K-ATLAS needs a relevance filter for every question in its bank. Your filter will be integrated as a callable function that the system can invoke when new papers arrive and need to be matched to open questions. You are not just doing an assignment — you are contributing infrastructure.

The full relevance pipeline has three stages. K-ATLAS provides Pass 1 and Pass 3 as infrastructure (inside process_submitted_pdfs.py). You build Pass 2.

Pass 1 checks domain membership: does this paper belong to the field of environmental psychology / built environment research? It does this by computing the maximum cosine similarity of the paper's abstract against the embeddings of all 130 K-ATLAS search questions. A paper scoring below 0.28 on this measure is almost certainly outside the domain — an economics paper cited in a footnote, a methods paper on a different topic, etc.

Pass 2 is your contribution: the question-specific filter calibrated to your assigned question. Papers that pass the domain filter but score below your calibrated threshold on question similarity are in the right field but answering a different question. The system flags them as potentially useful for other K-ATLAS questions — your Pass 2 output includes a best_other_question field showing which question they best match.

Pass 3 checks whether the paper is empirical. It runs on the full PDF text (not just the abstract), looking for methods-section language: sample sizes, statistical terms, data collection descriptions, physiological measure mentions. A paper that passes Passes 1 and 2 but fails Pass 3 is flagged REVIEW rather than SUBMIT — it may be a theoretical essay, a commentary, or a review article that does not count as one of your ten empirical papers.

Three Approaches

You must choose one of the following three approaches. They increase in sophistication and in the quality of results you can expect. The third is recommended unless you have no prior Python experience.

Approach A
Baseline
Concept keyword matching. Manually extract 8–12 key concepts from the search question (mechanism terms, theory names, outcome words, environmental features). Check how many appear in each abstract. Score = fraction present. Simple and transparent; brittle to synonyms and paraphrase. Use as a baseline comparison if implementing Approach C.
Approach B
Intermediate
TF-IDF cosine similarity. Represent the query as a pseudo-document; fit a TF-IDF vectorizer on all abstracts plus the query; compute cosine similarity between query vector and each abstract vector. No GPU needed; captures term weighting; still misses semantically equivalent terms using different vocabulary.
Approach C
Recommended
Sentence-transformer embedding similarity. Use a pre-trained sentence transformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, ~80 MB) to embed both the query and each abstract into a shared semantic space; compute cosine similarity. Captures paraphrase and synonym relationships. Runs on CPU in under a second per abstract.

Implementation Guide

Setup (Approach C — recommended)

pip install sentence-transformers scikit-learn numpy pandas

Core filter function

from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer import numpy as np # Load once; reuse across calls (slow to load, fast to run) _model = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2') def compute_relevance(question: str, paper: dict, threshold: float = 0.38) -> dict: """ Args: question : the search question (string) paper : dict with keys 'title', 'abstract' threshold: cosine similarity cutoff (calibrated per question) Returns: { 'score' : float, # cosine similarity in [0, 1] 'relevant' : bool, # True if score >= threshold 'method' : str, # 'sentence_transformer_MiniLM' 'model' : str # model name for reproducibility } """ # Concatenate title and abstract for richer signal paper_text = (paper.get('title', '') + ' ' + paper.get('abstract', '')).strip() q_emb = _model.encode(question, normalize_embeddings=True) p_emb = _model.encode(paper_text, normalize_embeddings=True) score = float(np.dot(q_emb, p_emb)) # cosine sim (vectors are normalised) return { 'score' : round(score, 4), 'relevant' : score >= threshold, 'method' : 'sentence_transformer_MiniLM', 'model' : 'all-MiniLM-L6-v2' }

Batch scoring your candidate pool

import pandas as pd df = pd.read_csv('my_candidate_papers.csv') # must have 'title', 'abstract' question = "What peer-reviewed evidence shows that..." # your question text results = [compute_relevance(question, row) for _, row in df.iterrows()] df['filter_score'] = [r['score'] for r in results] df['filter_relevant'] = [r['relevant'] for r in results] # Sort by score descending to see your best candidates first df = df.sort_values('filter_score', ascending=False) df.to_csv('my_candidates_scored.csv', index=False) print(df[['title', 'filter_score', 'filter_relevant', 'my_label']].to_string(index=False))
Approach B — TF-IDF alternative

If you choose Approach B, use sklearn.feature_extraction.text.TfidfVectorizer and sklearn.metrics.pairwise.cosine_similarity. Fit the vectorizer on a list containing the question plus all abstracts, then extract the query vector (index 0) and compute its cosine similarity with each abstract vector (indices 1+). The rest of the calibration and validation pipeline is identical.

Calibration and Validation

The default threshold of 0.38 is a starting point — it will not be optimal for your specific question. You must calibrate it using your manual labels and validate the result on a held-out set. The process is as follows.

Calibration set: take your manually labeled papers (minimum 20, ideally 25–30; at least 8 labeled 1 and 8 labeled 0). Compute filter scores for all of them. Plot precision, recall, and F1 as a function of threshold (try thresholds from 0.20 to 0.60 in steps of 0.02). Choose the threshold that maximises F1 on the calibration set. This is your calibrated threshold T*.

Held-out validation set: hold out 10–15 labeled papers not used for calibration. Apply the filter with T* and compute precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC on this set. This is your honest performance estimate.

Report all four metrics in your reflection report. Also produce two figures: a precision-recall-F1 vs. threshold curve (calibration), and a scatter plot with filter score on the x-axis and your manual label (0/1) on the y-axis, with the calibrated threshold shown as a vertical line.

Computing AUC-ROC

from sklearn.metrics import (precision_score, recall_score, f1_score, roc_auc_score) import numpy as np # held_out_labels: list of your manual labels (0 or 1) # held_out_scores: list of filter scores for the same papers threshold = 0.38 # replace with your calibrated T* preds = [1 if s >= threshold else 0 for s in held_out_scores] print('Precision:', precision_score(held_out_labels, preds)) print('Recall: ', recall_score(held_out_labels, preds)) print('F1: ', f1_score(held_out_labels, preds)) print('AUC-ROC: ', roc_auc_score(held_out_labels, held_out_scores))
Small-sample statistics deserve caution

With 10–15 papers in the validation set, all statistics will have wide confidence intervals. Report exact counts alongside percentages — "3 of 6 relevant papers were retrieved (recall = 0.50)" is more informative than just "recall = 0.50." Interpret your results with appropriate epistemic humility, and use the error analysis to make qualitative claims that the numbers alone cannot support.

Error analysis (required)

List every false positive (filter said relevant, you said not) and every false negative (filter said not relevant, you said yes) in the validation set. For each case, write one sentence explaining what the filter misunderstood. Then write a paragraph synthesising the pattern: what does the filter reliably miss, and why? This analysis is worth more to K-ATLAS than the numeric F1 score, because it tells the system's maintainers where the filter needs human review.

Query Variant Strategy

If your primary assigned query does not yield ten qualifying papers, apply systematic variations. Each variant probes a different sector of the literature. Document every variant in your search log with the query_variant field.

Variant 1: Change the evidence type signal

Replace the evidence type phrase with a different method. If the original asks for "experimental studies," try "meta-analyses," "post-occupancy evaluations," "longitudinal studies," or "neuroimaging evidence." Different methods address the same question from different angles and appear in different papers.

Variant 2: Change the population or context

If the original specifies "office workers," try "university students," "hospital patients," "urban residents," or "adults generally." Many environmental psychology findings are studied across multiple populations and the papers may not appear in searches tied to a specific group.

Variant 3: Change the theoretical anchor

If the original anchors to ART, try SRT, biophilia hypothesis, or prospect-refuge theory. Different theoretical communities produce papers that address the same empirical question under different names.

Variant 4: Decompose the question

If your question asks about both a mechanism and an outcome, search for each separately. A paper might not address the full question but might address the mechanism half — and still qualify if the abstract is directly relevant to the mechanism the question specifies.

Variant 5: Citation chaining via Connected Papers

Take the most relevant paper found so far and paste its title into Connected Papers (connectedpapers.com). The resulting graph shows papers that cite it or are cited by it — a productive way to find closely related work that shares neither your terminology nor your theoretical framing but addresses the same empirical question.

Using Elicit, Consensus, and Connected Papers

Elicit (elicit.org)

Elicit is particularly useful for two tasks. First, abstract screening: paste your query and Elicit returns papers with structured summaries (sample size, methods, key findings, effect sizes) that let you assess relevance faster than reading abstracts. Second, extraction: once you have your ten papers, use Elicit's extraction table to pull structured data — this will partially fill your CSV's abstract field and note column.

Elicit works best with research questions phrased as Google AI Citation queries (complete sentences, not Boolean). The results overlap with but do not duplicate Google AI Citation — typically 30–50% of Elicit's results are not in Google's top results.

Consensus (consensus.app)

Consensus synthesises evidence across papers and presents "consensus claims" — statements supported by a specified percentage of relevant papers. It is useful for quickly locating core papers in a well-studied area, and for confirming that your question is genuinely contested (if Consensus shows high agreement, the IC→DC update your papers can produce is large; if it shows mixed evidence, your papers are especially valuable for resolving uncertainty).

Connected Papers (connectedpapers.com)

Enter the DOI or title of the single most relevant paper you find. Connected Papers builds a visual graph of papers with similar references and citation relationships. Papers in the same "cluster" as your seed paper are strong candidates. Papers on the periphery of the cluster may represent adjacent theoretical traditions — worth examining, but apply the relevance filter before adding to your shortlist.

Suggested multi-tool workflow

Run Google AI Citation first (primary). For each paper found, check Elicit for structured extraction. Use Consensus to confirm theoretical framing. Take your best paper and run Connected Papers to expand into adjacent literature. Apply your relevance filter to all candidates before deciding inclusion. Document every step in your search log.

Duplicate Checking

A paper already in the K-ATLAS corpus does not count toward your ten qualifying papers. Before adding any paper to your final set, check its DOI against the corpus. There are two ways to do this.

From your student assignment page, use the built-in duplicate checker: paste the DOI into the check field and the system will return "already in corpus" or "not found." Alternatively, from a terminal with access to the K-ATLAS database:

python3 scripts/check_duplicate.py --doi "https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxx" # Returns: FOUND (already in corpus) or NOT FOUND (eligible)

If a paper is already in the corpus, it still appears in your search log and candidate CSV with exclusion_reason = already_in_corpus. Do not discard it from your records — it is evidence that your search is finding the right literature, even if the paper itself does not count.

Reflection Report

The reflection report is a 4–6 page academic document (APA format, with references) covering the following five sections. It is not a diary entry — it is a structured scientific account of your methodology, findings, and analysis.

Section 1 — The Question and Its Scope. Restate your assigned question and explain what theoretical claims it is designed to support in K-ATLAS. What kind of evidence would most directly answer it? What evidence would be relevant but insufficient? What evidence would be clearly out of scope? This section establishes the conceptual frame that justifies your inclusion and exclusion decisions.

Section 2 — Search Methodology. Summarise your search process: how many sessions, which tools, which query variants, and how many candidates each session yielded. Include a table derived from your search log. Describe any systematic gaps you noticed — topics adjacent to your question that the literature treats sparsely, or terminology communities that do not appear in each other's citation trails.

Section 3 — Relevance Filter. Describe your implementation approach, justify your choice, report your calibration procedure, and present your validation results (all four metrics plus the two required figures). Your error analysis — the detailed examination of false positives and false negatives — must be here. Conclude with a statement about what the filter's failure modes reveal about the boundaries of your question's scope.

Section 4 — The Ten Papers. Present your ten qualifying papers in a summary table (author, year, title, DOI, one-sentence finding). Then discuss in prose: which two or three papers are most directly relevant and why; whether the papers converge on a single answer to your question or whether they present conflicting evidence; and what the set of papers tells us about the state of evidence on this question (strong / mixed / emerging / under-studied).

Section 5 — Contribution to K-ATLAS. Estimate qualitatively what IC-to-DC update your ten papers might enable. Which warrants in K-ATLAS's knowledge base does this evidence support? Are there papers in your set that speak to neural or physiological mechanisms — the highest-value evidence type for strong credence updates? End with one paragraph on what you would search for next if you had another week.
