Track THREE: AI & VR Experiments

Learn to build controlled neuroarchitecture experiments from strong room assets. In this course you inspect and transform rooms in Blender, then deliver browser-runnable A-Frame scenes that students, TAs, and researchers can actually open, test, and critique.

Start Track 3 Setup → Learn about Track 3

Track Overview

Neuroarchitecture experiments need environments whose properties can be varied in a controlled way. Track 3 builds that missing layer: benchmark rooms, transformation logic, and one browser-runnable experimental scene. The aim is not to produce the most photorealistic environment possible. The aim is to produce a scene that preserves the logic of a real experiment and can be reviewed by the rest of the course without a heavy engine install.

The course contract: Blender is the asset-preparation tool. A-Frame is the official submission path for final Track 3 scenes. Unity and Unreal remain acceptable prototype environments, but they are not the grading contract and not the required student baseline.

Why This Matters

The Image Tagger (Track 1) analyzes 2D photos of rooms. But the experiments that validate those analyses need controlled 3D environments where one architectural variable can be changed while others are held stable: ceiling height, window area, wall curvature, material warmth, prospect/refuge, and so on. Real buildings are too costly and too rigid for that kind of rapid comparison. Track 3 exists to make those comparisons operational.

Course Workflow: Blender + A-Frame

The track is organized as a three-part workflow. Asset preparation and transformation happen first, scene delivery comes later.

Layer 1: Benchmark Rooms & Asset Cleanup
Find room assets that are worth transforming, inspect them in Blender, correct scale, note hierarchy, and document what kinds of edits the room can and cannot support.
Layer 2: Controlled Variants
Build one or more variants that change a specific factor cleanly. The important question is not “does it look nice?” but “can we say exactly what changed and what stayed constant?”
Layer 3: Browser Scene & Validation
Move the result into one browser-runnable A-Frame scene with condition labels, event hooks, and a log schema that another student can inspect without special hardware or an engine install.
Why the course uses A-Frame
  • Pros: browser-runnable scenes, easier Git workflow, easier TA review, easier handoff to future classes, and much lower setup friction than a full engine project.
  • Cons: lower fidelity than Unreal, less headroom for very ambitious lighting and physics, and weaker support for heavy headset-specific engineering.
  • Course judgment: for this class, shareability, reviewability, and experimental clarity matter more than maximal photorealism. That is why A-Frame is the official submission path.

Architectural Envelope Factors & Latent Variables

The emphasis in this track is on the architectural envelope — the built structure itself — rather than furnishings or decorative objects. Research in neuroarchitecture shows that ceiling height, wall curvature, light spectrum, surface materials, and spatial proportions have measurable effects on cognition and affect (Vartanian et al., 2015; Meyers-Levy & Zhu, 2007; Küller et al., 2006). Our taxonomy (see Track 1) assigns each of these effects a latent variable code. When you modify an architectural factor, you are manipulating one or more latent variables that researchers can then measure.

The table below maps the core architectural modifications to their target latent variables. These are the properties your rooms must support:

Architectural Factor What to Vary Target Latent Variables Key Research
Ceiling Height & Volume Height from 2.4 m → 5+ m; vaulted vs. flat; coffer depth L36 Awe/Grandeur · L25 Coziness/Warmth Meyers-Levy & Zhu (2007); Vartanian et al. (2015)
Wall Geometry & Curvature Rectilinear → curvilinear walls; faceted angles; alcoves and niches L24 Valence Potential · L19 Soft Fascination · L27 Perceptual Fluency Vartanian et al. (2013); Bar & Neta (2006)
Prospect & Refuge Window size/placement; vista type (nature, urban, sky); nooks and alcoves; room depth and width L03 Visibility Control · L17 Restorativeness · L18 Being-Away · L22 Mystery/Exploration Appleton (1975); Dosen & Ostwald (2016); Kaplan (1995)
Light Properties Color temperature (2700 K warm → 6500 K cool); intensity (lux); directionality; natural vs. artificial ratio L23 Arousal Potential · L24 Valence Potential · L26 Visual Comfort Küller et al. (2006); Knez (2001); Veitch & Newsham (1998)
Surface Materials & Texture Biophilic materials (exposed wood, stone, living walls); concrete vs. warm timber; texture roughness/grain L17 Restorativeness · L19 Soft Fascination · L25 Coziness · L33 Care/Maintenance Signal Kellert (2005); Nyrud & Bringslimark (2010); Sakuragawa et al. (2005)
Color Palette Hue, saturation, value across walls, ceiling, floor; warm vs. cool dominance; monochrome vs. polychrome L23 Arousal · L24 Valence · L25 Coziness · L39 Formality Mehrabian & Russell (1974); Küller et al. (2006)
Enclosure & Spatial Proportions Room aspect ratio; openness vs. containment; partition height; corridor width vs. length L01 Perceived Threat · L10 Visual Privacy · L14 Crowding Pressure · L36 Awe/Grandeur Stamps (2005); Stokols (1972); Appleton (1975)
Focal Architectural Elements Fireplaces, feature walls, built-in niches, columns, arches, bay windows L25 Coziness · L35 Identity/Placeness · L19 Soft Fascination Kellert (2005); Hildebrand (1999)
Floor Material & Texture Hardwood vs. stone vs. carpet vs. polished concrete; pattern scale; material warmth L25 Coziness · L33 Care/Maintenance · L05 Thermal Comfort Expectation Nyrud & Bringslimark (2010)
Architecture First, Furniture Second

Notice that the table above contains no furniture. The research literature shows that the architectural envelope — ceiling height, wall shape, light, materials, proportions — has stronger and more consistent effects on cognition and affect than what sits inside the room (Vartanian et al., 2015). Furniture matters for social interaction affordances (Track 1, L41–L58), but this track focuses on the structural variables that shape how a space feels before any objects are placed in it.

Where to Find Base Rooms

Students are not expected to model rooms from scratch. Begin with portable, inspectable assets, then modify their architectural properties to manipulate latent variables. Good sources for downloadable base rooms include:

  • Sketchfab: downloadable interiors in glTF/GLB, FBX, or OBJ. Prefer clean hierarchy and explicit licensing.
  • TurboSquid: useful when the mesh structure survives import well enough to support edits.
  • Poly Haven: excellent source of CC0 HDRIs, materials, and textures for browser-runnable scenes.
  • Quaternius / Kenney packs: lightweight props and interior parts that are easier to move through a web workflow than large engine-native packs.
  • Meshy / Tripo: best used for isolated props or exploratory drafts, not as the only source of the shared benchmark corpus.

Student Assignment: from benchmark room to browser scene

Your core outputs now combine the studio roadmap with the workbook. The track is not only about room images and not only about a final scene. It is about building a defensible chain from source asset to experimental artifact.

Core outputs: (1) benchmark room sheets, (2) factor and operator specifications, (3) one base-room plus variant package with a clear change log, (4) one browser-runnable vr/scene.html with explicit event hooks and a log schema, and (5) a short researcher-facing workflow or handoff note.

The point is not decorative scene building. It is producing controlled experimental stimuli and explaining exactly what the scene is meant to test, what changed, and what still remains uncertain.

Weekly Syllabus

Weeks Phase Activities & Deliverables
1 Setup & benchmark rooms Install Blender, gather candidate rooms, inspect them, and build the first shared benchmark shortlist with room sheets.
2 Factors & operator specs Define the reduced factor set, write operator sheets, and make the research-language to room-edit bridge explicit.
3–4 Proof of concept & operator library Produce one modest end-to-end proof, then compare operator behavior across rooms and document what is robust, partial, or experimental.
5 Browser scene build Build the first A-Frame scene, make the IV legible, and open a draft PR with vr/scene.html.
6 Instrumentation & translation pack Add event hooks and log schema, and document how research requests are translated into scene changes and measurement choices.
7–7.5 Workflow prototype & final bundle Package the room choice, variant logic, scene, logs, and quick-start workflow so another student or researcher can inspect the complete path.
Primary working stack: Blender for asset inspection and cleanup, A-Frame for final course scenes, and Unity / Unreal only when a student is prototyping a future extension and understands that it is not the grading contract.
Connection to Other Tracks:
  • Track 1 (Image Tagger): Your before/after room screenshots become the validation stimuli. The Tagger detects changes in biophilic materials, visual complexity, and spatial metrics — giving you objective evidence that your architectural modification shifted the intended latent variables.
  • Track 2 (Article Finder): The literature on neuroarchitecture tells you which envelope factors matter most. Ceiling height studies (Meyers-Levy & Zhu), curvature preference (Vartanian et al.), biophilic design (Kellert) — these provide the empirical grounding for your modification briefs.
  • Track 4 (Interaction Design): They design the interface through which researchers browse your room library, select base rooms, and request architectural modifications.
