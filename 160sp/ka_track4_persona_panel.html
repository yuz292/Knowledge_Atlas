What we are doing and why

This week's deliverable for the UX track is a user journey — at the very least, a serious attempt to understand what a real user of the Knowledge Atlas is trying to do, where they get stuck, and what would help. Walking the site as yourself is not enough; you already know how it works. The discipline of taking on a persona forces you to encounter the site the way a stranger does.

We will use AI panels as a leverage tool. Each of you will design a three-member panel — three AI agents, each instantiated with a slightly different version of your persona — and use the panel to generate, critique, and pressure-test journey observations. The quality of what the panel returns depends almost entirely on the quality of the prompt you write to instantiate it. Your work today is to write that prompt well.

Step 2 · Draft your panel-creation prompt

Write a prompt that, when handed to a fresh AI, will produce three usable panellists who can collectively role-play your persona on a tour of the site. Submit it via the form at the bottom of this page. Before you do, review the desiderata in Step 3 — your prompt will be graded against them.

Step 3 · Desiderata for a good panel-creation prompt

A good panel prompt does not say "act as a 160 student". It constructs three panellists with enough internal structure that their reactions are generative rather than generic. The desiderata below are the criteria the instructor will critique your submitted prompt against.

D1 · Specificity over generality

Each panellist has a named context, not a category. "A 160 student in week 4 of the quarter who has never touched FastAPI" beats "a CogSci undergrad".

D2 · Three deliberately-different angles

The three panellists should disagree productively. One sceptic, one enthusiast, one pragmatist — or one focused on speed, one on depth, one on social proof — works better than three near-clones.

D3 · Goals and constraints made explicit

Each panellist has a goal they came to the site to accomplish, a time budget, a device, and a competing tab they could switch to. These constrain the simulation usefully.

D4 · Knowledge state declared

What does this panellist already know about cognitive science, evidence weighting, or the topic the article is about? Vagueness here lets the AI smooth over the parts of the site that would actually confuse a real user.

D5 · Output protocol specified

Tell the panel the exact form their reaction should take: a step-by-step click trace, a list of three friction points with severities, a one-sentence verdict on the page. Without an output protocol you get prose that cannot be compared across panellists.

D6 · A falsifier built in

The prompt should make at least one prediction that the panel could falsify — e.g., "If the persona finds this page useful, list the reason; if not, list the first three things they would have wanted instead." A prompt that cannot disconfirm itself is not generating evidence, only narrative.

D7 · No flattery instructions

Avoid "be helpful", "be encouraging", or anything that biases the panel toward agreement. Negative findings are the main asset of this exercise.

D8 · Anchored to a specific URL or page

The panel walks a real page. Name the URL in the prompt. A panel asked to tour "the site" will produce hallucinated reactions to features that do not exist.

Step 5 · After critique — run the panel and document the journey

  1. Take the revised prompt and instantiate the panel in your AI tool of choice (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — the choice is itself part of the data).
  2. Walk the panel through the page in the order a real user would. Capture every reaction verbatim.
  3. Write a one-page journey note: what the panel encountered, what tripped them, what they could not find, and what would have helped. Tag each finding with one of Nielsen's heuristics where applicable.
  4. File your journey note as a Pull Request to the docs/ux/journeys/ directory.

What "good" looks like at the end of class

  • One revised panel-creation prompt, critiqued and improved.
  • One transcript of that panel walking a specific K-ATLAS page.
  • One journey note (≤ 1 page) with at least three actionable findings.
  • At least one of those findings filed as a GitHub Issue with the ux-journey label.
