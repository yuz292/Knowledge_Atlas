◎Goal

For each of three user archetypes, write one concrete scenario the archetype is likely to bring to the site. A scenario is a short, literal, testable description of a user's task — not a list of features they might want. The three scenarios become the evaluation instrument for T4.b.2 and for the T4.c usability pilot.

This deliverable is worth five points because its work product is small but load-bearing: every later Track 4 deliverable depends on the scenarios being sharp. A vague scenario ("a user wants to learn about lighting") cannot be walked through; a sharp scenario ("Aisha, a COGS 160 Track 1 student, wants to find the five topic bundles with the highest paper count that involve luminous, because she is picking the framework to anchor her tagging schema against") can.

›What you'll do

  1. Read the three archetype profiles below. Each links to the canonical persona section in the Knowledge Atlas persona doc.
  2. For each archetype, write one primary scenario and one alternate scenario (the archetype's second-most-likely task).
  3. A scenario must: name the user concretely, name the goal in terms the user would recognise, state what "success" looks like, and be executable by a second person without any further explanation from you.
  4. Save each archetype's scenarios as 160sp/tracks/t4/{your_id}/findings/T4.b_{role}.md (three files total).
  5. The three files are the direct input to T4.b.2 (variant evaluation) and to the T4.c pilot script.

✗Example of a naive approach

A scenario that describes an intent without a task is the commonest failure mode. The block below is the kind of first attempt that will not survive T4.b.2.

band 0–1 example — a scenario that is not a scenario

160_student scenario:

A student wants to learn about the Knowledge Atlas and see what's on it. They browse around and look at topics and theories. They might also want to submit an article.

Why this scores low. The "student" is undifferentiated (no track, no year, no current assignment context); the verb phrase "wants to learn" is not a task (what does success look like? when does the task end?); "browse around" is not something a UX reviewer can walk through; and the might also want to submit an article is a second task smuggled into the first. For T4.b.2 this scenario produces 15 identical walkthrough rows because it generates no per-variant differentiation. Band 3 requires a scenario that ends with an observable outcome a reviewer can independently check.

The schema for each archetype's scenarios:

# T4.b — {role} archetype scenarios

## Archetype profile (50–100 words)
{Who they are, what they bring to the site, what vocabulary they have, what
they do NOT know. Cite the persona doc.}

## Primary scenario
**Task**: {1-sentence, starts with user name + verb}
**Entry point**: {which page or URL they land on}
**Goal completion signal**: {what they see or do that ends the task}
**Success criteria**: {a reviewer-checkable statement of success}
**Expected duration**: {seconds, rough estimate}

## Alternate scenario
{same structure}

✓Verification

grading
  • Automated: three T4.b_{role}.md files exist with both scenarios populated.
  • Automated: each scenario has a user-name, an entry URL, and a goal-completion signal.
  • Paired-student check: a second Track 4 student executes each scenario on the live site and confirms the scenario is walkable. A scenario that needs clarification from you is a fail on Quality.
