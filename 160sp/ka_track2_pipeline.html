What Article Finder Is

At its simplest, Article Finder is the front half of the Knowledge Atlas pipeline: it searches the scientific literature for papers that might contain evidence the Atlas needs, scores them for how much they would change what we currently believe, downloads the PDFs that clear that bar, and hands them to Article Eater for structured extraction. Think of it as the part of the system that goes shopping, and Article Eater as the part that cooks.

At a deeper level, Article Finder is where the Atlas's epistemic priorities meet the world's publication record. Its Value-of-Information (VOI) scoring is a deliberate bet: studies that would fill a gap in the coherentist web of evidence are worth more than studies that merely reinforce what the Atlas already knows well. The scoring, the query generation, and the handoff contract to Article Eater are all under active design this quarter, and are the places your track-owned code will actually ship.

To understand the pipeline in more depth, read the Track 2 setup and intake pages: Track 2 Setup → and Track 2 Intake →. For the current implementation status — what is built, what is broken, and where student effort is most needed — see the Article Finder Status Brief →.

What Article Finder Actually Does

The Knowledge Atlas is built from scientific articles — primarily, articles that report experimental results about how built environments affect human cognition, perception, and affect. But there are millions of articles in the world, and only a few thousand are relevant to neuroarchitecture. Article Finder is the system that identifies those articles, collects their metadata, downloads their PDFs, and deposits them where Article Eater (the extraction engine) can process them.

This is a two-stage pipeline. Stage 1 (Discovery & Collection) finds candidate articles and gathers five things for each one: a full APA citation, the abstract, the DOI, the PDF file, and a database record linking them together. Stage 2 (Extraction) is handled by Article Eater itself — an LLM-based system that reads each PDF and pulls out structured findings (independent variable → dependent variable → effect size → statistical test). Your track owns Stage 1 and the handoff to Stage 2.

The critical idea is Value of Information (VOI). Not all articles are equally worth collecting. An article that fills a gap in the Atlas — say, the first study on how ceiling height affects creative divergent thinking — is far more valuable than the twentieth study confirming that natural light improves mood. VOI scoring helps you prioritize what to collect next, so the Atlas grows where it most needs to grow.

Worked Example: From VOI Query to Extracted Findings

Suppose the Atlas has 40 articles on how natural light affects mood but zero articles on how ceiling height affects divergent thinking. That gap makes ceiling-height + creativity a high-VOI target. Here is how the pipeline processes it, step by step:

1
VOI Query Generation. The system (or a student) constructs a targeted query: "ceiling height" AND ("creative" OR "divergent thinking" OR "ideation"). The query is informed by the taxonomy's architectural factors (Part I, Spatial Layout) and latent variables (L36 Awe/Grandeur, which Meyers-Levy & Zhu, 2007, link to abstract thinking). We now also want to generate the sort of questions that Google AI Scholar uses.
2
API Search. The query hits Semantic Scholar's graph API (already integrated in app/services/semantic_scholar.py). It returns ~180 candidate papers with title, abstract, DOI, citation count, and open-access flag. OpenAlex is used as a fallback for papers Semantic Scholar misses. At present there are no API searches offered on Google AI Scholar, so this would have to be done manually, with the results page marked and copied.
3
Relevance Filter. An LLM (or rule-based classifier) reads each abstract and asks three questions: Is this an empirical study (not a review, editorial, or theoretical paper)? Does it measure a behavioral or cognitive dependent variable? Does it manipulate or measure a built-environment property? Only papers passing all three survive. Perhaps 30 of the 180 pass.
4
VOI Scoring. Each surviving paper is scored on: (a) does it address a gap in the Atlas? (b) citation count (a proxy for influence), (c) recency, (d) is the PDF freely available? The 30 candidates are ranked. The top 20 are promoted to human review.
5
HITL Review. A human (you) looks at the top-20 list on the HITL review page. For each, you see the title, abstract, VOI score, and the system's reasoning. You accept, reject, or flag for discussion. This catches false positives (e.g., a paper on "ceiling effects" in statistics, not architecture) and false negatives the filter missed.
6
Metadata Collection & Validation. For each accepted article, the system collects or verifies: the full APA-7 citation (authors, year, title, journal, volume, pages, DOI), the abstract, and downloads the PDF. It validates that the DOI resolves, the abstract is present and non-empty, and the PDF is machine-readable (not a scan). Everything is inserted into the SQLite database (articles table).
7
PDF Drop & Handoff. The validated PDF is placed in the Zotero-compatible drop folder. Article Eater's worker picks it up, runs PDF ingestion (pdf_ingest.py), then LLM extraction with the appropriate template. Structured findings flow into the Atlas. This step is already built. Your job is to feed it well.

What's Built and What's Not

The Article Eater system has been in development since late 2025. Some parts of the pipeline are production-ready; others are skeletal or missing entirely. Understanding this maturity landscape is essential — it tells you where your effort is most needed.

Component Maturity What Exists What's Missing
Semantic Scholar API FUNCTIONAL Basic wrapper with rate-limit handling, returns title/abstract/DOI/citations No caching, no deduplication, no incremental updates
OpenAlex Fallback PRODUCTION Dual-API discovery with title matching via difflib; handles orphan PDFs Not yet integrated into the main discovery flow
Relevance Filter SKELETAL Basic article-type classification (empirical vs. theory) in student routes No LLM-based abstract screening; no on-topic classifier
VOI Scoring NOT BUILT Citation count and open-access flag stored in DB No gap-analysis integration, no scoring formula, no ranking
HITL Review Page NOT BUILT Student submission routes exist (5 endpoints) No review interface for VOI decisions or quality checking
Metadata Collection PRODUCTION SQLite schema with DOI, abstract, APA fields, corpus ID; unique indexes APA citation generation is manual; no auto-formatting
PDF Ingestion FUNCTIONAL pdfminer extraction, regex section parsing, sidecar fallback Brittle on non-standard layouts; needs scispy/ML sectioning
LLM Extraction PRODUCTION 16 article-type templates, 173 findings extracted, Pass 2 enrichment —

Student Tasks (6 Students × 3 Weeks)

Each task below is scoped for one student working over Weeks 3–5 (three weeks). Every task is specified to guide you through the 7-step AI-directed development method you learned in Week 2: Panel → Spec → Tests → Build → Verify → Diagnose → Iterate. The 7-step scaffolding for each task is given explicitly — follow it.

Reminder — The 7 Steps: 1 Panel (ask AI to simulate domain experts who argue about requirements) → 2 Spec (define inputs, outputs, success conditions, edge cases) → 3 Tests (write 3–5 concrete test cases before any code) → 4 Build (have AI build it against the spec) → 5 Verify (run tests on real data) → 6 Diagnose (when something fails, is the spec wrong or the implementation?) → 7 Iterate (fix and re-verify until all tests pass).

Task A: HITL Review Page (Priority — Week 3 First)

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: working web page + API endpoints

Build a general-purpose Human-in-the-Loop review page that displays items needing human judgment and records accept/reject/flag decisions. The first use case is VOI candidate review (Task D feeds items here), but the page must be reusable — it will later be used for relevance filter validation (Task B), extraction QA, and any future component that needs human checking.

Design requirements: Each item shows a title, abstract (or other summary), the system's score/reasoning, and three buttons: Accept, Reject, Flag for Discussion. Decisions are stored with a timestamp and reviewer ID. The page shows agreement statistics (if two reviewers see the same item) and a running accuracy log (how often does the human agree with the system?).

1Panel: Ask AI to simulate a UX researcher, a data-quality engineer, and an annotation-tool designer. Have them argue about: What metadata should each review item show? How do you handle disagreements between reviewers? Should there be a "not sure" option?
2Spec: Define the review-item schema (id, type, payload, system_score, system_reasoning, human_decision, reviewer_id, timestamp). Define the API: POST /review-items (create), GET /review-items?status=pending (fetch queue), PATCH /review-items/:id (record decision).
3Tests: (a) Submit 5 items, verify all appear in queue. (b) Accept 3, reject 2 — verify counts. (c) Two reviewers judge same item differently — verify disagreement is flagged. (d) Fetch accuracy stats — verify percentage calculation. (e) Edge case: empty queue renders correctly.
4Build: Implement with Flask (matches existing Article Eater stack) + vanilla HTML/JS frontend. Store in SQLite (new review_items table).
5Verify: Load 20 real Semantic Scholar results as review items. Have a classmate review them. Check that decisions are recorded, stats are correct.
6Diagnose: If the reviewer finds the abstract too hard to read on-screen, is that a spec problem (need more metadata?) or a UI problem? If agreement stats are wrong, trace the SQL query.
7Iterate: Incorporate feedback from at least one round of classmate testing. Ship a version that Task B, C, and D owners can use.

Task B: Relevance Filter (LLM-Based Abstract Screening)

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: classifier + benchmark report

Build an LLM-based classifier that reads an article abstract and answers three questions: (1) Is this an empirical study? (2) Does it study a built-environment factor as IV or context? (3) Does it measure a human cognitive, perceptual, or affective outcome? Articles must pass all three to be accepted. The classifier must produce a confidence score and a one-sentence justification for each decision.

1Panel: Simulate a systematic-review methodologist, a research librarian, and a cognitive scientist. Debate: How do you distinguish empirical from theoretical? What about mixed-methods papers? What about papers that study "nature exposure" without a built environment?
2Spec: Input: abstract text (string). Output: {is_empirical: bool, is_env_factor: bool, is_human_outcome: bool, confidence: float, justification: string}. Success: F1 ≥ 0.85 on a 50-article human-labeled test set.
3Tests: Create a 50-article gold set — 25 true positives (on-topic empirical), 25 true negatives (reviews, off-topic, non-empirical). Label them yourself. Measure precision, recall, F1.
4Build: Write a prompt that gives the LLM the three questions plus 3 labeled examples. Call via the existing Ollama/Gemini/OpenAI integration. Parse structured output.
5Verify: Run on the 50-article gold set. Report F1. Send the borderline cases (confidence < 0.7) to the HITL page (Task A) for human arbitration.
6Diagnose: For each false positive/negative, determine: was the prompt ambiguous? Was the abstract itself ambiguous? Classify errors by type.
7Iterate: Revise the prompt based on error analysis. Re-run. Document improvement per iteration.

Task C: Semantic Scholar Wrapper Upgrade (Caching, Dedup, Pagination)

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: upgraded API module + integration tests

The existing Semantic Scholar wrapper (app/services/semantic_scholar.py, 31 lines) works but is minimal. Upgrade it to handle: local caching (don't re-fetch papers already in the DB), deduplication (same paper returned by different queries), pagination (Semantic Scholar returns max 100 per call), and multi-API fallback (if SS is rate-limited, try OpenAlex). The goal is a robust discovery engine that can run 20 queries overnight without hitting rate limits or storing duplicates.

1Panel: Simulate an API integration engineer, a database architect, and a research librarian. Debate: Cache by DOI or by corpus_id? How do you detect that two APIs returned the same paper with slightly different titles? What's the right rate-limit backoff?
2Spec: Input: query string + max_results. Output: list of article dicts, each with DOI, title, abstract, year, citation_count, source_api. Constraints: no duplicate DOIs in output; cache hit means zero API calls; pagination fetches all results up to max_results.
3Tests: (a) Same query twice — second call returns cached results with 0 API calls. (b) Two queries returning overlapping papers — no duplicates in combined results. (c) Query for 250 results — verify pagination fetches 3 pages. (d) SS rate-limited — verify OpenAlex fallback fires. (e) Malformed API response — verify graceful error handling.
4Build: Extend the existing module. Add SQLite cache table. Add dedup by DOI + fuzzy title match. Add pagination loop. Add OpenAlex fallback from scripts/sync/fetch_orphan_pdfs.py.
5Verify: Run 5 real queries from the taxonomy topic list. Verify cache, dedup, and pagination all work. Measure total API calls vs. naive approach.
6Diagnose: If dedup misses a duplicate, is it a matching threshold problem or a missing field? If cache grows too large, is it storing full text when it shouldn't?
7Iterate: Tune matching threshold on 10 known-duplicate pairs. Document API call savings.

Task D: VOI Scoring Engine

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: scoring module + ranked output + evaluation

Build the Value of Information scorer that ranks candidate articles by how much they would contribute to the Knowledge Atlas. The score combines: (a) gap fill — does this paper address a topic with few existing articles? (b) citation impact — how influential is this paper? (c) recency — more recent studies use better methods; (d) accessibility — is the PDF freely available? The scorer must produce a numeric VOI score plus a human-readable explanation of why it ranked each paper where it did.

1Panel: Simulate an information theorist, a systematic-review methodologist, and a research librarian. Debate: How do you quantify "gap fill" when the Atlas topic structure is hierarchical? Should citation count be log-scaled? Is a 2024 paper always better than a 2005 classic?
2Spec: Input: article metadata (title, abstract, DOI, citation_count, year, is_open_access) + current Atlas topic coverage (counts per topic). Output: {voi_score: float 0–1, components: {gap_fill, citation, recency, access}, explanation: string}. Success: top-10 ranked articles have ≥ 80% human agreement (via HITL page).
3Tests: (a) Paper in empty topic scores higher than paper in saturated topic. (b) 500-citation paper scores higher than 5-citation paper, all else equal. (c) Two identical papers, one open-access — OA scores higher. (d) Explanation string mentions the dominant factor. (e) Score is deterministic for same inputs.
4Build: Write a scoring function with configurable weights for each component. Gap-fill uses the Atlas topic counts (query the DB). Citation uses log-scaling. Recency uses a decay function. Explanation is template-generated.
5Verify: Run on 50 real candidates from Task C output. Send the top 20 to the HITL page (Task A). Measure human agreement rate.
6Diagnose: If humans disagree with ranking, which component is wrong? Is gap-fill miscounting? Is citation over-weighted? Analyze the disagreement cases.
7Iterate: Adjust component weights based on HITL feedback. Re-run. Document weight changes and their effect on agreement.

Task E: Metadata Validator & APA Citation Generator

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: validation pipeline + citation formatter

When an article is accepted, five things must be correct before it enters the database: (1) the DOI resolves to an actual page, (2) the abstract is present and non-empty, (3) the APA-7 citation is correctly formatted, (4) the PDF is machine-readable (not a scanned image), and (5) there is no duplicate in the DB. Build the validation pipeline that checks all five, auto-generates the APA citation from CrossRef metadata, and reports any failures clearly so a human can fix them.

1Panel: Simulate a research librarian, a database engineer, and an APA style expert. Debate: What does "machine-readable" mean exactly — OCR text present, or extractable sections? How do you handle APA edge cases (20+ authors, no DOI, preprint)?
2Spec: Input: article metadata dict + PDF file path. Output: {valid: bool, checks: {doi_resolves, abstract_present, apa_correct, pdf_readable, no_duplicate}, apa_citation: string, errors: [string]}. The APA citation must follow APA-7 format: Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title. Journal, Volume(Issue), pages. https://doi.org/...
3Tests: (a) Valid article passes all 5 checks. (b) Bad DOI (typo) → doi_resolves fails, clear error. (c) Scanned PDF with no extractable text → pdf_readable fails. (d) Article already in DB → no_duplicate fails. (e) 23-author paper → APA citation truncates correctly ("Author, A. A., Author, B. B., ... Author, T. T.").
4Build: DOI check via CrossRef API. Abstract check via string length. APA generation from CrossRef metadata (authors, journal, volume, pages, year). PDF readability via pdfminer text extraction (≥ 80 chars). Duplicate check via DOI index in SQLite.
5Verify: Run on 20 articles already in the DB (known good) and 5 deliberately broken entries. All should classify correctly.
6Diagnose: If APA citation is wrong, is it a CrossRef metadata problem (missing fields) or a formatting bug? If PDF readability fails on a good PDF, is the threshold too high?
7Iterate: Handle the edge cases found in diagnosis. Add fallback for missing CrossRef fields (query Semantic Scholar for metadata instead).

Task F: VOI Query Generator (Taxonomy → Targeted Searches)

Owner: 1 student · Weeks 3–5 · Deliverable: query generator + coverage report

The taxonomy (Track 1) defines hundreds of environmental factors and latent variables. The Atlas currently has uneven coverage — some topics have 40 articles, others have zero. Build a system that reads the taxonomy, checks the Atlas's current article counts per topic, identifies the gaps, and generates targeted search queries designed to fill them. The output is a ranked list of queries that Task C's search engine can execute.

1Panel: Simulate a systematic-review methodologist, a search strategist, and a cognitive scientist. Debate: How do you turn a taxonomy term like "L36 Awe/Grandeur" into a Semantic Scholar query? Should you use MeSH terms? How many synonyms per concept?
2Spec: Input: taxonomy JSON + Atlas article-count-per-topic table. Output: list of {query_string, target_topic, current_count, expected_yield, priority}. Success: the top-10 queries, when executed, return ≥ 5 relevant papers each (verified by Task B's filter).
3Tests: (a) Topic with 0 articles generates query ranked above topic with 20 articles. (b) Generated query includes at least 2 synonym variants. (c) Query for "ceiling height" differs from query for "room height" but both map to the same taxonomy node. (d) Coverage report accurately reflects DB counts. (e) No query is a duplicate of another.
4Build: Parse taxonomy into topic nodes. Query Atlas DB for article counts per topic. For each under-represented topic, use an LLM to generate 3 query variants with synonyms. Rank by gap size. Output as JSON.
5Verify: Execute the top 10 generated queries through Task C's search engine. Pass results through Task B's relevance filter. Report yield per query.
6Diagnose: If a query returns many results but few pass the relevance filter, is the query too broad? If a query returns zero results, is the topic too narrow or are the synonyms wrong?
7Iterate: Refine queries for the bottom-performing topics. Generate a final coverage report showing before/after article counts.

Human-in-the-Loop Testing Strategy

Every automated step in the pipeline can go wrong. The relevance filter might reject good papers or accept irrelevant ones. The VOI scorer might over-weight citation count. The APA generator might mangle author names. You cannot trust any of these systems without measuring their performance against human judgment — and that means building HITL infrastructure from the start.

Week 3 Priority: Ship the HITL Page First

Task A (the HITL Review Page) should be the first thing built, ideally by the end of Week 3. Every other task depends on it for validation. The student assigned to Task A should treat it as a week-one sprint — get a minimal version working in 5 days so that Tasks B, D, E, and F have somewhere to send their outputs for human checking. The minimal version needs only: display item, accept/reject buttons, store decision. Statistics and multi-reviewer features can come in Weeks 4–5.

The HITL page is designed to be general-purpose. Each "review item" has a type field (e.g., voi_candidate, relevance_check, apa_validation, extraction_qa) and a payload field (JSON blob with whatever the source system provides). The frontend renders different layouts based on type. This means every component of the pipeline that needs human validation can use the same infrastructure — you build it once and reuse it for everything.

What to HITL-Test and When

Component What the Human Checks When Acceptance Threshold
Relevance Filter (Task B) Is the system's accept/reject decision correct for this abstract? After each filter run on new queries F1 ≥ 0.85 on 50-article test set
VOI Ranking (Task D) Do you agree this paper is in the right position in the ranked list? After each batch ranking ≥ 80% agreement on top-20
APA Citation (Task E) Is the generated citation correct? Check author names, year, journal, DOI. Spot-check 10% of generated citations ≥ 95% accuracy
Query Quality (Task F) Does this generated query look like it would find relevant papers? Before executing queries at scale ≥ 5 relevant results per top-10 query
