Week 5

Question for the week: How do you support material, lighting, and prospect/refuge changes without losing control of the scene?

Use AI well

Before you ask AI for help, make sure you can state what you are trying to learn this week and what evidence would count as a useful answer.

Class meetings

Meeting A — Add perceptually important but technically manageable factors
Start here: Ask what can be changed in light, materials, or refuge without turning the project into open-ended design styling.

In class

  • Focus on light properties, surface materials and texture, and prospect/refuge.
  • Break the work into three questions: what parameters are controllable, what side effects occur, and how will you show what changed to a non-expert user?
  • Use AI to gather examples of warm versus cool lighting, material substitutions, or refuge cues, but tie every suggestion back to an actual scene edit.

Home

  • Implement or prototype at least two of these factor families.
  • Collect before/after evidence that makes the intended perceptual difference visible.
  • Write notes on where the factor remains subjective and where the implementation is cleanly parameterized.
Meeting B — Package change logs and evidence
Start here: Assume a researcher will never read your code. What do they need to know from the package itself?

In class

  • Review before and after comparisons and ask whether the room edits are legible and traceable.
  • Discuss what metadata a researcher would need to understand the transformation without reading your code.
  • Create a standard transformation log format for the class.

Home

  • Apply the log format to your current transformations.
  • Prepare one mini package containing the room, the variant, and the log that explains what was changed and why.

Week deliverables

D8 Transformation log and mini package

Rationale
A researcher needs to know what changed, not just see that something changed. The log turns scene edits into an experimental artifact.

What to submit
One base room, one or two variants, and a concise log listing the edited factors, parameter values, intent, validation status, and outstanding issues.

What good work looks like
The package can be read by someone outside your team. The change log is precise rather than impressionistic. The evidence makes it possible to compare variants.

Example
Variant B raises ceiling from 2.7 m to 3.8 m, widens window opening by 20 percent, changes correlated color temperature from 3000 K to 4200 K, preserves door placement, and passes navigation check.
