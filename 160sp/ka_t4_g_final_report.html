◎Goalwhat this deliverable teaches

The final report is a five-section reflective document (≈ 1,500 words) that knits T4.a through T4.f into a single narrative. It is a short deliverable because the hard evaluative work has already happened; what remains is to write it up at a level an external reader could follow, with explicit claims about what the evidence shows, what the evidence does not show, and which redesigns you would defend if an engineering team asked you to justify spending three sprints on them.

This is the only deliverable in Track 4 where writing quality is graded explicitly. The department standard is Williams' clarity principles (character-action sentences, old-new flow, stress position at sentence end). We are not asking for polished prose; we are asking for prose an engineer would trust enough to act on.

›What you'll dothe steps

  1. Open your T4.a, T4.b, T4.b.2, T4.c, T4.d, T4.e, and T4.f output files side-by-side. Re-read each with a pen. Mark every claim that you can now defend with quantified evidence, and every claim that rests on one observation.
  2. Write Section 1 (≈ 200 words) — What we asked. Two scenarios, three archetypes, six variants. What the research question was at the start of the track.
  3. Write Section 2 (≈ 350 words) — What the evidence shows. Quote two pilot findings (T4.c) verbatim; report the recurrence distribution; report which backlog rows survived replication.
  4. Write Section 3 (≈ 350 words) — What we would change. The three redesigns from T4.f, summarised in one paragraph each. For each, state the pre-registered success metric you pre-registered.
  5. Write Section 4 (≈ 300 words) — What we cannot yet claim. Every user-research report has findings that are under-evidenced; name yours and state what would be needed to raise their confidence.
  6. Write Section 5 (≈ 300 words) — One methodological lesson. Pick one thing about the method (heuristic audit coverage, pilot moderator interventions, replication failure-mode distribution, pre-registration discipline) that the track taught you something non-obvious about. This is the section that distinguishes band 2 from band 3.
  7. Submit final_report.md under 160sp/tracks/t4/{your_id}/. Reference every prior deliverable by filename.

✗Example of a naive approachso you can see what scores low

The naive final report is a thank-you letter to the instructor plus an uncritical summary of one's own work. The excerpt below is a composite of three real band-0/1 submissions.

band 0–1 example — do NOT write a final report like this

Track 4 Final Report

This quarter I did Track 4. I learned a lot. First I did T4.a where I found some usability issues on the Knowledge Atlas website. Then I did some scenarios and then I ran a usability pilot with my friends who were really nice about it. Most of my findings reproduced in T4.e so the method works. Then I did three redesigns which I think would improve the site a lot. Overall the site has some problems but my redesigns would fix them. I really enjoyed the class and learned a lot about UX. The thing I learned most is that UX is really important for user experience. Thank you.

Why this scores band 0 or 1. The report is self-celebratory rather than evaluative: every sentence reports what the student did without what the student found. No finding is quoted verbatim; no severity distribution is reported; no replication outcome is tied to a specific finding. The claim "most of my findings reproduced" is the kind of summary T4.e is designed to prevent — the grader needs the distribution across FULL / PARTIAL / NONE / UNDERSPECIFIED, and needs to know which were which. The redesigns are asserted to "fix the problems" but no pre-registered metric is cited and no trade-off is acknowledged. The methodological lesson — "UX is really important for user experience" — is a tautology that contains no information; it could be written before running any study at all. A band-3 methodological lesson is a specific, risky claim about the method: for example, "pair-replication failed for three of my five highest-severity findings, which suggests my heuristic audit was over-confident when my own screenshots were present to anchor my memory."

The report skeleton. Copy this into final_report.md:

## 3. What we would change  (≈ 350 words)

- Redesign F-{id} — {one paragraph}

## References
{APA-style; include Nielsen, Buxton, Card/Moran/Newell, and any empirical paper you cited}

✓Verification

how your work is graded
  • Automated: the AI grader checks all five section headings are present and that Sections 2 and 3 contain numeric evidence (not only prose).
  • Automated: presence of ≥ 2 verbatim pilot quotes in Section 2 with session + timestamp citation is required for Quality band ≥ 2.
  • Automated: Section 5 is scanned for concrete claims; a report whose methodological-lesson section is ≤ 40 words of generalities caps Quality at band 1.
  • TA audit: one report per student is spot-read for Williams-style clarity; egregious zombie-noun density flags the report for a writing-quality deduction.

Scoring bands (0–3 on each criterion, rescaled to the 5-point value):
