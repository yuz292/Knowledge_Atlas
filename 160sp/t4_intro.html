TRACK 4 · OVERVIEW

GUI & Interactivity: Designing Evidential Journeys

The previous tracks build the Atlas's content. Track 4 asks how that content reaches a reader. You will design and prototype an evidential journey: a sequence of pages that takes someone from a question they typed in to an answer that is properly warranted, qualified, and rebuttal-aware in the Toulmin sense — not a finished fact handed down from on high, but a defeasible conclusion the reader could in principle defeat by adducing further evidence.

What this track is and is not

This is not a usability-testing track. The Atlas is not yet deployed and there are no real users to recruit; the conventional HCI playbook does not apply. What we have instead is a richer, less common problem: design the journeys for personas whose questions, methods, and adequacy conditions vary widely, before any of those personas have actually used the system. The students in this track will spend three weeks (and possibly half of a fourth) producing the artefacts that make journey design possible: a tagged corpus of the questions a chosen persona would actually ask, a working catalog that pairs each question with a canonical answer shape and a defeater economy, and one journey prototype taken end-to-end as a demonstration of what the previous artefacts buy you.

The track is heavy on conceptual scaffolding because the alternative — design without the scaffolding — produces journeys that read smoothly but that, on inspection, do not commit to anything specific. A journey that has not chosen its answer shape wanders; a journey that has not named its defeater surprises its reader; a journey that has not articulated the asker's adequacy condition cannot be assessed at all. The scaffolds are the corrective.

Personas: each student owns one

The Atlas serves at least eight personas — Maya the 160 student, Dr Okafor the researcher, Jordan the contributor, Prof. Bergström the theorist, Sam the practitioner, the TA, the newcomer, the senior PI. (See the persona panel for the canonical descriptions you each chose from at the start of the term.) Each student in a Track 4 team works on the persona they themselves chose; each team is therefore a constellation of four parallel pipelines, not a single shared pipeline with role specialisation.

The team's collective work is peer review and critique of the individual pipelines. Three milestones make this concrete. At the end of week five, each student presents their persona profile and three example questions for team scrutiny: are the questions persona-specific enough that they could only have come from that persona? At the end of week six, each student presents their chosen answer shapes and named defeaters for one corpus item, and the team interrogates the choice and the defeater catalog. During week seven, before any submission, the team walks through every prototype; each student runs the LLM-panel reader test on a teammate's prototype rather than their own. Reviewing someone else's journey is more pedagogically valuable than reviewing your own, and it is where the cross-persona perspective enters the design.

The carried exemplar: the researcher persona, all the way through

Throughout the three tasks, the researcher persona (P2, Dr Fatima Okafor) is carried as the canonical worked example. You will study what a finished Task 1 looks like by reading the researcher question corpus; you will see what a finished Task 2 looks like in the researcher fitting catalog; you will see what a finished Task 3 looks like in the working Q-29 journey prototype. The researcher exemplar shows the standard you are aiming for; you apply the same protocol to your own persona's pipeline.

Studying the exemplar is not optional. The single largest failure mode in past iterations of this kind of work is students inventing their own private interpretations of what "build a corpus" or "fit a shape" means rather than calibrating against a worked instance. The exemplar is the calibration, and Task 1 begins with you reading it.

The methodological backbone

Three frameworks recur across the three tasks and are worth naming explicitly at the start. The first is Toulmin's argument structure (Toulmin, 1958/2003): every defensible answer to a researcher's question can be diagrammed as data flowing to a qualified claim through a warrant licensed by some backing, with a rebuttal stating the conditions under which the claim would fail. Toulmin is one of the five canonical answer shapes in the catalog (see below); it is also the conceptual model behind the others. The second is Chinn & Brewer's (1993) seven-response menu for how readers actually respond when presented with evidence that conflicts with a theory they hold: ignore, reject, exclude, hold in abeyance, reinterpret, peripheral change, theory change. Designing a journey that surfaces a defeater without anticipating which of the seven responses the reader will take is designing in the dark. The third is the five-axis question taxonomy (cognitive purpose / answer shape / evidential demand / persona fit / theoretical commitment) we use to tag every question in the corpus, drawn from Walton's dialogue typology, the Hill-Cartwright tradition on causal inference, and the shape catalog that Track 4 itself produces.

The five canonical answer shapes

Every question in the corpus must be fitted to one of five canonical answer shapes before a journey can be designed for it. The shapes are visualised in detail in the answer- shape catalogue; in brief:

  1. Toulmin diagram — claim, data, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal in the canonical left-to-right layout. The default for theory-prober and inquiry-with-mechanism questions.
  2. Field map — a directed concept-map (after Novak & Gowin, 1984) of named positions in a literature, with labelled directional edges. The default for discovery questions and high/high cross-product questions.
  3. Procedure — a numbered sequence of steps with a provenance column. The default for sensors-seeker and tests-seeker questions whose adequacy depends on regulatory or ethical gates.
  4. Contrast pair — two to four options laid out side by side with rows of differentia. The default for measures-shopping and instrument-choice questions.
  5. Ranked brief — a five-to-seven row ranked list with reason, item count, and an honest evidence-quality column. The default for deliberation under brevity constraint.

Shapes can be composed (a field map may have a Toulmin nested inside one of its branches; a contrast pair may resolve into a ranked brief). What the journey designer may not do is leave a question unfitted: a journey without a chosen shape produces a wandering page sequence.

The three tasks, in brief

Each task is roughly a week's work, with the team collective milestone at the end of each week.

Task 1 (week 5) — Build a Persona Question Corpus. Generate forty to sixty real, specific, adequacy-conditioned questions for your persona, each tagged on the five axes. Methodology: LLM panels + literature mining of the PNU templates + cross-product synthesis walking the IV × DV hierarchies. Worked exemplar: the researcher corpus (forty questions across five sub-flavours).

Task 2 (week 6) — Winnow and Fit Answer Shapes. Run the four-stage winnowing protocol to cut the corpus to a working set; fit each surviving question to one of the five canonical answer shapes; name at least one citable defeater per question that the journey must surface. Worked exemplar: the researcher fitting catalog (ten questions covering all five shapes, with winnowing log).

Task 3 (week 7, optionally bleeding into half of week 8) — Prototype an Evidential Journey. Build one working HTML prototype that takes a reader from the typed-in question to a populated answer shape with a Chinn-Brewer rebuttal panel exposed by default and a reader-response stage at the end. Test it on three to five LLM-panel agents playing the role of readers from your persona. Worked exemplar: the Q-29 journey on Attention Restoration Theory and the affective confound.

How you will be graded

Each task is worth 75 points (225 total for the track). The grading is primarily individual — your corpus, your fitting catalog, your prototype — with a smaller team-grade component that rewards the collective-review rituals (did the team actually push back; did the persona-presentation round produce visible improvements; was the prototype walkthrough substantive). Detailed rubrics are on each task page.

One commitment to surface explicitly. The grading rewards honest defeater catalogs over polished prototypes. A beautiful prototype that hides its defeaters has missed the central point of the track; a plainer prototype that surfaces a real, citable threat to its own claim and lets the reader choose a Chinn-Brewer response is doing what the Atlas is for.

Where to start

Read this overview, then the operational brief (the directives extracted from the methodology documents). Then look at the answer-shape catalogue to develop a feel for the five shapes you will be fitting to. Then begin Task 1 by reading the researcher corpus as your worked exemplar.

The deeper background reading — the corpus document, the task-design document — is linked from the side nav. You do not need to read it before starting Task 1; you will reach for it during the work.
