TRACK 4 · TASK 1 · WEEK 5

Build a Persona Question Corpus

75 points. Due Friday 1 May 2026 (end of week 5). The first move in designing for an audience is to know what the audience is asking. The corpus you build this week is what makes every later design decision in Track 4 testable.

Why this task exists

A great deal of design work goes wrong because the designer imagines a generic user with a generic question and engineers a generic answer; the generic answer satisfies no one. The corpus you build this week is the corrective. It forces you to commit, on paper, to specific questions phrased in the way your specific persona would phrase them, with the adequacy condition — what would make the answer feel insufficient — written out explicitly. Without an adequacy condition you cannot judge whether a journey is succeeding; you can only judge whether it is finishing.

A second reason to begin here. Researchers, students, contributors, practitioners, theorists, and PIs do not ask the same questions. They do not even ask the same kinds of questions. Treating the Atlas as if it served a single user produces a system that is shaped to no actual user. Your corpus is the artefact that lets your team reason about persona variability concretely rather than as a slogan, because four students working on four different personas will produce four very different corpora and the contrast itself is instructive.

Read the researcher corpus first

Before you generate a single question for your own persona, read the researcher question corpus from start to finish. It is the carried exemplar for Track 4 — the standard of "done" that you are calibrating against. Pay particular attention to the five sub-flavours of the researcher persona (general, measures-seeker, sensors-seeker, tests-seeker, theory-prober) and to the way each question carries its adequacy condition explicitly.

You will produce something analogous for your persona — not necessarily five sub-flavours of exactly the same kind, but a similar structural commitment that captures the variation within your persona's question space. (Maya the 160 student has different sub-flavours than the researcher; Sam the practitioner has different ones again.)

What you produce

A spreadsheet (or equivalent JSON) with one row per question. Forty to sixty questions, covering the variation within your persona. Each row carries the following fields.

How to generate questions: three sources

You will use three sources in combination. Each source produces a different kind of question; only the combination produces a corpus that covers your persona's actual question space.

Source 1 — LLM panels

Configure three panels of eight to twelve agent variants each. Vary the agents along career stage, disciplinary home, methodological orientation, and theoretical commitment. Give each agent a scenario card — a one- paragraph situation in which your persona finds themselves and turns to the Atlas — and ask them to produce: the question they would type, the answer that would satisfy them, what would make the answer feel insufficient, and one further question they would want to ask once they got an initial answer.

The system prompt template, sample scenario cards, and a worked panel-agent run are in the task-design document §1.2 through §1.4. The Panel Architect on your team is responsible for standing up the panel infrastructure your whole team will use.

Source 2 — Literature mining

The Atlas's PNU template library (Article_Eater_PostQuinean_v1_recovery/data/templates/) is a record of questions the field already thought worth asking — roughly 166 templates as of this term, each one encoding a structured claim about a mechanism in the built environment. For each template you sample, write down the question the template answers, then write down three to five follow-up questions that a person who had just received the template's answer would next ask. The mining-prompt template is in the task- design document §1.5. The Mining Lead on your team coordinates which templates the team is sampling.

Source 3 — Cross-product synthesis

The Atlas joins two large hierarchical vocabularies — the Tagging_Contractor (~440 active tags across roughly twenty domains: affect, affordance, biophilia, cnfa, cognitive, color, complexity, fluency, isovist, material, provenance, science, smell, social, sound, spatial, style, touch) and the Outcome_Contractor (seven top-level domains across affect, behav, cog, health, neural, physio, social with roughly eighty level-2 outcome terms beneath them). A cross-product question is one of the form "how does X (an IV at level k) affect Y (a DV at level m), moderated by Z?". The four most informative corners are low/low, low/high, high/low, and high/high; generate at least three to five questions per corner. The Cross-Product Lead on your team coordinates.

How to tag questions

Every question carries five tags. The tagging codebook is maintained by the Codebook Custodian on your team so the tags mean the same thing across the four parallel corpora. The five axes:

  1. Cognitive purpose. What is the asker doing — filling a known gap (information-seeking), settling an open question (inquiry), choosing among options (deliberation), loading a position into a debate (persuasion), looking for the question they ought to be asking (discovery)?
  2. Answer shape. Which of the five canonical shapes will the answer take? You commit to a tentative shape in Task 1; you finalise it in Task 2.
  3. Evidential demand. What threshold of evidence would satisfy the asker — a single suggestive study, multiple converging lines, a credible mechanism, causal-with-mechanism, or measurement-grade (psychometric properties of an instrument)?
  4. Persona fit. Which sub-flavour of your persona would naturally ask this? If you find yourself unable to answer, the question is probably mistagged.
  5. Theoretical commitment. What ontological or methodological assumption does the question presuppose? A Kaplan-loyalist asking about restoration is not asking the same question as a Kaplan-sceptic; the journey will fail differently for each.

A worked tagging example for Q-12 from the researcher corpus is in the task-design document §1.7.

Team milestone — end of week 5

Before submitting your corpus, the team meets for a persona-presentation round. Each student presents their persona profile and three example questions. The team's job is to push back on whether the questions are persona-specific enough — would your persona, and only your persona, plausibly ask this? A question that any researcher could ask is not yet a researcher question; a question Maya could plausibly ask but so could Jordan is undercommitted on persona fit. The presentation round is where the cross-persona intelligence of the team enters the work.

Pitfalls

The single largest failure mode is questions that read smoothly but cannot be journey-designed because their adequacy condition is "I will know it when I see it". The Codebook Custodian on your team should gate hard on adequacy conditions during the week. A question without a one-sentence adequacy condition does not enter the corpus; it goes back for revision.

A second failure mode is over-mining: producing a hundred questions that all follow the same pattern from one source. Coverage of all sub-flavours and the four cross-product corners is the structural antidote.

What you submit at end of week 5. Your tagged corpus (40–60 questions, all five fields per row); a short methods file documenting your panel configurations and mining samples; the team's persona-presentation-round notes (for any one corpus from the team — not all four). Submit by opening a Pull Request on your track4/<your-name> branch.
