Overview — What You Are Doing and Why It Matters

K-ATLAS — the Knowledge Architecture for Translating Life-science and Affective-environment Science — is a living knowledge base for COGS 160. It is not a pre-built database. It is built by you, this quarter, from primary literature. Every paper you collect becomes part of the corpus that future students, TAs, and the ATLAS reasoning engine draw on when answering questions about how built environments affect human cognition, affect, and wellbeing.

This assignment is universal: every student in the class completes it in Week 1. There is no long research phase, no elaborate method selection, no coding. The task is straightforward: find good empirical papers, upload them with proper citations, and let the system classify and store them. The design principle is no makework — the system handles abstract retrieval, duplicate checking, article-type classification, and corpus logging automatically.

Your contribution
If 35 students each submit 20 papers across 2 questions, K-ATLAS gains roughly 700 new articles in a single week — with DOIs, abstracts, and article-type classifications already attached. This is the foundation everything else builds on.

You are assigned two questions drawn from the K-ATLAS question bank (130 questions across 34 topics). The questions are written in a format specifically designed for Google AI Scholar — you will learn exactly why below. For each question, you find and upload ten qualifying papers. The definition of "qualifying" differs between your two questions.

How to Search for Papers

The fastest and highest-quality approach for this assignment is Google AI Scholar. Your K-ATLAS questions are written specifically to take advantage of Google's AI Overview feature, which synthesises results from multiple sources and surfaces the most relevant papers for natural-language academic queries. Section 4 below teaches you exactly how to use this feature.

  1. Copy your assigned question from your upload page
    The question is pre-formatted as a Google AI Citation query — a complete sentence describing evidence type, mechanism, environment, and population. No Boolean operators needed.
  2. Paste into Google Scholar (scholar.google.com)
    Make sure you are in Scholar (not regular Google). If Google's AI Overview appears above the results, read it — it often identifies the most-cited papers directly. Click "All X results" to see the full list.
  3. Download PDFs for papers you want to include
    Many papers are available open-access via DOI links. For gated papers, use UCSD Library (VPN + library proxy) or Unpaywall (browser extension). You do not need to read the papers in full — scan the abstract and methods section to judge relevance.
  4. Note the APA citation and DOI for each paper
    Google Scholar provides a citation export button (⋮ → Cite → APA). The DOI appears in the paper header or at doi.org. If you cannot find the DOI, leave the field blank — the system will attempt to extract it from the PDF.
  5. Upload to your personal page and click "Fetch Abstracts & Classify"
    The system retrieves abstracts via DOI API (CrossRef), classifies article type, checks whether each paper is already in the K-ATLAS corpus, and shows you your progress toward ten qualifying papers.
No duplicate work
If a paper is already in the K-ATLAS corpus, the system flags it as "In Corpus" and still counts it toward your ten — you found it independently, which is evidence it is relevant. The corpus does not grow from duplicates, but your progress does.

Google AI Scholar — Why K-ATLAS Questions Are Already Optimised for It

Full Guide Available

For a deep-dive on query anatomy, worked examples, and common mistakes, see the complete Google AI Citation Guide.

Standard academic search uses Boolean operators: AND, OR, NOT, proximity operators, field tags. This approach treats search as a keyword-matching problem. Google's AI Overview treats search as a question-answering problem. It uses large language models to interpret your query semantically, cross-reference multiple indexed sources, and surface papers that address the specific intersection of concepts you describe — even if those papers do not contain your exact keywords.

K-ATLAS questions are written in Google AI Citation format: complete natural-language sentences that encode five components simultaneously. This is the optimal input format for Google AI Overview. You do not need to reformulate the question — paste it as-is.

The Five-Component Framework

Every K-ATLAS question contains these five components. Google AI Scholar's language model recognises each component and retrieves papers that address the specified intersection.

# Component What it specifies Example phrase
1 Evidence type Study design or methodology experimental evidence, neuroimaging studies
2 Mechanism / measure The cognitive or physiological process being studied attention restoration, cortisol reduction
3 Environmental condition The built or natural environment feature natural light exposure, open-plan offices
4 Population Participants or affected group office workers, hospital patients, children
5 Theoretical anchor Framework or theory linking environment to outcome Attention Restoration Theory, stress-recovery hypothesis

Anatomy of a K-ATLAS Query

Below is a sample K-ATLAS question with each component color-coded. Your assigned question follows this same structure.

What experimental evidence shows that directed attention fatigue is restored by exposure to natural or fractal-complexity environments in knowledge workers or students, as predicted by Attention Restoration Theory?
■ Evidence type ■ Mechanism ■ Environment ■ Population ■ Theory

Why This Format Outperforms Boolean Search

Boolean approach (traditional)
"attention restoration" AND "built environment" AND (workers OR employees) AND experiment*
Retrieves papers containing these exact tokens. Misses papers that use "directed attention fatigue," "restorative environments," "occupational stress," or related terminology. Precision depends entirely on term choice. Requires multiple query variants to achieve coverage.
Google AI Citation format (K-ATLAS)
What experimental evidence shows that directed attention fatigue is restored by natural or fractal-complexity environments in knowledge workers, as predicted by Attention Restoration Theory?
Google's language model recognises the conceptual relationships — restoration, attention, environment, workplace setting, ART — and retrieves papers that address this intersection regardless of specific terminology. A single query achieves broader semantic coverage than multiple Boolean variants. The AI Overview often identifies the three to five most-cited papers directly in the first paragraph of its summary.

Three Worked Examples from the K-ATLAS Question Bank

These show the range of topics and how the five-component structure varies across question types.

Neural mechanisms / IC→DC (SQ-037)
What neuroimaging evidence demonstrates that default mode network deactivation during spatial navigation tasks is modulated by the visual complexity and wayfinding legibility of indoor architectural environments?
Components: neuroimaging (evidence) · DMN deactivation (mechanism) · indoor architectural complexity (environment) · navigation tasks (implicitly all populations) · no single theory anchored but cognitively grounded.
Social interaction / SQ-032
What experimental or observational evidence shows that informal social interaction frequency among office workers is modulated by proximity and visibility afforded by open-plan versus cellular office layouts?
Components: experimental or observational (evidence) · social interaction frequency (mechanism) · open-plan vs. cellular office (environment) · office workers (population) · proxemics / affordance theory (theory).
Restorative environments / SQ-043
What EEG or physiological evidence shows that exposure to biophilic design features — living walls, water features, natural materials — reduces sympathetic nervous system arousal in hospital patients or medical staff?
Components: EEG/physiological (evidence) · SNS arousal reduction (mechanism) · biophilic design features (environment) · hospital patients or medical staff (population) · biophilic design theory / stress recovery (theory).
Practical tip — reading the AI Overview
When Google shows an AI Overview paragraph above your results, read it carefully. It typically names the two or three most-cited papers on the topic, often with author names. These are your highest-priority targets. Use them as seeds for forward/backward citation chaining to find additional papers.

Uploading Papers — Step by Step

Your personal upload page (linked in the sidebar) shows your name, your assigned questions, and your progress toward each requirement. The interface has two tabs — one per question — and a third brownie-point tab that unlocks after you complete both.

  1. Select your question tab (Q1 or Q2)
    Each tab shows the question text, the requirement (10-exp or mixed), and your current progress bar.
  2. Upload PDFs
    Drag and drop one or more PDFs onto the upload zone, or click to browse. You can upload multiple files at once. PDFs are stored immediately; classification happens when you click the Fetch button.
  3. Enter APA citation and DOI for each paper
    For each uploaded PDF, a row appears in the form below the upload zone. Paste the APA citation (from Google Scholar's cite button) and the DOI if you have it. The DOI field accepts both raw DOIs (10.1016/...) and full URLs (https://doi.org/...).
  4. Click "Fetch Abstracts & Classify"
    The system calls the CrossRef API for each DOI, retrieves the abstract, classifies the article type, and checks whether the paper is already in the K-ATLAS corpus. This takes 10–30 seconds depending on batch size. A spinner shows progress.
  5. Review the results table
    Each row shows: Title · Article Type · Abstract · PDF Present · In Corpus · Qualifies. Papers that qualify increment your progress bar automatically. Papers that do not qualify (for Q1: non-experimental) are stored but shown with a grey "Does not count" note.
  6. Repeat until your progress bar reaches 10
    Search again, download more papers, upload, fetch, review. Repeat as needed. The system remembers all previous submissions — you never re-enter papers you have already uploaded.
If the abstract is unavailable
CrossRef does not have abstracts for all papers. If a paper returns "Abstract not available," a text field appears. You may paste the abstract manually (copy from the publisher's page or from the PDF). This is optional — the paper is stored and classified regardless.

Brownie Points — Extra Credit

⭐

After completing both required questions, you can earn brownie points

Once your Q2 submission is complete, the system offers you an additional question from the K-ATLAS bank. Each additional question you complete earns one brownie point. Brownie points are taken into account when the final grade is assigned — they can tip a grade up, but they are not required and cannot tip a grade down.

Brownie-point questions follow the mixed format (all article types count) to keep the extra work manageable. You can accept as many brownie questions as you like. The system assigns them in the same priority order as required questions, ensuring that under-represented topics continue to receive coverage.

How brownie points appear in your record
Each completed brownie question is logged with a timestamp and question ID. The instructor's admin dashboard shows each student's brownie count alongside their required completion status. There is no cap on brownie points, but each one requires the same ten-paper standard.
