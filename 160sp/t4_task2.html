TRACK 4 · TASK 2 · WEEK 6

Winnow and Fit Answer Shapes

75 points. Due Friday 8 May 2026 (end of week 6). A corpus is necessary but not sufficient. The fitting move pairs every surviving question with a canonical answer shape and a defeater economy, and it is the move that makes a journey designable.

Why this task exists

Without commitment to what the answer to each question would look like, your corpus is a list. The fitting move — pairing a question with one of the five canonical answer shapes and naming at least one defeater the answer must surface — forces you to anticipate the journey before the journey is built. A page sequence that has not committed to an answer shape will wander; a journey that has not named a defeater will surprise its reader, and not in the helpful sense.

The pedagogical goal of Task 2 is to move you from "questions exist in five flavours" to "answers come in five shapes, and pairing question to shape is itself a design move that can go right or wrong." It is intellectually the most demanding task of the three, because it is where you commit to claims about your persona's literature and where you have to find real defeaters in that literature rather than inventing them.

Read the researcher fitting catalog first

The carried exemplar for Task 2 is the researcher fitting catalog. Read it before you begin winnowing your own corpus. It shows: the four-stage winnowing log with counts and gap acknowledgements; ten worked fittings covering all five answer shapes; the move from "question with a tag" to "question with a chosen shape, a shape sketch, and named defeaters"; and §3, which articulates what the catalog is meant to teach about fitting under genuine ambiguity.

As with Task 1, the exemplar is the calibration. You will produce something analogous for your persona — same template, same protocol, your persona's questions and your persona's literature.

Stage A — Winnow the corpus

Run the four-stage winnowing protocol in order, tracking counts at each stage. The numbers below are typical attrition rates from the researcher exemplar; expect similar magnitudes for your own persona.

  1. Deduplicate. Two questions are duplicates if they share all five tags and would be answered by the same journey. Merge and credit both sources. Typical attrition: ~30%.
  2. Adequacy gate. Drop any row whose adequacy condition the team cannot articulate in one sentence after a five-minute discussion. Expect 15–20%.
  3. Persona-fit gate. Move any item the team would re-classify to a different persona to the next-pass backlog. Do not discard. Expect 10%.
  4. Coverage balance. Inspect surviving items against the five axes and the four cross-product corners. Empty cells get targeted generation passes or written notes acknowledging the gap. Expect small additions, not subtractions.

The winnowing log itself is a deliverable. A Codebook Custodian who hides the dropped items hides the team's quality control. See the researcher catalog §1 for the format the log should take.

Stage B — Fit answer shapes

For every surviving question, choose one of the five canonical answer shapes from the answer-shape catalogue. The decision tree below is a starting point; ambiguous cases get team discussion at the week-6 milestone.

Composite shapes are allowed: a field map may have a Toulmin nested inside one of its branches; a contrast pair may resolve into a ranked brief once the differentia have been weighted. What is not allowed is leaving a question without a chosen shape — an unfitted question becomes an unconstrained journey design and the journey's quality cannot then be assessed.

For each fitted question, write a one-paragraph shape sketch describing how the shape would be filled in for this question — what would go in each part of the diagram, drawn from real literature where possible. The researcher catalog has ten worked sketches across the five shapes; use them as your model.

Stage C — Name the defeaters

For each surviving question, name at least one defeater that the journey must surface. A defeater is a finding, argument, or scope condition that, if the reader took it seriously, would force them to revise the answer the journey is delivering. Defeaters are the place your intellectual honesty is most visible to the grader and to your team.

To find them, ask four sub-questions of every entry:

  1. What study or finding would, if true, falsify the answer the journey would deliver?
  2. What alternative explanation does the answer not yet rule out?
  3. What scope condition is the answer assuming — what populations, settings, or instruments would the answer fail to cover?
  4. What theoretical commitment does the question embed that a sceptic would reject from outside?

A defeater that survives all four sub-questions and is grounded in a citable source is a strong defeater. Every question in your working corpus must carry at least one strong defeater; if you cannot find one in the literature, the question is flagged as under-supported rather than dressed up with a hypothetical defeater. Defeater laundering — inventing a defeater rather than finding one — is the most common form of dishonesty in this kind of work and is graded down accordingly.

Team milestone — end of week 6

Before submitting your fitting catalog, the team meets for a fitting critique. Each student presents their chosen shape and named defeaters for one item from their own catalog. The team interrogates the choice. Three questions to press on:

  • Is the chosen shape the right one for the question's purpose and adequacy demand, or could a different shape do better?
  • Is the shape sketch specific enough to constrain a journey design, or is it generic enough that it could apply to any similar question?
  • Are the named defeaters real (citable in the literature) or invented?

The Codebook Custodian's role pivots in this task to fitting consistency: the same kind of question across two students' catalogs should have been fitted to the same shape, or the difference should be explained.

Pitfalls

The most common failure mode is choosing a shape too quickly and sketching it generically. A "Toulmin" sketch that does not actually name the warrant is not yet a sketch; it is a label. Every sketch must name every part of the chosen shape, with the data and warrant grounded in actual literature where possible.

The second most common failure mode is defeater laundering. Writing a defeater that you have invented rather than found is dishonest in a way that compounds: it tells the journey designer to surface a threat that does not actually exist in the literature, which trains the reader to treat all the journey's defeater claims as equally hypothetical.
