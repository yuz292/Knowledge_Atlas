TRACK 4 · TASK 3 · WEEKS 7–8

Prototype an Evidential Journey

75 points. Due Friday 15 May 2026 (end of week 7), with a midpoint-of-week-8 extension to Wednesday 20 May 2026 if your team needs it. The first two tasks produced paper artefacts; this one produces a working one. A journey is not designed when it is described in prose — it is designed when a reader can click through it.

Why this task exists

The move from spec to prototype reveals everything that the spec elided. The prototype forces you to make choices the previous tasks could defer: which page does the reader land on; what is on the page above the fold; how is the rebuttal exposed; what is the next click. These choices are where journey design lives, and they cannot be made well without the corpus and the fitting catalog behind them. They also cannot be made well without actually building the artefact: a journey that exists only in description is a journey that has not been tested against the readers it was built for.

The pedagogical goal is integrative. After three weeks of building intellectual scaffolding, you produce one working thing that demonstrates what the scaffolding buys.

Study the Q-29 journey first

The carried exemplar for Task 3 is the Q-29 working journey on Attention Restoration Theory and the affective confound. Open it. Walk through it. It is designed to model what a finished prototype looks like: a typed-in question on the landing page; a Toulmin sequence drawn out across data, warrant-and-backing, qualified claim, and rebuttal; the rebuttal panel given equal visual weight to the data; the Chinn-Brewer widget exposed inline with all seven responses populated for the journey's specific defeater; and a reader-response stage that asks which of the seven responses the reader would take and stores it for the session.

You will produce something analogous for one question from your working corpus. The shape may not be Toulmin — if your chosen question is a deliberation rather than an inquiry, your journey will be a contrast pair or ranked brief. The structural commitments transfer regardless: landing page, populated answer shape, rebuttal panel, reader-response stage.

What you produce

Three artefacts.

(a) One working journey prototype. A small set of HTML files (no back-end required; no framework required) for one question from your working corpus. The prototype must include all four of the following pieces — every one of them is a separate line on the rubric:

  • ☐ Landing page that accepts the typed-in question (or shows it pre-populated).
  • ☐ Populated answer shape — a sequence of pages or a single page with disclosure regions implementing your chosen shape (Toulmin / field map / procedure / contrast pair / ranked brief), with the diagram drawn inline.
  • ☐ Chinn-Brewer rebuttal panel exposed by default (not behind a toggle), populated with the named defeaters from your Task-2 catalog. Skipping this is the most common Track-4 failure mode; see Pitfalls below.
  • ☐ Reader-response stage at the end that asks which Chinn-Brewer response the reader would take and why, and stores their answer for the session.

(b) An LLM-panel reader test. Run three to five LLM agents through the prototype as readers from your persona. Record per agent: where the agent stopped; which Chinn-Brewer response the agent took; what the agent said would have made the journey more adequate. This is not a substitute for human user testing but it is a defensible first-pass check. The reader-test prompt template is in the task-design document §3.3.

(c) A short written reflection. Two pages, in academic prose. What you learned in moving from spec to prototype; what the prototype revealed that the corpus and fitting passes had hidden; what your LLM-panel reader test surfaced; what you would change if you had three more weeks. The reflection prompt is in the task-design document §3.4.

Use the skeleton kit

The answer- shape catalogue is your skeleton kit. Each shape on that page can be lifted out as a starting template (the SVG for Toulmin and field map, the HTML grid for contrast pair and ranked brief, the ordered-list pattern for procedure). The CSS variables match the rest of the Atlas so you do not need to invent a colour scheme. The Chinn- Brewer widget is also drop-in: you can include it as an iframe or inline its source. Replace the placeholder anomaly text with one of your defeaters from Task 2.

The Atlas's house style is plain HTML and minimal CSS, consistent with the existing track pages. A team should be spending its last week on content and on reader testing, not on JavaScript framework configuration.

Team milestone — week 7 walkthrough

Before any submission, the team walks through every prototype together. Each student runs the LLM-panel reader test on a teammate's prototype rather than on their own. Reviewing someone else's journey is more pedagogically useful than reviewing your own — the reviewer is forced to occupy a different persona's adequacy condition than the one they spent three weeks internalising — and it is where the cross-persona intelligence enters the prototype evaluation.

Bring three findings to the team meeting from your teammate's prototype: one place the journey worked; one place it confused you; one Chinn-Brewer response you took with the rebuttal and why.

Pitfalls

The single largest failure mode is treating the prototype as a front-end exercise and skipping the rebuttal panel. A journey without an exposed Chinn-Brewer panel is not yet an evidential journey; it is a tutorial. The grading rubric weights rebuttal economy at 15% of Task 3 specifically because a beautiful prototype that hides its defeaters has missed the central point of the track.

The second-largest failure mode is writing a reflection that is essentially an advertisement for the prototype. The reflection is graded on honesty about what you found ambiguous, what your reader test surfaced, and what you remain uncertain about. A reflection that admits no uncertainty is read as either dishonest or insufficiently reflective.

What you submit at end of week 7 (or midpoint of week 8). Your prototype HTML file(s); your LLM-panel reader-test transcripts and notes; your two-page reflection; the team-collective walkthrough notes. Submit by Pull Request on your branch.
