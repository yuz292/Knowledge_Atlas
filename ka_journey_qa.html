Question-answering layer

Roles served public_visitor, practitioner, researcher Current status prototype Complexity tier Medium-high — hallucination vs. grounding is the central risk

◧What this page displaysthe information on offer

The question-answering (QA) layer accepts a natural-language question ("does exposure to daylight improve task performance?") and returns an answer that is grounded in the corpus: a short prose answer, a confidence band, a list of the warrants cited, and a disclosure of what the system would not answer (because the corpus is silent or inconsistent).

The QA surface is the one most vulnerable to LLM hallucination. The design problem is therefore not just "return a helpful answer" but "refuse to answer when the corpus does not support one, and never cite a warrant the retrieval step did not actually return." Grounding is the load-bearing requirement.

The prototype supports single-turn questions and does not yet handle follow-ups or comparative questions; it also does not route a question to the right Atlas surface (interpretation, EN, BN) — every question currently hits the same retrieval-plus-generation pipeline.

△Why this page is hard to designthe challenges

  1. Refusing to answer. The most important design affordance is a clean "the corpus does not support an answer to this" path. Most QA interfaces have a weak version of this; the Atlas needs a strong one because the cost of a confident wrong answer is higher here than in general-purpose QA.
  2. Citation fidelity. Every sentence in the answer should be anchored to at least one retrieved warrant. The UI must surface the anchor, and the backend must enforce it.
  3. Question routing. Some questions are best answered from the interpretation layer, some from the BN, some from the topic page. A single QA endpoint that routes internally is appealing; a visible router is more honest.
  4. Follow-ups. Users ask follow-up questions ("okay, what about older adults specifically?"). The prototype does not carry context forward.
  5. Disagreement. Corpus disagreement must produce an answer like "there are two positions, here is the evidence for each" — not a centroid.

✗The simple naive solution in place nowwhat you'll actually see

naive — the current implementation

The prototype accepts a text box, POSTs to a retrieval endpoint that returns the top five warrants by embedding similarity, and feeds those plus the question into an LLM. It returns the LLM's output with a list of the five warrant titles below. Confidence is not reported; refusal is not attempted; follow-ups are not supported.

Why this is inadequate. The prototype has the right bones — retrieval-augmented generation with cited warrants — but the two most important affordances (refusal and confidence) are missing. A user who asks a question outside the corpus's coverage currently gets an answer anyway; a user who asks about a well-studied area gets the same confident tone as a user who asks about an under-studied one. The grounding contract is not enforced at the UI layer: there is nothing stopping the LLM from citing a warrant in the wrong spot, and nothing showing the user that the citation is real.
