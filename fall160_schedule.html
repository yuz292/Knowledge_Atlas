Experiment Making · Week by Week

Students begin with a research question from the Knowledge Atlas and end with a completed pilot experiment — including VR stimuli, an adaptive preference instrument, sensor data collection, and a 4-minute presentation. Everything runs in a browser. Every deliverable is due Monday 6 pm.

Week
2
Theory · Hypothesis · What are you actually testing?
Students ground their question in a theoretical framework and write a falsifiable hypothesis with a predicted direction and a named mechanism.
All teams Due Mon 6 pm: Hypothesis form Autogradeable
＋
What students do

The key distinction this week: an experiment without a theory is just a demonstration. A theory makes a prediction about direction, mechanism, and boundary conditions. Students use the KA Theory Layer to find which theories make predictions about their IV–DV pair, read the relevant section, and write a hypothesis that is derived from the theory — not just consistent with it.

The hypothesis must be falsifiable: it must state a predicted direction (higher / lower / faster / slower) and the condition under which it would be wrong. Students who cannot state what result would falsify their hypothesis do not have a hypothesis — they have a wish.

  • Identify which theory (ART, SRT, Arousal, Biophilia, Predictive Processing, etc.) predicts an effect for your IV–DV pair
  • Read the theory's mechanism: what does it say happens between IV and DV?
  • Write a hypothesis in the form "If [IV], then [DV] will [increase/decrease/not change], because [mechanism]"
  • State the falsifying condition: "This hypothesis would be wrong if [result]"
  • Name your two conditions (Condition A vs. Condition B)

Week
3
VR Stimulus specification · What will participants see?
Students specify their two experimental conditions as VR environments — which base scene, which parameters vary, which are held constant.
VR design Due Mon 6 pm: Stimulus spec form Autogradeable
＋
What students do

Most experiments in this course use VR because it allows the experimenter to control everything: lighting, spatial density, sound level, presence of nature elements, movement, and social presence. The stimulus library contains pre-built A-Frame base scenes — natural outdoor, urban outdoor, open office, abstract/geometric — that students choose from and configure rather than build from scratch.

The critical constraint is control: the two conditions must differ on exactly the IV and match on everything else. Students specify what changes between Condition A and Condition B and justify why everything else stays the same. This is the confound-control step.

  • Browse the VR Stimulus Library — preview base scenes in browser (no headset needed for specification)
  • Choose a base scene that fits your paradigm
  • Specify Condition A parameters: sky, ground cover, object density, lighting level, sound, navigation mode
  • Specify Condition B parameters — list which parameters change and which are held constant
  • Justify each controlled parameter: why does holding it constant matter for your hypothesis?
  • Note any 3D assets you need that are not in the default library
VR Stimulus Library — needed. This is the most important new tool to build for the Fall course. It needs: (1) a browser-browsable catalogue of 8–12 base scenes with preview images and parameter descriptions; (2) a parameter specification form that enforces Condition A / Condition B structure; (3) a link to the Week 4 scene adapter that generates the actual A-Frame file from the spec. The Spring Track 3 students may be best placed to build a first version.

VR build Due Mon 6 pm: Two working scene URLs Auto-check: renders without errors

What students do

The Scene Adapter takes the Week 3 stimulus spec and generates a starter A-Frame HTML file for each condition. Students open the file in their browser, confirm it renders, then adapt it — moving objects, adjusting parameters, adding or removing 3D assets from the library. The goal is not a polished scene; it is a controllable, working scene where the IV is cleanly manipulated.

Students who need a 3D asset not in the default library can import from Sketchfab (free, educational licence) or use A-Frame primitives. The Scene Adapter checks that the A-Frame script tag is present and the scene ID matches the experiment record — this is what enables the auto-logger to attach in Week 8.

  • Use the Scene Adapter to generate starter HTML for Condition A and Condition B from your spec
  • Open each file in Chrome — confirm no console errors and the environment renders
  • Walk through each scene using WASD + mouse-look and confirm the IV is visually apparent
  • Add any missing 3D assets (primitives or Sketchfab imports) — keep the asset list short
  • Confirm scene ID is set correctly in both files (the logger uses this to tag events)
  • Screenshot or screen-record 10 seconds of each condition as preview

Week
5
Measurement instrument · Configure the Adaptive Preference tool
Students configure the course's primary measurement instrument — the Adaptive Preference survey — and verify it collects and auto-analyzes a self-test run.
Measurement Due Mon 6 pm: Working instrument + self-test data Autogradeable
＋
What students do

The Adaptive Preference instrument is the primary DV measure for most experiments in this course. It presents stimuli — screenshots or short clips of Condition A and Condition B — in a pairwise or rating format, adapts the next comparison based on the current response pattern, and at the end produces an automatic preference score and a Bradley-Terry ranking. Students do not write the analysis; the tool does it.

Configuration means: upload the stimuli (screenshots from Week 4), label the conditions, set the number of trials (12–20 for a pilot), and choose the response format (pairwise forced choice or 5-point rating scale). Then each team member completes a self-test run to confirm the instrument works.

Teams whose hypothesis involves a physiological DV (stress, arousal) also note here that they will add EmotiBit to the session in Week 8. The adaptive preference tool is still required as a secondary measure.

  • Open the Adaptive Preference Configurator — connect it to your experiment ID (auto-links with your scene files)
  • Upload condition screenshots (at minimum 3 images per condition, from different viewpoints)
  • Set trial count (12 for a pilot), response format (pairwise recommended), and condition labels
  • Both team members complete a self-test run — you are your own first pilot participants
  • Confirm that the auto-analysis report generates: preference score, Bradley-Terry ranking, trial-by-trial log
  • If using EmotiBit: note this in the instrument config — it will be linked in Week 8
Adaptive Preference Configurator — needed. The core new tool for this course. Needs: (1) stimulus upload (images or short clips from the VR scenes); (2) pairwise or rating-scale trial generator; (3) adaptive trial selection (simple: show each pair twice then choose most informative remaining pairs); (4) auto-analysis: preference score per condition, Bradley-Terry ranking, confidence interval; (5) downloadable trial log in standard schema. This is the tool that makes the course autogradeable and self-contained — invest the most here.

Phase 2 — Pre-registration and approval gate
📋
Phase 2 — Weeks 6–7 · Pre-registration and instructor approval
Before running any participants, every team submits a pre-registration: a public commitment to their hypothesis, design, and analysis plan. The instructor reviews and either approves or sends back with comments. No team runs participants until approved. This is not bureaucracy — it is the discipline that separates science from storytelling.
Week
6
Procedure write-up · Consent form · Pre-registration draft
Students write the full procedure from the participant's point of view and assemble the pre-registration document. Instructor gives first-pass feedback by Friday.
Pre-reg Due Mon 6 pm: Pre-registration draft Instructor review by Fri
＋
What students do

The procedure is written from the experimenter's script perspective — what you say, what you do, in what order. It must be detailed enough that a classmate who has never seen your experiment could run it correctly. The pre-registration form assembles all prior weeks' work (research question, hypothesis, stimulus spec, instrument config) plus the analysis plan into one document.

The analysis plan is simple: we will compute [DV] for each condition, compare conditions using [test], and consider the hypothesis supported if [statistical criterion]. Because the Adaptive Preference tool auto-runs the analysis, students mainly need to specify the criterion for "support" versus "null result."

  • Write the experimenter script: greeting → consent → instructions → Condition A → break → Condition B → debrief (or counterbalanced order)
  • Complete the consent form template (provided — fill in study title, risks, contact)
  • Specify the analysis plan: which DV, which test (t-test on preference scores), significance threshold (p < .05), and minimum effect size to report
  • Assemble the pre-registration form — it auto-pulls from weeks 1–5 submissions and asks you to fill in procedure and analysis plan
  • Submit the draft — the system timestamps it and sends to the instructor queue

What students do

Most pre-registrations come back with comments on the procedure (too vague) or the analysis plan (threshold not specified). Students revise and resubmit. Once approved, the system issues a green "Approved to run" status that is required before sensor setup in Week 8 can begin.

The dry run is a complete end-to-end walkthrough of the experiment with one team member as experimenter and the other as participant — using the real scenes, the real instrument, and following the script exactly. This catches: broken links, instrument not loading, script gaps, timing issues. The dry run generates a real data file (marked "dry-run" so it is excluded from analysis).

  • Read instructor feedback — address each comment specifically
  • Resubmit revised pre-registration (the system flags the revision and re-routes to instructor)
  • Once approved: run the complete dry run — experimenter role and participant role both performed
  • Upload the dry-run data file (labeled "dry_run" in the experiment runner — excluded from pilot analysis)
  • Note any issues the dry run revealed and how you fixed them

9
Pilot experiment · Run participants · Auto-analyze
Each team runs at least 3 participants — both team members plus one outside subject. The auto-analysis report is generated and reviewed in class Thursday.
Data collection Due Mon 6 pm: 3 data files + auto-analysis report Autogradeable
＋
What students do

Each team runs a minimum of 3 participants: Team Member 1 as participant (Team Member 2 runs), Team Member 2 as participant (Team Member 1 runs), and one external participant recruited from outside the class. Three data points is not enough to draw confident conclusions — and students are expected to say so — but it is enough to verify the pipeline works, see whether the direction of the effect is consistent with the hypothesis, and identify any procedure problems before a larger replication.

After the third session the auto-analysis report runs automatically. In Thursday's class, each team presents their preliminary result (not a conclusion — a finding) and gets 5 minutes of peer feedback. Students prepare a brief summary for this session but do not write the full report until Week 10.

  • Run Team Member 1 as participant — follow approved script exactly; upload data file immediately after
  • Run Team Member 2 as participant — same procedure; upload data file
  • Recruit and run 1 external participant (classmate from a different team, friend, family member — 18+)
  • After 3rd session: trigger the auto-analysis report from the experiment dashboard
  • Read the report — note the preference score direction, the confidence interval, and whether the result is consistent with the hypothesis
  • Prepare a 2-minute oral summary for Thursday class: what you found, what surprised you, what you are unsure about
Counterbalancing note. Participants should be randomly assigned to see Condition A first or Condition B first, to control for order effects. The Adaptive Preference Runner can handle this automatically if the "counterbalance order" setting is enabled during configuration. Make sure this is set before running participants — it cannot be changed mid-study.

Phase 4 — Week 10 · Write-up and 4-minute presentation
The write-up follows a provided template with fixed sections and word limits. The presentation is exactly 4 minutes — a strict limit enforced in class. Both are submitted by Monday 6 pm. The write-up is the primary graded artifact; the presentation is graded on clarity, not on the result.
Week
10
Write-up · 4-minute presentation · KA contribution
Complete the structured report, present findings in class, and submit results back to Knowledge Atlas — whether the hypothesis was supported or not.
Report due Mon 6 pm Presentations in class Thu Results go back into KA
＋
What students do

The write-up template has five sections with word limits enforced by the form. The template is intentionally short — this is a pilot report, not a journal submission. Students write in the first person. The result section is auto-populated from the Week 9 auto-analysis report; students interpret it, not re-describe it.

The 4-minute presentation follows a fixed format: 30 seconds on the research question, 30 seconds on the design, 1 minute on what participants experienced (show the VR scenes), 1 minute on the result, 1 minute on what to do next. The format is provided as a slide template with text already in the slide notes — students replace the notes text with their own content. Time is enforced; the chair cuts the speaker at 4:00.

The KA contribution step is what makes the course scientifically meaningful. Students submit their result — preference score and direction — to the Knowledge Atlas evidence intake. Even a null result is a contribution. KA records it as a new evidence node with the team's pre-registration as its provenance.

  • Complete the write-up form (5 sections, word limits — see below)
  • Use the provided presentation slide template — fill in your content in the note fields, then present from the slides
  • Submit your result to KA: preference score + direction + n + pre-registration link
  • In class: 4-minute presentation, then 2 minutes of Q&A from peers
