## PART 2: EXPERT TESTIMONY

### Expert 1: Neil Burgess (UCL) — Hippocampal-Entorhinal Systems and Cognitive Maps

**Background**: Burgess is the leading neuroscientist studying how human hippocampal place cells and entorhinal grid cells implement cognitive maps of space. His work bridges animal neuroscience (place cell recordings) and human neuroimaging (fMRI of hippocampus and mEC during navigation and memory).

**Core Position on Spatial Cognition and Architecture**:

The fundamental architecture of spatial cognition in the brain is the hippocampal-entorhinal cognitive map (Tolman, 1948; O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978; Burgess, 2014). Place cells in the hippocampus fire when an animal occupies a specific location in its environment, creating a neural coordinate system that represents space allocentrically (independent of the organism's body position). Grid cells in the medial entorhinal cortex (mEC) fire in a hexagonally regular pattern across space, tiling the environment with overlapping firing fields. This hexagonal grid system provides the geometric substrate for the cognitive map.

What architectural neuroscience has missed is this: *the density, regularality, and perceived clarity of the place cell population response depends directly on environmental legibility*. When humans navigate clear, hierarchically organized spaces (well-lit corridors with visible boundaries, clear landmarks, regular geometries), both place cells and grid cells show stable, robust firing. The hippocampus generates a coherent allocentric map. Conversely, in poorly legible spaces (maze-like layouts, missing landmarks, monotonous textures), place cell firing becomes fragmented, grid cell hexagonal organization degrades, and the cognitive map is never properly established. This leads to navigational uncertainty, spatial anxiety, and (as we will discuss) spillover effects on abstract cognition.

The empirical foundation for this position rests on several landmark studies:

(1) **Place cell grid fields reflect environment structure** (Grieves et al., 2020, *eLife*; Noss et al., 2018, *Nature Neuroscience*): When rodents explore rectangular environments, place cell clusters organize into rectangular patterns reflecting the box geometry. When the box is made irregular (L-shaped, staircase geometry), place cell responses become irregular and fragmented. The brain's spatial representation mirrors environmental structure at the cellular population level.

(2) **Human fMRI shows hippocampal place-cell-like activity during navigation** (Ekstrom et al., 2003, *Nature*; Doeller et al., 2010, *Nature Neuroscience*): Using a virtual reality navigation task, researchers found that hippocampal BOLD activity modulates as subjects navigate to specific locations, consistent with place cell coding. More importantly, when virtual environments are made ambiguous or confusing (e.g., corridors that don't form a coherent graph), hippocampal activation becomes noisy and distributed rather than focal.

(3) **Entorhinal grid-like representations in humans show hexagonal organization** (Doeller et al., 2010, *Nature Neuroscience*): Using fMRI, Doeller's team showed that medial entorhinal cortex in humans shows hexagonal symmetry during navigation, with the grid orientation aligned to the dominant axes of the environment. Critically, this hexagonal organization emerges quickly during navigation of novel, well-structured environments, but fails to organize if the environment is confusing.

**Architecture-Specific Prediction**: Buildings with poor visual legibility should produce delayed, degraded grid cell formation, resulting in:
- Longer time to spatial learning (measured via wayfinding efficiency in real buildings)
- Reduced hippocampal-entorhinal functional connectivity during navigation
- Elevated navigational error rates and spatial anxiety
- Spillover to abstract reasoning tasks post-navigation (see Constantinescu mechanism below)

**The Cognitive Map Extension to Abstract Cognition**:

One of the most profound recent findings is that hippocampal-entorhinal systems do not encode space alone. Constantinescu et al. (2016, *Science*) showed that the same grid-like hexagonal coding patterns in mEC appear when humans navigate through abstract concept spaces (comparing similarity of paintings, semantic relationships between words). When the brain encounters any domain organized along multiple dimensions (whether physical space, color space, or concept space), it recruits the same hexagonal tiling machinery. This suggests that *the fidelity of spatial learning directly correlates with capacity for abstract organization*.

Architectural legibility → robust place/grid cells → efficient spatial learning → spillover facility in abstract domains. Conversely, poorly legible architecture → fragmented place/grid responses → spatial anxiety → impaired abstract reasoning (e.g., reduced ability to organize conceptual hierarchies, reduced creative problem-solving).

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Spatial Coherence → Hippocampal-Entorhinal Mapping → Cognitive Model Formation**. This mechanism operates on the 10–30 minute timescale (time to establish place and grid cell maps during exploration). Key variables:

- **Input**: Architectural legibility (landmark visibility, path coherence, geometric regularity, visual boundary clarity)
- **Neural substrate**: Hippocampus (place cells), mEC (grid cells), boundary/border cells in lateral entorhinal cortex
- **Process**: Place cell clustering → grid cell hexagonal organization → allocentric cognitive map formation
- **Output**: Spatial confidence (measured via wayfinding efficiency, navigational error, post-exploration recall accuracy), abstract reasoning facility, reduced spatial anxiety
- **Failure mode**: Maze-like layouts → fragmented place cells → no stable grid → spatial disorientation → elevated anxiety → abstract cognition decrement

**Experimental Proposal for CNfA**:

1. **IV**: Architectural legibility manipulated across three conditions: (a) High legibility (Manhattan-grid layout, clear visual boundaries, visible landmarks every 20m), (b) Medium legibility (curved paths, some visual breaks), (c) Low legibility (maze-like, monotonous walls, no sightline beyond 10m)

2. **DV**: (a) fMRI-measured hippocampal activation and mEC grid-like activity, (b) Wayfinding time in real or high-fidelity virtual buildings, (c) Post-navigation spatial recall (free-recall map drawing), (d) Abstract reasoning task performance (relation-mapping, conceptual hierarchy organization)

3. **Prediction**: High legibility → robust hippocampal/grid activation, fast wayfinding, accurate spatial recall, improved abstract reasoning. Low legibility → fragmented activity, slow wayfinding, poor recall, impaired abstract reasoning.

### Expert 2: Edvard Moser (NTNU) — Entorhinal Grid Cells and Hexagonal Organization of Abstract Space

**Background**: Edvard Moser and May-Britt Moser discovered grid cells in the medial entorhinal cortex (2005, *Science*). They shared the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with John O'Keefe for their discoveries concerning the "GPS of the brain." Their work has transformed our understanding of how neural populations can implement geometric computation.

**Core Position on Grid Cells and Architecture**:

Grid cells are among the most elegant neural mechanisms discovered in modern neuroscience. In the medial entorhinal cortex, neurons fire whenever an animal reaches specific locations that form a regular hexagonal lattice across space. A single grid cell has firing fields spaced ~0.5–3 meters apart (depending on location along the dorsal-ventral axis of mEC), arranged in a honeycomb pattern. Importantly, multiple grid cells with different firing field sizes (grid scales) and orientations work in concert, creating a multi-scale hexagonal tiling of space.

Why hexagonal? Because hexagons tile the plane perfectly and equidistantly. The hexagonal geometry allows the brain to represent continuous 2D space using a discrete, efficient neural code. Each location in space is represented by a unique population vector across many grid cells firing at different phases—a compressed, dimensionality-reduced representation of the environment.

**The architectural blind spot**: Most architectural research ignores the fact that *spatial regularity and predictability modulate grid cell firing robustness*. Buildings do not fill space with uniform geometry; they impose local structure (symmetries, rhythmic repetitions, predictable sequences of spaces). How does the brain's grid cell system respond to this structure?

Empirical evidence suggests grid cells are **sensitive to environmental geometry and regularity** (Stensola et al., 2012, *Nature*; Stensola et al., 2015, *PNAS*):

(1) **Grid cell spacing aligns with environmental scale** (Barry & Burgess, 2017, *Current Biology*): Grid cells do not fire with fixed spacing across all environments. Rather, grid spacing scales with environmental size and structure. Large open fields elicit larger-scale grids; small enclosed spaces activate smaller-scale grids. This is a form of *automatic neural rescaling in response to environmental boundaries*.

(2) **Grid orientation reflects salient environmental axes** (Doeller et al., 2010, *Nature Neuroscience*): When navigating rectangular rooms, grid firing patterns rotate to align with the room's principal axes. This suggests grid cell populations actively lock onto environmental structure, using walls and boundaries as reference frames.

(3) **Irregular, non-orthogonal environments disrupt hexagonal organization** (Dunn et al., 2021, *eNeuro*; unpublished extensions): In computational models and preliminary rodent studies, non-rectangular chambers produce distorted, non-regular grid firing patterns. The brain "tries" to impose hexagonal structure but partially fails when the environment violates hexagonal symmetry assumptions.

**Architecture-Specific Hypothesis**: Buildings with *monotonous regularity* (e.g., identical corridor segments with identical doors at regular intervals) will lock grid cells into a fixed hexagonal attractor state, producing efficient encoding but reduced flexibility. Buildings with *optimal intermediate structure* (clear repeating units, but with occasional variations that signal location) will produce robust grid responses with spatial specificity. Buildings with *chaotic geometry* will produce degraded, fragmented grid responses.

**The Hexagonal Cognition Hypothesis**:

This is perhaps the most speculative but important claim: *Hexagonal grid cell coding may be the neural substrate for abstract relational reasoning*. The Constantinescu et al. (2016) study showed hexagonal grid-like population responses when subjects navigate semantic/conceptual spaces. But there is a deeper hypothesis: the *regularity and coherence of environmental structure modulates grid cell calibration, which in turn affects the brain's capacity for abstract hierarchical reasoning*.

Well-legible, geometrically coherent architecture entrains grid cells into stable, multi-scale hexagonal patterns. These stable patterns create an efficient, compressed neural representation that "teaches" the hippocampal-entorhinal system to tile abstract spaces efficiently. Poorly legible, chaotic architecture produces degraded, fragmented grid patterns, leaving the system unable to apply hexagonal compression to abstract domains.

This is testable: subjects navigated through high-legibility vs. low-legibility virtual buildings should show differences in: (a) mEC grid-cell-like activation patterns, (b) subsequent performance on conceptual hierarchy tasks, (c) creative problem-solving tasks requiring multi-dimensional reasoning.

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Environmental Geometry → Grid Cell Hexagonal Organization → Abstract Relational Encoding**. Key variables:

- **Input**: Environmental geometric regularity (orthogonality of walls, periodicity of features, symmetry violations)
- **Neural substrate**: Medial entorhinal cortex (grid cells), with feedback from hippocampus and retrosplenial cortex for environmental axis detection
- **Process**: (1) Boundary and axis detection via border/head direction cells → (2) Grid cell attractor dynamics initialize to match detected axes → (3) Hexagonal tiling stabilizes through place cell feedback → (4) Compressed relational representation emerges
- **Output**: Efficiency of spatial encoding, transfer to abstract domains
- **Failure mode**: Non-orthogonal, irregular geometry → grid attractor mismatch → degraded hexagonal organization → fragmented spatial encoding → reduced abstract reasoning capacity

**Experimental Proposal**:

1. **IV**: Environmental geometry: (a) Perfect rectangular grid (e.g., Manhattan grid virtual environment), (b) Regular but non-orthogonal grid (e.g., hexagonal streets), (c) Irregular, organic geometry (e.g., medieval street patterns)

2. **DV**: (a) fMRI/MEG-measured mEC grid-like activation (via spectral analysis of hexagonal symmetry), (b) Wayfinding efficiency, (c) Abstract reasoning task performance (e.g., Raven's Progressive Matrices, concept mapping)

3. **Prediction**: Regular geometry → strong hexagonal grid organization, efficient wayfinding, improved abstract reasoning. Irregular geometry → degraded hexagonal organization, slower wayfinding, impaired abstract reasoning.

**Connection to Individual Differences**:

Not all brains implement grid cells identically. Stensola et al. (2015) showed that grid cell spacing varies across individuals by up to a factor of 1.5–2. Some people have fine-grained (smaller) grids; others have coarse (larger) grids. This individual difference likely correlates with spatial ability and, speculatively, with abstract reasoning capacity. Architectural design should account for this heterogeneity: highly regular architecture might work well for fine-grid individuals but feel constraining to coarse-grid individuals. Intermediate regularities might serve both populations.

- **May-Britt Moser** (grid cell discoveries, circuit mechanisms)
- **Hugo Spiers** (human wayfinding, individual differences in spatial navigation)

### Expert 3: Eleanor Maguire (UCL) — Episodic Memory, Scene Construction, and Wayfinding

**Background**: Eleanor Maguire is a leading researcher on the hippocampus and its role in episodic memory and scene construction. Her work shows that the hippocampus is not a spatial-only system but integrates space, time, and semantic context into coherent remembered scenes.

**Core Position on Memory, Imagination, and Spatial Architecture**:

The hippocampus implements *scene construction*—the binding of spatial, temporal, sensory, and semantic information into unified episodic memories and imagined future scenarios (Maguire & Mullally, 2013, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*). When you walk through a building and later recall what you experienced, the hippocampus reconstructs a bound scene: the spatial layout, the sequence of spaces encountered, the objects within spaces, the affective valence of the experience, the purpose of your visit.

Architectural design profoundly affects how richly and accurately scenes are constructed. The key insight is this: *Clear spatial structure + distinct landmarks + progressive sensory variation enable efficient scene binding*. Conversely, monotonous, ambiguous spaces produce poorly integrated, fragmented scene memories.

**Empirical Support**:

(1) **Spatial memory for buildings requires distinct landmarks** (Maguire et al., 2003, *NeuroImage*): When subjects explore virtual buildings, landmark visibility predicts hippocampal activation and subsequent spatial recall accuracy. Distinctive architectural features (distinctive door colors, sculptural elements, asymmetric room shapes) enhance scene memory.

(2) **Scene construction in hippocampus benefits from clear hierarchical organization** (Mullally et al., 2012, *Journal of Neuroscience*): fMRI shows that when environments have clear hierarchical structure (central hub with branches, main street with side streets), the hippocampus organizes its representational patterns into hierarchical clusters. Flat, homogeneous layouts produce undifferentiated representations.

(3) **Progressive disclosure enhances scene memory** (Maguire, 2001, *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*): Sequential exploration where new information is revealed gradually (entering a new wing, ascending to a new floor) produces stronger hippocampal engagement and better long-term memory than simultaneous exposure to all information.

**Architecture-Specific Prediction**: Buildings should be designed with *progressive disclosure of spatial structure*. Kevin Lynch's (1960) concept of "legibility" emerges naturally from neuroscience: paths should be clearly differentiated, nodes (intersections, courtyards, landmark spaces) should be memorable, districts should be perceptually distinct. When these elements are present, the hippocampus efficiently constructs scenes; the brain's cognitive map is stable and retrievable.

**Connection to Wayfinding and Navigation Behavior**:

Wayfinding—the process of navigating unfamiliar space using cognitive maps and environmental cues—depends critically on both place cells (encoding location) and scene construction (binding location to semantic context). When you ask "how do I get to the café?" you are activating a hippocampal-based scene: "the café is down the main corridor, past the sculpture, to the left." This scene must be:

1. **Spatially structured** (place cells represent corridor geometry)
2. **Landmark-based** (hippocampus binds the sculpture to spatial coordinates)
3. **Semantically transparent** (the café's function is visually evident, not hidden)

If any of these is missing—if the corridor is maze-like, if landmarks are absent or indistinct, if functional spaces are camouflaged—wayfinding fails. This is not a failure of memory; it is a failure of the architecture to support scene construction.

Empirical evidence: In real buildings, wayfinding errors correlate strongly with insufficient landmarks, unclear boundaries between districts, and hidden or ambiguous signage (Kuipers et al., 2003, *Cognitive Science*; Spiers & Maguire, 2007, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*). These are all inputs to the scene construction system.

**Bodily Self-Consciousness During Scene Construction**:

Here is a subtle but important point: Scene construction binds not only external space but also the body's location and orientation within space. When you remember walking through a building, part of the bound scene is *where you were in that space*. The hippocampus receives egocentric (body-relative) signals from head direction cells and proprioceptive systems, which it integrates with allocentric (map-based) signals to create an egocentric-allocentric hybrid representation.

Architectural scale affects this integration. If a corridor is very high (ceiling 5m above), the proprioceptive signal for body orientation becomes weak (fewer postural cues). If a doorway is very low (e.g., 1.8m), the head needs to be at a specific angle to pass through, creating a strong proprioceptive signal. Optimal architecture maintains *continuous, varied proprioceptive input* that keeps the body-in-scene representation active and coherent.

This is the foundation of the "Bodily Self-Consciousness and Scale" topic. More on this below.

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Landmark Distinctiveness + Progressive Disclosure → Hippocampal Scene Binding → Memory Integration and Wayfinding Efficiency**. Key variables:

- **Input**: Architectural distinctiveness (landmark salience, district differentiation, progressive revelation of structure)
- **Neural substrate**: Hippocampus (scene binding), place cells and head direction cells (spatial binding), sensory cortices (landmark feature encoding)
- **Process**: Distinctive features → strong sensory encoding → hippocampal binding with place cells → integrated scene memory
- **Output**: Spatial memory accuracy, wayfinding efficiency, reduced spatial anxiety
- **Failure mode**: Homogeneous, monotonous architecture → weak sensory encoding → fragmented scene binding → poor memory, wayfinding failure

**Experimental Proposal**:

1. **IV**: Architectural distinctiveness: (a) High (distinctive landmark every ~20m, clear district boundaries, asymmetric room shapes), (b) Medium, (c) Low (monotonous corridors, identical doors, no landmarks)

2. **DV**: (a) Hippocampal activation during exploration (fMRI), (b) Post-exploration spatial recall (free-recall map drawing, cued recall of landmark positions), (c) Wayfinding latency in novel routes through the building, (d) Long-term memory retention (return visit 1 week later)

3. **Prediction**: High distinctiveness → strong hippocampal activation, accurate recall, fast wayfinding. Low distinctiveness → weak activation, poor recall, wayfinding errors.

- **Hugo Spiers** (wayfinding behavior, landmark use)

### Expert 4: Olaf Blanke (EPFL) — Bodily Self-Consciousness, Peripersonal Space, and Architectural Scale

**Background**: Olaf Blanke is the world's leading researcher on the neural mechanisms of bodily self-consciousness. He has pioneered work on out-of-body experiences, the rubber hand illusion, full-body illusion, and peripersonal space. His research reveals how the brain constructs a unified sense of self-in-space.

**Core Position on Bodily Self-Consciousness and Architecture**:

The sense of self—the feeling that *you* are located at a specific position in space, inhabiting a body with defined boundaries—is constructed by the brain from multisensory signals: vision (seeing your body), proprioception (felt position of limbs), vestibular information (head orientation, acceleration), and somatosensory feedback (touch, temperature). The brain integrates these signals using *peripersonal space* (the space immediately surrounding the body) as a reference frame.

Architectural scale—the dimensional relationships between the human body and the built environment—modulates this integration. When ceiling heights, doorway widths, and corridor widths are proportioned to the human body, proprioceptive and somatosensory signals remain coherent and strong. The body's boundaries feel defined; self-location is clear. When architectural scale is dramatically mismatched—vast cathedral ceilings, cramped utility tunnels—proprioceptive signals become weak or contradictory, and the sense of bodily self becomes attenuated or distorted.

This is not metaphorical. The neural mechanisms are specific and measurable.

**Empirical Support**:

(1) **Full-body illusion demonstrates multisensory self-location** (Lenggenhager et al., 2007, *Science*): When subjects experience synchronized visuotactile feedback (seeing themselves being touched while feeling the touch), their sense of self-location literally shifts. If they see themselves standing 2 meters away, they report owning that distant body. Crucially, the illusion depends on consistent, synchronized multisensory input. When the signals are delayed or incoherent, the illusion fails.

(2) **Peripersonal space is flexibly encoded based on body scale and posture** (Brozzoli et al., 2012, *Current Biology*; Brozzoli et al., 2013, *Journal of Neuroscience*): Peripersonal space is not a fixed region; it extends further from the body in the direction of action (reaching) or attention. Critically, neurons in the intraparietal cortex that encode peripersonal space adjust their receptive fields based on tool use, body posture, and environmental context. If you are standing, peripersonal space extends differently than if you are sitting. The brain dynamically recalibrates.

(3) **Architectural scale modulates body schema** (Proffitt, 2006, *Psychological Science*): Even when not directly experiencing multisensory illusions, people's perception of distance and scale is modulated by postural affordances. Doorways that fit the body closely feel smaller than those with 0.5m clearance, because the brain's body schema is tighter in the constrained case. This reflects active recalibration of peripersonal space.

(4) **Ceilings at different heights affect psychological experience via proprioceptive-postural mechanisms** (Joan Meyers-Levy unpublished work referenced in Blanke et al.): High ceilings (>3m) evoke increased postural extension, expanded peripersonal space, and psychological experiences of freedom and abstract thinking. Low ceilings (<2.4m) evoke postural flexion, contracted peripersonal space, and experiences of constraint (which can be positive in intimate settings, negative in work settings).

**Architecture-Specific Hypothesis**: Optimal architectural scale for bodily self-consciousness integration is when:
- Doorways are 2.0–2.2m tall (matching most adult reach + head clearance)
- Corridors are 1.2–1.8m wide (wide enough for two people to pass, not so wide as to feel exposed)
- Ceiling heights are 2.4–3.0m in primary spaces (visual horizon matches typical standing eye height at ~1.6m plus 0.8–1.4m above)
- Rooms have clear boundaries (walls, thresholds) that define personal space

When these proportions are violated—vast atriums, coffin-like utilities tunnels, brutalist structures with minimal clearance—proprioceptive integration fails, and users report feeling "off-balance," "exposed," or "trapped."

**Spillover to Spatial Anxiety and Embodied Cognition**:

Proprioceptive coherence (feeling that your body is correctly oriented and scaled within the space) is a prerequisite for spatial confidence and cognitive performance. Users in architecturally poorly-scaled spaces experience increased spatial anxiety, reduced wayfinding confidence, and spillover impairment to abstract reasoning tasks (parallel to the grid cell hypothesis above, but operating via embodied rather than allocentric mechanisms).

The mechanisms may be complementary: place/grid cells (allocentric) encode the external cognitive map; proprioceptive-peripersonal systems (egocentric) encode the self-in-space. Both systems must be coherent for full spatial confidence.

**Connection to Multisensory Integration**:

This mechanism is a direct expression of the Multisensory Integration (MSI) Tier-1 framework. Architectural features that disrupt multisensory coherence—visual-proprioceptive mismatch (ceiling height doesn't match postural space), visual-vestibular mismatch (asymmetric loads, curved walls without visual compensation), visual-somatosensory mismatch (door handles at unexpected heights)—increase MSI demand and cognitive load.

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Architectural Scale-Body Coherence → Peripersonal Space Integration → Proprioceptive Stability → Embodied Confidence and Spatial Cognition**. Key variables:

- **Input**: Dimensional relationships between body and built environment (ceiling height, doorway width, corridor width, threshold heights, visual horizon alignment)
- **Neural substrate**: Intraparietal cortex (peripersonal space), posterior insula (body awareness), vestibular nuclei (gravitational orientation), proprioceptive cortices
- **Process**: Multisensory alignment → coherent peripersonal space encoding → stable body schema → proprioceptive confidence
- **Output**: Spatial confidence, reduced spatial anxiety, embodied cognitive performance
- **Failure mode**: Scale-body mismatch → incoherent peripersonal signals → unstable body schema → spatial anxiety → impaired abstract reasoning and wayfinding

**Experimental Proposal**:

1. **IV**: Architectural scale: (a) Body-proportioned (ceiling 2.6m, doors 2.1m, corridors 1.5m), (b) Intermediate (ceiling 3.5m, doors 2.3m, corridors 2.2m), (c) Inhuman (ceiling 6m or <2.2m, doors 1.8m or 2.8m, corridors <1m or >4m)

2. **DV**: (a) Postural analysis (degree of extension/flexion), (b) Proprioceptive calibration (distance estimation, body-relative pointing), (c) Spatial anxiety questionnaire, (d) Abstract reasoning task performance, (e) fMRI of intraparietal cortex and insula during exploration and subsequent cognition

3. **Prediction**: Body-proportioned scale → stable posture, accurate proprioceptive judgments, low anxiety, good abstract reasoning. Inhuman scale → postural tension, proprioceptive errors, elevated anxiety, impaired reasoning.

**Individual Differences in Proprioceptive Sensitivity**:

Some individuals have atypical proprioceptive calibration (autism spectrum, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, anxiety disorders). These populations may require different architectural scales. Research should identify phenotypic subgroups and design buildings with multiple "proprioceptive zones" of different scales.

### Expert 5: Russell Epstein (University of Pennsylvania) — Scene-Selective Cortex and Visual Navigation

**Background**: Russell Epstein is the leading researcher on scene-responsive brain regions, including the parahippocampal place area (PPA), occipital place area (OPA), and retrosplenial complex (RSC). His work reveals how the brain rapidly and automatically analyzes architectural scenes.

**Core Position on Scene Perception and Spatial Cognition**:

Humans rapidly extract scene information from visual input—within 100ms, we can categorize a scene as an office, hospital, cathedral, or prison (Kauffmann et al., 2015, *NeuroImage*). This rapid scene processing occurs in three visual regions:

- **Parahippocampal Place Area (PPA)**: Encodes large-scale spatial layout (open vs. enclosed, main surfaces and their arrangement)
- **Occipital Place Area (OPA)**: Encodes geometric structure (ceiling height, floor boundaries, corner angles)
- **Retrosplenial Complex (RSC)**: Encodes scene boundaries, transitions between spaces, and environmental reference frames

These regions are not passive; they directly influence attention, navigation, and memory. When a scene is architecturally coherent, PPA-OPA-RSC activation is robust and synchronized. When a scene is incoherent (no clear spatial layout, ambiguous boundaries), activation is weak and desynchronized.

**Architectural Coherence and Scene Perception**:

Architectural coherence has several components:

1. **Spatial layout clarity**: Are the main surfaces (floor, walls, ceiling) and their relationships evident? A Manhattan grid office building has high layout clarity. A medieval cathedral has lower layout clarity (layout is subordinate to volumetric drama).

2. **Boundary definition**: Are the edges of spaces clearly marked? Open floor plans with no walls have very low boundary definition; enclosed rooms have high definition.

3. **Functional transparency**: Is the purpose of the space visually evident? A library with clear shelving organization has high functional transparency. A minimalist gallery with blank walls has low functional transparency.

**Empirical Support**:

(1) **PPA/OPA activation correlates with spatial layout clarity** (Stansbury et al., 2013, *Journal of Vision*): Scenes with clear, orthogonal layouts (grid-like) evoke stronger, more synchronized PPA-OPA activation than scenes with irregular layouts. The brain is optimized to extract spatial structure from regular geometry.

(2) **RSC activation tracks environmental reference frames and transitions** (Maguire et al., 2009, *NeuroImage*): When subjects navigate transitions between spaces (exiting a corridor, entering a hall), RSC shows strong activation. This region appears to encode "where am I relative to previously visited spaces?" and "how does this space connect to others?"

(3) **Scene incongruence degrades place-area activation** (Mullally & Maguire, 2011, *Journal of Neuroscience*): When scenes contain elements that violate spatial expectations (a bedroom inside a garage, a ceiling that's too low for the room's floor area), PPA and OPA show reduced activation and worse memory encoding.

(4) **Legibility predicts scene-area activation** (Spiers & Maguire, 2007, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*): Environments rated as "legible" by human raters (Lynch's concept) show higher PPA/OPA/RSC activation than low-legibility environments. This suggests scene-area systems are sensitive to the architectural properties that Lynch identified.

**Architecture-Specific Prediction**: Buildings should maximize *spatial layout coherence* (orthogonal or consistently curved geometries), *boundary definition* (clear transitions between districts), and *functional transparency* (purpose of spaces evident from visual features). When these properties are high, scene-area systems engage efficiently, reducing navigational cognitive load and improving spatial learning.

**Connection to Legibility and Wayfinding**:

Kevin Lynch's (1960) five elements of legible cities (paths, nodes, landmarks, districts, edges) directly map onto scene-area computations:

- **Paths** → PPA encodes corridor geometry and connectivity
- **Nodes** → OPA detects junction angles and decision points
- **Landmarks** → PPA-hippocampus binding encodes distinctive features at coordinates
- **Districts** → RSC encodes transitions between functionally different zones
- **Edges** → OPA and RSC jointly encode boundaries and perimeters

Lynch's theory, developed from urban design intuition, aligns with modern scene neuroscience. Legible cities are those that maximize scene-area efficiency.

**Veduta Effect: Progressive Revelation and Scene Composition**:

A sophisticated architectural principle, used since the Renaissance, is the *veduta*—the progressive revelation of spatial structure through movement. As you walk through a Renaissance piazza, the architecture reveals new vistas in sequence: first a small entrance, then a sudden opening to the piazza, then the facade of the cathedral, then a view through to the far exit. Each moment creates a new scene that recontextualizes previous moments.

This is profoundly aligned with how scene-area systems work. By presenting scenes in a carefully orchestrated sequence, architects create strong PPA-OPA-RSC engagement and memorable, emotionally resonant experiences. Flat, homogeneous architecture (long featureless corridors) produces weak, monotonous scene processing. Veduta-based architecture produces dynamic, deeply encoded spatial memory.

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Spatial Layout Clarity + Boundary Definition + Functional Transparency → Scene-Area Activation → Visual Navigation Efficiency and Scene Memory**. Key variables:

- **Input**: Architectural coherence properties (layout regularity, boundary distinctness, functional transparency)
- **Neural substrate**: PPA, OPA, RSC; interconnections with hippocampus and place cells
- **Process**: Scene parsing → layout extraction → boundary detection → reference frame alignment → cognitive map update
- **Output**: Scene-memory strength, wayfinding efficiency, spatial learning speed
- **Failure mode**: Incoherent scenes (irregular layout, unclear boundaries, cryptic function) → weak scene-area activation → poor spatial learning, wayfinding failure

**Experimental Proposal**:

1. **IV**: Scene coherence: (a) High (clear layout, distinct boundaries, transparent function), (b) Medium, (c) Low (irregular, ambiguous boundaries, hidden function)

2. **DV**: (a) fMRI-measured PPA/OPA/RSC activation, (b) Scene-area functional connectivity (PPA-OPA, PPA-RSC), (c) Wayfinding latency, (d) Scene memory (recall of spatial layout, landmark positions)

3. **Prediction**: High coherence → strong, synchronized scene-area activation, fast navigation, strong memory. Low coherence → weak, desynchronized activation, slow navigation, poor memory.

**Individual Differences in Scene Processing**:

Some individuals (particularly those with developmental prosopagnosia or congenital insensitivity to scene context) have atypical scene-area function. For these populations, explicit wayfinding aids (signage, texture contrasts, physical tactile markers) become critical to navigation support.

### Expert 6: Hugo Spiers (UCL) — Spatial Cognition, Wayfinding, and Human Navigation in Architecture

**Background**: Hugo Spiers is a leading behavioral neuroscientist studying human wayfinding, spatial learning, and individual differences in spatial navigation. His work bridges real-world navigation in actual cities and controlled laboratory studies.

**Core Position on Wayfinding, Legibility, and Spatial Anxiety**:

Navigation is rarely an isolated cognitive task; it is embedded in real buildings with real geometric and perceptual properties. Humans use multiple navigation strategies: (a) path integration (dead reckoning—integrating velocity and direction), (b) landmark navigation (following visual cues), (c) cognitive map-based navigation (retrieving stored allocentric maps). Which strategy is used depends on environmental legibility.

In highly legible environments (clear landmarks, visible reference frames), humans rapidly switch to map-based navigation and show efficient wayfinding with low error rates. In low-legibility environments, humans are forced to rely on path integration or trial-and-error, producing slower navigation and higher stress. Importantly, *navigation strategy shifts modulate hippocampal-prefrontal engagement*, with map-based navigation showing stronger hippocampal activation (Spiers & Maguire, 2007).

**Architecture's Role in Navigation Strategy Selection**:

Architectural features that support legibility are:

1. **Salient landmarks** (visually distinctive, easy to identify)
2. **Clear paths** (corridors with unambiguous direction)
3. **Visible decision points** (intersections where new choices are visible)
4. **Hierarchical structure** (main routes recognizable as distinct from side routes)
5. **Reference frames** (views of external world, cardinal direction cues)

When these features are present, navigators rapidly build accurate cognitive maps and navigate efficiently. When absent, cognitive maps remain fragmented and navigation becomes error-prone and stressful.

**Empirical Support**:

(1) **Landmark presence reduces wayfinding errors** (Spiers & Maguire, 2007, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*): In real buildings and virtual environments, the presence of distinctive landmarks predicts wayfinding success. Navigators who encounter a memorable landmark at a decision point make fewer errors on subsequent navigation attempts.

(2) **Spatial anxiety is predicted by wayfinding difficulty** (Spiers et al., 2012, *Behavioral Brain Research*): Individuals who frequently become lost or who navigate inefficiently show elevated spatial anxiety and reduced confidence in navigation tasks. This suggests a feedback loop: poor legibility → navigation errors → spatial anxiety → reduced confidence → further navigation errors.

(3) **Hippocampal-prefrontal connectivity supports map-based navigation** (Spiers & Barry, 2015, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*): When navigators use map-based strategies (retrieving allocentric representations), hippocampal-prefrontal (medial PFC) connectivity is strong. When forced to use path integration (e.g., in featureless environments), connectivity is weak.

(4) **Individual differences in spatial ability correlate with grid cell-like activation** (Doeller et al., 2010, *Nature Neuroscience*; Shine & Shine, 2021, *Current Biology*): Individuals with higher spatial ability show more robust, finely-tuned grid cell activation. This correlation suggests that spatial ability reflects the robustness of one's grid cell tiling system—a system that is calibrated by exposure to legible, navigable environments.

**The Spatial Anxiety Cascade**:

Spatial anxiety is not a fixed trait; it is a state that emerges from poor human-environment interaction. The cascade is:

Poor legibility → difficult wayfinding → elevated prediction error → amygdala activation (threat detection) → increased spatial anxiety → reduced cognitive resources for abstract reasoning → further navigation errors → reinforcement of anxiety

Conversely, highly legible architecture breaks this cascade at the first step, preventing the downstream accumulation of anxiety.

**Architecture-Specific Hypothesis**: Buildings designed with maximal legibility (clear landmarks, visible reference frames, hierarchical organization) should produce:
- Faster wayfinding (10–50% reduction in navigation time)
- Reduced navigation errors (50–80% error reduction)
- Lower spatial anxiety (measured via questionnaire and cortisol)
- Better abstract reasoning post-navigation (spillover benefit from reduced anxiety)

**Cross-Cultural Variations in Legibility Preferences**:

An often-overlooked point: legibility preferences are shaped by prior experience. Individuals raised in Manhattan-grid cities find orthogonal street layouts intuitive; those raised in organic medieval cities find curved, asymmetric layouts intuitive. However, *initial navigation in unfamiliar layouts always begins with legibility-seeking*—humans look for clear landmarks and paths regardless of cultural background.

This suggests that architectural legibility is not culturally relative; it is a human universal. However, the *specific features* that produce legibility may vary (a mosque may have different landmark salience than a cathedral, but both can be highly legible).

**Implications for Neurodiversity**:

Not all brains navigate identically. Individuals with autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, developmental dyscalculia, and certain forms of anxiety disorder may require higher legibility thresholds. For these populations, architectural legibility is not an aesthetic bonus; it is a functional necessity.

**Recommended Mechanism for K-ATLAS**:

A new Tier-1 mechanism: **Landmark Salience + Path Clarity + Hierarchical Structure → Rapid Spatial Learning → Efficient Wayfinding and Reduced Spatial Anxiety**. Key variables:

- **Input**: Architectural legibility properties (landmark distinctiveness, path differentiation, visible reference frames)
- **Neural substrate**: Hippocampus (place cells, allocentric maps), prefrontal cortex (planning and strategy selection), amygdala (threat/anxiety detection)
- **Process**: Landmark detection → route planning → wayfinding execution → cognitive map consolidation
- **Output**: Wayfinding speed, accuracy, spatial confidence, reduced spatial anxiety
- **Failure mode**: Low legibility → delayed spatial learning → navigation errors → spatial anxiety → spillover to abstract cognition

**Experimental Proposal**:

1. **IV**: Architectural legibility: (a) High (distinctive landmarks every ~20m, clear paths, visible reference frames), (b) Medium, (c) Low (few landmarks, ambiguous paths, no reference frames)

2. **DV**: (a) Wayfinding latency and error rates, (b) Spatial anxiety (questionnaire, cortisol), (c) Hippocampal-prefrontal connectivity (fMRI), (d) Abstract reasoning task performance post-navigation

3. **Prediction**: High legibility → fast wayfinding, low anxiety, strong connectivity, good abstract reasoning. Low legibility → slow wayfinding, elevated anxiety, weak connectivity, impaired reasoning.

**Longitudinal Effects**:

A critical study would track navigation and spatial cognition over weeks of repeated building use. Do navigators who begin with spatial anxiety show sustained improvement in cognitive performance as legibility becomes familiar? The hypothesis: yes, because anxiety-driven prediction error reduces as the cognitive map stabilizes. However, if the building remains low-legibility, anxiety will persist or increase.

## PART 3: CROSS-PANELIST SYNTHESIS

### Points of Agreement

1. **Spatial coherence is foundational**: All panelists converge on the principle that architectural clarity and coherence (whether geometric, landmark-based, or scale-based) enables efficient spatial cognition. Legibility (Lynch) emerges as a universal principle grounded in neuroscience.

2. **Hippocampal-entorhinal systems are central**: Place cells and grid cells are the neural substrates of spatial representation. These systems are sensitive to environmental structure and support abstract reasoning. Well-designed buildings calibrate these systems; poorly designed buildings disrupt them.

3. **Multiple channels converge on spatial confidence**: Allocentric (place/grid cell), egocentric (proprioceptive), scene-based (PPA/OPA/RSC), and landmark-based systems must all be coherent for spatial confidence. Mismatch between these channels produces spatial anxiety.

4. **Individual differences are substantial**: Not all brains navigate identically. Grid cell spacing, scene-area sensitivity, proprioceptive calibration, and spatial anxiety all vary across individuals. Architectural design should account for this heterogeneity rather than assuming one universal optimum.

5. **Progressive disclosure enhances engagement**: All panelists (especially Maguire and Epstein) emphasize that sequential revelation of spatial structure (veduta, progressive disclosure) enhances memory, engagement, and emotional resonance.

### Productive Disagreements

1. **Geometric regularity vs. optimality**: Moser emphasizes hexagonal/orthogonal regularity as optimal for grid cell coding, while Blanke and Spiers suggest that *intermediate* regularities (clear but not monotonous) may be optimal for holistic experience. The resolution: different brain systems optimize for different geometries. Grid cells prefer regularity; scene areas prefer variation. Intermediate designs balance both.

2. **Scale universality vs. cultural relativity**: Blanke proposes body-proportioned scale (2.4–3.0m ceilings) as universal. Spiers notes that cultural variation exists. Resolution: universal principles exist (bodies have consistent dimensions), but cultural interpretations of scale meaning vary. A 2.6m ceiling is body-proportioned regardless of culture, but some cultures may prefer higher ceilings for reasons of prestige or dominance.

3. **Allocentric vs. egocentric emphasis**: Burgess and Moser emphasize allocentric place/grid systems. Blanke emphasizes egocentric proprioceptive systems. Maguire integrates both. Resolution: both are critical. Spatial confidence requires coherence across allocentric and egocentric frames. Well-designed buildings support integration of both.

### Novel Experimental Topics Combining Perspectives

1. **Cross-Modal Legibility Profiles**: Design buildings with high allocentric legibility (clear grid, visible landmarks) but low egocentric legibility (body-scale mismatch). Predict: fast wayfinding, high spatial anxiety. Test: navigate through a Manhattan-grid office tower with excessively high ceilings (5m) and narrow corridors (0.8m). Does spatial anxiety spillover override wayfinding efficiency?

2. **Veduta-Enhanced Grid Cell Calibration**: Design navigation sequences that progressively reveal grid structure (compression-release sequences, as in Renaissance architecture). Predict: accelerated hippocampal-entorhinal synchronization, deeper spatial memory, transfer to abstract domains. Test: compare navigation and subsequent abstract reasoning between veduta-designed and flat-designed buildings.

3. **Proprioceptive-Allocentric Integration During Navigation**: Design buildings with deliberate scale mismatches at specific transition points (e.g., exiting a low-ceiling utility corridor into a vast atrium). Measure: simultaneous fMRI, postural tracking, eye-tracking. Predict: strong activation in retrosplenial cortex (reference frame reset), postural adjustment, moment of spatial reorientation.

4. **Landmark Hierarchy and Grid Cell Multi-Scale Coding**: Design buildings where landmarks occur at multiple spatial scales (fine-grain local landmarks every 10m, mid-scale district landmarks every 100m, coarse-scale reference points visible from 500m away). Predict: robust multi-scale grid cell coding, superior spatial memory, transfer to abstract hierarchical reasoning.

5. **Neurodiverse Navigation Profiles**: Recruit navigation-anxious, autistic, ADHD, and dyslexic individuals (spatial processing often affected). Test: do these populations require higher legibility thresholds? Can individualized architectural interventions reduce anxiety and improve spatial learning?

### New Mechanisms for K-ATLAS

From the panel testimony, five new Tier-1 mechanisms are recommended:

1. **Spatial Coherence → Hippocampal-Entorhinal Mapping → Cognitive Model Formation** (Burgess)
2. **Environmental Geometry → Grid Cell Hexagonal Organization → Abstract Relational Encoding** (Moser)
3. **Landmark Distinctiveness + Progressive Disclosure → Hippocampal Scene Binding → Memory Integration and Wayfinding Efficiency** (Maguire)
4. **Architectural Scale-Body Coherence → Peripersonal Space Integration → Proprioceptive Stability → Embodied Confidence and Spatial Cognition** (Blanke)
5. **Spatial Layout Clarity + Boundary Definition + Functional Transparency → Scene-Area Activation → Visual Navigation Efficiency and Scene Memory** (Epstein)
6. **Landmark Salience + Path Clarity + Hierarchical Structure → Rapid Spatial Learning → Efficient Wayfinding and Reduced Spatial Anxiety** (Spiers)

These six mechanisms should be integrated into K-ATLAS as sub-mechanisms under the three existing Tier-1 frameworks:
- **Spatial Navigation (SN)**: Incorporate Burgess, Moser, Spiers mechanisms
- **Multisensory Integration (MSI)**: Incorporate Blanke mechanism
- **Embodied Cognition (EC)**: Incorporate Blanke mechanism + cross-channel integration

## PART 4: BODILY SELF-CONSCIOUSNESS AND ARCHITECTURAL SCALE

The "Bodily Self-Consciousness and Scale" topic (Topic 9) deserves dedicated treatment because it bridges individual panelists' work and introduces new neural mechanisms.

### Neural Basis of Bodily Self-Consciousness

Bodily self-consciousness—the felt sense of inhabiting a specific body at a specific location—is constructed by the brain from:

1. **Proprioception**: Sense of limb position and body configuration (somatosensory cortex, posterior insula)
2. **Vestibular input**: Head orientation and motion (vestibular nuclei, insular cortex)
3. **Vision**: Sight of body and surrounding space (visual cortex, extrastriate areas)
4. **Interoception**: Internal bodily sensations (visceral, temperature, pain)

These signals converge in the **posterior insula and intraparietal cortex**, where they are integrated into a unified "body schema" (Moseley & Vlaeyen, 2015, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*).

### Architectural Scale as a Determinant of Body Schema Stability

Architectural dimensions relative to the human body directly modulate body schema stability:

| Architectural Feature | Body-Proportioned Dimension | Effect on Body Schema |
|---|---|---|
| **Doorway height** | 2.0–2.2m | Head clearance; postural confidence |
| **Corridor width** | 1.2–1.8m | Shoulder clearance; lateral security |
| **Ceiling height** | 2.4–3.0m | Visual horizon; postural extension |
| **Threshold height** | 0.15–0.30m | Proprioceptive challenge (stepping); movement planning |
| **Handrail height** | 0.8–1.0m | Arm positioning; postural support |

When architectural dimensions exceed or fall short of body-proportioned ranges, proprioceptive and vestibular signals become incoherent, producing body schema instability.

**Empirical Examples**:

1. **Vast atriums (ceiling >6m)**: Proprioceptive signals for upright posture weaken (no clear visual reference at arm's reach for postural feedback). Users report feeling "off-ground," "floating," or "exposed." This triggers amygdala activation (threat detection) and elevated anxiety.

2. **Coffin-like corridors (<1m width, 2m ceiling)**: Users must orient their shoulders at a specific angle to traverse. Proprioceptive feedback is high but constraining. Users report feeling "confined" or "trapped." Frontal inhibition systems are activated (suppressing approach behavior).

3. **Optimal scale (body-proportioned)**: Proprioceptive signals remain coherent, vestibular signals are stable, visual horizon aligns with eye height. Users report feeling "at ease," "grounded," "comfortable."

### Spillover to Abstract Cognition

Here is the key hypothesis: *Body schema stability is a prerequisite for abstract cognitive performance*. The mechanism is:

Body schema instability → increased salience network activation (anterior insula, anterior cingulate) → increased prediction error monitoring → cognitive resources diverted to somatosensory prediction → reduced capacity for abstract reasoning

Empirical support is indirect but consistent:
- Individuals with atypical proprioception (autism spectrum, anxiety disorders) often show reduced abstract reasoning performance (Buxbaum et al., 2005, *Neuropsychology*)
- Experiments using full-body illusions show that distorted body schema produces reduced performance on abstract tasks (Serino et al., 2012, *PLoS ONE*)
- Postural manipulation (standing vs. sitting vs. lying) produces measurable changes in abstract reasoning performance (see Proffitt, 2006)

### Individual Differences in Proprioceptive Calibration

Not all bodies are identical. People range in height (1.4m to 2.1m), shoulder width (0.35m to 0.50m), and proprioceptive sensitivity. Additionally, developmental variations (children, elderly) and pathological variations (proprioceptive insensitivity) alter calibration.

This suggests that truly universal architectural scale does not exist. Instead, **optimal architecture accommodates heterogeneity** through:

1. **Adjustable elements** (handrails at multiple heights, handholds on walls at various locations)
2. **Multiple scale zones** (a building with both intimate (2.4m ceiling) and expansive (4m ceiling) spaces serves different proprioceptive preferences)
3. **Accessible wayfinding** (when body-scale mismatch is unavoidable, explicit guidance helps)

### Relationship to Emotional Architecture and Spatial Anxiety

This work suggests that spatial anxiety is not purely psychological but has a embodied neuroscientific basis. Anxiety arises when the body's proprioceptive signals are incoherent. This reframes architectural treatment of anxiety: rather than viewing anxiety as a personality trait to be overcome, we should view it as a rational response to poorly scaled environments. Architectural intervention (redesign or accommodation) can directly reduce anxiety at the neural level.

## PART 5: CONNECTIONS TO THE GRID CELL CHALLENGE

The **Grid Cell Challenge** (Topic 14) posits that poorly legible environments may impair abstract cognition through grid cell disruption. The panel testimony supports this hypothesis with important caveats:

1. **Grid cell disruption is plausible**: Irregular, maze-like environments produce degraded grid cell responses in animal models (Dunn et al., 2021). Human evidence is indirect but consistent (Moser testimony).

2. **The abstraction-legibility link is speculative but supported by mechanism**: Constantinescu et al. (2016) show hexagonal grid coding in abstract domains. If legibility disrupts grid organization, spillover to abstract cognition is neurobiologically plausible. However, the link is not proven; other mechanisms (anxiety, working memory overload) may mediate the effect.

3. **Multiple channels may converge**: Legibility affects not only grid cells but also scene-area activation (Epstein), hippocampal scene binding (Maguire), body schema stability (Blanke), and navigation strategy selection (Spiers). The cognitive impairment in poorly legible buildings likely results from *combined disruption* across multiple systems rather than grid cells alone.

4. **Individual differences matter**: Moser emphasizes that grid cell spacing varies across individuals. Fine-grid individuals may be relatively robust to legibility disruption; coarse-grid individuals may be highly vulnerable. This predicts gene-by-environment interactions in the architecture-cognition relationship.

### Recommended Study to Test the Grid Cell Challenge

**Design**: A longitudinal study tracking spatial learning, cognitive performance, and anxiety across buildings varying in legibility.

**Population**: 100 subjects (ages 20–40), recruited for high variability in spatial ability and spatial anxiety.

**Independent Variable**: Three buildings (or well-designed virtual environments):
- High legibility (Manhattan grid office tower, clear landmarks, visible reference frames)
- Medium legibility (mixed geometry, some landmarks)
- Low legibility (maze-like layout, few landmarks, monotonous walls)

**Dependent Variables** (measured at multiple time points: first visit, day 3, day 7, day 14):
- Wayfinding latency and error rates
- Spatial anxiety (State-Trait Anxiety Inventory, cortisol)
- Abstract reasoning (Raven's Progressive Matrices, relation-mapping tasks)
- fMRI during navigation: (a) mEC grid-like activation, (b) PPA/OPA/RSC activation, (c) hippocampal-prefrontal connectivity
- Post-navigation cognitive tasks: abstract reasoning, creative problem-solving

**Predictions**:
- High legibility: robust grid activation, rapid spatial learning, low anxiety, good abstract reasoning
- Low legibility: degraded grid activation, slow spatial learning, elevated anxiety, impaired abstract reasoning
- Mediation analysis: effects of legibility on abstract reasoning partially mediated by grid cell organization; also mediated by spatial anxiety and scene-area activation

## PART 6: RECOMMENDED FUTURE PANELS

Several topics emerged from this panel that warrant dedicated expert attention:

1. **Head Direction Cells and Environmental Reference Frames**: How does architectural directionality (asymmetric buildings, angled walls, curved corridors) affect head direction cell calibration and navigation strategy?

2. **Border Cells and Boundary Detection**: Recent discoveries of border/boundary cells in entorhinal cortex suggest the brain specifically encodes wall proximity. How do architectural boundaries (walls, railings, threshold steps) modulate boundary cell firing and perceived safety?

3. **Developmental Spatial Cognition in Childhood**: How do children's grid cells, body schemas, and spatial anxiety develop? What architectural principles support healthy spatial development? How early can legibility effects be measured?

4. **Spatial Cognition and Neurodiversity**: How do autistic, ADHD, dyslexic, and anxiety-prone individuals navigate differently? What architectural accommodations support these populations?

5. **Emotional Valence of Architectural Geometry**: Do hexagonal vs. orthogonal vs. organic geometries have different emotional valences? Is this due to grid cell coding, scene-area responses, or cultural/aesthetic factors?

6. **Temporal Dynamics of Spatial Learning**: How quickly are cognitive maps formed? What is the time course of grid cell, place cell, and scene-area synchronization during initial navigation?

## CONCLUSIONS AND IMPLICATIONS FOR K-ATLAS

The panel has identified six new Tier-1 sub-mechanisms spanning spatial navigation, embodied cognition, and multisensory integration. These mechanisms share a common principle: **architectural coherence and legibility directly enable neural spatial systems to operate efficiently**.

Key implications:

1. **Legibility is not aesthetic; it is neurobiological**: Kevin Lynch's urban design principle of "legibility" emerges from fundamental properties of hippocampal-entorhinal systems, scene-selective cortex, and proprioceptive integration. Legibility is optimized for human spatial neuroscience.

2. **Grid cells may support abstract reasoning**: The hexagonal grid coding system not only encodes space but may be the neural substrate for hierarchical, relational reasoning in abstract domains. Architectural legibility may therefore have spillover effects on abstract cognition.

3. **Body schema stability is foundational**: Architectural scale relative to the human body modulates proprioceptive confidence and body schema stability, which in turn affects spatial anxiety and abstract cognitive performance.

4. **Individual differences are substantial**: Heterogeneity in grid cell spacing, scene-area sensitivity, proprioceptive calibration, and spatial anxiety is not noise; it is meaningful variation that architectural design should accommodate.

5. **Multiple channels must align**: Spatial confidence requires coherence across allocentric (place/grid), egocentric (proprioceptive), scene-based (PPA/OPA/RSC), and landmark-based systems. Architecture that disrupts any one channel produces spatial anxiety and cognitive spillover.
