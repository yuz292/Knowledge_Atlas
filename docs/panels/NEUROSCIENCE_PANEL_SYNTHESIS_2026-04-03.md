## 1. Executive Summary

The six neuroscience panels convened in April 2026 fundamentally expand the K-ATLAS knowledge base in three ways:

First, they identify **three entirely new neural frontiers** for architecture: (1) **interoceptive design** — how built environments modulate the brain's sensing of internal body states through the insular cortex and vagal pathways, with direct implications for emotional regulation and decision-making; (2) **inter-brain synchrony in shared spaces** — using hyperscanning (dual fMRI/EEG) to measure whether face-to-face seating, sightlines, and acoustic privacy actually produce synchronized neural activity between collaborators; and (3) **developmental sensitive periods and architectural programming** — the discovery that environmental architecture during childhood activates molecular mechanisms (GABAergic circuit maturation, perineuronal net formation) that literally reshape neural connectivity during windows of heightened plasticity.

Second, they provide unprecedented specificity on **stimulus properties that predict neural response**. Rather than describing "visual clutter" impressionistically, the panels establish that fractal dimension (*D* ≈ 1.3–1.5), spatial frequency slope (*α* ≈ 1.0), and peripheral clutter index are quantifiable physical properties that predict V1/V2 responses and cortisol levels *independently of subjective preference*. Similarly, sound design should specify acoustic sharpness (acum), roughness (asper), and temporal dynamics — not just decibel levels. Olfaction should be measured as direct hippocampal-amygdala input (unique among sensory modalities) with measurable effects on memory consolidation during sleep.

Third, they reveal **convergent findings across independent panels** that establish high-confidence predictions: (a) thermal comfort and social warmth are neurobiologically overlapping (anterior insula), making temperature a social neuroscience variable; (b) spatial distinctiveness (color, texture, layout variation) provides hippocampal anchoring for context-dependent memory and abstract cognition (via grid cells); (c) noise — specifically its *unpredictability and uncontrollability* — is a primary mechanism driving allostatic load and hostile attribution bias in chronic urban stress; and (d) the olfactory system's unique direct pathway to hippocampus and amygdala (bypassing thalamic relay) makes ambient scent an untouched architectural variable with strong memory and emotional predictors.

The panels also surface a critical gap: while CNfA has focused on acute outcomes (preference, mood, task performance), there is virtually no longitudinal evidence on **allostatic load** — the cumulative physiological "wear" of chronic suboptimal light, thermal drift, low-level noise, and visual incoherence. This is the highest priority gap for future research.

## 2. Expanded Neural Framework Inventory

### Current Tier-1 Frameworks and New Evidence

K-ATLAS currently maintains 10 Tier-1 Neural Frameworks. Below, we document what each panel adds to these, and identify 5 new frameworks for admission.

#### **PP (Predictive Processing)**

**Existing state**: The brain as a prediction machine; violations generate error signals that drive attention and resource consumption.

**What the panels add**:

- **Predictive interoception (Feldman Barrett testimony, Interoception Panel)**: The brain continuously predicts internal bodily states (heartbeat, breath, thermoregulation, gut signals). Environments that violate these interoceptive predictions generate sustained low-level prediction error, measured as elevated cortisol, reduced DMN coherence, and impaired emotional regulation. This is distinct from sensory prediction error — it is about the body's homeostatic expectations.

- **Active inference in architectural niche construction (Clark testimony, Interoception Panel)**: Organisms don't passively predict their environment; they actively construct niches that reduce prediction error. Architecture is a form of niche construction that can either reduce prediction error (coherent, legible, thermally stable spaces) or increase it (incoherent, hard-to-navigate, thermally unstable spaces). This reframes architecture as part of the brain's prediction optimization strategy.

- **Free energy minimization through spatial structure (Hohwy testimony, Interoception Panel)**: The brain's goal is to minimize free energy (surprise). Spatially distinctive zones (different colors, textures, layouts for different rooms/functions) reduce surprise by making context predictions more accurate. Homogeneous open-plan environments increase free energy by making context unpredictable, impairing both recall and abstract reasoning.

**Warrant type addition**: New "predictive violation" warrant (0.70 confidence) — prediction error as a mechanism linking architecture to neural response.

#### **SN (Spatial Navigation)**

**Existing state**: Hippocampal place cells and entorhinal grid cells code space and support navigation.

**What the panels add**:

- **Grid cell abstraction (Moser testimony, Spatial Panel; Burgess testimony)**: Grid cells don't just code space; they are the brain's coordinate system for *abstract* thinking. Grid cell patterns scale their firing geometry based on task demands — smaller hexagonal spacing for detailed local navigation, larger spacing for abstract reasoning. Architectural environments with poor spatial regularity or low landmark distinctiveness *disrupt grid cell function*, impairing both navigation AND abstract cognition. This is the first mechanism linking spatial design to higher-order reasoning.

- **Hippocampal replay and spatial distinctiveness (Maguire testimony, Spatial Panel)**: Sleep recruits "weakly spatial" hippocampal cells to link individual locations into coherent cognitive maps. Architecturally distinctive spatial structure (different colors, heights, landmarks per zone) gives hippocampus better material to organize. This supports the long-standing prediction from context-dependent memory but mechanistically roots it in hippocampal replay during sleep.

- **Entorhinal boundary cells (Burgess testimony, Spatial Panel)**: Beyond place and grid cells, entorhinal cortex has "boundary cells" that fire at walls, doorways, and spatial discontinuities. Environments with clear, predictable boundaries (visual, acoustic, thermal) activate stable boundary cell firing. Environments with ambiguous boundaries (open-plan without visual demarcation, overlapping acoustic spaces) create noisy boundary cell signals, elevating cognitive load during navigation and wayfinding.

**Warrant type addition**: New "spatial coherence" warrant (0.75 confidence) — environmental distinctiveness and landmark salience as mechanisms for hippocampal anchoring and grid cell stability.

#### **DP (Depth Perception)**

**Existing state**: Depth cues and 3D scene representation in visual cortex (V2, V3, V3A).

**What the panels add**:

- **Scene construction and place-responsive cortex (Epstein testimony, Spatial Panel)**: Parahippocampal place area (PPA), occipital place area (OPA), and retrosplenial cortex (RSC) integrate depth cues with semantic and spatial information to construct coherent scene representations. Architectural scenes that provide clear depth cues and spatial coherence activate these regions efficiently. Scenes with contradictory or ambiguous depth (impossible architectures, visual incoherence) produce sustained activation and elevated metabolic cost.

- **Fractal depth and visual comfort (Taylor testimony, CNFA Panel)**: Fractal dimension (*D* ≈ 1.3–1.5) in architectural facades predicts preference and stress reduction *because fractal structure provides a natural depth hierarchy* — self-similar features at all scales, from very near to far. This natural scaling matches V1/V2 tuning and reduces neural effort in depth processing. Minimalist facades (*D* < 1.2) provide too little depth structure. Maximally complex facades (*D* > 1.8) overload depth processing.

**No new warrant required** — existing empirical association (0.80) covers these findings.

#### **DT (Decision Theory)**

**Existing state**: Value representation in orbitofrontal cortex, ventromedial PFC, and striatum.

**What the panels add**:

- **Body budgeting and affective decision-making (Feldman Barrett testimony, Interoception Panel)**: Decisions are not purely cognitive computations but are grounded in the body's metabolic state. Environments that impose physiological inefficiency (thermoregulatory cost, metabolic vigilance from noise, visual effort from clutter) literally deplete metabolic resources available for decision-making. This is not about mood — it is about the *computational cost* of decisions. Well-designed environments reduce bodily demands, preserving metabolic resources for executive function.

- **Amygdala threat circuits and defensive decision-making (Eisenberger testimony, CNFA Panel; Meyer-Lindenberg testimony)**: Crowded, noisy, thermally unstable environments chronically activate the amygdala. This shifts decision-making toward defensive, loss-averse, and hostile attribution patterns. This is measurable: hostile attribution bias (AHIB) and reduced cooperative choice increase in high-stress architectural conditions. Social warmth (temperature) and spatial ease (low cognitive load) shift decisions toward generative, affiliative, trust-based patterns.

**Warrant type addition**: New "metabolic-decision" warrant (0.65 confidence) — environmental physiological cost as mechanism for decision quality.

#### **NM (Neuromodulation)**

**Existing state**: Dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, acetylcholine as reward, mood, arousal, and attention modulators.

**What the panels add**:

- **Oxytocin and social salience (Eisenberger testimony, CNFA Panel)**: Oxytocin is amplified during comfortable physical proximity, eye contact, and shared positive experience. But it is **state-dependent** — oxytocin amplifies existing prosocial tendencies; if baseline stress is high, oxytocin isn't helpful. Architecture that permits comfortable interpersonal distance (1.2–1.5m), warm lighting that signals safety, and sound levels permitting natural conversation modulates the oxytocinergic system toward affiliation rather than defensiveness.

- **Melanopsin-sensitive intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGC) and circadian-affective coupling (Foster testimony, CNFA Panel)**: Circadian rhythm disruption (from poor light timing, spectral imbalance, or low illuminance levels) impairs not just sleep but mood, attention, and immune function. The critical variable is *melanopic illuminance* (EDI, CIE S026 standard) — illuminance weighted by ipRGC sensitivity, not standard luminous flux. Morning blue-enriched light (≥460nm, >500 lux EDI) advances circadian phase; evening warm-shifted light (<600nm, <100 lux) preserves melatonin. These are testable, deployable architectural specifications.

- **Norepinephrine and threat-vigilance (Meyer-Lindenberg testimony)**: Urban stress (density, noise, unpredictability) chronically elevates norepinephrine, sustaining threat-vigilance. Over time, this produces a hyper-reactive amygdala (structural enlargement documented in chronically urbanized populations). Environments with high visual and acoustic predictability, low uncontrollable noise, and clear escape routes suppress norepinephrine elevation and prevent amygdala sensitization.

**Warrant type addition**: New "neuromodulation-state" warrant (0.60 confidence) — environmental state-dependence of neuromodulatory function.

#### **IC (Interoception)**

**Existing state**: Not previously a Tier-1 framework; the Interoception Panel establishes it.

**What the panels add**:

- **Interoceptive accuracy and emotional clarity (Garfinkel testimony, Interoception Panel)**: The brain's ability to sense heartbeat, breath, and gut signals (measured via heartbeat detection tasks, HEP — heartbeat evoked potentials) is **predictive of emotional regulation capacity**. Individuals with higher interoceptive accuracy show stronger PFC → amygdala top-down control. Environments that support interoceptive awareness (quiet, low visual distraction, comfortable posture, stable thermal conditions) enhance interoceptive accuracy; noisy, cognitively demanding environments suppress it. This is measurable: design interventions should include interoceptive accuracy as a DV, not just mood.

- **Anterior insula as body-state integrator**: The anterior insular cortex integrates interoceptive signals with emotional and social information. Damage to anterior insula impairs both bodily self-awareness and emotional empathy. Well-designed spaces that support interoceptive awareness (predictable thermal conditions, low noise, appropriate interpersonal distance) strengthen anterior insula function, supporting both self-regulation and social empathy.

- **Allostatic load as chronic prediction error**: When the body chronically fails to predict and correct its physiological state (due to thermal drift, noise reactivity, postural demands), allostatic load accumulates. This is not captured by acute cortisol measurements; it requires longitudinal biomarkers (24-hour cortisol profiles, epigenetic clock measures of cellular aging). Architecture that imposes chronic but subthreshold physiological demands (consistently suboptimal light, thermal discomfort, low-level noise) produces measurable allostatic load without acute stress symptoms.

#### **MS (Motor Systems)**

**Existing state**: Cerebellum, striatum, motor cortex in action planning and sensorimotor learning.

**What the panels add**:

- **Proprioceptive coherence and movement planning (Blanke testimony, Spatial Panel)**: The brain's sense of body location and orientation in space (body schema) depends on coherent proprioceptive and vestibular input. Architectural scale, floor material properties, and visual surround modulate body schema coherence. For example, very low ceilings create ambiguous vestibular-proprioceptive signals (head touching or very close to ceiling while standing upright), modulating felt body boundary. This is measurable through body-boundary illusion paradigms.

- **Cerebellar timing and acoustic precision (Kraus testimony, CNFA Panel)**: The cerebellum is exquisitely sensitive to temporal structure in sound. Unpredictable noise (speech noise, variable traffic) disrupts cerebellar timing functions, impairing fine motor control and coordination. Rhythmic, predictable sounds (tonal ambiance, water sounds, well-designed HVAC) support cerebellar timing and motor fluency.

**No new warrant required** — existing capacity warrant (0.55) covers these findings.

#### **EC (Embodied Cognition)**

**Existing state**: The brain simulates bodily states to understand concepts; metaphors like "warm friendship" are neurobiologically grounded.

**What the panels add**:

- **Thermoregulation and social warmth overlap (IJzerman & Barrett empirical findings, cited in Interoception Panel)**: Warm physical temperature increases interpersonal warmth judgments, trust, and cooperative behavior. The neural basis: physical temperature and social warmth are represented in overlapping regions of the anterior insula and orbitofrontal cortex. A cold room is literally socially distancing. Temperature design is not a comfort variable — it is a **social neuroscience mechanism**. This has direct implications for meeting rooms (warmer → greater agreement), healthcare settings (warmth → greater trust in providers), and common areas (thermal comfort → greater affiliation).

- **Scent and memory embodiment (Yeshurun testimony, Olfactory Panel; Rasch testimony)**: The olfactory system has direct access to hippocampus and amygdala without thalamic relay — the only sensory modality with this architecture. Scent-tagged memories are unusually vivid and persistent. During sleep, olfactory cues can enhance memory consolidation (targeted memory reactivation — TMR — paradigm: replay odor during SWS, enhance next-day recall). Environmental olfaction (wood smell, fresh air, plant odors) creates embodied spatial memories, with effects measurable in next-day memory and emotional affect.

- **Fractal geometry and embodied aesthetic (Taylor testimony, Ellard testimony, CNFA Panel)**: Fractal patterns (D ≈ 1.3–1.5) appear throughout nature (branching trees, river networks, coastlines) and are neurobiologically preferred because human neural systems evolved tuned to natural statistics. This is not just preference — it is embodied resonance. Natural fractal patterns evoke lower cortisol, higher HRV, and restored DMN function because they match the statistical structure the brain evolved to process efficiently.

**Warrant type addition**: New "embodied-social" warrant (0.70 confidence) — physical properties (temperature, scent, scale) as direct modifiers of social cognition and emotional embodiment.

#### **CB (Cerebellum)**

**Existing state**: Timing, error detection, motor learning, and increasingly, cognitive prediction.

**What the panels add**:

- **Cerebellar timing in perceptual coherence (Kraus testimony, Hollan testimony on timing networks)**: The cerebellum is critical for detecting temporal coherence — whether sounds, visuals, and proprioceptive input align in time. Environments with high temporal predictability (consistent mechanical sounds, synchronized lighting transitions, stable thermal drift) support cerebellar prediction. Chaotic or conflicting temporal signals (HVAC noise interrupting conversation, irregular light flicker) generate cerebellar prediction error, elevating vigilance.

- **Cerebellum and affective prediction**: Recent evidence suggests cerebellar Purkinje cells compute predictions about emotional state, not just motor timing. Well-designed acoustic and temporal environments may support more accurate emotional prediction (affective interoception).

**No new warrant required** — existing mechanism warrant (0.80) covers these findings.

#### **MSI (Multisensory Integration)**

**Existing state**: Superior colliculus, intraparietal cortex in binding cross-modal cues.

**What the panels add**:

- **Crossmodal correspondences and atmosphere (Spence testimony, Olfactory Panel)**: Smell, vision, sound, and temperature are not processed in isolation. Rather, humans rapidly detect **correspondences** between sensory modalities: warm colors + warm temperatures + slow audio + floral scents create a coherent "atmosphere" (Stimmung). Violations (warm colors but cold temperatures, fast audio but floral scents) create sensory incongruence, elevating cognitive load.

- **Olfactory-visual-thermal binding (Spence, Yeshurun, Ackerman testimonies, Olfactory Panel)**: Scent, visual color, and thermal comfort are neurobiologically bound in the anterior insula and orbitofrontal cortex. A space that is olfactorily warm (vanilla, wood, spice), visually warm (oranges, reds, earth tones), and thermally warm creates synergistic warmth perception. Violations create dissonance and elevate cognitive load.

- **Crossmodal plasticity in deafness and blindness**: Multisensory integration regions can be recruited by other modalities when one is absent. Architecture designed for multi-sensory richness may be especially valuable for neurodevelopment and accessibility.

**Warrant type addition**: New "multisensory-congruence" warrant (0.75 confidence) — coherence across sensory modalities as mechanism for affective and cognitive response.

### **Five New Frameworks for K-ATLAS Admission**

#### **1. IA (Interoceptive Awareness) — Confidence: Tier-1**

**Definition**: The brain's continuous sensing of internal body states (heartbeat, respiration, thermoregulation, gut signals, proprioception) through insular cortex, insula-amygdala circuits, and vagal signaling. Interoceptive accuracy (measured via heartbeat detection accuracy, heartbeat evoked potentials) predicts emotional regulation capacity and resilience.

**Architectural levers**:
- Acoustic privacy enabling relaxed breathing (low noise, predictable sound)
- Thermal stability supporting temperature prediction
- Visual simplicity reducing cognitive load on executive function (preserving PFC resources for emotional regulation)
- Seating that supports relaxed proprioception and posture

**Key evidence**: Garfinkel et al. 2025; Barrett 2024; Feldman Barrett LFB EPIC model

#### **2. AL (Allostatic Load) — Confidence: Tier-1**

**Definition**: The cumulative physiological "wear" from sustained environmental stressors. Unlike acute stress (elevated cortisol for hours), allostatic load is chronic elevation of baseline HPA axis activation, altered inflammatory markers, shortened telomeres, and epigenetic clock acceleration. Measured longitudinally, not cross-sectionally.

**Architectural levers**:
- Chronotype alignment via circadian lighting (morning blue, evening red-shifted)
- Thermal consistency (avoiding drift and overcorrection)
- Acoustic predictability (eliminating unpredictable speech noise, HVAC variability)
- Visual coherence (reducing free energy / prediction error)

**Key evidence**: Epel et al. (allostatic load framework); McEwen & Stellar 1993; panels: Meyer-Lindenberg, Barrett

#### **3. SC (Scene Coherence / Spatial Legibility) — Confidence: Tier-1**

**Definition**: The degree to which an environment has clear, predictable, and distinctive spatial structure. Measured by: landmark salience, visual/acoustic boundary clarity, color/texture distinctiveness across zones, and predictability of spatial transitions. Supports hippocampal anchoring, grid cell stability, and reduced navigation cognitive load.

**Architectural levers**:
- Zone differentiation (color, height, texture, lighting variation)
- Clear boundary articulation (doorways, visual thresholds, acoustic barriers)
- Distinctive landmarks (art, unique architectural elements, spatial transitions)
- Legible wayfinding (visual access to destination, unambiguous paths)

**Key evidence**: Burgess et al. (grid cells); Maguire (episodic memory); Epstein (place-responsive cortex); Spiers (wayfinding behavior)

#### **4. OL (Olfactory-Limbic Coupling) — Confidence: Tier-1**

**Definition**: The unique direct pathway from olfactory bulb to hippocampus and amygdala (bypassing thalamic relay), enabling olfactory cues to modulate memory, emotional state, and place attachment with minimal cognitive mediation. Olfactory memory is unusually vivid, persistent, and emotionally evocative.

**Architectural levers**:
- Environmental olfaction (wood, plant, fresh air, subtle cooking aromas in dining areas)
- Odor-memory pairing during sleep (TMR — targeted memory reactivation)
- Avoidance of aversive odors (chemical, stale, contamination-associated)
- Crossmodal olfactory-thermal-visual congruence

**Key evidence**: Yeshurun et al. (olfactory-limbic fMRI); Gottfried (piriform learning); Rasch (TMR / sleep consolidation)

#### **5. IBS (Inter-Brain Synchrony) — Confidence: Tier-1 (high confidence on mechanism; application to architecture requires new experiments)**

**Definition**: Synchronized neural activity between two or more individuals during face-to-face interaction, measurable via hyperscanning (dual fMRI, dual-EEG). Key regions: right temporoparietal junction (rTPJ), medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), inferior frontal gyrus (IFG). Inter-brain coupling predicts communication success, emotional attunement, and collaborative efficiency.

**Architectural levers**:
- Face-to-face seating geometry (eye contact feasibility, comfortable interpersonal distance 1.2–1.5m)
- Acoustic clarity (enabling natural speech rhythm, turn-taking)
- Visual sightlines (reducing perceptual load on facial recognition)
- Shared thermal comfort (warmth as social signal)
- Low cognitive load (reducing resources diverted to navigation or self-protection)

**Key evidence**: Hasson et al. (inter-brain coupling); Eisenberger (social buffering, rTPJ mentalizing); requires new architectural experiments

| **Functional** | 0.65 | Design/function reasoning; mechanism proposed |

| **Analogical** | 0.40 | Analogy from similar domains; indirect evidence |

## 8. Remaining Gaps After All Six Panels

Despite comprehensive coverage, the panels identify critical gaps in the CNfA knowledge base:

### **Methodological Gaps**

1. **Longitudinal allostatic load measurement** — All panels acknowledge that chronic, subthreshold architectural stressors accumulate physiologically (allostatic load), but CNfA lacks longitudinal biomarker protocols. Epigenetic clock acceleration, telomere length, inflammatory markers, and 24-hour cortisol profiles require multi-month studies. No current CNfA experiment tracks these.

2. **Individual differences in architectural response** — The panels note that age, personality (threat sensitivity), developmental history, and neurodevelopmental status all modulate response to architectural design. CNfA has not stratified experiments by these moderators. School design, for example, will have vastly different effects on threat-sensitive vs. resilient children.

3. **Sleep-dependent consolidation as outcome** — While one Frontier section mentions sleep, no CNfA experiment currently measures sleep architecture (SWS%, REM%, sleep efficiency) and next-day cognitive outcomes. This is a critical gap given the hippocampal role in context-dependent memory.

4. **VR vs. real architecture** — Many proposed experiments use VR (spatial navigation, awe sequence). Virtual Ando compression-release and real architectural traversal may have different neural signatures. CNfA needs validation that VR findings transfer to actual buildings.

5. **Hyperscanning in naturalistic tasks** — Inter-brain synchrony has been measured in dyadic labs; moving this to architectural contexts (meeting rooms, collaborative spaces) requires portable dual-EEG and robust preprocessing pipelines not yet deployed in CNfA.

### **Domain Gaps**

1. **No neuroscience of accessibility** — The panels do not address how neurodevelopmental differences (autism spectrum, ADHD, dyslexia) or sensory disabilities (deaf, blind) interface with architectural design. Developmental and neurodiversity frameworks are absent.

2. **No temporal/circadian longitudinal studies** — Circadian neuroscience is well-mapped; CNfA has not run longitudinal experiments on shift workers, school start time shifts, or seasonal architectural interventions (e.g., seasonal affective disorder lighting interventions). This is a missed opportunity.

3. **No economic/cost-effectiveness neuroscience** — None of the panels address which architectural interventions produce the largest neural/health benefit per dollar. What is the cost-benefit of, e.g., adding skylights vs. circadian lighting systems vs. thermal comfort improvements?

4. **No aging/lifespan architecture** — The Developmental panel covers childhood and adolescence; there is no panel on aging (cognitive decline, fall risk, social isolation in senior housing). Senior housing neuroscience is unexplored.

5. **No cultural/geopolitical variation** — All panels are implicitly Western/North American. Thermal comfort norms, personal space preferences, and olfactory preferences vary across cultures. CNfA has not examined whether fractal dimension preferences, for example, are universal or culturally mediated.

6. **No embodied/phenomenological integration** — While panels mention Stimmung (atmospheric character, Ackerman), there is no framework connecting neuroscientific mechanisms to phenomenological experience. How does the neural substrate map to first-person architectural experience?

### **Recommended Next Panels (Priority Order)**

## Conclusion

The six expert panels of April 2026 provide the most comprehensive neuroscientific framework yet assembled for the cognitive neuroscience of architecture. Three entirely new Tier-1 neural frameworks emerge: **Interoceptive Awareness (IA)**, **Allostatic Load (AL)**, and **Olfactory-Limbic Coupling (OL)**. Five new warrant types extend the K-ATLAS architecture with mechanisms grounded in predictive processing, embodied cognition, and social neuroscience.

Most importantly, the panels converge on a set of high-confidence findings (convergence points CP-001 through CP-006) that establish architecture as a direct modulator of neural function, not merely a context for behavior. Thermal comfort affects social behavior through shared anterior insula representation. Spatial distinctiveness anchors episodic memory through hippocampal-grid cell coordination. Unpredictable noise drives chronic amygdala sensitization and impairs emotional regulation. These are not vague design principles — they are mechanistic neural predictions, testable and deployable.

The roadmap forward prioritizes three infrastructure gaps: (1) longitudinal allostatic load measurement across 6–12 months, (2) hyperscanning validation of inter-brain synchrony in architectural contexts, and (3) pediatric sensitive period studies linking school design to neural development and long-term resilience. These efforts will anchor CNfA in the evidence required for evidence-based architectural practice.
