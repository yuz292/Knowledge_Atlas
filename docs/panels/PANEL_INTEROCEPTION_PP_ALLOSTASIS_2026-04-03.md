## Panel Testimony

### 1. Sarah Garfinkel — Interoceptive Accuracy and the Heartbeat as Neural Window

**Context**: Garfinkel's research operationalizes interoceptive awareness—the conscious perception of bodily signals—and relates it to emotional processing, decision-making, and resilience. Her work with heartbeat-evoked potentials (HEPs) provides a direct electrophysiological window into insular cortex function during interoceptive processing.

The anterior insular cortex is recognized across neuroscience as the primary sensory cortex for interoception (Craig, 2009, 2015). What is often missed in architectural research is the distinction between *interoceptive accuracy* (objective performance on heartbeat counting tasks), *interoceptive sensibility* (self-reported perception of bodily signals), and *interoceptive awareness* (metacognitive accuracy about one's own interoceptive performance). These three dimensions dissociate empirically and neurobiologically, and all three matter for how the built environment is experienced and remembered.

The heartbeat-evoked potential—a cortical response time-locked to each heartbeat, measurable via EEG—serves as an implicit marker of ongoing interoceptive cortical processing. When humans enter a space that generates prediction errors (wayfinding confusion, unexpected temperature shifts, clashing architectural scales), HEP amplitude increases as a function of emotional salience and uncertainty. This is not merely a correlate; it reflects the brain's active inference process: comparing sensory predictions with interoceptive signals and upweighting precision when conflict is detected.

**What CNfA is missing**: Current K-ATLAS models treat interoception as a general "homeostatic" input but do not distinguish between different channels of interoceptive signal (cardiac, respiratory, thermal, gastric, proprioceptive). Architectural features have heterogeneous effects on these channels. A building's thermal properties affect skin temperature and core temperature tracking through different neural pathways (cf. Craig's thermoreceptive mapping). HVAC noise and air quality affect respiratory interoception and CO₂ sensitivity. These should be modeled as separate stimulus dimensions in K-ATLAS, not collapsed into "interoceptive load."

Moreover, CNfA has not operationalized how *chronic* interoceptive prediction error (e.g., repeated thermal surprises, persistent indoor air quality mismatches, postural demands that violate proprioceptive expectations) degrades interoceptive accuracy over weeks and months. This is a critical gateway into the allostatic load mechanism I describe below.

**Experimental proposals**:

1. **Heartbeat-Evoked Potential and Spatial Prediction Error**: Measure HEP amplitude in two conditions: (a) navigation in a well-organized, predictable wayfinding environment; (b) navigation in a space with subtle architectural incoherence (e.g., inconsistent column rhythm, perspective breaks, unexpected level changes). Hypothesis: HEP amplitude increases in condition (b) as a function of prediction error magnitude, mediated by supramarginal gyrus (SMG) and anterior insula connectivity. Within-subject correlations: individual interoceptive accuracy (baseline heartbeat counting) predicts the magnitude of HEP enhancement to prediction error.

2. **Thermal Interoception and Allostasis**: Combine high-resolution thermal imaging of skin surface with interoceptive accuracy testing in rooms with subtle (0.5–2°C) deviations from predicted thermoregulation. Use fMRI to track insular cortex (particularly posterior insula, which processes thermoceptive signals) and hypothalamus (which drives allostatic adjustment) responses to thermal mismatch. Prediction: individuals with poor baseline interoceptive accuracy show larger hypothalamic response to thermal surprises, indicating compensatory allostatic effort.

3. **Respiratory Interoception in Variable Air Quality**: Use end-tidal CO₂ (ETCO₂) measurement and retrospective verbal reports to assess respiratory interoceptive accuracy in spaces with dynamically varying air quality (CO₂, humidity, particle count). Measure cortisol and heart rate variability as allostatic markers. Hypothesis: mismatch between predicted and actual air quality predicts reduced respiratory interoceptive accuracy and elevated allostatic load.

**Specialist consultation**: Recommend consulting with **Hugo Critchley** (University of Sussex), whose work on interoception and emotion regulation provides complementary perspectives on how chronic architectural stress impairs emotional resilience through interoceptive desynchronization.

### 2. Lisa Feldman Barrett — Body Budgeting and the Energetic Cost of Spatial Prediction

**Context**: Barrett's construction of emotion theory (2006, 2017) reconceives emotion not as a set of innate discrete programs but as a construction process in which the brain predicts the internal energy state of the body (the "body budget") and issues visceromotor commands to regulate it. This framework is uniquely powerful for understanding how built environments tax metabolic resources.

The human brain does not sit at the top of a processing hierarchy, receiving sensory data and issuing motor commands. Instead, the brain is primarily an organ of *allostatic regulation*—it generates predictions about what the body will need in the near future and proactively adjusts metabolism, autonomic tone, immune signaling, and motivated behavior to meet those anticipated demands. Emotions, in this framework, are the subjective correlates of these allostatic predictions: fear feels like arousal because the brain has predicted that your body will need energy mobilization resources; fatigue feels depleting because the brain has exhausted its metabolic reserves.

The anterior insular cortex, which Garfinkel studies with HEPs, is the visceral sensory cortex. But emotion construction requires more than sensory reception; it requires the *prediction* of what those sensory signals mean in terms of body budget. This is where agranular visceromotor cortices—including medial prefrontal cortex, orbital frontal cortex, and cingulate cortex—issue downward commands. In Barrett's Embodied Predictive Interoception Coding (EPIC) model, these regions implement a hierarchical generative model of body state. When the predicted body state matches the actual sensory signals from the periphery, the system is in homeostasis and emotion is minimal. When prediction and sensation mismatch, prediction error signals cascade upward, driving attentional and motor corrections.

**What CNfA is missing**: Current K-ATLAS models incorporate "attention restoration" (borrowed from Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989) and "stress" as high-level categories, but do not model the *metabolic specificity* of architectural demands. A building's lighting affects circadian cycles and cortisol oscillations; thermal variability increases thermogenesis and glucose utilization; poor wayfinding increases navigational cognitive load, which is energetically expensive (prefrontal cortex activation consumes disproportionate glucose); sound complexity increases arousal and sympathetic tone; social density affects oxytocin and cortisol in opposite directions depending on social expectancy.

CNfA also does not model how *chronic* low-level prediction errors accumulate into what I call "metabolic debt." A person working in a building with variable temperature, unpredictable acoustic surprises, and wayfinding uncertainty does not experience dramatic acute stress responses. Instead, their brain continuously makes small allostatic adjustments: slightly elevated cortisol, slightly reduced parasympathetic tone, slightly increased prefrontal metabolic demand. Over weeks, this accumulates into reduced glucose availability, impaired prefrontal function, worse decision-making, and feeling of exhaustion—a state I have called "body budget depletion." It is a form of chronic allostatic load that builds without any single dramatic stressor.

**Experimental proposals**:

1. **Metabolic Cost of Prediction Errors**: In an fMRI scanner, expose subjects to two architectural environments (block design): (a) a coherent, high-predictability space (consistent columns, clear sightlines, stable thermal and acoustic environment); (b) a space with low-level, continuous prediction errors (subtle irregularities in structure, intermittent acoustic surprises, thermal drift). Measure: prefrontal cortex (especially medial PFC and OFC) metabolic demands via BOLD signal; simultaneous pupil diameter as a proxy for arousal; saliva cortisol pre/post session. Hypothesis: prediction error environment shows higher metabolic demand in visceromotor cortices and higher post-session cortisol, proportional to the accumulated magnitude of prediction errors.

2. **Body Budget Depletion and Cognitive Performance**: Conduct a 4-week randomized controlled trial where healthy adults work in two office environments: (a) optimized for interoceptive congruence (stable temperature, low acoustic variability, clear spatial logic); (b) baseline office (variable temperature, acoustic surprises, wayfinding friction). Measure weekly: (i) self-report fatigue and mood; (ii) objective cognitive tasks (Wisconsin Card Sorting Test, delay discounting); (iii) fasting blood glucose, cortisol diurnal rhythm, heart rate variability. Hypothesis: the baseline office group shows degraded metabolic profile (lower glucose, flattened cortisol rhythm, reduced HRV) and impaired prefrontal task performance, consistent with body budget depletion.

3. **Thermal Prediction and Thermoregulatory Efficiency**: Measure whole-body energy expenditure (via indirect calorimetry) in two thermal conditions: (a) stable, predictable temperature; (b) unpredictable temperature fluctuations (±2°C). Simultaneously measure insular cortex and hypothalamus activation via fMRI. Prediction: unpredictable temperature requires higher thermoregulatory effort (increased energy expenditure) and activates hypothalamic allostatic centers more strongly than predictable temperature, despite comparable mean temperatures.

**Specialist consultation**: Recommend **Janice Kiecolt-Glaser** (Ohio State University College of Medicine), whose work on stress, immune function, and wound healing provides mechanistic evidence for how chronic environmental stress impairs physiological resilience.

### 3. Jacob Hohwy — Predictive Processing, Precision Weighting, and Spatial Incoherence

**Context**: Hohwy's predictive processing theory emphasizes that the brain is fundamentally a Bayesian model that minimizes surprise by generating downward predictions and using sensory prediction errors to update internal models. His account differs from Clark's (see below) in emphasizing the encapsulated nature of cortical prediction—the cortex is relatively insulated from the body and world, generating models of them rather than transparently interacting with them.

Architecture is, in Hohwy's framework, a system for *modulating precision weights* on sensory prediction error signals. Every sensory error has both a magnitude and a weight; the brain determines the weight based on the reliability of the sensory channel and the salience of the stimulus. A prediction error about spatial layout (e.g., unexpected column placement) activates brain regions involved in attention and precision weighting—primarily the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex—which upweight that error signal's influence on model updating and behavior.

What makes architecture neurobiologically distinctive is that it generates *sustained*, *unavoidable* prediction errors at scales that force continuous model updating. Unlike a single misplaced object (which can be attended to and re-encoded), architectural incoherence—a facade rhythm that breaks mid-way, a ceiling height that violates proportional expectancy, a wall alignment that contradicts established spatial frames—generates repeated, inescapable errors. This chronic upweighting of attention and precision signals is energetically costly and, over time, becomes a source of allostatic load (see Barrett's testimony above).

**What CNfA is missing**: CNfA's Tier-1 framework for Predictive Processing (PP) is currently a stub. It does not operationalize: (1) the distinction between different types of prediction error (sensory vs. proprioceptive vs. thermal); (2) how precision weighting mechanisms (anterior cingulate, dorsolateral prefrontal) gate the influence of architectural errors; (3) the temporal dynamics of error accumulation (does a brief spatial incongruence produce a sharp error burst that fades, or a sustained error landscape that persists as long as the subject navigates the space?); (4) individual differences in prediction error tolerance, related to default mode network architecture and anterior insula interoceptive accuracy.

Moreover, CNfA has not modeled how *different architectural traditions* instantiate different prediction error landscapes. Classical architecture (symmetric, hierarchically organized, with high redundancy) minimizes prediction errors because spatial logic is highly predictable. Deconstructivist or fragmentary architecture deliberately violates prediction, generating higher sustained precision weighting demands. Neither is inherently "good" or "bad"—but they create measurably different neural costs, which should be quantified in a comprehensive model.

**Experimental proposals**:

1. **Anterior Cingulate and Precision Weighting to Spatial Incongruence**: Use fMRI with simultaneous behavioral measurements during navigation in two virtual environments: (a) classically coherent (symmetrical, hierarchical, high redundancy); (b) deconstructivist (asymmetric, layered, low redundancy). Measure: dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) activity as a function of spatial prediction error magnitude; dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) engagement as a function of error resolution difficulty. Hypothesis: dACC and dlPFC show higher sustained activation in the deconstructivist environment; individual differences in dACC responsiveness correlate with self-reported cognitive fatigue post-navigation.

2. **Temporal Dynamics of Prediction Error Accumulation**: Use ERP recordings during real-time virtual navigation, measuring the N1 and P3 components (which track initial sensory mismatch and attentional resource allocation). Systematically vary the density and regularity of architectural prediction errors (e.g., column spacing violations at different frequencies). Hypothesis: high-frequency, unpredictable errors produce sustained P3 enhancement (chronic precision upweighting) while regular, predictable errors show P3 habituation. Post-task cortisol and subjective fatigue correlate with total accumulated P3 area.

3. **Prediction Error Landscapes in Real Buildings**: Conduct a naturalistic fMRI study (using a portable 3T scanner or high-precision mobile MEG) in two real architectural spaces: (a) a building designed for spatial coherence; (b) a building with known wayfinding difficulties. Measure continuous brain activity during 30-minute naturalistic exploration. Map prediction error signals (measured via temporally resolved BOLD or spectral power) onto actual architectural features. Hypothesis: higher prediction errors localize to architectural features with low regularity, poor sightlines, or unexpected transitions.

**Specialist consultation**: Recommend **Anil Seth** (University of Sussex), whose theoretical work on predictive processing and consciousness explores how prediction errors cascade through hierarchical brain networks and become consciously experienced as surprise and discomfort.

### 4. Andy Clark — Extended Embodied Cognition and Cognitive Niche Construction

**Context**: Clark's predictive processing account differs from Hohwy's in one critical way: Clark emphasizes that the cognitive system is not confined to the brain, but extends into the body and the structured environment. The brain predicts and acts; the body and world are not passive targets but *active participants* in prediction minimization. Architecture, in Clark's framework, is a *cognitive niche*—a deliberately structured environment that reduces the computational demands on the brain by externalizing problem-solving into the world.

A well-designed building is one that "cooperates" with the brain's predictive machinery. Clear sightlines reduce the need for hippocampal spatial model updating because the brain can directly perceive layout. Consistent materiality and wayfinding cues reduce memory load. Intuitive acoustic design allows auditory predictions to be reliably met, reducing attention demands. In Clark's framework, a building that violates these principles is not merely unpleasant; it is *cognitively uneconomical*—it forces the brain to do work that good design could delegate to the environment.

Clark further emphasizes *active inference*: the brain does not passively predict and react to prediction error. Instead, the brain generates predictions and then *acts* to make those predictions come true—moving to afford better sensory evidence, restructuring the environment to match expectations. In architectural terms, people occupying a poorly designed space do not simply suffer prediction errors; they adapt their behavior, expending energy to circumvent the environment's failures (taking circuitous routes because wayfinding is unclear, seeking warmer/cooler zones because thermal design is poor, congregating in acoustically favorable areas). This active inference cost is a hidden source of allostatic load.

**What CNfA is missing**: CNfA's treatment of extended cognition is minimal. The framework focuses on internal neural mechanisms but does not adequately model how architectural features *externalize* cognitive load or, conversely, how incoherent architecture *internalizes* (brings into the brain) computational work that good design would externalize. CNfA should develop metrics for "cognitive niche quality"—a quantitative measure of how well a space reduces brain metabolic demands by providing stable, predictable, semantically rich cues.

Further, CNfA does not track the *behavioral active inference cost* of poor design: the extra energy expended by occupants to navigate, regulate temperature, and seek acoustic refuge. This cost is often invisible to traditional post-occupancy evaluation (POE) surveys, which focus on satisfaction ratings, but it is neurobiology.

Finally, CNfA has not engaged with Clark's concept of *cognitive offloading*: humans deliberately structure spaces to reduce cognitive load (e.g., organizing a desk so that task-relevant items are visible, reducing working memory demands). Buildings that support cognitive offloading should show reduced prefrontal activation during naturalistic task performance; buildings that require sustained attention and working memory should show elevated prefrontal load even during routine activities.

**Experimental proposals**:

1. **Cognitive Niche Quality and Prefrontal Metabolic Demand**: Develop a naturalistic protocol: healthy adults complete a standardized task set (e.g., finding specific locations, retrieving information from signage, completing a transaction at a reception desk) in two versions of an architectural space: (a) optimized for cognitive offloading (clear sightlines, intuitive wayfinding, visible information displays); (b) baseline (standard commercial design with moderate wayfinding friction). Use fNIRS (functional near-infrared spectroscopy) to measure dorsolateral prefrontal cortex activation during task performance. Hypothesis: cognitive niche quality predicts reduced dlPFC load; performance is equivalent, but neural cost differs.

2. **Active Inference and Behavioral Energy Expenditure**: Conduct a multi-day naturalistic study in two office environments with accelerometer data, continuous thermal imaging, and behavioral video annotation. Measure: (i) total movement distance (proxy for active inference search behavior); (ii) time spent in thermally stable zones (proxy for thermoregulatory active inference); (iii) congregating behavior in acoustically favorable areas. Hypothesis: poorly designed environments show higher total movement distance, more thermal zoning behavior, and more clustered acoustic refuge behavior—all signatures of active inference costs.

3. **Sightline Clarity and Hippocampal Load**: Use fMRI during virtual navigation in two conditions: (a) high sightline clarity (can see destination from most locations); (b) low clarity (destination obscured, must rely on memory and signage). Measure hippocampal and medial parietal cortex activation (regions involved in spatial model building). Hypothesis: low clarity conditions show higher hippocampal engagement (sustained spatial model updating) and worse post-task spatial memory. Sightline clarity acts as a cognitive offload, reducing hippocampal computational load.

**Specialist consultation**: Recommend **Vittorio Gallese** (University of Parma, creator of mirror neuron theory and embodied simulation frameworks), whose work on how we simulate others' actions in our own motor system extends to understanding how bodies simulate and embody architectural affordances.

### 5. Elissa Epel — Allostatic Load, Cellular Aging, and Environmental Stress Physiology

**Context**: Epel is a leading researcher in psychoneuroendocrinology and co-developer of the allostatic load framework (building on Bruce McEwen's foundational 1998 work). Allostatic load is the cumulative physiological wear accumulated from chronic or repeated activation of stress response systems. Unlike allostasis (the adaptive activation of stress hormones and autonomic tone to meet environmental demands), allostatic load represents *dysregulation*—when the brake on stress hormones is faulty, or when stressors are relentless, the system remains in a heightened state and tissues accumulate damage.

The biomarkers of allostatic load span multiple physiological systems: dysregulated cortisol rhythms (flattened diurnal curve, elevated evening cortisol); elevated inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP, TNF-alpha); dysregulated blood pressure and heart rate variability; impaired glucose metabolism and elevated fasting insulin; and accelerated cellular aging, measured via telomere shortening in peripheral blood mononuclear cells.

Epel's distinctive contribution is demonstrating that *psychological stress and perceived control* are primary drivers of allostatic load, sometimes more powerful than objective stressor intensity. An environment perceived as unpredictable and uncontrollable—even if the "stressor" is subtle—produces disproportionate allostatic load. This connects directly to the work above: architectural prediction errors create subjective unpredictability, which triggers allostatic mechanisms even in the absence of overtly noxious stimuli.

**What CNfA is missing**: CNfA's treatment of allostatic load is conceptual but not mechanistic. The framework acknowledges that "chronic stress affects cognition" but does not operationalize which architectural features map to which allostatic load biomarkers, and does not track the time-course of biomarker change in response to sustained architectural exposure. CNfA should develop a "allostatic load sensitivity catalog"—a compendium of architectural features (thermal variability, acoustic complexity, wayfinding friction, social density, etc.) ranked by their empirically measured impact on cortisol, HRV, inflammatory markers, and cellular aging.

Further, CNfA has not distinguished between *acute* allostatic activation (which is adaptive and needed for performance) and *chronic* allostatic load (which damages health). Some architectural challenges—navigating a complex building, social density at a conference—should produce brief allostatic activation that resolves once the challenge is met. But when challenges are chronic and inescapable, or when the building's design prevents habituation, allostatic load accumulates. This distinction requires longitudinal measurement, not cross-sectional snapshots.

Finally, CNfA must grapple with individual differences in allostatic load sensitivity. Early life adversity, trauma history, and genetic variations in glucocorticoid receptor sensitivity make some individuals exquisitely sensitive to architectural environmental stress, while others are more resilient. Designing buildings for neurodiversity means designing for multiple allostatic load sensitivity profiles.

**Experimental proposals**:

1. **Longitudinal Allostatic Load in Real Buildings**: Conduct a 12-week prospective cohort study with healthy adults assigned to work in two office buildings: (a) designed for physiological congruence (optimal lighting, stable thermal environment, acoustic design, clear wayfinding); (b) baseline commercial building (variable conditions, moderate wayfinding friction). Measure at baseline, 4 weeks, 8 weeks, and 12 weeks: (i) cortisol (morning, afternoon, evening via saliva collection); (ii) heart rate variability (24-hour Holter monitor); (iii) high-sensitivity CRP and IL-6 (blood draw); (iv) telomere length (if budget permits); (v) self-reported sleep quality and mood. Hypothesis: baseline building group shows accelerating allostatic load biomarkers over 12 weeks; cortisol fails to suppress in evening; HRV decreases; inflammatory markers increase. The optimized building group shows stable or improving biomarkers.

2. **Cortisol Response to Acute Architectural Unpredictability**: Use a single-session laboratory design. Healthy adults navigate two virtual environments matched for difficulty but differing in predictability: (a) predictable (architectural features follow discernible logic); (b) unpredictable (architectural features seem arbitrary). Measure cortisol at baseline, immediately post-navigation, and 30 minutes post-navigation; also measure subjective sense of control. Hypothesis: unpredictable environment produces elevated acute cortisol response and sustained elevation at 30-minute follow-up, mediated by reduced sense of control. Individual differences in baseline interoceptive accuracy (Garfinkel's measure) predict magnitude of cortisol response.

3. **Perceived Predictability as a Moderator of Thermal Stress**: Thermally challenge subjects (mild cold: 16°C for 30 minutes) in two conditions: (a) predictable thermal challenge (told the temperature in advance, know the duration); (b) unpredictable thermal challenge (temperature is a surprise, duration is uncertain). Measure: cortisol, heart rate, and skin conductance during challenge; self-reported discomfort; post-challenge blood glucose (as an indirect measure of metabolic allostatic load). Hypothesis: unpredictable thermal challenge produces greater cortisol, elevated sympathetic tone, and higher blood glucose despite identical thermal stimulus.

**Specialist consultation**: Recommend **Teresa Seeman** (UCLA School of Medicine, co-developer of allostatic load measurement frameworks) and **David Steptoe** (UCL Institute of Epidemiology & Health Care), whose work on psychosocial factors and physiological aging provides mechanistic evidence for how environmental stress accelerates aging processes.

## Cross-Panelist Synthesis

### Where Panelists Agree

1. **The Insula is Central**: All panelists agree that the anterior and posterior insular cortex represent a critical convergence point where bodily self-awareness, predictive error signals, and allostatic regulation meet. Garfinkel's HEP work measures insular activity directly; Barrett's body budgeting requires insular predictions; Hohwy's precision weighting involves insular-cingulate circuits; Clark emphasizes that embodied cognition is grounded in insular interoceptive signals; Epel's stress physiology is mediated through insular-hypothalamic connections. No other brain region plays this multi-functional role.

2. **Prediction Error is Costly**: All panelists agree that chronic mismatch between predicted and actual sensory input is metabolically expensive and, over time, becomes pathological. Whether framed as "interoceptive prediction error" (Garfinkel), "body budget depletion" (Barrett), "precision-weighted surprise" (Hohwy), "active inference cost" (Clark), or "allostatic load" (Epel), the underlying mechanism is identical: the brain must work harder to regulate itself, and this work accrues as systemic wear.

3. **Architecture Externalizes or Internalizes Cognitive Labor**: All panelists agree that well-designed environments reduce the brain's computational burden by providing stable, predictable, semantically rich cues (a point Clark emphasizes). Conversely, incoherent architecture forces the brain to do work—sustained attention, continuous model updating, active inference search—that good design would offload to the environment. This distinction is neurobiology, not aesthetics.

4. **Individual Differences Matter**: All panelists emphasize that the same architectural feature will produce different neural costs in different individuals. Interoceptive accuracy, baseline cortisol sensitivity, prefrontal working memory capacity, and experience-dependent expectations (how much you know about a building) all modulate architectural effects. Designing for a single "average" user is neurobiologically unjustified.

### Where Panelists Productively Disagree

1. **Encapsulation of the Cortex (Hohwy vs. Clark)**: Hohwy emphasizes that cortical predictions are relatively insulated from direct bodily and environmental input—the cortex builds *models* of body and world. Clark argues that this distinction is overstated—the brain is tightly coupled to body and environment through active inference loops. This disagreement matters for architecture: Hohwy suggests that poorly designed buildings force the brain into prolonged model-building (expensive, cognitively effortful); Clark suggests that the brain can directly use environmental cues to reduce model-building demands if design affords it. Both are true, but they emphasize different design strategies. Hohwy's view supports *semantic clarity* in architecture (unambiguous spatial logic that the brain can quickly model); Clark's view supports *perceptual richness* (direct sightlines, tactile materiality, acoustic intelligibility that reduce the need for modeling altogether).

2. **Emotion as Constructed vs. Emotion as Primitive (Barrett vs. Garfinkel)**: Barrett's constructed emotion theory argues that emotions like fear and sadness are not pre-wired programs but emergent constructions from interoceptive predictions and semantic/contextual factors. Garfinkel's research is more agnostic on this question—she focuses on how interoceptive accuracy predicts emotional responsiveness, without committing to whether emotions are constructed or primitive. This disagreement is relevant because it affects how we interpret architectural effects: does a poorly designed space *generate* negative emotion by failing body budget expectations (Barrett's view), or does it *amplify* pre-existing emotional tendencies by increasing interoceptive salience (Garfinkel's view)? Likely both—the disagreement is more about mechanism than outcome.

3. **Temporal Scope of Stress Measurement (Epel vs. Hohwy)**: Epel's allostatic load framework emphasizes long-term physiological changes (months to years of dysregulation). Hohwy's precision-weighting framework operates on millisecond-to-minute timescales (how quickly does the brain reweight sensory errors during navigation?). This creates ambiguity about *when* architectural stress effects emerge: acute (within minutes of exposure), medium-term (hours to weeks), or chronic (months to years). The evidence suggests all three are real—acute prediction errors produce immediate precision-weighting and autonomic responses (Hohwy's domain); sustained prediction errors deplete body budget reserves over hours (Barrett's domain); repeated or chronic architectural stress accumulates allostatic load over months (Epel's domain). CNfA should measure at all three timescales.

### Novel Experimental Topics Emerging from Synthesis

1. **The Interoceptive Habituation Problem**: One panelist noted that prediction error responses typically show *habituation*—repeated exposure to the same unexpected stimulus produces diminishing error signals and autonomic responses. This is neurobiologically adaptive (it allows us to habituate to unchanging stressors) but architecturally problematic. If a building's thermal variability or wayfinding friction produces initial prediction errors that habituate, users might *report* adaptation ("I've gotten used to it") while their physiology shows signs of unresolved allostatic load (elevated cortisol, poor sleep, inflammation). This decoupling of subjective adaptation and objective allostatic wear deserves investigation: do occupants of poorly designed buildings show *behavioral* habituation (fewer active inference attempts) while maintaining *physiological* dysregulation? This would explain why people "say they're fine" in dysfunctional buildings but show accelerated aging.

2. **Precision Weighting and Social Transmission of Predictive Models**: Architecture is not experienced in isolation; humans observe others navigating spaces and update their spatial predictions based on social cues. A building might generate high initial prediction errors, but if a guide or experienced user demonstrates a coherent spatial model, new occupants can adopt that model and reduce their own prediction error burden. This is a form of *social active inference*. Implications: buildings might be designed to support model sharing and social guidance, reducing the individual cognitive load. Conversely, buildings that isolate occupants prevent model sharing and leave each individual to resolve prediction errors alone—a design failure not captured by current K-ATLAS models.

3. **Prediction Error and Creative Insight**: While chronic, inescapable prediction errors are pathological, *moderate, manageable* prediction error might be beneficial for creative cognition. The salience-heightening effects of prediction error (elevated anterior cingulate, enhanced precision-weighted attention) might facilitate the kind of flexible, exploratory thinking needed for design work or complex problem-solving. A building designed for neuroticism (zero prediction error) might be dull and unchallenging, while a building with carefully calibrated, manageable prediction error might enhance cognitive engagement. This inversion of the "prediction error is bad" assumption deserves empirical test: at what level of prediction error do buildings transition from cognitively engaging to cognitively depleting?

4. **Body Budget Heterogeneity and Architectural Design Profiles**: Barrett's body budgeting framework assumes that all humans allocate metabolic resources similarly (spending more glucose on prefrontal engagement, for instance). But metabolic allocation varies. Some individuals are naturally sympathetic-dominant (energy mobilization), others parasympathetic-dominant (energy conservation). Some have energy-demanding cognitive styles (high working memory load), others are more perceptual. A building optimized for one metabolic profile might be suboptimal for another. This suggests that truly inclusive design requires buildings with *multiple architectural niche types*—spaces that support different energy management strategies and metabolic profiles. Currently, buildings are monolithic; future designs should be pluralistic.

## New Mechanisms for K-ATLAS

Based on panelists' testimony, the following mechanisms should be added to K-ATLAS at Tier 1 or Tier 2:

1. **Interoceptive Prediction Error (IPE)**: Distinct from general sensory prediction error, IPE captures mismatch between predicted and actual bodily signals (cardiac, respiratory, thermal, proprioceptive). Substrate: anterior and posterior insular cortex, with feedback to visceromotor cortices (mPFC, OFC, ACC). Strength d-value: **0.85** (strongly mechanistic, well-characterized neural substrate, but multiple subtypes of IPE may require independent models).

2. **Precision-Weighted Attention to Spatial Incongruence**: The degree to which the brain upweights attention and prediction error signals in response to architectural incoherence, mediated by dorsal anterior cingulate and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. Substrate: dACC, dlPFC, inferior parietal lobule. Strength d-value: **0.80** (well-characterized in predictive coding literature, but mapping from architecture to specific dACC neurons is underdeveloped).

3. **Body Budget Depletion**: Cumulative metabolic exhaustion from sustained prediction error correction and allostatic regulation. Substrate: medial prefrontal cortex, hypothalamus, anterior insula (interacting with glucose metabolism, cortisol signaling). Strength d-value: **0.75** (conceptually clear, biomarkers measurable, but causal mechanisms in humans remain partially speculative).

4. **Active Inference Search Behavior**: The energy expended by occupants to circumvent architectural friction through behavioral adaptation (extra movement, seeking thermal zones, acoustic refuges). This is a *behavioral* mechanism, not purely neural. Substrate: action selection systems (striatum, pre-supplementary motor area) and reward learning (ventromedial prefrontal cortex). Strength d-value: **0.70** (measurable behaviorally, but neural implementation in architectural contexts is understudied).

5. **Cognitive Niche Quality**: The degree to which architectural design externalizes cognitive labor, reducing the brain's model-building demands. Substrate: hippocampus, medial parietal cortex (spatial modeling), dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (working memory). Strength d-value: **0.75** (operationalizable, but currently lacks standardized measurement metrics).

6. **Allostatic Load Accumulation (Environmental Stress Physiology)**: The physiological wear accumulated from chronic or repeated mismatch between expected and actual environmental conditions. Substrate: hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, sympathetic nervous system, inflammatory signaling (IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP pathways). Biomarkers: cortisol, heart rate variability, inflammatory markers, telomere length. Strength d-value: **0.80** (well-characterized in stress physiology, measurable biomarkers, but causal architecture-to-biomarker links remain incomplete).

## New Bridge Warrant Types and Modifications

Current K-ATLAS warrant types range from d=1.0 (constitutive, definitional) to d=0.25 (theory-derived). Based on panelists' input, the following modifications are recommended:

1. **Interoceptive Congruence Warrant (d=0.75)**: A warrant that an architectural feature supports rather than violates bodily self-awareness and interoceptive accuracy. Example: "Thermal stability in this space supports interoceptive accuracy for thermoceptive signals → improved emotional regulation." Higher strength than analogical (d=0.40) because there is direct neural mechanism; lower than mechanistic (d=0.80) because interoceptive effects are context- and individual-dependent. **Justification**: This warrant is more specific and mechanistically supported than general analogical claims but less bulletproof than direct mechanistic warrants because interoceptive accuracy is multidimensional and individually variable.

2. **Prediction Error Magnitude Warrant (d=0.70)**: A warrant that an architectural feature produces measurable prediction error (assessed via behavioral, autonomic, or ERP measures) and that this error magnitude predicts downstream outcomes (fatigue, allostatic load). Example: "Irregular column spacing in this building's facade generates ongoing spatial prediction errors → elevated anterior cingulate precision-weighting signals → increased cortisol and reduced HRV over 8 weeks." **Justification**: Prediction error effects are empirically robust and neural substrates identified, but the specificity of error magnitude to particular architectural features is still being mapped.

3. **Body Budget Depletion Warrant (d=0.65)**: A warrant that an architectural environment requires sustained metabolic expenditure for allostatic regulation and that this expenditure accumulates into measurable physiological dysregulation. Example: "Unpredictable thermal environment requires chronic thermoregulatory effort → elevated baseline cortisol, flattened cortisol rhythm, reduced glucose regulation." **Justification**: This warrant is conceptually sound and biomarkers are measurable, but the causal chain from architecture to body budget depletion in humans remains largely correlational rather than experimental.

4. **Cognitive Niche Quality Warrant (d=0.70)**: A warrant that an architectural design successfully externalizes cognitive labor and supports information offloading, reducing the prefrontal metabolic demands for spatial modeling or wayfinding. Example: "High sightline clarity in this space reduces hippocampal spatial model-building load → lower prefrontal activation during task execution, despite equivalent task difficulty." **Justification**: This warrant is mechanistically sound and behaviorally measurable, but individual differences in cognitive offloading efficiency mean the effect size is variable.

5. **Modify Allostatic Load as a Core Warrant Category** (elevate from conceptual to mechanistic): Current K-ATLAS treats "stress" as a high-level category. Recommend breaking this into: (a) **Acute Allostatic Activation** (d=0.75), appropriate for buildings designed to motivate performance under time pressure (e.g., emergency response facilities); (b) **Chronic Allostatic Load Accumulation** (d=0.70), a maladaptive warrant for buildings that impose unavoidable, inescapable prediction errors and stress (to be avoided); (c) **Allostatic Load Resilience** (d=0.65), a warrant that a building includes features that support stress recovery and parasympathetic activation (biophilic elements, restorative spaces, social support structures).

## New Stimulus Properties for the CNfA Measurement Toolkit

Current CNfA stimulus properties focus on lighting (circadian entrainment), acoustic complexity, and social density. Based on panelists' input, add:

1. **Thermal Variability (°C, temporal frequency)**: Deviation from predicted/comfortable temperature and the frequency of temperature changes. Measured as: root-mean-square deviation from occupant's expected setpoint; spectral power in frequencies >0.5°C per 10 minutes. **Rationale**: Affects both thermal interoception (posterior insula) and thermoregulatory allostasis (hypothalamus). Currently unmeasured in architectural post-occupancy evaluation (POE).

2. **Wayfinding Friction (bits of uncertainty per unit time)**: Quantify the decision load and information requirements for navigation. Measured as: number of decision points per unit path length; entropy of sightline clarity; consistency of architectural signaling (do materiality, scale, rhythm consistently signal spatial logic?). **Rationale**: Directly affects hippocampal and medial parietal spatial model-building load; a primary driver of acute and chronic prediction errors.

3. **Proprioceptive Demand (postural variability required)**: The degree to which a space requires non-standard or variable postures for navigation or work. Examples: multiple floor levels without ramps, ceiling heights that vary non-predictably, floor materials with varying compliance. Measured as: coefficient of variation in postural angles required; unexpectedness of proprioceptive demands. **Rationale**: Affects proprioceptive interoception and active inference search costs; currently unmeasured.

4. **Acoustic Spectral Complexity**: More granular than "noise level." Decompose acoustic environment into: (a) predictable periodic components (e.g., HVAC hum); (b) random components (traffic, occupant noise); (c) unexpected transients (doors slamming, alarms). Measure as spectral entropy across frequency bands and temporal variability. **Rationale**: Affects respiratory interoception (CO₂/breathing-linked sounds), attention precision-weighting, and startle-related allostatic responses.

5. **Visual Prediction Error Density (prediction violations per visual scan)**: Quantify the degree to which visual sightlines contain unexpected elements. Examples: interruptions in expected symmetry, perspective breaks, scale incoherence. Measured as: number and magnitude of deviations from predicted visual patterns per saccade; information gain of unexpected visual features. **Rationale**: Directly drives V1-to-insula prediction error cascades and anterior cingulate precision-weighting.

6. **Interoceptive Awareness Affordances**: A qualitative/quantitative assessment of whether the space supports or disrupts bodily self-awareness. Examples: spaces with mirrors, spaces with comfortable seating that supports postural awareness, spaces with olfactory richness (affects interoception of chemical homeostasis). **Rationale**: Craig's work on the sentient self emphasizes that continuous interoceptive awareness is foundational to wellbeing and emotion regulation. Buildings should be evaluated for whether they support or undermine this awareness.

## Recommended Next Panels

This panel identified several domains of expertise that remain unaddressed:

1. **Developmental Neuroscience and Architectural Habituation**: How do children's brains build spatial models and interoceptive calibration in architectural environments? Do early experiences with incoherent architecture (e.g., overcrowded schools, noisy homes) durably change developmental trajectories of insular, hippocampal, and prefrontal development? This panel should include developmental neuroscientists (e.g., **Beatriz Luna**, Temple University, on prefrontal development; **Thalia Wheatley**, Dartmouth, on social brain development in contexts).

2. **Neuroarchitecture and Neurodiversity**: How do individuals with autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, anxiety disorders, and sensory processing disorder experience and respond neurobiologically to architectural features (acoustic complexity, thermal variability, spatial incoherence)? Current panel assumed a "neurotypical" baseline; future work must address heterogeneous sensory and interoceptive profiles. Recommend **Henry Markram** (brain-mind institute, Lausanne) on autism neurobiology; **Jasper Cohen** (Mount Sinai) on sensory processing disorder.

3. **Social Neuroscience and Architectural Affordances for Social Interaction**: How do architectural features (interpersonal distance, acoustic coupling, sightlines) affect mirror neuron activation, mentalizing, and social buffering? Current panel touched on this (Clark's active inference in social contexts, Eisenberger's social buffering from the prior panel) but did not address it deeply. Recommend **Vittorio Gallese** and **Marco Iacoboni** (on mirror neuron theory and embodied social cognition).

4. **Circadian Neurobiology and Architectural Light Design**: The prior panel (Hasson et al., 2025) included Foster's work on circadian entrainment via melanopsin/ipRGC cells. A deeper panel is needed on how *specific* architectural light quality (spectral composition, timing, spatial distribution) affects circadian phase, sleep architecture, and metabolic health. Recommend **Charles Czeisler** (Harvard Medical School, circadian neurobiology); **Samer Hattar** (NIH, ipRGC biology).

5. **Psychiatric and Neurological Applications**: How do the mechanisms identified in this panel (interoceptive prediction error, allostatic load, body budget depletion) model psychiatric and neurological conditions? Do people with depression show different insular-anterior cingulate prediction error circuits, making them exquisitely sensitive to architectural prediction errors? Do Parkinson's patients have compromised active inference and thus need architecturally simpler, more predictable environments? Recommend **John Krystal** (Yale, psychiatry neurobiology); **Todd Heatherton** (Dartmouth, social neuroscience of anxiety).

6. **Computational Modeling and Optimization**: This panel has identified numerous mechanisms and warrant types, but K-ATLAS currently lacks a computational engine that integrates them. A panel of computational neuroscientists and mathematical modelers should develop a quantitative, predictive model of how architectural features map to neural mechanisms, allostatic load biomarkers, and long-term health outcomes. Recommend **Karl Friston** (UCL, computational neuroscience); **Paul Cisek** (University of Montreal, decision neuroscience); **Russell Poldrack** (Stanford, computational models of learning and decision-making).

## Summary: The Integrated Model Emerging from Panel Testimony

The panel converges on an integrated model of how architectural experience operates at neural and physiological levels:

1. **Perception**: An occupant enters a built environment. The sensory features of the space (visual, acoustic, thermal, proprioceptive) are immediately compared against the brain's predictive models, which are based on past experience and current expectations.

2. **Prediction Error Generation**: Where actual sensory input mismatches predictions, prediction errors emerge. These errors signal to the anterior cingulate cortex and other precision-weighting systems that something unexpected requires attention. Simultaneously, interoceptive prediction errors (mismatch between predicted and actual bodily signals) activate the insular cortex.

3. **Allostatic Adjustment**: The brain's visceromotor systems (medial prefrontal cortex, hypothalamus, amygdala) issue downward allostatic commands: elevate cortisol, increase heart rate, suppress parasympathetic tone, mobilize glucose. These adjustments are adaptive in the short term—they prepare the body for environmental challenge. But they are metabolically expensive.

4. **Active Inference**: Simultaneously, the occupant's behavior changes. They search for wayfinding cues, seek thermal comfort, cluster in acoustically favorable zones, adjust posture. These active inference behaviors are attempts to resolve prediction error and match the environment to expected conditions. This behavioral effort is also metabolically expensive.

5. **Accumulation**: If prediction errors persist—if the architectural environment chronically violates expectations—allostatic adjustment and active inference become sustained. Over days and weeks, this accumulation manifests as: flattened cortisol rhythm, elevated baseline inflammation, reduced heart rate variability, impaired glucose metabolism, accelerated cellular aging. This is allostatic load.

6. **Cognitive Consequence**: As the body budget becomes depleted (Barrett), the prefrontal cortex, which is metabolically expensive, becomes underresourced. Executive function, working memory, emotional regulation, and decision quality all degrade. The occupant "has a hard time thinking straight" in the space—not because of acute stress, but because chronic architectural mismatch has left the body budget empty.

7. **Interoceptive Consequence**: Chronic interoceptive prediction error, sustained over weeks, appears to degrade interoceptive accuracy itself (Garfinkel's dimension). The individual becomes less aware of their own bodily signals, less able to track their own emotional state, more vulnerable to anxiety and depression. They also become more rigid in their behavioral responses, less able to flexibly adapt.

The design implication is clear: **buildings should be designed to minimize chronic, inescapable prediction error, especially interoceptive prediction error**. This means prioritizing thermal stability, acoustic clarity, spatial coherence, and wayfinding intelligibility. Not because these features are "aesthetically pleasing," but because they reduce a specific, measurable neural cost that accumulates over time into systemic physiological damage.
