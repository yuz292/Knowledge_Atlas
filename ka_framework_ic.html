Interoceptive-Constructionist Affect

Emotion is not a read-out from dedicated "emotion centres" but the brain's ongoing inference about the state of the body, assembled from bodily signals and context.

At a glance

In plain English. The Interoceptive-Constructionist account of affect holds that emotions are not hardwired responses triggered by fixed stimuli but constructed inferences the brain makes about what its body is doing and why — a view that joins William James's original insight to modern predictive-processing neuroscience.

Physiologically, three streams converge. Vagal afferents carry heart-rate, respiration, and visceral signals from the nucleus of the solitary tract up through the parabrachial nucleus and thalamus to the posterior, mid-, and anterior insula, which together build a progressively more integrated representation of bodily state. Anterior insula (AI) generates interoceptive predictions and compares them to the actual interoceptive signal, producing interoceptive prediction error. Parallel input from the HPA axis (cortisol), the autonomic nervous system (sympathetic and parasympathetic tone), and the amygdala (threat-relevant salience) feeds the ongoing inference. Affect emerges when the AI's inference about bodily state is integrated with context by orbitofrontal and medial prefrontal cortex.

The framework's strongest claim — that emotion is constructed rather than read off — is supported by meta-analyses showing that the same stimulus produces different emotions across cultures and contexts, that interoceptive accuracy predicts emotional experience, and that AI is central to both interoception and emotion. Contested territory includes how much of emotion really requires construction (some basic responses — startle, disgust to decay — look more stimulus-driven), how universal the interoceptive hierarchy is across species, and whether "emotions" carve out neural-kind categories or just useful linguistic ones. The debate between the constructionist view (Barrett) and the basic-emotions view (Ekman, Panksepp) is active and productive.

For environments, IC is the framework that explains why the same space can feel restorative one day and oppressive the next. Interoceptive state is a function of sleep, recent stress, time since eating, and hormonal phase — and these all set the priors the brain uses to interpret ambient signals. Spaces that support autonomic balance (calm sound, predictable thermal conditions, natural light) let the constructionist machinery settle; spaces that continually challenge autonomic homeostasis produce chronic allostatic load. Design should aim for bodily predictability.

Deep dive — the framework in full

From James to Craig to Barrett: a century-long arc

William James proposed in 1884 that emotion follows rather than precedes bodily response: we do not tremble because we are afraid; we are afraid because we tremble. James's account was correct about the direction of causation for some visceral contributions but was criticised for seeming to ignore the cognitive and cultural shaping of emotion. The framework lay in uneasy stasis through most of the twentieth century until Bud Craig's work in the 2000s mapped the ascending interoceptive pathway from lamina I of the spinal cord through the parabrachial nucleus and ventromedial thalamus to the posterior and then anterior insula. Craig's work identified the anterior insula as the cortical endpoint of interoception and the neural substrate for a subjective "feeling" of bodily state. Lisa Feldman Barrett's constructionist theory (2017) joined Craig's interoceptive hierarchy to a predictive-processing account: the brain generates ongoing predictions about its body, and emotions are the categorisations the brain applies to the current interoceptive prediction error in context. Antonio Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis — that bodily states serve as learned evaluations of alternatives during decision-making — is a complementary strand of the same lineage.

The interoceptive hierarchy: from viscera to experience

The interoceptive pathway is unusually well-traced. Visceral signals — heartbeat, respiration, gut stretch, blood chemistry — originate at peripheral sensors and ascend via the vagus nerve and other cranial and spinal afferents to the nucleus of the solitary tract (NTS) in the medulla. NTS neurons project to the parabrachial nucleus in the pons, which relays to the ventromedial thalamus and thence to the posterior insula. Posterior insula is the cortical site of primary interoceptive representation: activity here tracks physiological state. Mid-insula integrates interoceptive input with exteroceptive signals (touch, temperature, nociception). Anterior insula is the cortical convergence zone where interoceptive prediction error is computed against top-down predictions descending from orbitofrontal and medial prefrontal cortex. The AI's right-hemisphere preference in humans (a feature somewhat unusual in primates) is thought to reflect the cortical correlate of the subjective "feeling of being alive." Parallel influences come from the HPA axis (hypothalamus → pituitary → adrenal cortisol), from the autonomic nervous system (sympathetic and parasympathetic balance), and from the amygdala, which adds threat-salience weighting to any interoceptive signal flagged as potentially dangerous.

Evidence: what is well-established

Three bodies of evidence anchor the framework. First, the anatomy is traced: lesion studies, tract-tracing in non-human primates, and human diffusion imaging agree on the ascending interoceptive pathway and on the anterior insula as its cortical terminus. Second, AI activity reliably tracks both interoceptive experience (heartbeat detection tasks show AI correlates with accuracy) and emotional experience (meta-analyses of hundreds of emotion neuroimaging studies place AI among the most consistently activated regions). Third, disruption of interoceptive input disrupts emotional experience in predictable ways: patients with pure autonomic failure, peripheral neuropathies affecting vagal function, or insula lesions all show blunted affective responses and, in the insula cases, blunted subjective feeling of bodily state. The bidirectional link between interoception and emotion — stronger interoceptive accuracy producing more differentiated emotional experience — has been demonstrated across perceptual, behavioural, and neural measures (Critchley & Garfinkel, 2017; Chen et al., 2021).

Where it is contested

Two active debates. The constructionist vs. basic-emotion debate concerns whether emotions like fear, disgust, and joy correspond to distinct neural circuits (Ekman, Panksepp) or are constructed from more primitive ingredients in context (Barrett). The evidence has moved toward Barrett's position for complex emotions and for emotional variety across cultures, but basic-emotion theorists have a reasonable case for a few phylogenetically old responses. The interoception universality debate concerns whether every species' interoceptive hierarchy follows the same anatomical plan humans use; comparative work suggests the ascending pathway is broadly conserved in mammals but that the cortical elaboration is substantially richer in humans and great apes. A third, narrower debate: whether the two-dimensional valence-arousal representation adequately characterises affect or whether more dimensions are needed (approach-avoidance, dominance, appraisal content).

Clinical and developmental relevance

Interoceptive dysfunction appears in a wide range of clinical conditions, and each teaches the framework something. In alexithymia — difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions — interoceptive accuracy is reduced and anterior insula responsiveness is blunted, consistent with the claim that emotion is constructed from interoceptive signal. In panic disorder and anxiety, interoceptive hypervigilance is elevated: small bodily signals are amplified into categorical fear. In somatic-symptom disorders, an interoceptive prior that expects illness sustains bodily experience consistent with that prior even in the absence of peripheral pathology. In anorexia nervosa, interoceptive signals from hunger are down-weighted against cognitive priors about body shape. These patterns have opened the door to interoceptive-focused therapies — mindful body awareness, heartbeat-detection training, breath-focused meditation — which modify the interoceptive signal or its weighting. Developmentally, interoceptive accuracy rises through childhood and adolescence, with individual differences predicting emotion-regulation competence and mental-health trajectories (Khalsa et al., 2018). The framework's clinical reach is part of why it has moved quickly from theory to practice.

How this connects to the other T1 frameworks

IC shares AI with SN: the same region mediates interoceptive construction of affect and detection of behavioural salience. Predictive Processing (PP) supplies the Bayesian machinery — affect is the AI's inference about bodily state, continuously revised by interoceptive prediction error. Neuromodulatory Systems (NM) provide the hormonal and autonomic tone that the interoceptive hierarchy samples; cortisol, noradrenaline, and vagal tone are the substrate in which affect is constructed. Chronobiological Regulation (CB) determines the daily rhythm of the interoceptive signal (cortisol diurnal pattern, heart-rate variability modulation by circadian phase). Default-mode & Place (DP) contributes the long-term contextual memory that shapes the priors on which constructionist inference runs.

Implications for environmental cognition

If affect is constructed rather than read off the environment, then environments do not "contain" emotional content — they contribute interoceptive signals and contextual priors that the brain will use to construct experience. Four design implications follow. First, thermal, acoustic, and respiratory environments directly shape autonomic tone and therefore the interoceptive signal the brain is inferring about: a stuffy room that degrades respiratory signal quality produces mild interoceptive uncertainty that the brain may construct as vague unease. Second, contextual priors matter: the same ambient condition feels different in spaces that culturally prime stress (hospitals, airports) versus those that prime recovery (libraries, gardens). Third, recovery requires time — allostatic load accumulates across hours and days, and spaces occupied only briefly cannot reset interoceptive set-points. Fourth, individual differences are large and principled: occupants with heightened interoceptive sensitivity (anxiety disorders, somatic-symptom conditions) respond more strongly to small environmental perturbations, and designs aimed at well-being should budget for that variance rather than optimise for an average occupant. Track 3 (VR) and Track 4 (UX) can both measure these effects tractably via heart-rate variability, respiration, and galvanic skin response.
