ThursdayDay 2
AI-Assisted Article Finding — Lab session

David is away. A lab member will be present to assist. The goal is hands-on: find and submit at least 10 PDFs of relevant scientific articles before the end of the session. Use a mix of search tools — do not rely on a single one.

Start by visiting the Topics page to identify which research area you want to search in. Then use the Question Maker to turn a topic into search-ready queries (the point is to move beyond simple Boolean strings toward richer, semantically-grounded queries).

  • Visit Topics — pick one or two research areas that interest you
  • Use the Question Maker to generate search queries that go beyond keyword Boolean logic
  • Search using at least three of: Google Scholar AI, Elicit, OpenAlex, Consensus, Scholar GPT (ChatGPT plugin)
  • Download PDFs for any article that looks relevant and well-cited
  • Submit 10 PDFs to the article archive before end of session
  • Start a plain-text note of any site friction you encountered — confusing labels, missing links, anything that slowed you down
Due by end of Thursday

10 PDFs submitted to the Knowledge Atlas article archive via the Article Propose page. These contribute directly to the shared evidence base all tracks will use.

ThursdayDay 4
ATLAS Exploration Session — Seven Self-Guided Tasks

Today's lab is structured differently from Week 1. Rather than open-ended article finding, you will work through seven guided tasks designed to take you deeper into the ATLAS system — as a curious newcomer, a design practitioner, a methodological sceptic, and other roles. Each task involves navigating real ATLAS pages, posing questions, and noting observations.

The goal is not to complete every task in full but to become genuinely familiar with the system's evidence base, limitations, and interface — knowledge you will apply during the site-audit phase in Weeks 8–10.

  • Work through the seven tasks in order — each takes 10–15 minutes
  • Record your most interesting observation from each task in a markdown file: notes/atlas_exploration_week2.md
  • Note any friction, gaps, or surprising findings — these are exactly the observations the Phase 3 audit will formalise
  • If you are on Track 4, pay particular attention to tasks 1, 2, and 5 — they directly inform the GUI assignment
Due by end of Thursday

Push notes/atlas_exploration_week2.md to your personal branch with at least one observation per task. Continue article submissions if time permits — aim for 20 total across weeks 1 and 2.

Phase 3 — Fall Site Debugging
🔬
Phase 3 — Weeks 8–10 · Debugging the Fall COGS 160 site
The last three weeks shift from building to testing. Each team is assigned a section of the Designing_Experiments repository — the Fall COGS 160 experiment-making site being developed in parallel. Teams fork the repo, audit their section by simulating a naïve student walkthrough, fix every resolvable issue, write formal success conditions for each page function, and open a Pull Request with a hand-off report. The goal is a site that a new cohort of Fall students can actually use without instructor hand-holding. Sensor integration and adaptive psych instrument work (Q-sort, IAT) are available as alternatives for interested students.

Grading

Detailed grading rubrics for the track work (Weeks 3–7) and the Fall site debugging (Weeks 8–10) will be posted on this page before those phases begin. The Week 1–2 article-finding work is assessed on completion (10 PDFs submitted by end of Week 1 Thursday; continued submissions through Week 2). Usability notes are assessed on specificity — a generic comment such as "the site was confusing" does not count; a note such as "the Article Propose page does not explain whether uploaded PDFs replace a DOI entry or supplement it" does.
