About the Knowledge Atlas

A working research atlas joining cognitive neuroscience to environmental and architectural design. Maintained by Prof. David Kirsh and the COGS 160 Spring 2026 collaborators at UC San Diego.

What the Atlas is

The Knowledge Atlas indexes peer-reviewed work in environmental-cognition research, organises it under eleven foundational neuroscience frameworks, and maps each finding to testable architectural implications. It is a living document, regenerated on a weekly cycle, that is as honest about what the field does not yet know as it is about what it does.

The Atlas covers three intertwined sub-fields: cognitive neuroscience — how perception, attention, affect, and memory actually work in the brain; environmental psychology — how built and natural environments shape human behaviour and well-being; and evidence-grounded design — how to translate both into practice that can be measured and falsified rather than merely asserted.

Who the Atlas serves

Four audiences share the site, each with a slightly different navigation path. Researchers use it to scan topics, evidence, and gaps for scholarly work. Practitioners — architects, landscape designers, building scientists — use it to find evidence that bears on a specific design decision. Contributors add articles, tag images, and help run the pipeline that keeps the corpus current. COGS 160 students use it as the working surface of their quarter-long course in the Cognitive Science department at UCSD.

Epistemics: how evidence is evaluated

Every article enters through a Quinean coherentist evaluation. Claims are tagged with warrant type (observation, inference, composition, etc.), a confidence score, and a contested-status flag. The scoring procedure — developed jointly with the Article Eater project — combines Spohn-style ranking theory, Bayesian updating, and explicit contestability tracking. Evidence is not filtered to what the Atlas expects or prefers — it is displayed with its uncertainty intact, so readers can form their own view of how far any particular claim generalises.

Two principles are load-bearing. First, neural plausibility is not evidence: a mechanistically elegant story about brain circuitry does not, by itself, make a behavioural claim more likely to be true. Second, replication is weighted heavily: a single striking finding is recorded but does not advance a topic's confidence rating until independent replication lands. These rules make the Atlas slower than a popular-science summary but better calibrated to the actual scientific state of play.

How the Atlas is built

The Atlas is assembled by a collaboration between human maintainers, an expert panel of neuroscientists and architects, and a pipeline of AI workers. Articles are proposed by students and researchers, ingested by the Article Eater pipeline, graded against the Quinean coherentist procedure, and then rendered as per-article PNU (Plausible Neuroscience Underpinning) pages. Mechanism-profile documents, framework deep-dives, and topic pages are generated on the same pipeline but flagged as LLM-drafts pending panel review before they carry authoritative weight.

The site is static HTML served from a canonical repository. A weekly scheduled task refreshes its machine-readable manifests; on-demand refresh from the maintainer's machine is available through the admin console. Evidence and gaps are folded into each topic page as sub-tabs, keeping navigation flat.
