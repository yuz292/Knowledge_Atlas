Epistemic Network (Web of Belief) viewer

Roles served researcher, 160_student, admin Current status naive Complexity tier High — visual density is the core design problem

◧What this page displaysthe information on offer

The Epistemic Network (EN) — Quine & Ullian's Web of Belief (1978) rendered as a directed graph — has claims for nodes and evidential relations for edges. Edges come in four flavours: support, undercutting defeat, rebutting defeat, and coherence. The viewer answers one question well: given a claim you care about — say, "exposure to fractal patterns reduces self-reported stress" — which other claims in the Atlas support it, which undercut it, and how densely connected is the neighbourhood?

At corpus scale the graph has thousands of nodes, so the viewer needs two modes. A local mode anchors on one claim and shows its support/defeat neighbourhood at once. A global mode shows where the network is dense and where it is sparse without node-level detail. The two join through a Shneiderman-style (1996) zoom-and-filter affordance — overview first, zoom and filter, then details on demand.

Every edge in the EN is backed by at least one warrant: a structured record naming the source paper, the extraction confidence, the discounting applied, and the rule under which the extraction was made. The viewer must surface these warrants on demand but not permanently — a network whose edges cannot be inspected back to their source is not an epistemic representation, it is an art installation.

☰Who needs this, and whyfunctions served, per user type

  • Public visitorLikely not well served by the EN directly; a public visitor is better served by the interpretation layer, which already consumes the EN and summarises it in prose.
  • COGS 160 studentTrack 2 students use the EN to see which of their candidate papers are already represented as claims, and which are orphans. Track 3 students use it to pick a well-supported claim to operationalise in VR.
  • ResearcherTo check whether a claim they are considering writing into a paper is supported or contested by the corpus. The EN is the fastest way to see a claim's "local epistemic neighbourhood".
  • PractitionerTo skim whether a claim they are about to cite in a design-decision memo stands up to the network's consensus.
  • AdministratorTo detect over-confidence — nodes with many support edges and zero defeat edges are either genuinely settled or the extraction pipeline is not finding disagreements, and the admin wants to know which.

△Why this page is hard to designthe challenges

  1. Density. With ≈ 1,400 papers and an average of ≈ 4 claims each, the full graph has ≈ 5,600 nodes. Force-directed layouts at this scale are unreadable; the viewer must use semantic zoom, topic clustering, or a local-only default view.
  2. Asymmetric edge types. Support, undercutting defeat, and rebutting defeat look the same in a naive node-link drawing. Pollock (1995) and Haack (1993) require the distinction, so the viewer must colour-code or shape-code edges without visually overloading the drawing (Bertin's 1967 retinal-variable budget).
  3. Warrant provenance. Each edge should be inspectable back to the warrant that produced it — with confidence, discounting, and rule — but not permanently displayed. This is a classic details-on-demand problem.
  4. Claim identity. Two papers may make the same claim in different words. The viewer's node identity is therefore an NLP clustering decision, and the user needs to see when two visually-distinct nodes are in fact the same claim (and when the clustering is wrong).
  5. Temporal evolution. The EN changes as papers are added and re-extracted. A static viewer hides this; a dynamic one risks overloading the frame. A timeline scrubber is appealing but perishes in the hands of users who do not read the axis.

✗The simple naive solution in place nowwhat you'll actually see

naive — the current implementation

A single force-directed node-link graph of the entire EN, drawn in D3. Black circles for nodes, grey straight lines for edges. Clicking a node centres the layout and darkens its first-order neighbour edges. No edge-type distinction, no filter, no warrant panel, no search, no zoom, no legend.

Why this is inadequate. The page is a network drawing — the minimum structural requirement — and fails every epistemic and ergonomic requirement the representation was designed for. A reader cannot tell support from defeat (the structural point of an epistemic network). They cannot find a specific claim (no search). They cannot see why any edge exists (no warrant on hover). And they cannot survive the 5,600-node layout (the browser pins one CPU for minutes). It is a placeholder, not a viewer.

  • missingtopic_membership_indexPer-node topic-bundle tags, so that filtering by topic is possible. Currently absent; blocks the "show me the EN just for lighting papers" use case.

→Status and next steps

The EN is the single page with the highest design leverage on the site — it touches every claim the Atlas holds. Four T1 theoretical frameworks (Quine & Ullian 1978, Pollock 1995, Haack 1993, Bender & Koller 2020) directly constrain what the viewer must show; any redesign has to take a position on all four.
