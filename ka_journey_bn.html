Bayesian Network viewer

Roles served researcher, practitioner, 160_student Current status naive Complexity tier High — abstractness of probabilistic variables is the core problem

◧What this page displaysthe information on offer

The Bayesian Network (BN) viewer shows a directed acyclic graph of causal variables from the Atlas's mechanism layer. Architectural features (window-to-wall ratio, reverberation time) sit at the parent end; intermediate cognitive and affective states (attention restoration, cortisol level) sit in the middle; outcomes (self-reported stress, task accuracy) sit at the child end. Nodes carry conditional probability tables (CPTs), or — where the evidence is thin — qualitative influence labels.

The viewer answers three kinds of question, and architectural design calls on different ones. A predictive question: given a setting of features, what is the probability distribution over outcomes? An interventional question in Pearl's (2009) sense: if I set feature X to value v — do(X = v) — what happens to Y? A counterfactual question: had X been different, what would Y have been for this specific case? The three need different UI affordances; viewers that collapse them into one are misleading.

Probabilities in the Atlas's BN are rarely tightly constrained by data — they are elicited from the corpus via meta-analysis plus expert elicitation. The viewer must therefore show uncertainty on every edge (credibility intervals, sample sizes, elicitation method) rather than quoting point values that invite false precision.

☰Who needs this, and whyfunctions served, per user type

  • Public visitorA public visitor will not know what a conditional probability table is and should be routed either to the Question-Answering layer or to a simplified plain-language summary generated from the BN.
  • COGS 160 studentTrack 3 students use the BN to pick a causal chain to operationalise in VR; Track 4 students use it as a redesign target, because CPT tables are visually hostile and invite an obvious critique.
  • ResearcherTo test whether a proposed causal story is coherent with the corpus — a BN of your own theory overlaid on the Atlas's lets you see where your story adds edges the corpus does not support.
  • PractitionerDesign-decision support. "If I increase window-to-wall ratio from 20% to 40%, what happens to reported stress?" The BN is the only representation that answers this in quantified form.
  • AdministratorTo audit for isolated subgraphs — variables with no parents and no children — which usually indicate an extraction error.

△Why this page is hard to designthe challenges

  1. Probability is abstract. CPT tables are the representation experts expect and the representation novices flee. The viewer needs to present at least three levels of detail: a plain-language qualitative summary, an interactive probability slider, and the raw CPT for researchers.
  2. Causal assumptions are hidden. A BN asserts a causal direction on every edge. The viewer must make the assumption visible ("this arrow means the paper made a causal claim; 2 of 4 cited papers instead made a correlational claim") to avoid overselling.
  3. Interventions vs. observations. Pearl's do-calculus distinguishes conditioning from intervening, and the UI must too. A slider that sets a value needs to be clearly labelled as intervention (do(X = v)) not observation.
  4. Uncertainty propagation. Propagating uncertainty through a BN is computationally manageable but visually overwhelming. The viewer must show how much uncertainty is "in" an answer and avoid reporting a tight posterior when the priors were wide.
  5. Inference cost. Exact inference is #P-hard; even approximate inference on ≈ 200-node networks takes seconds in a browser. The viewer must queue, cache, and show progress.

✗The simple naive solution in place nowwhat you'll actually see

naive — the current implementation

The current page renders a static DAG produced by Graphviz, with node labels and edge arrows but no CPTs, no sliders, no query affordance, and no uncertainty display. Clicking a node opens a modal with the node's name and a raw JSON dump of its CPT.

Why this is inadequate. The page shows the BN but does not let the user use it. None of the three question types (predictive, interventional, counterfactual) is supported. The raw JSON CPT modal exposes machine-readable structure but not the semantic content a researcher or practitioner is trying to extract. A practitioner asking the core use-case question — "what happens to stress if I double the window area?" — has no way to ask it.

☷Data files and endpoints to populate this pagewhat a real implementation would call

  • datadata/ka_payloads/bayesian_network.jsonNode list with CPTs, edge list with causal direction and source-paper count, plus credibility-interval metadata.
  • api/api/bn/queryAccepts a query of the form {evidence: {X: v}, intervention: {Y: w}, target: Z} and returns a posterior distribution over Z with credibility intervals.
  • libBN_graphical/bn_inference.pyThe Bayesian-network inference module from the sibling BN_graphical repo. Needs an HTTP adapter exposed to the Atlas.
  • datadata/ka_payloads/bn_uncertainty_meta.jsonPer-edge uncertainty metadata: sample size, elicitation source, credibility bounds.
  • missingka_bn_render.jsBrowser-side renderer supporting semantic zoom, intervention sliders, and uncertainty bars. Not yet implemented.
