Salience Networks

The system that decides what gets noticed, when the brain switches between inward reflection and outward task, and how threat, novelty, and behavioural relevance compete for attention.

At a glance

In plain English. The Salience Network is the brain's relevance detector — a small coalition of regions, centred on the anterior insula and the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex, that tags incoming signals as either worth responding to or safe to ignore, and then hands control of attention to whichever system should act next.

Three linked structures do most of the work. The anterior insula (AI) pools interoceptive, exteroceptive, and valuation signals; the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) monitors conflict and anticipated error; the temporoparietal junction (TPJ) pulls attention toward unexpected events. Together they function as a dynamic switch, toggling activity between the Default Mode Network (inward, self- referential processing) and the Central Executive Network (outward, task-focused processing). Von Economo neurons in AI and dACC — large projection cells found in only a handful of species — provide rapid long-distance signalling. Noradrenergic tone from the locus coeruleus and dopaminergic input from the VTA both modulate salience gain.

The framework is well supported in its core claim — AI and dACC do coordinate switching between inward and outward processing — and the evidence spans resting-state fMRI, lesion studies, and transcranial magnetic stimulation. Contested territory concerns the *precise* division of labour between salience, ventral-attention, and orienting systems, and whether salience in abstract contexts (social signals, aesthetic preferences) reflects the same physiology as salience for threat. Individual differences in salience-network reactivity predict anxiety and ADHD traits, making this a pathway with clinical as well as cognitive relevance.

For environments, salience is the mechanism that makes some spaces exhausting and others restorative. Persistent low-level novelty — chatter, intermittent notifications, peripheral motion — keeps the Salience Network recruiting attention even when nothing requires a response. The design question is not to remove stimulation but to make stimulation resolvable: signals the system can classify quickly and deprioritise are far less costly than ambiguous ones it cannot let go.

Deep dive — the framework in full

Origins and anatomical substrate

The Salience Network crystallised as a distinct construct in 2007, when Seeley and colleagues identified, using resting-state fMRI, two dissociable intrinsic networks that both involved anterior insula and cingulate cortex. One network — the salience network proper — centred on bilateral AI, dACC, and related subcortical structures (amygdala, ventral striatum, substantia nigra/VTA); the other — the executive control network — centred on dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and posterior parietal cortex. The framework extended earlier work on attention-orienting systems (Posner, Corbetta & Shulman) by placing salience-based switching in a distinct network with its own anatomy and its own time-course. The AI is the central hub: it sits at the intersection of interoceptive, autonomic, and exteroceptive streams, and its von Economo neurons — large, rapidly-conducting projection cells — appear in a small set of highly social species and are thought to support the fast decisions the network makes.

The switch mechanism: DMN, CEN, and the anterior insula

The network's best-documented function is switching between two large-scale networks in anti-phase. The Default Mode Network (DMN) — medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus — dominates during mind-wandering, autobiographical memory, and self-referential processing. The Central Executive Network (CEN) — dlPFC, posterior parietal cortex — dominates during externally focused task performance. The Salience Network, led by right anterior insula, produces a phasic signal that suppresses DMN and amplifies CEN when a task-relevant stimulus appears, and the reverse when external demands abate. This switching operation has been demonstrated causally: TMS to right AI disrupts DMN-CEN toggling, lesion studies show the same effect, and the temporal sequence of AI activation preceding DMN deactivation and CEN activation is reliable at sub-second resolution (Menon & Uddin, 2010; Goulden et al., 2014). Individual differences in the stability of this switching predict attention complaints, anxiety, and the fluctuating attention characteristic of ADHD.

Evidence: what is well-established

Several findings have accumulated enough weight that they are no longer seriously disputed. The anatomical coherence of the network — AI, dACC, amygdala, ventral striatum, substantia nigra/VTA — emerges robustly from resting-state fMRI across hundreds of studies and thousands of individual participants, with right AI consistently the most central node. Causal role in switching between DMN and CEN is supported by lesion studies (AI/dACC damage reliably impairs task switching), by TMS (disrupting right AI produces the predicted behavioural slowing), and by the temporal order observed with high-temporal-resolution fMRI and MEG: AI activation precedes DMN suppression by several hundred milliseconds, which precedes CEN engagement. Pharmacological work with atomoxetine (a selective noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor) shows predicted modulation of salience-network connectivity and behavioural vigilance, consistent with LC-NA control of salience gain. Clinical populations with documented salience-network dysfunction — ADHD, anxiety disorders, early-stage fronto-temporal dementia (where von Economo neurons degenerate selectively) — show exactly the attentional and switching deficits the framework predicts. These findings do not fully disambiguate the network from overlapping ventral-attention circuitry, but they establish that AI and dACC operate as a coherent switching hub beyond serious contest.

What counts as "salient"? — three kinds of signal

The Salience Network does not respond uniformly to all surprises. At least three gradients matter. Physical salience — bright, loud, moving, contrastive — drives TPJ and ventral-attention responses within roughly 150–300 ms of stimulus onset. Motivational salience — food cues in a hungry person, social cues in a lonely one — recruits the amygdala and ventral striatum as well as AI, and carries a longer time course reflecting neuromodulatory influences. Behavioural salience — a signal that requires an action now — engages dACC and prefrontal areas through the conflict-monitoring circuit. These gradients are partly separable but interact: a physically-subtle stimulus can become behaviourally salient through learned contingency, and AI integrates across all three. This integrative role is why AI has been described as the brain's "attentional bottleneck" for awareness: signals that do not pass through AI typically do not enter conscious report.

Neuromodulation: noradrenaline and dopamine

Two neuromodulators shape salience-network dynamics. Noradrenergic projections from the locus coeruleus track unexpected uncertainty, setting the gain on salience signals: high LC tone lowers the threshold for detecting new signals (promoting exploration), while low LC tone favours exploitation of current task set. Aston-Jones and Cohen's (2005) adaptive-gain hypothesis formalises this. Dopaminergic projections from the VTA carry reward-prediction error and help distinguish motivationally salient stimuli from merely surprising ones. Pharmacological manipulations that modulate these systems — atomoxetine, methylphenidate, stimulant medications — produce measurable changes in salience-network resting-state connectivity and in behavioural switching speed, which is part of why ADHD treatment targets these pathways.

Where it is contested

Three live critiques. First, the boundary between the salience network and the ventral-attention/orienting network remains fuzzy: both involve right TPJ and AI, and whether they are cleanly separable or share substrate is debated. Second, the canonical "triple network" model (SN, DMN, CEN as the three large-scale networks that account for most task-related activity) is a useful simplification that glosses over substantial heterogeneity within each network — particularly within the CEN, where fronto-parietal subsystems show distinct task profiles. Third, the extension of salience-network logic to abstract domains (social salience, aesthetic salience) is supported by imaging but not yet by convergent behavioural or pharmacological evidence; it may be a useful metaphor rather than a literal mechanism (Uddin, 2021).

Clinical and developmental perspectives

Salience-network dysfunction appears across a range of clinical conditions, each illuminating a different facet of the normal architecture. In ADHD, the switching signal between DMN and CEN is weakened, and resting-state connectivity of right AI is reduced; stimulant medications partly restore both measures alongside behavioural attention. In anxiety disorders, AI reactivity to threat-relevant stimuli is elevated and the threshold for recruiting the salience network is lowered. In behavioural-variant fronto-temporal dementia, von Economo neurons in AI and dACC degenerate selectively and early, producing the characteristic disinhibited and socially uncalibrated behaviour before broader atrophy appears. In schizophrenia, aberrant salience — inappropriate tagging of stimuli as personally relevant — is a plausible neurocognitive substrate for delusions of reference. Developmentally, salience-network coherence strengthens through childhood and adolescence, with individual differences in maturation predicting attentional flexibility and emotion-regulation trajectories into young adulthood. These clinical patterns give the framework its causal claims: they are the natural experiments that show what changes when the salience network is altered, and they converge on the same anatomy that resting-state imaging identifies in healthy adults.

How this connects to the other T1 frameworks

Salience Networks tag prediction errors (PP) that carry high precision weight — this is the neural implementation of precision weighting in the Predictive-Processing scheme. Neuromodulatory Systems (NM) supply the gain signals (LC noradrenaline, VTA dopamine) that scale salience responses. Default-mode & Place (DP) is the network SN suppresses to free up attention. Interoceptive-Constructionist Affect (IC) shares AI as its primary substrate — AI simultaneously monitors bodily state (for affect) and external salience (for attention). Implicit-Explicit Dual-Process Theory (IE_DPT) decides whether salience is processed implicitly (fast capture) or explicitly (endogenous attention).

Implications for environmental cognition

The Salience Network does not tire in the way a muscle tires, but its repeated recruitment in the absence of resolvable signal produces measurable cognitive fatigue. Environments that continually trigger salience detection without delivering information the system can use — intermittent motion on the edge of vision, repetitive low-level sound, unpredictable HVAC thermal fluctuations — produce a specific pattern of attentional depletion. The behavioural signature is fragmentation: sustained attention tasks show shorter coherent runs, and the subjective report is of "not being able to settle." By contrast, environments that deliver resolvable signal — predictable natural background sound, stable peripheral visual field, coherent thermal envelope — let AI and dACC rest. The design rule is not to remove stimulation but to ensure stimulation carries classifiable meaning: ambient information that can be categorised and then ignored is restful; ambient information that cannot is depleting. Track 4 UX research should target this distinction when instrumenting occupant experience in long-duration settings.

Measuring salience-network engagement in environmental research is tractable with off-the-shelf instruments. Pupillometry provides a continuous proxy for locus-coeruleus activity and therefore for the gain modulation the salience network imposes; galvanic skin response tracks rapid salience-driven arousal changes at a time resolution usable in occupied space; heart-rate variability indexes the autonomic side of salience engagement. None of these requires neuroimaging. What they enable is quantitative comparison of environments that otherwise differ only in subjective report — two rooms scoring equally on a conventional satisfaction survey can show markedly different salience-network load over a two-hour working session. Designing for restoration is, in large part, designing environments whose salience signature allows the network to rest after the initial orienting sweep. The coffee shop that feels concentrated despite ambient chatter usually achieves this by making the chatter classifiable and repetitive (therefore quickly deprioritised), not by eliminating it.
