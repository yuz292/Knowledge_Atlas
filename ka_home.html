What is Knowledge Atlas?

A knowledge system built on
rigorous epistemic foundations

Knowledge Atlas is not a search engine, a chatbot, or a literature database. It is a coherentist knowledge representation system — a structured network of beliefs, evidence nodes, warrants, and defeat conditions — designed to make the epistemic state of a scientific field legible, queryable, and improvable.

Every claim in Knowledge Atlas carries an explicit credence value, a provenance chain traceable to primary sources, and a set of warrant relationships that formalize why and to what degree that evidence supports the belief it is linked to. When evidence conflicts, the system does not average or suppress — it represents the conflict structurally and surfaces it to the user. When evidence is absent, the Value of Information framework quantifies exactly what a new study would be worth, making research prioritization a principled calculation rather than a guess.

The system is built for human factors and cognitive science research, but its epistemic architecture is domain-general. The same machinery that evaluates evidence on workplace lighting and attention can, in principle, represent any field where controlled studies accumulate toward contested theoretical claims.

Theoretical Architecture

Five frameworks, unified

Knowledge Atlas draws on five distinct intellectual traditions. Each contributes a different structural layer to the system. The author established formal proofs that these frameworks are mutually consistent — a non-trivial result given that they were developed independently, in different research communities, using different formalisms.

🕸️
Coherence Theory of Justification
Thagard · BonJour · Haack
Beliefs are justified not by their derivation from foundational axioms but by their coherence with the overall web of belief. Knowledge Atlas operationalizes coherence as a scored graph property: beliefs with greater mutual support and fewer contradictions receive higher credence.
⚖️
Defeasible Warrant & Entrenchment
Pollock · Spohn · Cartwright · Mayo
Evidence does not entail conclusions — it warrants them, defeasibly. Every evidence-to-belief link carries a typed bridge warrant (constitutive, mechanism, empirical association, functional, capacity, analogical, or theory-derived), a strength score decomposed into severity, replication, confound risk, and source quality, and an explicit defeat condition. Public Atlas pages display the canonical bridge type; extraction-layer claim classes are tracked separately in detailed views rather than being confused with the warrant taxonomy itself.
🔢
Bayesian Belief Networks
Pearl · Jensen · Koller & Friedman
The belief network is implemented as a Bayesian graphical model, enabling probabilistic propagation of evidence updates. Conditional independence assumptions are stated explicitly, and the network supports both forward inference (what does this evidence imply?) and backward inference (what evidence would most change this belief?).
🗣️
Argumentation Theory
Walton · Dung · Bench-Capon
Conflicts between evidence nodes are represented as argumentation frameworks with explicit attack and support relations. The acceptability semantics (grounded, preferred, stable extensions) determine which beliefs survive under a given set of incoming attacks — making the system's conflict resolution transparent and auditable.
📊
Value of Information Theory
Howard · Good · Lindley
The VOI framework quantifies the expected reduction in decision uncertainty that a new observation would produce. Knowledge Atlas uses VOI scores to rank research gaps by epistemic priority, making it possible to identify exactly which missing studies would most improve the overall coherence and decision-relevance of the knowledge base.
The Author's Contribution

New proofs establishing formal consistency
across five independent frameworks

Each of the five frameworks above was developed in isolation, in different research communities, using different mathematical formalisms. Their simultaneous deployment in a single computational system raises a non-trivial question: are they mutually consistent? Do they make incompatible demands on how credence should propagate, how defeat should be handled, or how information value should be calculated?

The author established a series of original proofs addressing exactly these questions. The proofs show that under the specific bridge conditions implemented in Knowledge Atlas, the five frameworks do not merely coexist — they reinforce each other. Coherence scores constrain Bayesian priors in a principled way. Warrant types determine the functional form of the likelihood update. Argumentation-theoretic defeat conditions map cleanly onto Bayesian network d-separation. And VOI calculations are grounded in the same credence values that the coherence and warrant layers produce.

  • 1 Coherence–Bayes bridge: Thagard-style coherence maximization converges to the same steady-state credence distribution as Bayesian updating from a uniform prior, given equivalent evidence. The proof establishes the translation function between the two update mechanisms.
  • 2 Warrant–likelihood correspondence: Each bridge-layer warrant type (constitutive, mechanism, empirical association, functional, capacity, analogical, theory-derived) maps to a distinct likelihood-ratio profile. The proof characterizes these profiles and shows they are formally distinct rather than merely heuristic labels.
  • 3 Defeat–d-separation equivalence: Undercutting defeat in the argumentation layer is formally equivalent to the introduction of a blocking variable that induces d-separation in the Bayesian network. Rebutting defeat corresponds to the introduction of a contradictory node. The proof shows the two representations are interchangeable without information loss.
  • 4 VOI–coherence monotonicity: Under the bridge conditions established above, VOI scores are monotonically related to the coherence gap they would close. This means gap prioritization by VOI and prioritization by coherence improvement are equivalent orderings — a consistency result that validates using either metric interchangeably for research planning.

Operational Layers

Four epistemic inspector pages

Knowledge Atlas is not just article cards and topic lists. The site exposes the warrant, argumentation, annotation, and interpretation layers directly so you can inspect how claims are supported, what has been flagged, what frontier questions remain, and how changing a warrant weight would propagate through the network. Each layer inspector has a Critique and Support explorer for any selected claim.

⚖️
Warrants Inspector
7 warrant types · sensitivity simulator
Inspect the seven warrant types (constitutive to theory-derived), adjust discount multipliers with the sensitivity slider, and see which claims are most affected by changing a theory weight.
🕸️
Argumentation Layer
-- support edges · brief
The support and conflict structure of the corpus. Use the Critique Explorer to see how any claim has been attacked and where its vulnerabilities lie, or the Support Explorer to see what backs it and what would strengthen it further.
📝
Annotation Layer
-- active annotations · brief
The review overlay for calibration notes, replication flags, sensitivity warnings, and other signals that help keep weak beliefs from being overpromoted.
🧭
Interpretation Layer
-- high-VOI questions · brief
The planning surface that shows active-boundary beliefs, frontier questions, and where the field remains strategically incomplete.
