Did you know?

Three findings from cognitive neuroscience for architecture that the Atlas finds most worth explaining clearly.

CNFA · Predictive Processing

Your brain predicts a building before experiencing it.

Visual, acoustic, and thermal cues generate top-down predictions at every level of cortex. Subtle violations — an almost-right angle, a misplaced acoustic reflection — leave persistent unresolved prediction error that occupants can rarely articulate but reliably feel.

CNFA · Chronobiology

Morning daylight is a neurochemical signal.

Light above roughly 250 melanopic lux in the first hours of the day activates intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells, advances the circadian clock, and produces measurable gains in daytime alertness, memory, and sleep quality. Window placement is neurochemistry.

Chronobiological Regulation →
CNFA · Embodied Cognition

Spaces you only see are spaces you implicitly inhabit.

Premotor and parietal circuits activate during perception of action-relevant geometry, not only during action itself. A handrail invites grip simulation in passing; a corridor invites walking simulation in reading. Design choices recruit motor cortex whether or not the occupant moves.

About the Atlas

A living research tool maintained by Prof. David Kirsh and the UCSD COGS 160 Spring 2026 collaborators. It indexes peer-reviewed work in environmental-cognition research, organises it under eleven foundational neural-underpinning frameworks, and maps each theory and mechanism to testable architectural implications.

Evidence and epistemics

Every article enters through a Quinean coherentist evaluation: claims are tagged with warrant type, confidence, and contested status. Evidence is not filtered to what the Atlas likes — it is displayed with its uncertainty intact.
