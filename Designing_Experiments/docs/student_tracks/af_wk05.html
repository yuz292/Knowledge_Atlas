The Insight: Platform Choice Is Epistemological Choice

Last week you tested interfaces. This week you test what the platforms actually find. A claim can have strong evidence in Scite (many papers support it, few dispute it) while appearing weaker in Elicit (fewer high-quality studies meet the inclusion criteria) simultaneously. Neither platform is wrong. They index and rank differently. When ATLAS chooses to integrate Semantic Scholar instead of Consensus, ATLAS is not choosing a tool. It is choosing which evidence gets counted as evidence.

This week's work makes that choice deliberate and defensible. You will measure each platform's recall (did it find the papers we know exist?) and precision (when it returned results, how many were actually relevant?). You will test the Consensus confidence meter: when it says 70% of studies support a claim, does the citation distribution actually show 70% support? You will identify papers that one platform finds and another misses entirely, and ask: what indexing decision explains that gap?

By Friday, you will have the data needed to write D2, the full Usability Evaluation Report (8–10 pages). This report will sit at the center of ATLAS's platform selection decision. It is not just a class assignment. It is a research document that will be read by engineers building the production system.

Learning Outcomes

By completing this week's work, you will:

  • Detect indexing biases: When a paper is cited 50 times in Scite but never appears in Elicit, that is not random. It reflects indexing choices. You will learn to ask: what data does each platform have access to?

Three Core Concepts: Recall, Precision, and Citation Context

Recall: Did the Platform Find It?

Recall = (# of relevant papers the platform returned) / (# of relevant papers that exist)

You create a "gold set" of papers you know are relevant to biophilic design research. This gold set is your ground truth. You run a search query on each platform and count how many gold-set papers it retrieves. If your gold set has 10 papers and Semantic Scholar returns 7 of them, Semantic Scholar's recall is 70%.

Recall answers: "Can I trust this platform to NOT make me miss important evidence?" High recall means the platform is thorough. Low recall means you might be working with incomplete information.

Precision: When It Found Something, Was It Right?

Precision = (# of relevant papers the platform returned) / (# of papers the platform returned, total)

If OpenAlex returns 200 results for "biophilic design" but only 30 of them are actually relevant (the rest are about other topics), precision is 30/200 = 15%. Precision answers: "When this platform says a paper is relevant, can I trust that judgment?"

High precision means fewer false positives (wasted time reading irrelevant papers). Low precision means the platform returns a lot of noise.

Citation Context: How Is a Paper Actually Used?

Scite classifies how papers are cited: as supporting (the citing paper agrees with or builds on the cited paper), disputing (the citing paper challenges or contradicts the cited paper), or neutral (the citing paper mentions it without taking a stance). This matters. A paper can have 100 citations but be cited 90 times as a warning ("this study is flawed") and only 10 times as support. Scite shows this; most other platforms hide it.

Task 6: Validate Consensus's Confidence Meter

Consensus displays a "consensus meter" for some claims (e.g., "72% of studies found that biophilic design reduces stress"). Test whether this matches reality:

  1. Find a claim in Consensus that shows a consensus percentage (try a few searches).
  2. Note the percentage claimed (e.g., "71% positive," "19% negative").
  3. Go to Scite and search for the same papers on that topic.
  4. In Scite, use the citation classification: count supporting vs. disputing vs. neutral citations.
  5. Compare: Does Scite's citation distribution match Consensus's percentage?

Example:

  • Consensus says: "72% of studies agree that biophilic design reduces stress."
  • You go to Scite and check 50 citing papers: 38 are supporting (76%), 7 are disputing (14%), 5 are neutral (10%).
  • Scite shows 76% support; Consensus claimed 72%. Close enough? You note: "Consensus meter is reasonably calibrated but slightly optimistic."

Document your findings: Is the Consensus meter reliable?

Thursday: Precision & Gap Analysis

Task 7: Estimate Precision on Your Best Query

Pick your best-performing query from Week 4 (e.g., "Does biophilic design reduce stress?"). Run it on all five platforms. Take the top 20 results from each platform and rate whether each result is relevant to your topic.

Relevance scale: Clearly relevant (1) / Somewhat relevant (0.5) / Not relevant (0).

Precision = (sum of relevance scores) / 20.

Example: Semantic Scholar returns 20 papers for "biophilic design stress." You rate 15 as clearly relevant (15 points), 3 as somewhat relevant (1.5 points), 2 as not relevant (0 points). Precision = 16.5/20 = 82.5%.

Record precision for all five platforms.

Task 8: Gap Analysis – Papers Missed by Platforms

For any paper in your gold set that was NOT found by a platform, try to understand why:

  • Is the paper indexed at all in that platform's database? (Try searching the author name.)
  • Is the paper's DOI available in that platform?
  • Does the platform's search simply not match the paper's keywords?

Document 2–3 examples: "Paper X was found by Semantic Scholar but not Consensus. When I search [full title] in Consensus, it returns no results, suggesting Consensus lacks this paper's full-text or metadata."

Frequently Asked Questions

What counts as "found" in recall measurement?
A paper is "found" if the platform returns it in the top 50 results for your query. You must be able to identify it by title, DOI, or author—either as a clickable result or in metadata you can extract. If you have to click and read the abstract to confirm it is your gold paper, that still counts as "found."
My gold set is all papers from Week 3. That's too narrow.
Then add 2–3 additional papers from the references of those papers. Use Semantic Scholar's "cited by" feature to find papers that cite your D1 papers—pick a few that seem relevant. Your gold set should represent the breadth of biophilic design research: some on stress, some on cognitive performance, some on physical health. Diversity helps.
Should I count a paper "found" if it appears in a sidebar or "related papers" section?
Only if it appears in the main results list for your query. Sidebars and recommendations don't count—they are separate features. You are measuring search effectiveness, not recommendation quality.
What if a platform doesn't let me see 50 results?
Use whatever limit the platform allows (e.g., if it shows 20, record Recall@20 instead of Recall@50). Note this in your Methods section. Some platforms paginate results; if you can page through, go to 50. If not, use what you can access.
For precision, how do I decide if a paper is "relevant"?
Clearly relevant = the paper directly addresses one of your research questions (e.g., "Does biophilic design reduce stress?"). If the paper is about biophilic design but studies something unrelated to your focus (e.g., cost-benefit of biophilic design for real estate), it is somewhat relevant (0.5). If it is not about biophilic design at all, it is not relevant (0). Be consistent: create a rubric before rating and stick to it.
Should I include my D2 Part 1 (Week 4 usability matrix) data in my D2 final report?
Yes. Your D2 report should include a section (or subsection) summarizing Week 4 usability findings (think-aloud notes, interface observations), then build on that with Week 5 recall/precision data. Think of D2 Part 1 as your data collection; D2 final is your research synthesis.

The Deeper Question: Platform Selection as Epistemology

This week's work forces a crucial realization: when ATLAS chooses to query Semantic Scholar instead of Consensus, ATLAS is not just choosing a tool. It is choosing a theory of knowledge.

Semantic Scholar assumes that papers with more citations are more important. Consensus assumes that papers published more recently reflect current evidence. Scite assumes that how a paper is cited matters more than how many times it is cited. Each embeds a philosophical claim about what counts as valid evidence.

In Week 7, when you design the VOI-to-Query bridge, you will make this choice explicit. You will write logic like: "If the VOI question asks about current practice ('What is the state of the art?'), route to Semantic Scholar for recency. If it asks about consensus ('Do researchers agree?'), route to Consensus." This is not just smart engineering. This is epistemic design—intentionally choosing which theory of knowledge governs each query.

Your D2 report documents the evidence for these choices. It will sit in the codebase as a comment above the SourceSelector class, explaining to future engineers: this is why we made this decision, and this is what evidence supports it.
