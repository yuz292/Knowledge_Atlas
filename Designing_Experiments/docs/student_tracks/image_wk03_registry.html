Week 3 · Assignment D1b

The Registry Speaks One Language. You Will Add a Second.

Every scientific tag in the CNFA registry has a precise technical definition. None of them carry the plain-English words a participant uses when describing what they see — the words a vision model was trained on. This assignment builds that bridge.

Due: Sunday, Week 3 (11:59 PM) Weight: 5% of course grade Team size: 2 students Deliverable: Augmented registry entries (10 tags) as JSON + one visual mapping diagram

Ask a study participant to look at a photograph and describe it, and they will say something like "it feels cramped in there" or "there's a nice calm quality to it" or "it's a bit busy." They will not say "this image has a luminance variance of 0.62 and a fractal dimension of 1.34." The registry was built by researchers who speak the second language. Participants — and the vision models trained on human-generated descriptions — speak the first. For the tagging system to produce values that predict how people actually experience environments, both languages must live in the same registry entry.

There is also a validation reason. The pipeline checks each scientific extractor against VLM common-sense ratings: does a high fractal_dimension score correlate with images that VLMs call "complex" or "intricate"? That comparison is only possible if you know in advance which natural-language words a VLM would use to describe high values of each tag. The bilingual fields you add this week are what make that correlation study possible.

Learning Outcomes

  • 1Articulate the difference between scientific tag vocabulary and common-sense perceptual vocabulary, and explain why both are needed.
  • 2Add bilingual fields to 10 registry entries following the augmentation schema.
  • 3Identify synonyms, near-misses, and antonyms for each tag that will be useful for VLM convergent validation.
  • 4Produce a visual mapping diagram showing the relationship between three or more tags and their common-sense vocabulary clusters.

Part 1 — Two Languages Describing the Same Photograph

Scientific tag vocabulary is written for precision and reproducibility. The phrase fractal dimension of edge structure computed via box-counting at scales 4–128 px is unambiguous to someone who knows the field. It is meaningless to most people looking at a photograph, and it is not the register in which a vision-language model was trained. Ask GPT-4V to rate an image and it responds: "this scene feels complex and intricate, with lots of fine detail at multiple scales." That is useful. It is also not "this scene has a fractal dimension of 1.7." Both descriptions are about the same photograph. Your job is to map the territory between them.

The gap matters. Here is an illustration:

🔬 Scientific vocabulary

  • fractal_dimension — D ≈ 1.0–1.9 via box-counting
  • spatial_openness — visual penetration, low occlusion
  • color_entropy — Shannon entropy of HSV hue channel
  • edge_density — Canny edge pixels per unit area
  • luminance_variance — σ² of photometric luminance
  • bilateral_symmetry — mirror-image correlation score

🗣 Common-sense vocabulary

  • complex, intricate, organic, detailed, natural-looking, busy
  • open, spacious, airy, expansive, unenclosed, breathing room
  • colorful, varied, vivid, rainbow-like, muted, dull, grey
  • busy, sharp, crisp, textured, rough, fine-grained
  • bright, contrasty, high-key, dark, moody, flat, even
  • balanced, formal, regular, ordered, harmonious, centered
The bilingual layer maps each scientific construct to its natural-language neighbors — and documents where the mapping breaks down.

Why the mapping can break down

The common-sense words for a construct are not always what you would expect. Consider fractal_dimension. High fractal dimension means geometrically complex self-similar structure — trees, coastlines, ferns. In everyday language, people say such images look "natural," "organic," or "complex." But they also say some highly fractal scenes look "chaotic" while others look "peaceful." The emotional valence is not determined by the fractal dimension alone; it depends on what is generating the fractal structure. A carefully validated tag needs to know which common-sense words correlate reliably with the scientific measure and which are confounded by other factors.

Part 2 — The Augmentation Schema

The current registry entry format looks like this (simplified):

Current registry entry (before augmentation)
{
  "id": "fractal_dimension",
  "phase": "phase_0",
  "definition": "Hausdorff-Besicovitch fractal dimension of edge structure, computed via box-counting at multiple scales.",
  "range": [0.0, 1.0],
  "normalization": "(D - 1.0) / 1.0 where D is the raw log-log slope"
}

You will add a "bilingual" block to each entry containing the following fields:

Augmented entry format (with bilingual block)
{
  "id": "fractal_dimension",
  "phase": "phase_0",
  "definition": "Hausdorff-Besicovitch fractal dimension...",
  "range": [0.0, 1.0],
  "normalization": "(D - 1.0) / 1.0...",

  // ← NEW BLOCK
  "bilingual": {
    "common_name": "visual complexity / naturalness",
    "lay_definition": "How geometrically intricate a scene looks — the degree to which small details repeat and branch at multiple scales, the way tree branches or coastlines do.",
    "synonyms_high": ["complex", "intricate", "organic", "detailed", "natural-looking", "textured"],
    "synonyms_low":  ["smooth", "simple", "minimal", "geometric", "man-made", "clean"],
    "antonyms":      ["monotonous", "uniform", "plain", "featureless"],
    "near_misses": [
      {"word": "busy",   "note": "Correlates with high fractal_dimension but also with high object count — confounded."},
      {"word": "chaotic", "note": "Implies disorder; fractal_dimension is neutral on disorder — a forest is high-fractal but not chaotic."}
    ],
    "vlm_prompt_positive": "Does this image have complex, intricate, self-similar visual structure at multiple scales — like a forest, a fern, or a rough coastline?",
    "vlm_prompt_negative": "Is this image visually smooth, uniform, or geometrically simple — like a blank wall, a calm sea, or a flat sky?",
    "participant_probe":   "If you were describing this scene to a friend over the phone, would you say it looks complex and detailed, or simple and clean?",
    "validation_status":   "supported",
    "validation_notes":    "Hagerhall (2004) r=0.69 with preference ratings in natural scenes. Taylor (1999) confirms fractal D distinguishes natural from man-made at D≈1.3 boundary."
  }
}

Field definitions

FieldTypeDescription
common_namestring1–4 word plain English label — what you'd call this tag in conversation
lay_definitionstring30–60 word definition without jargon; use concrete analogies
synonyms_highstring[]Words that describe a scene with a high value of this tag
synonyms_lowstring[]Words that describe a scene with a low value of this tag
antonymsstring[]Words directly opposed to the high-value sense of the tag
near_missesobject[]Words that seem like synonyms but capture something subtly different — explain the distinction
vlm_prompt_positivestringQuestion you would ask a VLM to probe the high end of this tag (binary yes/no format)
vlm_prompt_negativestringMatching question probing the low end
participant_probestringNatural-language question you could ask a study participant to indirectly elicit ratings on this dimension
validation_statusenumOne of: provisional / supported / contested / deprecated
validation_notesstring1–2 sentence summary of the evidence for the validation status

Part 3 — Worked Example: Two Contrasting Tags

We show one entry that is relatively clean (the scientific-to-common-sense mapping is tight) and one that is messy (the mapping is contested). Notice how the near_misses and validation_notes fields do the intellectual heavy lifting in the messy case.

EXAMPLE A — CLEAN MAPPING color_entropy — Colorfulness / Variety

🔬 Scientific layer

Definition: Shannon entropy of the hue-channel histogram in HSV color space. High entropy = many distinct hues present in roughly equal proportions. Low entropy = dominated by one or two hues.

Range: 0.0 (monochrome) to 1.0 (maximally varied hue distribution)

Normalization: H_raw / log₂(256) where H_raw is histogram entropy in bits

🗣 Common-sense layer

Common name: colorfulness / hue variety

Lay definition: How many different colors appear in the image, and how evenly they are distributed — a rainbow garden scores high; a grey concrete wall scores near zero.

High synonyms: colorful, vivid, varied, rainbow-like, rich, polychromatic

Low synonyms: monochrome, muted, grey, bleached, washed-out, dull

Near miss: "saturated" — high saturation means intense colors, not varied colors. A deep red monochrome is saturated but has low color entropy.

Validation status: SUPPORTED (r = 0.77 with human colorfulness ratings in Hasler & Süsstrunk, 2003)
EXAMPLE B — CONTESTED MAPPING spatial_openness — Spaciousness / Expansiveness

🔬 Scientific layer

Definition: Degree to which a scene affords visual penetration to distance without occlusion — operationalized as sky-pixel fraction in top third + inverse of vertical occluder density in mid-ground band.

Range: 0.0 (completely enclosed) to 1.0 (panoramic outdoor)

Phase note: PROXY — the photographic proxy captures outdoor openness well but misses large interior spaces.

🗣 Common-sense layer

Common name: spaciousness / room to breathe

Lay definition: How open and uncrowded the space looks — whether you can see far into the distance without things blocking your view, and whether you'd feel you have room to move.

High synonyms: open, spacious, airy, expansive, unenclosed, wide, vast

Low synonyms: enclosed, cramped, narrow, hemmed-in, tunnel-like, confined

Near misses: "vast" (implies scale as well as openness), "empty" (lack of objects, not absence of enclosure), "cozy" (low openness but positively valenced)

Validation status: CONTESTED — proxy valid for outdoor scenes only; interior scope unresolved
What the examples teach you Example A shows a tag where the bilingual mapping is almost a one-to-one translation — the scientific measure directly captures what people mean by "colorful." Example B shows a tag where the mapping requires qualification: the scientific proxy captures outdoor spatial openness, and the near_misses field documents exactly where the everyday words go in a different direction. Your augmentations should reflect this level of nuance.

Part 4 — Your Assignment

Step 1: Select 10 tags to augment

Choose 10 tags from the registry. At least 6 must be from the phase_0 list. At least 2 must be from the stub list (where you will need to propose a bilingual framing even though the scientific definition is not yet finalized). The remaining 2 can be phase_1 tags of your choosing.

1
Generate synonym lists using AI + your own judgment

For each tag, start with an AI-generated list of synonyms. Ask Claude: "List 10 words a person might use to describe a scene that is high on [tag name]. List another 10 for a scene that is low on [tag name]. Then identify 3 words that seem like synonyms but actually capture something subtly different." Then revise this list based on your own experience with the images. The AI will get most of the obvious ones; the near-misses require your judgment.

2
Write the VLM prompts carefully

The vlm_prompt_positive and vlm_prompt_negative fields will be used in the validation pipeline. A VLM will be asked these questions for every image in the corpus, and the binary yes/no responses will be correlated against your scientific extractor. This means the prompts must be (a) answerable from a photograph, (b) specific enough to avoid confounds, and (c) phrased in the natural register that VLMs respond reliably to. Test your prompts on 5 images before finalizing them.

3
Write the augmented JSON entries

Add your "bilingual" block to each of the 10 entries. Save your output as data/registry_v0.2.8_bilingual_[TeamName].json — do not overwrite the original. Your file should contain only the 10 augmented entries (not all 424 tags), each as a complete JSON object in an array.

4
Produce the vocabulary mapping diagram

Choose 3 of your 10 tags and draw a vocabulary cluster diagram showing how common-sense words group around the scientific constructs. Tags that share vocabulary should have overlapping clusters; near-misses should appear at the edge of a cluster with a dotted connection. The diagram can be a hand-drawn scan, a Miro/FigJam export, or a simple HTML/SVG file — format does not matter, but it should be legible and labeled.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can we use AI to generate all 10 bilingual blocks automatically? ▼

AI can generate the first draft of synonyms, the lay definition, and the VLM prompts. But the near_misses and validation_notes must come from you. The near_misses field requires you to understand what the scientific tag is actually measuring and how it differs from nearby constructs — that understanding comes from reading the primary literature (even briefly) and looking at real images. The validation_notes field requires you to cite actual empirical evidence.

A practical workflow: use AI to draft the lay_definition and synonyms, then read the primary reference for the tag (often one paper is enough), then revise the draft based on what the paper actually says the construct correlates with. That revision process is where the intellectual work happens.

What counts as a "near miss" — how different does the word have to be? ▼

A near miss is a word that a naive person would reasonably use as a synonym but that fails in at least one identifiable condition. The test is: can you describe a specific type of image where the near-miss word applies but your tag value is not high (or vice versa)? If yes, it is a near miss and worth documenting. If you cannot construct such an example, the word is probably a genuine synonym.

For example: "bright" for luminance_variance. A bright image (high mean luminance) is not the same as a high-contrast image (high luminance variance). A foggy scene can be quite bright (high mean luminance) while having low variance. That is the specific counterexample that makes "bright" a near miss for luminance_variance.

How do we test our VLM prompts on 5 images? ▼

Open Claude or GPT-4V, upload 5 images from data/corpus_sample/, and send your prompt as a message. You want to cover the range: pick 2 images you expect the model to answer "yes," 2 you expect "no," and 1 ambiguous case. Check: (a) does the model answer yes/no cleanly, or does it hedge? (b) does it explain its reasoning in terms you recognize as relevant to your scientific construct? (c) does the ambiguous image produce a hedged answer or a confident wrong answer?

If the model consistently gives confident wrong answers on your ground-truth known cases, your prompt is not working. Common fixes: add more context about what the tag means, mention a concrete example image type, or rephrase from a binary question to a rating question and then threshold the rating yourself.

What should the vocabulary cluster diagram look like? ▼

Think of it as a Venn-diagram-style layout where each scientific tag is a labeled circle, and the common-sense words float around the circle in proportion to how strongly they associate with that tag. Words that appear in multiple tags' synonym lists appear in the overlap region between circles. Near-miss words appear at the periphery of a circle with a dashed connection line.

The diagram does not need to be beautiful. The requirement is that it makes the vocabulary relationships visible: which tags share common-sense vocabulary, and which are distinct. A hand-drawn version with labeled bubbles is entirely acceptable. If you use Miro, FigJam, or a draw.io file, export as a PNG or PDF for submission.
