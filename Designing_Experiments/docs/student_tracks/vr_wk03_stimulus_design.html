Literature Review, Apparatus Training, Stimulus Dimension Specification

Where You Are in the Project
What Came Before

This Week (Week 3)
You formalize your stimulus design: choose a dimension, identify literature evidence, specify ≥3 levels to test, create a held-constant list, write rationale, and predict preference functions.
What Comes Next

Learning Outcomes
  • Design Specification: Articulate a specific stimulus dimension (e.g., spatial openness, lighting quality) grounded in cognitive science or human factors literature, with a clear operational definition.
  • Stimulus Levels: Identify ≥3 distinguishable levels of your dimension and justify the choice of levels based on literature and design constraints.
  • Held-Constant Variables: List and justify all design parameters that will remain constant across stimulus levels to isolate the effect of your chosen dimension.
  • Preference Prediction: Generate predictions about how participants' preferences will vary across stimulus levels, based on theory and prior findings.
The Stimulus Design Plan (D1)

This deliverable is a detailed specification of the VR scenes you will build in Weeks 4–5. It is not a literature review or essay; it is a structured design document that an engineer could use to build your scenes without ambiguity. It must include evidence from your literature search to justify each design choice.

D1 Must Include These 5 Sections
Section 1
Stimulus Dimension & Rationale
Define your chosen dimension in one paragraph. Name ≥2 citations that support why this dimension matters for human perception or preference in your domain (e.g., lighting and attention, spatial openness and restoration). Be specific about the mechanism (e.g., "color temperature affects circadian arousal via melanopsin pathways").
Section 2
Stimulus Levels & Operationalization
List ≥3 levels of your dimension (e.g., cool daylight 5600K, warm incandescent 2700K, fluorescent 4000K). For each level, provide a specific, measurable description (units, values, asset names). This section is a checklist for your production team.
Section 3
Held-Constant Parameters
List all design variables that will NOT vary across your stimulus levels (e.g., room dimensions 4m × 4m × 3m, furniture set {table, chairs}, avatar presence yes/no, interaction mechanics). Format as a table. This ensures your dimension is isolated.
Section 4
Design Rationale & Predicted Preference Function
Explain why you predict participants will prefer one level over others. Ground prediction in theory (e.g., inverted-U function for arousal, linear for authenticity). Include a simple sketch or table showing predicted preference × stimulus level. What prior studies support your prediction?
Section 5
Technical & Production Notes
Briefly note any Unity assets, plugins, or rendering techniques you'll use to achieve each stimulus level (e.g., "HDRP Post-Process Volume for color temperature grading," "Quixel Megascans for plant assets"). This helps peers understand feasibility.

Suggested Length: 4–6 pages. Format: PDF, Word, or Google Doc. Due: End of Week 3 (class submission portal). Submit As: Team PDF named D1_StimulusDesignPlan_[YourNames].pdf

Step-by-Step Workflow
1
Review Your D0 Reflection & Identify a Stimulus Dimension

Open the reflection you wrote in Week 2. What design question did you pose? What gap in the literature did you identify? Choose ONE dimension that directly addresses your question.

Example: If your question was "Does restorativeness depend on spatial geometry?" choose "spatial openness" as your dimension.
If unclear, discuss with your pair and TA this week (office hours).
2
Conduct Targeted Literature Search (AI-Assisted)

Build on your Week 2 search. Use Google Scholar to find papers specifically about your chosen dimension. Look for:

Empirical studies testing your dimension in lab or field settings
Psychophysical measurement papers (e.g., how to quantify color temperature, spatial scale)
Prior VR/3D visualization studies on the same dimension

Target: ≥5 new papers, at least 2 empirical studies.

3
Define Stimulus Levels (with AI Assistance)

Using literature + domain knowledge, decide on ≥3 levels of your dimension. Each level must be:

Operationalized: Expressed in measurable units (e.g., "2700K–3000K warm," "5600K–6500K cool")
Distinguishable: Visually or perceptually different enough that humans notice
Feasible: Can be implemented in Unity without excessive complexity

Example Levels for Lighting Quality:

Level 1 (Cool): 5600K daylight, diffuse (overcast sky), no visible shadows
Level 2 (Neutral): 4000K (fluorescent), moderate directional light, soft shadows
Level 3 (Warm): 2700K incandescent, strong directional source, sharp shadows
4
Create a Held-Constant Checklist (with AI Assistance)

List every design variable in your scenes that will NOT change across stimulus levels. This isolates your dimension and ensures comparability. Use a table format:

Variable              | Value / Constraint
Room dimensions       | 4m (W) × 4m (D) × 3m (H)
Base furniture        | 1 table (2m × 1m), 4 chairs, 1 bookcase
Flooring material     | Light oak hardwood
Avatar presence       | Yes (default avatar, no customization)
Interaction mechanic  | Teleport locomotion only (no smooth movement)
Sound                 | Ambient background (35 dB SPL constant)
Temporal setting      | Daytime (implied from lighting)
Think broadly: geometry, materials, objects, interactivity, audio, temporal context, weather/sky.
5
Draft Design Rationale & Prediction

Write 1–2 paragraphs (with AI help) explaining:

Why this dimension matters: What does literature say about its psychological or perceptual effects?
Your prediction: How will preference vary across your levels? (linear increase, inverted U, threshold effect?)
Citation support: Name ≥2 studies that support your prediction.

Example (Spatial Openness): "We predict an inverted-U function: moderate spatial openness (semi-open room) will maximize restoration ratings compared to enclosed corridor or fully open atrium. This aligns with Kaplan & Kaplan's (1989) information-rate theory: environments with moderate complexity and visual depth optimize attentional recovery."

6
Identify Assets & Tools You'll Use (Brief Technical Section)

List Unity plugins, asset libraries, or rendering techniques that will enable your stimulus levels. Be specific:

For Lighting: HDRP (High Definition Render Pipeline) + Post-Process Volume (for color temperature grading), or Colorgrading LUT textures
For Spatial Geometry: ProBuilder (built-in geometry tool) or custom meshes from Blender
For Biophilic Content: Quixel Megascans (free in-engine plant library), or SpeedTree for vegetation

Research feasibility. Do you have licenses? Do tutorials exist? Can your pair learn the tool in 2–3 days? If a tool is too hard, pivot to a simpler alternative.

7
Attend Mandatory VR Training Lab Session

Schedule and attend during lab hours (see section above). Complete all training modules. Take notes on motion control, safety, and build/deploy mechanics—you'll need this for production starting Week 4.

8
Assemble D1 & Submit

Compile Sections 1–5 into a PDF or shared Google Doc. Check that all sections are complete, citations are present, and technical notes are feasible. Submit to the course portal by end of day Friday.

Use a consistent format (headings, tables, figures where helpful).
Have your pair review before submission (peer check).

Where AI Tools Help Most This Week
Literature Evidence Review & Citation Synthesis: Use Claude or ChatGPT to accelerate your search results analysis. Paste abstracts and ask the tool to summarize the effect sizes, sample sizes, and key findings. This cuts reading time from 4 hrs to 1.5 hrs.
"I found these 3 papers on lighting and mood. Summarize the main finding, effect size, and sample size for each. Which studies are most robust?"
Held-Constant Checklist Creation: AI is excellent at generating comprehensive checklists. Describe your scene concept, and ask the tool to propose a held-constant list. You'll refine it, but this saves 30 minutes of brainstorming.
"I'm designing VR scenes to test spatial openness (enclosed vs. semi-open vs. fully open). What variables must I hold constant to isolate that effect?"
Design Rationale & Prediction Writing: Once you have outline notes, use AI to draft your 1–2 paragraph rationale. Give the tool your stimulus levels, predicted preference function, and 2–3 key citations. Edit the output for accuracy and clarity (AI often overstates certainty—watch for that).
"Write a design rationale for why I predict preference will be highest for moderate spatial openness (semi-open room) compared to enclosed and fully open. Here are my key sources: [paste citations]."
Technical Feasibility Assessment: Ask AI whether your chosen dimension and tools are realistic for a 2-student, 2-week production sprint. AI can flag potential pitfalls (e.g., "Advanced Shader Graph color temperature grading has a steep learning curve; consider using simpler Post-Process Volume adjustment + LUT instead").
"Can a pair of students with no Unity experience build 5 VR scenes with varying color temperatures in 2 weeks using HDRP? What's the fastest path?"
Format & Structure Checking: Before submitting D1, use AI as a proofreader. Paste your draft and ask it to flag missing sections, unclear operationalizations, or unsupported claims. This ensures D1 is complete and professional.
"Review this D1 outline against the 5-section rubric. Are all sections present? Is the held-constant list comprehensive?"

Frequently Asked Questions
Q: Can I change my stimulus dimension after Week 3?
A: It's possible but costly. If you realize your dimension is infeasible or uninteresting by mid-Week 3, talk to the TA immediately. You can pivot, but you'll lose 1–2 days of progress. Best to commit by Thursday so you enter production Week 4 confidently.
Q: How many literature citations do I really need for D1?
A: Minimum: 3 citations (1 for why the dimension matters, ≥2 for your prediction). Ideal: 5–7, showing you engaged deeply with the literature. Quality beats quantity—understand what you cite, not just find papers that mention keywords.
Q: What if I don't know Unity yet? Can I still design scenes?
A: Absolutely. This week is design and planning; implementation happens in Weeks 4–5. Use this week to learn Unity basics (see Resources). The VR training lab covers fundamentals. By Week 4, you'll be ready to build.
Q: How detailed should my technical notes section be?
A: Detailed enough that another student could understand what tools you'll use, but not so detailed that you're writing code. 1–2 paragraphs naming assets, plugins, and rendering techniques is perfect. Save implementation details for Week 4 design documents.
Q: Can our pair do different stimulus dimensions?
A: No. Your pair chooses one dimension and builds all scenes together. This ensures you develop shared expertise and produce coherent scenes. The course structure gives each 3-person track a different dimension (see VR Track Overview), so you're not duplicating work.
