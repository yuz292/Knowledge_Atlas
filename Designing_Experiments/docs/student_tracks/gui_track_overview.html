1. Track Overview & Learning Objectives

Big Picture: The ATLAS system is built for multiple user types—researchers, architects, domain experts, administrators, students, and the general public. But not all of them experience the interface well. Your job is to systematically evaluate ATLAS for usability across all these user types, identify the pain points, propose improvements, and prototype AI-powered help systems that make the interface more effective. By the end of this track, you'll deliver a comprehensive usability evaluation and improvement roadmap that will guide development for the Fall 2026 COGS160: AI & VR course.

Learning Objectives

  • User-centered design thinking: Understand how to design for multiple user archetypes, not just the "expert" user
  • Usability evaluation methods: Conduct think-aloud protocols, analyze video transcripts for confusion points, and synthesize findings into actionable recommendations
  • Human-AI interaction: Design context-sensitive AI help systems that augment human understanding without creating dependency
  • Iterative design: Use simulation and critique to propose improvements, implement them on Git branches, and validate using UI evaluation agents
  • Classroom-scale system design: Design for a specific learning context (COGS160: AI & VR Fall) with constraints on time, background knowledge, and motivation

4. Detailed Task Breakdown

4a. User Type Identification & Persona Creation

What You're Building

A comprehensive catalog of all ATLAS user archetypes, with rich personas that make them vivid and understandable. This is not a generic list—each persona should have a name, face (AI-generated avatar), background, specific goals, frustrations, and critical use cases.

Step 1: Identify All User Types

From the ATLAS system design documentation, enumerate every distinct user archetype:

  • Admin: Manages the ATLAS instance, configures databases, monitors system health
  • Researcher: Uses ATLAS to find and organize evidence for a meta-analysis or review
  • Architect/Designer: Uses ATLAS to find design precedents and environmental psychology principles for a project
  • Student (COGS160: AI & VR Fall): Encounters ATLAS for the first time with limited prior knowledge; must complete assignments
  • Domain Expert: Academic specialist in environmental psychology, cognition, or related field
  • ANFA Member: Architect/designer from a professional organization seeking applied guidance
  • Curious Public: General interest in environment and psychology; no prior training
  • KA Builder/Developer: Contributes evidence or builds features within ATLAS

Step 2: Create Vivid Personas

For each user type, create a persona document with:

  • Name & Avatar (AI-generated image for visual reference)
  • Background: Education, experience, domain familiarity
  • Goals: What they want to accomplish with ATLAS (2–3 primary goals)
  • Frustrations: What might confuse or block them
  • Technical Comfort: Comfort with databases, academic search interfaces, coding
  • Time Budget: How much time do they have to learn the system?
  • Critical Use Cases: Specific workflows they must complete

AI Help: Persona Generation Prompt

Prompt for Claude:

You are helping design personas for the ATLAS system. I will give you a user type. Generate a vivid persona with: - Name and brief background (2-3 sentences) - 3-4 specific goals they want to accomplish with ATLAS - 3-4 likely frustrations or confusion points - What they already know vs. what is new to them Make it realistic and memorable, not generic.

Step 3: Conduct Simulated First-Time User Walkthroughs (Think-Aloud Protocol)

This is critical: for each user type, recruit ONE person (from other tracks or friends) who matches that persona, and record them using the ATLAS site for the first time. No prior instructions—they should come in cold.

  • Setup: Zoom recording with screen share + audio. User is in their own environment.
  • Script: "You are [PERSONA]. You need to [TASK]. Go ahead and try to complete it. Talk out loud about what you see, what you're thinking, where you're clicking, and whether things make sense to you."
  • Example Task Scenarios:
    • Architect: "Find evidence about how window size affects cognitive performance in office buildings"
    • Student: "Find 3 studies about color and mood, then summarize their main findings"
    • Curious Public: "I want to learn about how plants affect mental health. Where would you go on this site?"
    • Admin: "Check the system's current data ingestion rate and verify the annotation pipeline is running"
  • Record: Save the Zoom recording; transcribe it (AI-assisted transcription is fine)

Step 4: Identify Confusion Hot Spots

Once you have the transcript, feed it to Claude:

AI Help: Confusion Point Identification Prompt

Prompt for Claude:

Below is a transcript of a user trying ATLAS for the first time. Analyze it and identify: 1. Every moment where the user seemed confused, uncertain, or stuck 2. What they were trying to do at that moment 3. What about the interface or workflow made it unclear 4. What they needed to know that wasn't visible Format as a table: [Timestamp] | [Action] | [Confusion] | [Root Cause] | [Needed Info]

Aggregate the confusion findings across all your user recordings into a "Confusion Hot Spots" table. Prioritize by frequency.

4b. COGS160: AI & VR Fall Class Usability

What You're Solving For

In Fall 2026, 30 students will use ATLAS for the first time. They will have no prior context, about 50 minutes to get oriented, and 8 weeks to use the system for coursework. Your job: anticipate what will confuse them.

Key Question

What will block a Fall 2026 COGS160: AI & VR student in their first week on the system?

  • What is the onboarding flow? Is it clear?
  • What workflows are critical for Fall assignments? Are they discoverable?
  • What terminology is non-obvious?
  • What features exist but are hidden?

Method

Recruit classmates from OTHER tracks (Image Tagger, Article Finder, VR) and have them simulate a "naïve first-timer" on ATLAS. You spring students will have forgotten your first experience—your classmates haven't.

Run the same think-aloud protocol, but focus the task on Fall-specific workflows:

  • "You are a COGS160: AI & VR student in Fall. Your assignment is to find 5 studies about lighting and mood, then write a one-paragraph summary of what the evidence says. Try that now on ATLAS."
  • "You need to compare two different effect sizes from two different studies. Can you do that? How would you?"
  • "Your prof said ATLAS has information about what phenomena are well-supported vs. speculative. Where would you look for that?"

Deliverable for D1

Include in your D1 a "Fall Readiness Assessment" section noting: expected friction points, recommended onboarding improvements, and critical workflows that need to be visible/intuitive.

4c. AI Help System Prototype

What You're Building

A design document and working prototype showing how Claude (or another LLM) can provide context-sensitive help on the ATLAS site. The help system should know: what page the user is on, what they just clicked, what they typed, and what they seem to be trying to do. Then it offers relevant guidance in real time.

Step 1: Design the AI Help Architecture

  • Context Tracking: What information should the help system track?
    • Current page/URL
    • Last user action (click, form submission, search query)
    • User type (if known from login)
    • What the user typed (search query, filter values, etc.)
    • Error messages encountered
  • Help Triggers: When should help appear?
    • User clicks a help icon
    • User pauses for 10+ seconds on a page
    • User triggers an error
    • User is on a page that historically causes confusion
  • Response Generation: How does Claude formulate the response?
    • Feed context (page, action, user type) to Claude
    • Claude generates a short, task-focused explanation
    • Response includes: "What you're trying to do", "How to do it here", "Related features"
    • Keep responses under 150 words

Step 2: Write Example Prompts

Create concrete prompts that show how Claude should behave in different contexts:

AI Help: Context-Sensitive Help Prompts

Example 1 — User on Image Tagger Page, Searching by Tag:

User context: - Page: /images/tagger - Last action: User clicked on "biophilic_richness" tag - User type: Student - Error: None visible Generate a short help response explaining: 1. What "biophilic_richness" means in this context 2. How to use this tag to search for images 3. What they can do next (e.g., combine with other tags)

Example 2 — User on VOI Page, Confused:

User context: - Page: /evidence/voi-matrix - Last action: User hovered over a gap cell for 3+ seconds - User type: Architect - Search query from page: "window size cognitive" Generate a help response explaining: 1. What a "VOI gap" is and why it matters 2. How to interpret the color coding 3. What to do next (e.g., find literature, propose research)

Step 3: Build a Prototype

Create a simple, static HTML/JavaScript prototype showing the help system in action. You don't need a backend—this is a mock-up showing the UX flow.

  • Display a fake ATLAS page
  • Include a "Help" button (or trigger on hover)
  • Show context information (page, last action, user type)
  • Display Claude's response in a help panel (you can use a made-up response or actual Claude-generated text)
  • Show how help updates when user clicks different areas

Step 4: Synthesize Pair Reports

Both Pair 2 and Pair 3 should contribute to a collective "AI Help & System Improvements Synthesis" document that you present to Prof. Kirsh. This document should include:

  • The help system architecture and example prompts
  • Projected impact: which user types benefit most?
  • Limitations and failure modes
  • Implementation roadmap

Prompt for Claude:

A sample context state (page, action, error) 2. The AI help response Claude should generate 3. How this help addresses the root cause Then assess: which user types gain the most benefit?

4e. UI Evaluation Agent Critique

What You're Solving For

The ATLAS system may already have a UI evaluation agent (or similar tool) that simulates user interactions and identifies usability issues. Your job: understand what it does, test it critically, identify its blind spots, and improve it if possible.

Step 1: Locate & Understand the UI Eval Agent

  • Search the codebase for UI evaluation agent code (look in `services/`, `agents/`, or `tools/`)
  • Read its documentation: What does it measure? What user types does it simulate? What issues can it detect?
  • Review its architecture: How does it interact with ATLAS? What is the output format?

Step 2: Test the Agent Against Your User Personas

Run the agent on simulated tasks for each user type:

  • Task for Admin: "Monitor the system's data ingestion pipeline"
  • Task for Researcher: "Find all studies on biophilic design and mental health"
  • Task for Student: "Complete the Week 3 assignment: find 5 studies on color and mood"
  • Task for Architect: "Find design precedents for a high-restorativeness office environment"

For each task, capture:

  • What issues did the agent identify?
  • Were those issues real and important?
  • Did the agent miss any issues YOU noticed in user testing?

Step 3: Write a Deficiency Report

Assess the agent honestly:

  • What the agent does well: List genuine strengths
  • Blind spots: What kinds of usability issues does it miss?
    • Does it miss context-dependent confusion? (e.g., students vs. researchers have different mental models)
    • Does it miss workflow issues? (e.g., the task is technically doable but requires 10 clicks instead of 2)
    • Does it miss aesthetic issues? (e.g., visual hierarchy, color contrast)
    • Does it miss accessibility issues?
  • Suggested improvements: How could the agent be better?

4f. System Viewers Evaluation

What You're Solving For

ATLAS has several "admin viewers" and data visualization tools for understanding how the system works. These are meant to be transparent—they show you what's happening under the hood. But are they actually understandable? Would a student in Fall be able to use them to learn about the system?

Key Question

If a Fall 2026 COGS160: AI & VR student looked at these viewers, would they understand what they're seeing?

Step 1: Identify All System Viewers

Look at the Streamlit app (or web interface). Pages 11–14 typically include:

  • VOI Stream Viewer: Live feed of new evidence being added to the system
  • Annotation Types Viewer: What kinds of annotations are users making? Statistics.
  • Interpretation Monitor: How interpretations are being rated and voted on
  • EN Graph Viewer: The Evidential Network (how evidence interconnects)
  • Any others you find

Step 2: For Each Viewer, Assess:

  • Clarity: Do you understand what you're looking at on first glance?
    • Are axes labeled? Are colors explained?
    • Is there a legend or explanatory text?
    • What is the purpose of this visualization?
  • Pedagogical Value: What can a student learn from this?
    • Does it show system health?
    • Does it show user activity and engagement?
    • Does it show the quality or type of contributions?
    • Does it help students understand the ATLAS design?
  • Scaffolding: Is there enough explanatory support?
    • Is there a caption explaining what you're seeing?
    • Are there example interpretations or scenarios?
    • Could a student answer questions like "what does this spike mean?" just by looking?
  • Interaction: Can students explore the data or are they just passive observers?
    • Can they hover for details?
    • Can they filter or zoom?
    • Can they compare time periods or user segments?

Step 3: Improvement Proposals

For each viewer, propose specific improvements:

  • Add captions: 2-3 sentence explanation of what the viewer shows and why it matters
  • Add legends/color keys: Ensure all visual encodings are explained
  • Add example scenarios: Show what different patterns would look like (e.g., "a healthy system looks like this; a problem state looks like this")
  • Add interactivity: Hover tooltips, filters, comparison tools
  • Restructure the layout: Maybe the most important information isn't visible first

Step 4: Deliverable for D3 & D5

Your "System Viewers Assessment" should be part of the D3 "Simulated Expert Panel Critique" and expanded in D5. Include:

  • A table assessing each viewer on the four criteria above
  • Specific improvement proposals with mockups or before/after designs (if possible)
  • Priority ranking: which viewers need the most help?

Success Criteria

  • All major system viewers have been evaluated
  • Each viewer has been assessed on clarity, pedagogical value, scaffolding, and interactivity
  • Improvement proposals are concrete and implementable
  • Assessment reflects realistic expectations for Fall students

7. Frequently Asked Questions

❓ How do I recruit people to simulate user types if I only know spring COGS160: AI & VR classmates?
Great question. You have options: (1) Ask classmates from OTHER tracks (Image Tagger, Article Finder, VR). They have similar preparation to you but can act as "first-timers" if they haven't tried ATLAS yet. (2) Ask friends outside the course who match the persona (e.g., a friend who's an architecture major for the architect persona, a grad student in psychology for the domain expert, etc.). (3) Recruit via UCSD research participation boards or ask your TA for suggestions. The key: find people who genuinely haven't used ATLAS before and can give honest first reactions. Paying them $10-15 as thank-you for 30 min of their time is often worth it.
❓ What if I can't implement the GUI improvements I propose? Can I just submit mockups or designs?
Absolutely. If implementation is blocked (you don't know the tech stack, it's too complex), you can submit: (1) Detailed wireframe or mockup (Figma, hand-drawn scans, or HTML sketches) (2) Pseudocode or a description of the code changes needed (3) A note on what would be required to implement it (e.g., "requires React component refactor") The point is to demonstrate you've identified real problems and thought through solutions, even if you can't code them all. Prof. Kirsh values the analysis and design thinking as much as the finished implementation.
❓ How long should each user testing session be? And how many users per persona do I need?
Guidelines: **Duration**: 20-30 minutes per session. Give them a clear task and let them work through it. **Number of users per persona**: Ideally 2-3 per persona. This gives you statistical power (you'll see repeated patterns) without requiring massive recruiting effort. For 8 personas, that's 16-24 sessions total—manageable if split across your pair over 2-3 weeks. **If you only get 1 per persona**: That's okay, but note the limitation. Your findings are suggestive, not definitive. **Red flag**: If you only test with your close friends or classmates, you'll get biased results (they're likely similar to you). Try to get some demographic/background diversity.
❓ The "AI Help System" sounds complex. Do I really need to build a backend?
No. You can—and probably should—mock this up first: (1) Build a static HTML prototype showing ATLAS + a help panel (2) Use JavaScript to track simulated "user actions" (clicks, form inputs) (3) Paste in example Claude responses for different contexts (you can generate these once, then reuse) (4) Show how the help panel updates as the user "interacts" with the fake site This demonstrates the UX and the prompt design without needing a real backend. If Prof. Kirsh wants a backend integration later, that's phase 2. For Week 6 D4, a polished mockup is perfect.
❓ What if the UI evaluation agent doesn't exist or I can't find it?
Good catch. If the agent doesn't exist: (1) Build a simple one yourself. You can write a Python script that: - Takes a user task as input - Simulates a user clicking through the site - Records what they successfully completed vs. where they got stuck - Outputs a report of usability issues (2) Or write a text-based agent using Claude's API: - Give Claude the ATLAS site structure - Ask it to simulate a user of type X trying task Y - Have it output issues found This becomes a nice deliverable even if it's your own creation. Pair it with a critique explaining what a production UI agent should do.
❓ How do I balance being critical of ATLAS while not being unfairly harsh?
This is the heart of good evaluation. Best practices: (1) **Ground criticism in evidence**: Don't say "this is confusing." Say "User 4 and User 5 both got stuck here; the transcript shows they spent 3+ minutes looking for X." (2) **Separate the interface from the system**: A design problem doesn't mean the underlying system is bad. ATLAS's information model might be great; the UI might just need help. (3) **Acknowledge context**: A feature that's confusing for a student might make perfect sense to a researcher. Document that nuance. (4) **Celebrate what works**: In your findings, note what's working well too. Don't let problems overshadow strengths. (5) **Propose solutions**: Criticism without solutions is not useful. Always pair a problem with 2-3 possible fixes. Prof. Kirsh wants honest evaluation, but framed constructively.
❓ Can I use Claude to help analyze the user testing transcripts? Or does that "cheat"?
**Absolutely use Claude.** In fact, the system-wide guidance requires it. Here's the distinction: **Your job**: Watch the videos, take notes, notice patterns, form hypotheses. **Claude's job**: Accelerate the analysis. Feed it the transcript and ask structured questions (confusion points, root causes, missing information). Use Claude to organize and summarize your observations, not replace them. **What would be cheating**: Handing Claude a transcript, skimming Claude's output without verification, and claiming that as your finding. **What's good practice**: Watch the video yourself, note key moments, then ask Claude to systematically extract confusion points. Verify Claude's output against the video. Iterate. This is AI-augmented analysis, which is exactly what this course is training you for.

Key Takeaways for GUI Evaluation Students

  • User type matters: The same feature that's intuitive for a researcher might confuse a student. Design for heterogeneous users.
  • Think-aloud protocol is gold: Video transcripts of real users reveal blind spots no amount of hypotheticals can match.
  • AI helps, but human judgment decides: Claude can generate personas, analyze transcripts, and propose improvements. You interpret what matters and why.
  • Fall readiness is the north star: Every recommendation should connect back to: "Will this help Fall students succeed?"
  • Before/after is powerful: Screenshots and Git branches make your improvements tangible and reviewable.
