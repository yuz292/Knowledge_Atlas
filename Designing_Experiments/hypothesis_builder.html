Formulate Your Hypothesis

A hypothesis is not a guess. It is a specific, falsifiable set of predictions about what you will observe — grounded in what you know about the system you are studying. That grounding can come from deep theory, from measurement science, or from well-established empirical regularities. In the behavioral sciences, all three are legitimate starting points.

Before You Begin

From Interest to Testable Predictions

Students often arrive at this step with a topic of interest and call it a hypothesis. "I'm interested in how nature affects mood" is not a hypothesis — it is the starting point of a journey that ends with one. Understanding the stages between an interest and a set of testable predictions is essential, because each stage represents a different degree of intellectual commitment, and experiments can only test the final form.

The journey from interest to testable predictions passes through several stages, each more specific than the last:

1

Interest or Topic

An interest is a domain you find compelling. It has no structure, no commitment, no testable content. It is where curiosity begins — and where many students stop.

Example: "I'm interested in how plants affect people in offices."

This is not wrong — it is simply not yet scientific. It names a domain but commits to nothing testable.

↓ narrow the scope
2

Research Question

A research question narrows the interest to a specific relationship between variables. It asks something concrete about the world — but it does not yet commit to an expected answer. A well-formed research question specifies the environmental variable (what you will manipulate), the human outcome (what you will measure), and the population (whom you will study).

Example: "Does the presence of living plants in a VR-simulated office environment affect directed attention capacity in undergraduate students who have performed a cognitively demanding task?"

This is a genuine question — it could be answered either way. It specifies the IV (plants vs. no plants), the DV (directed attention), and the context (VR office, post-fatigue). But it does not predict the answer.

↓ commit to specific, observable predictions
3

Predictions Grounded in Measurement Science

A set of predictions specifies what you expect to observe and how you will observe it. In the behavioral and biological sciences, this grounding often comes not from deep causal theory but from measurement science — the accumulated empirical knowledge of how to detect and quantify the phenomena you care about. You know that stress shows up in salivary cortisol ~20 minutes after onset, because endocrinology has established this. You know that directed attention can be measured by the Attention Network Test, because cognitive psychologists have validated it. You do not need a complete theory of why the HPA axis responds the way it does — you need the well-established empirical fact that cortisol tracks stress.

This is an important distinction from how prediction works in physics or chemistry, where predictions typically follow deductively from fundamental theory. In our science, the systems are complex, and findings are typically about the behavior of that complex system. The theoretical account of how it works comes later — after you have established what happens and how to detect it. A set of well-grounded predictions based on measurement science and empirical regularities is perfectly legitimate science. It is not a lesser form of hypothesis.

Example: "I predict that nature exposure will reduce physiological stress, measured by salivary cortisol at T+20 minutes and by increased HRV measured continuously via chest-strap monitor. I also predict improved directed attention, measured by the ANT. I know cortisol tracks HPA-axis stress response and HRV tracks parasympathetic activation because the measurement literature establishes this — not because I have a theory of why nature affects these systems."

This is testable, falsifiable, and grounded in empirical measurement science. It specifies what to measure, when to measure it, and what outcome would disconfirm the prediction. It is a legitimate hypothesis even though the grounding is empirical rather than theoretical.

↓ optionally: ground it in a theory of why
4

Theory-Grounded Hypothesis

A theory-grounded hypothesis goes beyond measurement-based predictions by adding a causal explanation: why you expect what you expect. It says: "Theory T claims mechanism M. If M operates under conditions C, then we should observe outcome O." This is the form of hypothesis familiar from philosophy of science textbooks, and it is especially powerful because a negative result challenges the theory — not just the prediction. But it is important to understand that this level of theoretical commitment is an ambition, not a prerequisite. Most environmental psychology operates with well-grounded predictions, and theoretical explanations are built up gradually from empirical findings.

Example: "Attention Restoration Theory (Kaplan, 1995) holds that natural environments restore directed attention capacity by engaging involuntary attention through 'soft fascination.' This predicts that participants who spend 15 minutes in a VR office with living plants will score higher on the Attention Network Test than those in a matched VR office without plants, specifically because the plants engage involuntary attention and allow the directed attention system to recover from induced fatigue."

Now the prediction is embedded in a causal story. We know which theory generated it, what mechanism it proposes, and what would count as evidence against the theory. A negative result here does not just tell us "plants didn't help" — it tells us something about ART's claim that soft fascination is the active ingredient.

Two Legitimate Kinds of Grounding
In the hard sciences, Popper's picture — derive a prediction from theory, then test it — works fairly cleanly. But environmental psychology studies complex systems whose behavior we can characterize empirically long before we understand the mechanisms producing that behavior. There are two fully legitimate ways to ground a prediction:
MEASUREMENT GROUNDING

"I know stress shows up in cortisol levels ~20 minutes later, and in respiration patterns, because I looked up how to detect stress. I don't have a deep theory of why the system produces these signals — but I have well-validated ways to measure what I care about."

THEORETICAL GROUNDING

"ART predicts attention restoration specifically through involuntary attention engagement. I predict improved ANT scores because the theory's mechanism — soft fascination engaging the ventral attention network — should produce this specific pattern."

Both are scientific. Measurement grounding tells you what to look for and how to detect it. Theoretical grounding additionally tells you why to expect it and what a failure would mean for our understanding. The former is where most environmental psychology operates; the latter is what the field aspires to build over time.

Section A

What Level of Hypothesis Are You Aiming For?

Now that you understand what a hypothesis is, the next question is: how ambitious should yours be? Your answer shapes everything that follows — the form of your hypothesis, what you need to measure, and how informative your experiment will be. There is no shame in Level 1 or 2; much of the best environmental psychology operates there. But you should choose deliberately, not by default.

1 Descriptive Does the effect exist? How large is it?
2 Effect-Hunting Does the theory's specific prediction hold?
3 Mechanism-Probing Does the proposed mechanism actually operate?
4 Critical Which competing theory is right?

Level 1 — Documenting the Phenomenon

At Level 1, you are establishing whether an effect exists, estimating its size, and specifying how to measure it. You ground your predictions in measurement science — what you know about how to detect and quantify the outcomes you care about — rather than in a deep theory of why the system behaves this way. This is how most empirical research in the behavioral sciences begins, and it is perfectly legitimate. You are studying a complex system; characterizing what it does comes before explaining why.

Example — with measurement grounding

"Participants will show lower physiological stress after 15 minutes in a VR nature environment compared to a matched urban VR environment, measured by salivary cortisol collected at T+20 minutes (cortisol tracks HPA-axis stress response with a ~15–20 minute lag; Kirschbaum & Hellhammer, 1994) and by increased high-frequency HRV during exposure (HF-HRV indexes parasympathetic cardiac control; Task Force, 1996). They will also report higher positive affect on the PANAS."

Notice that this prediction is grounded — not in a theory of why nature reduces stress, but in well-established measurement science about how to detect stress. You know cortisol tracks the HPA axis because endocrinology established this. You know HRV tracks parasympathetic activation because cardiac physiology established this. That is your grounding.

What Level 1 gives you

Level 1 tells you whether something happens and how big it is. If confirmed, you now have a phenomenon that demands explanation — and multiple theories will compete to provide one. You are mapping the territory. That is how science of complex systems works: first characterize the behavior, then explain the mechanism.

Level 2 — Theory-Derived Effect Prediction

At Level 2, you go beyond documenting the phenomenon and measuring it well — you now derive a specific prediction from a named theory. You are asking: "Does the world behave as Theory T says it should?" This requires you to (a) name the theory, (b) state its relevant mechanism, and (c) derive a prediction that follows from that mechanism. The prediction must be specific enough that a negative result would count against the theory, not just against the bare prediction.

The difference from Level 1 is not that Level 1 is "ungrounded" — Level 1 predictions are grounded in measurement science. The difference is that Level 2 adds a causal story: an account of why the effect occurs, via what mechanism, which produces additional testable commitments.

Deborah Mayo
"A hypothesis passes a severe test if it would very probably have been found wanting if it were false or incorrect."
Your Level 2 hypothesis is severe to the extent that a false theory would probably have produced a result different from what you predict.
Example

"Attention Restoration Theory predicts that directed attention capacity (measured by the Attention Network Test, alerting and executive networks) will improve significantly after 20 minutes of exposure to a VR nature environment high in soft fascination, compared to a VR urban environment, in participants who have been pre-fatigued by 40 minutes of sustained-attention task performance."

Most theories do not predict universal effects. ART requires prior cognitive fatigue; SRT requires prior physiological stress; arousal theory's inverted-U depends on task complexity. State the boundary conditions that must hold for the prediction to apply.

Level 3 — Mechanism Hypothesis

At Level 3, you are not merely testing whether the predicted effect occurs — you are testing whether it occurs through the mechanism the theory proposes. This requires decomposing the theoretical mechanism into measurable steps and designing measures that can distinguish the proposed causal pathway from alternative pathways that could produce the same endpoint.

Peter Machamer
"Mechanisms are entities and activities organized such that they are productive of regular changes."
A Level 3 hypothesis decomposes the mechanism into entities (attention systems, physiological regulators) and activities (fatigue, fascination, recovery) — then tests each link in the chain.
Example

"If ART's mechanism is correct, then nature-induced attention restoration should be mediated by involuntary attention engagement. Specifically: (a) nature environments will increase involuntary attention engagement (measured by eye-tracking gaze dispersion and EEG ventral attention network activation); (b) involuntary attention engagement will predict subsequent directed attention recovery (ANT scores); and (c) when involuntary attention engagement is statistically controlled, the direct effect of nature exposure on directed attention recovery will be significantly reduced (mediation test)."

The mechanism must be operationalized into measurable quantities. What specific instruments, physiological signals, or behavioral indicators will you use?
The temporal decomposition requirement

To test a mechanism, you typically need to measure the mediating variable between the treatment and the outcome — not just at the same time as the outcome. If you only measure the mediator and the outcome simultaneously, you cannot establish temporal ordering. Plan your measurement timeline: treatment → mediator measure → outcome measure.

Level 4 — Critical Hypothesis (Theory Competition)

At Level 4, you have found a condition where two theories make incompatible predictions — and you are testing which one holds. This is the most informative kind of experiment because, regardless of the result, at least one theory's prediction fails. The key intellectual challenge is identifying the point of genuine divergence: most theories agree on most predictions, and finding where they part company requires deep understanding of both.

Imre Lakatos
"A 'crucial experiment' is one whose outcome is inconsistent with one of two competing research programmes while being predicted by the other."
Level 4 experiments produce progress regardless of outcome — they either vindicate one programme or refute the other. This is why they are the gold standard.
Example

"ART predicts that nature exposure restores directed attention even in non-stressed participants (because the mechanism is cognitive, not physiological). SRT predicts that nature exposure produces wellbeing benefits only in stressed participants (because the mechanism is parasympathetic recovery). In a 2×2 design crossing stress induction (yes/no) with environment (nature/urban), these theories make opposing predictions about the non-stressed + nature cell: ART predicts attention restoration, SRT predicts no benefit."

It is not enough that the theories "emphasize different things." You must show that they make opposite predictions about the same observable outcome in the same condition. Spell out the logical chain from each theory's core commitment to its specific prediction in the divergence condition.
Critical experiments rarely produce clean wins. What result would be indeterminate — supporting neither theory cleanly? How would you interpret partial support (e.g., attention improves somewhat, stress physiology also changes somewhat)?
The auxiliary hypothesis problem

Critical experiments test theories plus their auxiliary assumptions. If SRT's prediction fails, a defender could argue that the stress induction was insufficient, the cortisol measure was unreliable, or the nature stimulus was not "natural enough." You cannot avoid this — Lakatos and Quine showed that no single experiment definitively refutes a core theory. But you can minimize escape routes by using validated measures, established induction procedures, and preregistered analysis plans.

Section B

What to Measure

Your hypothesis specifies what you predict. Now you must specify how you will observe it. The measurement plan follows from your hypothesis level: Level 1 needs an independent variable and a dependent variable. Level 3 adds mediating variables. Level 4 adds divergence measures that are diagnostic for one theory but not the other.

Section C

Why Does This Hypothesis Matter?

A hypothesis is not worth testing just because it is testable. It matters because confirming or disconfirming it would teach the field something it does not already know. The motivation for a hypothesis draws on three sources: what the literature has already established, what the theory predicts but nobody has yet tested, and what practical consequences follow from the answer.

Motivate Your Hypothesis

Summarize the 2–3 most relevant findings from the research front you are working in. What do we already know? What is the state of evidence? Use the Knowledge Navigator to review the claims in your front.
Identify the specific gap in knowledge that your experiment fills. This should follow logically from the literature review: "We know X, but we do not yet know Y."
Who would care about this result, and what would they do differently? Think about both theoretical implications (which theory is supported?) and practical applications (how should architects design spaces?).
COGS 160 emphasizes VR as a scientific instrument. What does VR let you manipulate or control that a field experiment cannot? Be specific about the methodological advantage.
Nancy Cartwright
"Theories describe capacities — real powers that operate under specific local conditions."
Your motivation section should make clear what capacity you are testing, under what conditions, and why establishing those conditions in VR is scientifically advantageous.
