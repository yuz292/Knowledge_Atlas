Methods Taxonomy

Experimental paradigms are tools. Which tool you choose depends on the question you're asking and what you already know. This taxonomy helps you navigate 14 paradigm families by matching them to your research goal, resources, and constraints.

Level 1: "What happens?" (Descriptive/Exploratory)
Establishing phenomena, estimating effect sizes, and understanding ecological validity
Use when: You need baseline data, population estimates, or real-world behavior patterns
POE
Post-Occupancy Evaluation
Surveys of existing building occupants about their actual experiences and satisfaction.
Strengths
High ecological validity; real occupants in real spaces
Can measure long-term adaptation and satisfaction
Builds direct relationships with practitioners
Limitations
No control over design variables; confounded naturally
Retrospective bias and self-report measurement error
Cannot infer causation from correlations
VR: Low
L1
Example Question
Are office workers satisfied with open-plan seating arrangements?
MAR/EMA
Momentary Assessment / Experience Sampling
Real-time capture of experience via smartphone prompts throughout the day.
Strengths
Minimizes recall bias; captures true in-the-moment experience
High ecological validity; participants in natural contexts
Rich temporal dynamics; can model state changes
Limitations
Requires participant compliance and sustained engagement
Selection bias if drop-out is non-random
No experimental control; observational only
VR: Low
L1
Example Question
How does indoor air quality correlate with momentary attention and mood throughout the workday?
CCC
Cross-Cultural Comparison
Behavioral or survey measurements across multiple cultures or geographic regions.
Strengths
Tests generalizability and cultural boundaries of psychological principles
Large sample heterogeneity; high external validity
Reveals culture-dependent vs. universal mechanisms
Limitations
Confounds cultural factors with history, economics, climate
Measurement equivalence and translation validity are challenging
Expensive and logistically complex
VR: Medium
L1
Example Question
Do warmth preference ratings for spaces differ systematically between subtropical and arctic-region participants?
LES
Longitudinal Study
Repeated measurements of the same participants over weeks, months, or years.
Strengths
Directly measures change trajectories and adaptation over time
Can model individual growth curves and heterogeneity
High ecological validity; natural contexts preserved
Limitations
Attrition bias; participants drop out non-randomly over time
Practice effects and testing reactivity contaminate measures
Very time-consuming and resource-intensive
VR: Low
L1
Example Question
How do office workers adapt to a newly renovated environment over 6 months?
Level 2: "Does the predicted effect hold?" (Effect Testing)
Testing specific predictions with controlled manipulation and inferring causation
Use when: You have a hypothesis about cause-effect and want to test it experimentally
CLE
Controlled Lab Experiment
Tight experimental control with random assignment and isolated manipulation.
Strengths
Gold standard for causal inference; highest internal validity
Clear cause-effect logic; confounds minimized
Reproducible and replicable; clean effect sizes
Limitations
Low ecological validity; contrived settings and demand characteristics
Often uses participant pools (students); limited generalizability
Environmental complexity stripped away; mechanisms may not transfer
VR: High
L2
Example Question
Does warm color temperature in a room causally increase perceived spaciousness?
FE
Field Experiment
Random assignment and causal manipulation in real-world settings.
Strengths
Maintains causal inference logic while preserving ecological validity
Real participants in natural contexts; high external validity
Effects generalize to target population more reliably
Limitations
Hard to control confounds in uncontrolled environments
Ethical constraints; cannot assign harmful conditions
Expensive and logistically complex; slow data collection
VR: Low
L2
Example Question
Does adding plants to an actual office causally reduce stress and sick days?
PCP
Paired-Comparison Preference
Direct pairwise comparison and preference measurement between design alternatives.
Strengths
Simple and intuitive; reveals direct preference rankings
Avoids absolute judgment scales; relative preference is more reliable
Can aggregate preferences into a complete ordering
Limitations
Only measures preference, not cognitive mechanisms or reasons
Many comparisons needed for full ordering (n choose 2 pairs)
Intransitivity and context-dependence common; not truly ordinal
VR: High
L2
Example Question
Which layout of a room do architects prefer: A vs. B vs. C vs. D?
PVS
Perceived Virtual Space
Participants view images or videos of designed spaces and rate them or make choices.
Strengths
Cheap, quick, and scalable; can test many designs at low cost
Participants can see final designs before construction
Precise control over visual variables; isolate lighting, color, form
Limitations
Missing sensory channels: sound, smell, thermal, haptic
2D images lack depth cues; spatial perception differs from 3D
Attention patterns shift with screen-based vs. embodied viewing
VR: Medium
L2
Example Question
Do variations in ceiling height in rendered images affect perceived crowding ratings?
IVW
Immersive Virtual World
Participants interact with fully 3D environments via VR headsets with high sensorimotor fidelity.
Strengths
High immersion and presence; closer to real environments than images
Precise experimental control while maintaining embodiment
Can measure behavior (navigation, gaze, reach) and psychophysiology simultaneously
Limitations
Cybersickness and simulator discomfort in some participants
Expensive equipment and development cost
Still missing some sensory modalities (smell, full thermal gradient)
VR: Native
L2 L3
Example Question
Does wayfinding difficulty in a complex VR building causally increase cortisol and reduce task performance?
Level 3: "Does the mechanism operate?" (Mechanism Probing)
Measuring intermediate steps, temporal decomposition, and mediation pathways
Use when: You want to understand HOW or WHY an effect happens, not just that it does
BMT
Biometric Tracking
Continuous physiological monitoring (heart rate, skin conductance, respiration) throughout an experiment.
Strengths
Objective, continuous data; no self-report bias
Reveals temporal dynamics and state transitions in real-time
Wearable sensors allow field measurement; portable
Limitations
Equipment artifacts and drift; requires careful calibration
Individual differences in baseline physiology; difficult to standardize
Devices are intrusive; awareness can reactivity affect behavior
VR: High
L3
Example Question
What is the temporal relationship between window view and parasympathetic activation (HRV)?
PPM
Psychophysiological Measurement
Laboratory-based physiological measures: EEG, galvanic skin response, pupil dilation, eye-tracking.
Strengths
High temporal and spatial resolution; millisecond precision
Multiple modalities reveal different mechanisms (attention, emotion, effort)
Objective and replicable; not subject to demand characteristics
Limitations
Equipment is expensive and requires expertise to operate
Constrained lab environment reduces ecological validity
Biological variability across participants can obscure signals
VR: High
L3
Example Question
Does asymmetrical architectural framing (golden ratio vs. other) modulate EEG alpha power indicating attention allocation?
NI
Neuroimaging
Brain imaging (fMRI, PET, or NIRS) to localize neural correlates of spatial perception and emotion.
Strengths
Reveals neural substrate and brain regions engaged by environmental features
Can dissociate competing theories via spatial selectivity
Convergent validity; combines with behavior for complete picture
Limitations
Extremely expensive; requires access to shared scanning facilities
Reverse inference problem; brain activation ≠ cognitive process
Artificial scanning environment; severely limits realistic spatial experience
VR: Medium
L3
Example Question
Which brain regions activate differentially when navigating complex vs. simple spatial layouts in fMRI?
CFA
Computational Feature Analysis
Automated quantification of environmental properties (color, luminance, complexity, symmetry) from images or 3D models.
Strengths
Objective and reproducible; removes observer bias from coding
Can extract features humans cannot consciously access
Enables prediction modeling and hypothesis testing via regression
Limitations
May miss perceptually relevant features; feature engineering is hard
Requires large datasets for validation; correlations may not be causal
Assumes measured features match psychological mechanisms
VR: High
L3
Example Question
Is fractal dimension (self-similarity) of room facades a predictor of perceived elegance across diverse architectural styles?
Level 4: "Which theory is right?" (Critical Experiments)
Combining multiple methods to achieve diagnostic power and differentiate competing theories
Use when: Two or more theories make opposite predictions; you need multiple dependent measures
Multi-Method
Critical Experiment Design
Combines methods from Levels 2 and 3 to test predictions that differentiate theories.
Typical Combination
Example: IVW (Level 2) + BMT + PPM (Level 3) + behavioral measures
  • IVW provides causal manipulation in immersive setting
  • BMT measures autonomic response (sympathetic vs. parasympathetic)
  • PPM measures attention (eye-tracking) and cognitive effort (EEG)
  • Behavior: wayfinding latency, accuracy, help-seeking
Why Multiple Measures?
Theory A predicts: arousal → avoidance (BMT + behavior)
Theory B predicts: cognitive load → approach-then-frustration (PPM + behavior)
Only convergent evidence distinguishes them.
Strengths
Highest diagnostic power; rules out alternative mechanisms
Combines internal and external validity
Results are convincing and publishable in top venues
Limitations
Expensive, time-consuming, and technically complex
Requires expertise across multiple methods
Large sample size needed for power across multiple measures
VR: Native

Method Profiles

POE — Post-Occupancy Evaluation
Internal Validity
Low — observational only
Best Combined With
CLE, IVW (validation)
External Validity
High — real occupants
Time to Data
3–6 weeks
Common Mistake: Students treat POE as experimental and claim causation from correlations. It's exploratory. Use POE to identify phenomena that later hypotheses will test causally.
MAR/EMA — Momentary Assessment
Internal Validity
Low — confounds abound
Best Combined With
FE, LES (validation)
External Validity
High — natural contexts
Time to Data
4–12 weeks
Common Mistake: Assuming smartphone compliance is uniform across all participants. Build attrition analysis and check if drop-out predicts outcome variables.
CCC — Cross-Cultural Comparison
Internal Validity
Low — culture is aggregate
Best Combined With
POE, CLE (bounds testing)
External Validity
High — diverse populations
Time to Data
3–6 months
Common Mistake: Attributing between-culture differences to "culture" rather than climate, economics, or architecture. Always measure confounds explicitly.
LES — Longitudinal Study
Internal Validity
Low — no manipulation
Best Combined With
FE, CFA (mechanism)
External Validity
High — natural change
Time to Data
6–24 months
Common Mistake: Ignoring attrition bias. If participants who leave differ systematically, your final sample is no longer representative. Use multiple imputation or selection models.
CLE — Controlled Lab Experiment
Internal Validity
High — random assignment
Best Combined With
FE, BMT (replication + mechanism)
External Validity
Low — student samples
Time to Data
1–3 weeks
Common Mistake: Testing only on undergraduates, then claiming results generalize to all people. A clean lab effect may not appear in the field. Always follow up with FE.
FE — Field Experiment
Internal Validity
High — randomization maintained
Best Combined With
CLE (theory), PPM (mechanism)
External Validity
High — real setting
Time to Data
2–4 months
Common Mistake: Underestimating contamination: occupants talk, treatments leak across units. Use cluster randomization at the building level, not the individual.
PCP — Paired-Comparison Preference
Internal Validity
Medium — controls for scale
Best Combined With
PVS, IVW (stimuli), PPM (mechanisms)
External Validity
Medium — preference context matters
Time to Data
3–7 days
Common Mistake: Assuming preferences are transitive or complete. Some participants will prefer A over B, B over C, but C over A. This is real data — don't discard it.
PVS — Perceived Virtual Space
Internal Validity
Medium — visual control strong
Best Combined With
IVW (adds immersion), CLE (validation)
External Validity
Low — 2D/missing senses
Time to Data
1–4 days
Common Mistake: Assuming image-based preference predicts real behavior. A room may look spacious in photos but feel cramped when you walk through it. Validate with IVW or FE.
IVW — Immersive Virtual World
Internal Validity
High — full experimental control
Best Combined With
FE (replication), PPM (mechanisms)
External Validity
Medium — presence helps, but still lab
Time to Data
2–4 weeks
Common Mistake: Assuming all VR effects transfer to reality. Cybersickness, habituation, and affordances differ. Always validate field results with real spaces.
BMT — Biometric Tracking
Internal Validity
High — objective measurement
Best Combined With
Behavior measures, PPM (specificity)
External Validity
High — mobile, field-ready
Time to Data
1–2 weeks
Common Mistake: Assuming one physiology variable (heart rate) tells you the whole story. Different emotions produce similar arousal. Always include multiple measures (HR, HRV, SC, respiration).
PPM — Psychophysiological Measurement
Internal Validity
High — precise lab control
Best Combined With
IVW, BMT (field validation)
External Validity
Low — lab constraints
Time to Data
1–3 weeks
Common Mistake: Over-interpreting brain activation as evidence for a psychological process. fMRI is correlative. Show convergent validity with behavioral and subjective measures.
NI — Neuroimaging
Internal Validity
High — precise localization
Best Combined With
CLE, PPM (converging evidence)
External Validity
Low — scanner environment
Time to Data
4–8 weeks
Common Mistake: Attempting scanning-based studies without pilot behavior data. Know your effect size from CLE first; fMRI needs large, reliable effects to resolve.
CFA — Computational Feature Analysis
Internal Validity
High — repeatable algorithms
Best Combined With
POE, PVS (prediction), CLE (mechanism)
External Validity
High — applies across spaces
Time to Data
2–4 days
Common Mistake: Using black-box features (deep learning) without understanding what they measure. Validate that algorithmic features correlate with human perception before claiming psychological relevance.
