The Transferable Skill

By Week 5, you will be able to sit down with two or three AI systems and, in a single afternoon, produce a working component that meets a defined specification. We call this "The Afternoon with Three AIs" — and it is the skill that every employer, graduate program, and research lab will value in you.

The workflow: Panel → Spec → Contracts → Implement → Verify → Iterate. Every step has specific techniques. This course teaches them all, using a real system as the practice substrate.

The Practice Substrate: Knowledge_ATLAS

Knowledge_ATLAS is a large knowledge system that maps what the scientific literature says about how built environments affect human cognition, emotion, and behavior. It contains a Web of Belief with 1,900 evidence items drawn from 760 scientific papers, organized into 11 active research topics. It has a Bayesian causal bridge (architecturally complete, awaiting real experimental data) that will predict what will happen if you change a building. It has a nine-stage extraction pipeline that scores 80.8/100 on the AESHI quality audit — strong on contract compliance and theoretical rigor, with extraction throughput as the known bottleneck.

It also has six major capability gaps — things it should be able to do but can't yet. Your job is to close those gaps. Each student track addresses one or more gaps. By the end of the quarter, KA will be measurably more capable than it is today, and you will have the methodology to build systems like it in any domain.

The Method: AI-Directed System Building

This is not a "prompt engineering" course. This is a course in directing AI systems to build verified knowledge artifacts. The distinction matters: prompt engineering is about getting better chatbot answers; AI-directed system building is about producing components that meet formal specifications, pass defined tests, and integrate correctly with a larger system.

The Seven-Step Workflow

1

Define the Problem

What exactly should this component do? What questions must it answer? Who is the user? Start with use cases, not features. "A researcher asks: what is the strongest evidence for biophilic design effects on stress?" is a better problem definition than "build an evidence browser."

2

Convene an Expert Panel

Don't guess what the system should do — ask experts. Direct an AI to simulate 3–5 domain experts, each grounded in named researchers and their published positions. Include at least one adversarial voice. Include a methodologist. The panel argues about what matters and what "good" looks like.

3

Produce a Spec with Success Conditions

Convert the panel's output into a contract: inputs, outputs, invariants, and measurable success conditions. "Precision > 0.85 on a 50-paper test set" is a success condition. "It should work well" is not. You cannot verify what you cannot measure.

4

Force Planning Before Implementation

Direct the AI to produce a strategy document: what approach, what architecture, what risks, what testing plan. The prompt says: "Do NOT write any code. Produce a strategy document." This prevents the most common AI failure: producing 400 lines of professional-looking code that solves the wrong problem.

5

Implement with Parallel AIs

Independent components go to different AIs simultaneously. One builds the keyword scorer, another builds the journal quality scorer. Division of labor with clear interfaces. Each AI receives the spec and the vocabulary — not just a vague instruction.

6

Verify Against the Spec

Run the output against the success conditions. Compare the AI's self-assessment ("this should work well!") with the actual numbers. The AI that builds should not be the sole evaluator of its own work. Use cross-AI verification, automated tests, and human spot-checks.

7

Iterate with Ruthless Prompts

Where output fails the spec, write a ruthless correction: show the specific error, explain why it's wrong, demand a specific fix, forbid the pattern that caused the error. Ruthlessness is for execution, not exploration. It is precise, not angry.

Why This Works

What looks like "an afternoon's work with three AIs" is the compressed output of years of hard-won craft. Students who skip the panel step produce systems that solve the wrong problem. Students who skip the spec step produce systems that cannot be tested. Students who skip verification produce systems that look impressive and are subtly wrong. The methodology exists because every shortcut has been tried and has failed.

The Seven AI Failure Modes

AIs fail in predictable ways. Knowing these patterns lets you prevent them. The first five emerge in single prompts or short sessions. The last two — Attention-Induced Deletion and Drift — are slow-acting; they emerge over long sessions and compound silently until the damage is severe.

1. Plausible Hallucination

The AI generates content that reads correctly, uses domain vocabulary, and is completely made up. Fake citations, invented effect sizes, fabricated study descriptions.

2. Wrong-Problem Drift

The AI starts on task X and gradually drifts to task Y because Y is easier. Common in multi-step prompts where attention degrades.

3. Self-Congratulatory Report

The AI implements, tests its own work, and reports success. The tests test what the code does, not what it should do.

4. Consensus Fabrication

When synthesizing multiple perspectives, the AI smooths over disagreements. This destroys exactly the information that matters most.

5. Premature Abstraction

The AI produces abstract, general output when concrete, specific output was needed. "Several studies found..." instead of "3 studies (Mehta 2012, N=45; Steidle 2013, N=80) found..."

6. Attention-Induced Deletion

The AI focuses on a subset of files to make changes, then writes back only those files — silently dropping everything outside its attention window. The repo shrinks instead of growing. In one real case a 2.4 MB repo was reduced to 340 KB after a refactoring session because the AI treated its working set as the entire project. Fix: enforce size-monotonicity on every commit (block any commit that reduces total repo size without explicit approval), quarantine removed files rather than deleting them, and alert on any reduction so a human can verify.

7. Drift

Over a long session the AI gradually loses track of the original goal. Each individual step looks reasonable — "while I'm here, I should also fix X" — but the cumulative trajectory diverges until the AI is working on something the user never requested. A 12-line formula fix can silently expand to 3,200 lines across 14 files over 40 prompts. The danger is that AIs are trained to be helpful, so they solve every adjacent problem they notice without announcing the course change. Fix: anchor every session with a task list (checked every 10 prompts), freeze scope boundaries (which files and patterns are in play), and maintain a session log so drift becomes visible before it compounds.

Knowledge_ATLAS

Knowledge_ATLAS (Architecture for Typed, Layered Assessment of Science) is a computational knowledge system that maps what science knows about how built environments affect human cognition, emotion, and behavior. It is the practice substrate for this course — the system you will improve. Everything described below is live and explorable; each card links to the relevant page.

What It Contains (Current State)

Web of Belief — 1,900 Evidence Items

Interconnected claims, findings, and epistemic commitments organized as a directed multigraph. Each item carries a credence (0–1), a warrant type drawn from a panel-calibrated taxonomy of seven types, and traced connections to its source paper and verbatim quote. Explore the Evidence →

760 Scientific Papers

Processed through a nine-stage lifecycle: discovered → searched → retrieved → stored → typed → extracting → extracted → synthesizing → archived. Extraction uses a family-specific V5 pipeline (classification by Gemini Flash, warrant-aware claim extraction, cross-AI verification by Claude). Article types span empirical research, narrative review, qualitative, theoretical, systematic review, meta-analysis, and methods papers. Browse the Corpus →

Seven Warrant Types (Panel-Calibrated)

Every evidence link carries a warrant — the reason we believe it. The seven types, calibrated by an expert panel (Mayo, Illari, Woodward, Cartwright, Stegenga; Feb–Mar 2026), range from Constitutive (d = 1.0, definitional certainty) through Mechanism (0.80), Empirical Association (0.80), Functional (0.65), Capacity (0.55), Analogical (0.40), down to Theory-Derived (0.25, untested prediction). These discount factors drive every confidence calculation in the system. Inspect Warrant Sensitivity →

11 Research Topics & 9 Documented Gaps

The corpus is organized into 11 active research topics — theory × construct intersection points like Attention Restoration Theory, Stress Recovery Theory, Circadian Lighting, Biophilia, Wayfinding, and Neuroaesthetics — each with credence values (0.40–0.87), maturity ratings, and contradiction counts. Nine research gaps identify where the evidence is thin and new work would have the highest value. Explore Topics →   Explore Gaps →

40 Stimulus Images

Figures, photographs, and diagrams extracted from the corpus papers — workspace interiors, environmental conditions, experimental setups. These are the raw material for the Image Tagger track: each image will be annotated with spatial predicates (naturalness, enclosure, biophilia density, prospect/refuge) to bridge visual features to psychological predictions. Open the Tagger →

System Health: AESHI 80.8 / 100

The ATLAS Epistemic System Health Index (assessed March 2026) scores the entire pipeline. Subscores: contract compliance 97 (excellent), theoretical rigor 87 (strong), web + BN integration 72 (good), pipeline integrity 55 (extraction is the bottleneck), stability 100. All five hard gates pass. The score is honest — the system works but has known weaknesses, especially in extraction throughput and isolated web nodes (25% of nodes lack coherence links).

The Three-Layer Architecture

ATLAS separates what we know from what we can predict. The first layer is production-grade. The second is implemented. The third is theoretically complete but awaits real experimental data — which is part of what this course will eventually produce.

EN

Epistemic Network ● Production

A labeled directed multigraph representing our state of knowledge. 1,900 evidence items from 760 papers, connected by typed warrant edges. Contains multiple evidence types, latent intermediates, theoretical claims, parallel edges, and cycles. The seven warrant types (Constitutive, Mechanism, Empirical Association, Functional, Capacity, Analogical, Theory-Derived) are calibrated by expert panel with explicit discount factors. Evidence Explorer · Warrant Inspector

π

Projection Function ● Implemented

Maps from EN to BN. Translates knowledge into prediction using the four-factor formula: logit(ptarget) = d(τ) · ω · δ · logit(plab). Each factor captures a distinct epistemic dimension: d(τ) is the warrant-type discount, ω is warrant strength (link count, independence), δ is population transfer (how well lab results predict field conditions), and plab is the laboratory effect size. The warrant sensitivity simulator lets you explore how each factor affects predictions. Try the Simulator →

BN

Bayesian Network ● Awaiting Real Data

A directed acyclic graph with conditional probability tables — the operational prediction engine. The architecture is complete: three layers (visual attributes → psychological mediators → outcomes), Pearl's do-calculus for causal reasoning, uncertainty quantification, and API endpoints. All code passes validation on synthetic data. But it has no real human ratings yet. Activating this layer requires collecting real experimental data: at minimum 50 stimulus images rated by 30+ participants on the spatial predicates that the Image Tagger track will define. The BN is infrastructure awaiting the data that your work will produce.

What It Can't Do Yet (The Gaps)

These are the questions KA should answer but currently can't. Each gap is a concrete failure — a real question from a real user type that the system cannot satisfy. Your track work will close them.

Gap 1 — Equipment-Conditioned Recommendations

"I have an EmotiBit and a VR headset. What's the highest-value experiment I can run?"

KA has a sensor catalogue and a gap explorer, but they don't talk to each other. There is no way to input your available equipment and get recommendations conditioned on what you can actually measure. The VOI values in the gap explorer are heuristic, not computed from Bayesian expected value of sample information.

Gap 2 — Mechanism Pathway Tracing

"Does natural light improve performance, and through what mechanism?"

KA has 1,900 evidence items with warrant types, but no interface traces the full causal chain from IV to DV through intermediate mechanisms. The warrant sensitivity simulator shows discount factors but not the pathway itself: light → melanopsin → SCN → cortisol rhythm → arousal → attention → performance. The data to reconstruct these chains exists; the visualization does not.

Gap 3 — Graph-Based Exploration

"Show me everything about lighting → circadian rhythm and let me follow the paths."

The epistemic network is a graph, but no page lets you see it as one. There is no node-and-edge visualization where you can click a construct, see what's connected, and follow links outward. The evidence explorer is tabular; it shows claims one at a time rather than as a navigable network.

Gap 4 — Practitioner Translation

"Should I put plants in this hospital lobby?"

A designer or facilities manager needs a plain-language verdict with honest uncertainty — not a table of effect sizes and p-values. KA currently presents everything at researcher depth. There is no "practitioner mode" that translates evidence into actionable recommendations with explicit confidence and caveats.

Gap 5 — Hypothetical Simulation

"If someone proved that fractal patterns reduce cortical prediction error, how would that change KA's beliefs?"

The belief-updating machinery exists in the backend (theory_updating.py), but there is no interface where you can pose a hypothetical finding and watch its consequences propagate through the web. This is essential for understanding which experiments would be most informative — it's the computational form of Value of Information.

Gap 6 — Visual-to-Psychological Prediction

"Given this photo of a hospital room, predict the occupant's stress level and explain why."

The Bayesian Network layer is architecturally complete (visual attributes → psychological mediators → outcomes) but runs only on synthetic data. There are no real human ratings, no validated spatial predicates, and no trained models. Closing this gap requires defining the predicate vocabulary (Image Tagger), collecting human ratings (experimental data), and validating the causal model against real observations. This is the deepest gap and the longest road.

Gap 7 — Literature Discovery

"The evidence on circadian lighting is thin. Find me the 10 best papers published since 2022 that would fill this gap."

KA has 760 papers, but adding new ones is entirely manual. There is no automated pipeline that takes a research gap, generates search queries, retrieves candidates from Semantic Scholar or other databases, scores them for relevance, and acquires open-access PDFs. The article search page exists as a static UI; it does not connect to any external API.

Prompt Design

This course teaches prompt design as a component of a larger methodology: using AI as a directed instrument in the construction of verified knowledge artifacts. The goal is not "getting good output" but producing output that meets a formal specification, passes defined tests, and integrates correctly with a larger system.

The Five Components of a Production Prompt

Component 1

Role Assignment

Who is the AI pretending to be? Specific enough to activate domain knowledge. "A bibliometrician who specializes in systematic review methodology" — not "a helpful assistant."

Component 2

Task Definition

What exactly should the AI produce? Defined in terms of output format, not just intent. JSON schema, not prose. Testable, not aspirational.

Component 3

Constraints and Prohibitions

The "DO NOT" pattern. AIs have systematic failure modes; constraints prevent them. "DO NOT invent citations. DO NOT summarize when asked to extract. DO NOT evaluate your own output."

Component 4

Context Loading

Vocabulary sheets, canonical term lists, reference documents. The AI doesn't know your domain vocabulary — teach it explicitly, or it will map "illuminance" to "enlightenment."

Component 5

Output Format Specification

The exact structure of the response. JSON schema with required fields, not free text. The format is the contract between the AI's output and the next step in your pipeline.

Prompt Design Cheat Sheet

Before You Prompt

  • Do I have a contract with success conditions?
  • Do I have a test set to verify against?
  • Do I have a vocabulary sheet for my domain?
  • Do I know which failure modes to watch for?
  • Have I chosen the right model for this task?

The Prompt Itself

  • Role assignment (specific, not generic)
  • Task definition (output format, not just intent)
  • Constraints and DO NOTs (at least 3)
  • Context loading (vocabulary, examples, reference docs)
  • Output format specification (schema, not prose)

After You Get Output

  • Does it meet the contract? (Check every field)
  • Does it pass the success conditions? (Run the tests)
  • Is it suspiciously good? (Check for hallucination)
  • Did the AI evaluate its own work? (Don't trust self-assessment)
  • Would a different AI produce different output? (Cross-verify)

When It Fails

  • Identify the failure mode (which of the 5?)
  • Write a ruthless prompt (show error, explain why, demand fix, forbid pattern)
  • Re-run and verify improvement
  • Add the failure to your constraint set for next time
  • If it fails 3 times, restructure the prompt or switch approach
