COGS 160 — Pre-4-Track Era Design History

What we built before the track model, why it was superseded, and what ideas should carry forward

Archived: 2026-03-24 · Owner: David Kirsh, UCSD Cognitive Science · Status: Read-only historical record
Archive Notice. The pages documented here represent the first-generation COGS 160 site design, built before the 4-track contributor model (Track 1: Image Tagging; Track 2: Article Finding; Track 3: VR Production; Track 4: GUI Evaluation & Experiment Design) was adopted as the course structure in March 2026. The files remain accessible in Knowledge_Atlas/Designing_Experiments/ and are linked from the current KA pages via "Previous pages" footer links. They must not be deleted — they contain research data and design ideas of continuing value.

Why the Redesign Happened

The original COGS 160 site was organised around research topics (the 20 CNFA clusters) and disciplinary categories (acoustic, thermal, social, biophilic, cognitive). Students navigated a landscape of findings and were expected to self-direct into experiment design. In practice this was too open-ended: students needed more structured role assignments, clearer deliverables, and a clearer sense of what they were contributing to a collective project.

The 4-track model solves this by assigning each student to a concrete production role — tagger, article finder, VR producer, or GUI evaluator — with a defined deliverable sequence, milestone table, and quality gate. The research topic landscape is still present (via ka_topics.html), but it now serves the contributors' workflows rather than being the entry point itself.

Open Design Ideas From the DE Era

The following ideas appear in DE pages but have no KA equivalent yet. They are listed here so future contributors and Track 4 GUI evaluators can decide whether to develop them:

  • A theory browser mapping theories (ART, CLT, Biophilia) to research fronts, constructs, and experiment designs — analogous to ka_sensors.html but for explanatory frameworks rather than measurement instruments.
  • A methods taxonomy page cataloguing the 11+ paradigm families (CLE, FE, PCP, etc.) with VR-adaptability scores, so students can see which methods are available for a given construct-environment pair.
  • The Bayesian Network Navigator as a visual construct-space exploration tool — potentially useful as a Track 4 GUI evaluation scenario target.
  • The Epistemic Network Navigator as a graph-based way to explore how constructs are related across research fronts — a candidate KA data visualisation page.
  • An "Experiment Sketch" generator — the REPORTS data already contains automated experiment sketches (research question, IV, DV, suggested paradigms) for each of the 20 research fronts. These could be surfaced in ka_topics.html as an "Experiment Ideas" section per topic card.
  • Missing paradigm alerts — the REPORTS data flags which paradigm families are absent from each research front. A Track 2 or Track 4 tool could surface these as "unexplored method opportunities" for experiment design.

Track 4 GUI Evaluation — Specific Implications

Track 4 students assigned to GUI Evaluation & Experiment Design should review the DE pages with the following questions in mind:

  • Which navigation paradigm served users better — the topic-browse model (knowledge_navigator) or the track-based workflow model (current KA)? What evidence from your user scenarios supports that judgment?
  • What did the knowledge_navigator page do that ka_topics.html does not yet do? (e.g., showing effect-size ranges, paradigm profiles, VR-readiness scores per topic.)
  • Which archived pages should be revived as KA tools, and with what redesign? Use the Page Function Specification framework to define their objective, users, and uses before proposing a rebuild.
  • The transition from a topic-browse model to a contributor-track model changes the primary user mental model. Do the current KA pages communicate this shift clearly to a first-time visitor?
