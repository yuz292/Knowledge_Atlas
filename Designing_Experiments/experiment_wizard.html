Ground Your Experiment in Theory

Before choosing a paradigm, consider: which theory predicts the effect you want to study, and through what mechanism? An experiment grounded in theory can test specific predictions and distinguish between rival explanations. An experiment without theory can only document whether an effect exists — it cannot tell you why.

The major theories in environment–cognition science include Attention Restoration Theory (ART — nature restores directed attention through soft fascination), Stress Recovery Theory (SRT — nature triggers parasympathetic stress reduction), Biophilia (innate affiliation with living systems), Arousal Theory (environments modulate arousal along an inverted-U), Environmental Affordances (environments offer action possibilities), and Prospect-Refuge Theory (evolved preference for seeing without being seen).

What is an experimental paradigm?

An experimental paradigm is a standardized way of setting up an experiment to test a particular kind of question. It specifies how you manipulate variables, what you measure, and how you control for confounds. Different paradigms are suited to different research questions — for example, a Controlled Lab Experiment gives you tight control over variables but may sacrifice ecological validity, while a Field Experiment captures real-world behavior but makes it harder to isolate causal factors.
