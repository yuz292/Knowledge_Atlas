Module 0: Foundations of Experimental Logic

Common Core  ·  ~45 minutes  ·  8 self-check questions

Before You Begin

This module covers the conceptual foundations every experimenter must understand. Complete it before entering the Experiment Wizard. No statistics required — just clear thinking about cause and effect.

1. What Makes Something an Experiment?

An experiment has three defining features: the researcher manipulates at least one variable (the independent variable), randomly assigns participants to conditions, and measures the outcome (the dependent variable) while holding other variables constant. If any of these three is missing, you have a useful study — but not an experiment, and you cannot draw causal conclusions from it with the same confidence.

Consider a researcher who measures stress levels in people who chose to work in open-plan versus private offices. She finds that open-plan workers report higher stress. Can she conclude that open-plan offices cause stress? No — because no one was randomly assigned. The people who chose open-plan offices may differ systematically from those who chose private offices in personality, seniority, job type, or a dozen other variables. This is a quasi-experiment, and it has a selection threat to internal validity.

Self-Check Question
A researcher measures cortisol levels in students who chose to study in a library versus a café. She finds café students have lower cortisol. Is this an experiment?
Yes — she measured a biological outcome (cortisol), so it's an experiment.
No — participants chose their own study location, so there's no random assignment.
Yes — there are two comparison groups (library vs. café), which makes it an experiment.
Correct. Without random assignment, this is a quasi-experiment. Students who prefer cafés may differ from library-goers in sociability, caffeine habits, or noise tolerance — any of which could explain the cortisol difference.
Not quite. Having two groups or a biological measure does not make something an experiment. The defining feature is random assignment — and here, students chose their own condition.

2. The Counterfactual Logic of Causation

When we say "X caused Y," we mean that Y would not have occurred (or would have occurred differently) if X had not been present. This is the counterfactual definition (Holland, 1986; Rubin, 1974). The fundamental problem of causal inference is that we can never observe the same person in both the treatment and control conditions at the same time. Random assignment solves this problem statistically: it creates groups that are, on average, identical on all variables except the one we manipulated.

3. Internal vs. External Validity

Internal validity asks: "Did the manipulation actually cause the observed difference?" Threats to internal validity include confounds, demand characteristics, experimenter effects, maturation, history, and selection bias (Shadish, Cook & Campbell, 2002).

External validity asks: "Would this finding generalize to other people, places, and times?" A study can be internally valid (the manipulation really caused the effect) but externally limited (it only worked in this specific lab with these specific undergraduates).

The key insight: internal validity takes priority. A study that generalizes beautifully but has a confound tells you nothing reliable. A study with perfect internal validity but a narrow sample tells you something real about that sample — and you can always replicate with other populations.

4. Designs: Between, Within, and Mixed

In a between-subjects design, different people experience different conditions. In a within-subjects design, the same people experience all conditions. In a mixed design, some factors are between and some are within.

Example from Research Front: Ceiling Height & Creativity

Between-subjects: 60 people are randomly assigned to a high-ceiling room (N=30) or a low-ceiling room (N=30) and take the Remote Associates Test. Within-subjects: 30 people take the RAT in both rooms, counterbalanced. The within-subjects version is more powerful (each person serves as their own control) but creates a risk of order effects.

5. The Role of the Control Condition

A control condition is not "nothing happens." It is a comparison standard that isolates the effect of the manipulation. Without it, you cannot tell whether the treatment caused the outcome or whether participants would have shown the same outcome anyway.

6. Why Sample Size Matters

Small samples are noisy. With N = 10 per group, even a real effect of moderate size has a high probability of being missed (a Type II error). This is not a statistical nicety — it means that if you run an underpowered study and find "no effect," you cannot conclude the effect does not exist. You simply did not have enough data to detect it.

7. Confounds, Demand, and Experimenter Effects

A confound is a variable that changes systematically along with the IV. If you compare a room with windows to a windowless room, daylight and view both change — you cannot attribute mood differences to either one alone. Demand characteristics occur when participants figure out the hypothesis and adjust their behavior. Experimenter effects occur when the researcher's expectations unconsciously influence the outcome (Rosenthal, 1966).

8. Pre-Registration

Pre-registration means writing down your hypotheses, design, and analysis plan before collecting data, and posting it on a public repository like OSF or AsPredicted. This prevents two problems: HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known) and p-hacking (running multiple analyses until one is significant). Pre-registration does not prevent exploratory analysis — it distinguishes confirmatory from exploratory clearly (Nosek et al., 2018).

Self-Check Question
A student pre-registers the hypothesis that noise reduces attention (measured by Stroop RT). After collecting data, she also discovers that noise increases error rate on the Stroop task. Can she report this?
No — she only pre-registered RT, so she cannot analyze error rate.
Yes — but she must label the error-rate analysis as exploratory, not confirmatory.
Yes — pre-registration is just a formality and doesn't restrict what you report.
Correct. Pre-registration does not forbid exploratory analysis. It requires transparency: the RT analysis is confirmatory (pre-registered), the error-rate analysis is exploratory (discovered post-hoc). Both can be reported, clearly labeled.
Not quite. Pre-registration does not lock you into a single analysis, nor is it merely a formality. Its purpose is to clearly separate what you predicted from what you discovered after looking at the data.
Foundations complete. You now understand the logic of experiments, the priority of internal validity, and why pre-registration matters. Next step: Build Your Hypothesis →

Misconception Traps

Common Core  ·  5 scenarios  ·  Based on Simons & Chabris (2012); Butterfield & Metcalfe (2001)

These five scenarios target the most common wrong intuitions students bring to experimental design. Each one lets you commit to your answer before revealing the error — because research on the hypercorrection effect shows that high-confidence errors, once corrected, are remembered better than low-confidence ones.

Trap 1: "Correlation Proves Causation If the Effect Is Large"

The Scenario

A large study (N = 5,000) finds a correlation of r = 0.65 between office plants and employee wellbeing. Employees in offices with more plants report substantially higher job satisfaction. An executive concludes: "Plants cause happiness — let's put plants in every office."

Trap Question
Is the executive's causal conclusion justified?
Yes — r = 0.65 is a large correlation and the sample is huge. This is strong evidence for causation.
No — no matter how large the correlation, this is observational data and cannot establish causation.
Maybe — it depends on whether r = 0.65 is statistically significant.
Correct. The size of the correlation is irrelevant to causation. Companies that invest in office plants may also invest in better lighting, ergonomic furniture, and higher salaries. Any of these confounds could fully account for the wellbeing difference. Only random assignment (e.g., randomly assigning some offices to receive plants) can establish that plants caused the improvement.
The trap here is thinking that a large correlation or a large sample establishes causation. It never does. Correlation quantifies the linear relationship between two variables, but says nothing about which one causes the other or whether a third variable explains both.

Trap 2: "More Participants Is Always Better"

The Scenario

A study with N = 5,000 finds that blue walls reduce anxiety by 0.2 points on a 100-point scale (p = 0.001, d = 0.04). A reviewer calls this "a highly significant finding with robust statistical evidence."

Trap Question
Is this an impressive, practically meaningful result?
Yes — p = 0.001 means there's only a 0.1% chance this is a fluke.
No — d = 0.04 is a trivially small effect. Statistical significance ≠ practical significance.
Correct. With N = 5,000, you can detect effects so small they have no practical relevance. A d of 0.04 means blue walls reduce anxiety by four hundredths of a standard deviation — a difference no human could notice. The p-value tells you the effect is probably real, but "real" and "meaningful" are different questions.
This is the trap: confusing statistical significance with practical importance. A tiny p-value means the effect is unlikely to be zero, but it says nothing about whether the effect matters. Always look at the effect size (d), not just p.

Trap 3: "A Non-Significant Result Means No Effect"

The Scenario

A pilot study with N = 12 per group tests whether natural light improves task performance. The result: d = 0.45, p = 0.18. The student writes: "The manipulation had no effect on performance."

Trap Question
Is the student correct that the manipulation "had no effect"?
No — the study was underpowered. With N = 12, you have only ~20% power to detect a medium effect. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
Yes — p = 0.18 is not significant, so we accept the null hypothesis.
Correct. A d of 0.45 is a medium effect — not nothing. But with only 12 participants per group, the study had roughly 20% power to detect this effect at α = 0.05. That means there's an 80% chance of missing a real effect of this size. The correct conclusion: "This study was too small to detect the observed effect."
We never "accept the null hypothesis." A non-significant result means the data are consistent with the null, but they may also be consistent with a meaningful effect that the study was too small to detect. You need a power analysis to know whether your null result is informative.

VR Ethics Framework

Standard IRB training does not cover VR-specific risks (Madary & Metzinger, 2016). Complete all six domains before submitting your protocol:

  1. Simulator Sickness Risk Assessment — Rate expected level, plan SSQ administration and stopping rules
  2. Psychological Intensity Screening — Pre-screen for phobias if stimuli are threatening
  3. Body Representation Ethics — Informed consent for avatar embodiment, especially cross-identity
  4. VR Data Privacy — Eye tracking and head kinematics are biometric identifiers
  5. Aftereffects Protocol — 5-minute post-VR acclimation, brief perceptual check
  6. Withdrawal Protocol — Easy exit mechanism, monitoring for distress

Data Preparation

From raw sensor output to analysis-ready data

Section A: General Principles (All Tracks)

Before analyzing anything, your data must be clean, formatted correctly, and documented. This section covers the principles that apply regardless of what you measured.

Tidy Data

One observation per row, one variable per column. Long vs. wide format. Why this matters (Wickham, 2014).

Missing Data

MCAR, MAR, MNAR — what they mean, how to detect them, when to drop vs. impute.

Outlier Detection

Z-scores, IQR method, Mahalanobis distance. When to exclude vs. winsorize.

Assumption Checking

Normality, homogeneity, sphericity. What to check, how to check it, what to do when it fails.

Section B: Behavioral Data

IAT D-Score Computation

The Implicit Association Test uses a specific scoring algorithm (Greenwald, Nosek & Banaji, 2003): remove practice blocks, apply 300–10,000 ms latency trimming, compute D = (Mean_incongruent – Mean_congruent) / SD_all_trials, apply the built-in exclusion criteria (>10% trials <300ms).

Adaptive Preference

Staircase procedures converge on a threshold. Fit a psychometric function (logistic or Weibull) to the response data. Exclude non-convergent staircases (those where the staircase has not reversed at least 6 times).

Survey Composites

Check for reverse-scored items, compute composite means, verify reliability (target: α ≥ 0.70 or ω ≥ 0.70).

Section C: Physiological Data — EmotiBit

EmotiBit records PPG (→HR), EDA, accelerometer, gyroscope, temperature. This section covers extraction and cleaning for each signal.

Section D: Physiological Data — Polar H10

Polar H10 provides single-lead ECG. R-peak detection, RR interval extraction, ectopic beat correction, and export from Polar Sensor Logger.

Section E: Multi-Modal Synchronization

EmotiBit, Polar H10, VR system, and behavioral responses all have different clocks. Learn time-stamping strategies (LSL, NTP, event markers) and post-hoc alignment.

Classification Exercise
A PPG signal shows regular pulse peaks for 30 seconds, then 5 seconds of flat line, then peaks resume. How do you classify this segment?
Clean — the signal returned to normal, so the flat section was just a rest period.
Electrode contact failure — exclude the flat segment, do not interpolate PPG morphology.
Minor artifact — interpolate across the gap using surrounding peak intervals.
Correct. A flat line in PPG means the sensor lost contact with skin. Unlike ECG where you can interpolate short gaps, PPG morphology changes fundamentally with contact loss. Exclude the segment entirely.
A flat line in PPG is not a rest period or a minor artifact. It indicates loss of sensor contact. The correct approach is to exclude these epochs — interpolation would invent data that never existed.

Always Report Effect Sizes

For t-tests, report Cohen's d. For ANOVA, report η² (eta-squared). Never report only p-values — they tell you whether an effect exists, not how large it is.

Replication Exercise

Common Core  ·  Pashler's "Replicate First" pathway

Before designing a novel experiment, replicate an existing finding. This teaches you more about experimental design than any lecture — because you will discover exactly where published methods are underspecified.

How It Works

  1. Select a published finding from the V5 claims database (5,027 claims with DOIs and effect sizes)

  3. Identify specification gaps — a checklist reveals what the authors did not report
  4. Build the replication protocol — fill every gap using Steps 2–5 of the wizard

Assumption Checking Walkthrough

Common Core  ·  Tabachnick & Fidell (2019)

Complete this walkthrough before running any statistical test. For each test type, you will check the relevant assumptions and learn what to do when they are violated.

Normality

Shapiro-Wilk (N < 50) or Q-Q plot (N ≥ 50). Visual gallery of "normal enough" vs. "clearly non-normal."

Homogeneity

Levene's test. What "p < .05 means you have a problem" means. Solution: Welch's version.

Sphericity

Mauchly's test (within-subjects only). Fix: Greenhouse-Geisser correction.

When Assumptions Fail

Decision tree: transform, use robust alternative, or switch to non-parametric test.
