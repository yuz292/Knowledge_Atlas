T1 · Foundational Framework

Motor-Sensory Integration

Every movement the body makes is predicted before it happens, and the prediction is used to interpret the sensory feedback that follows. Perception and action are one closed loop, not two processes.

At a glance

In plain English. When the brain sends a motor command, it simultaneously sends a copy of that command ("efference copy") to its own sensory regions so they can predict the sensory consequences of the action before they arrive. This is why you cannot tickle yourself — your motor system predicted the touch and the sensory system down-weighted it.

Physiologically, the loop rests on a suite of well-characterised circuits. Premotor and primary motor cortices issue commands and send copies to posterior parietal cortex and cerebellum. The cerebellum maintains internal models of body dynamics — a forward model that predicts sensory consequences, and an inverse model that plans motor commands to achieve desired sensory outcomes. Vestibular, proprioceptive, and visual reafference are compared against predictions in cerebellum and parietal multimodal regions. Mismatches are used to update both the motor plan and the internal model. Purkinje cells in cerebellum carry the fine-grained learning signal that refines forward models across minutes and hours of motor practice.

Evidence for the framework is strong. Efference-copy suppression of self-generated sensation has been demonstrated across species and modalities. Cerebellar patients show characteristic deficits — they can move but cannot calibrate movement to sensory consequence, producing the classic overshoot-and-correction pattern of cerebellar ataxia. The vestibular-ocular reflex, one of the best-understood circuits in neuroscience, is an MSI loop distilled to its simplest form. Contested territory concerns the relative roles of cerebellum vs. basal ganglia vs. cortex in different motor-sensory tasks, and how to extend the framework to extended reaching and whole-body movement through space.

For environments, MSI is the framework that explains why some spaces feel navigable and others disorienting. Vestibular input that matches proprioceptive and visual input — steady floor, predictable acoustic spatial cues, consistent gravity signals — allows the motor-sensory loop to run smoothly. Spaces that deliver inconsistent cues (ambiguous horizon lines, reflective floors, loud reverberation, unstable thermal gradients requiring postural adjustment) force the MSI system to work harder, with resulting cognitive cost. Good architecture respects the motor loop as much as it respects the visual system.

Deep dive — the framework in full

Origins: from reafference to forward models

The theoretical foundations of MSI lie in two mid-twentieth-century insights. Erich von Holst and Horst Mittelstaedt's 1950 reafference principle proposed that the brain distinguishes self-generated from externally generated sensory input by comparing incoming signals against an "efference copy" of the motor command. Their key observation: when a fly's head is rotated 180°, it spins in circles — because the motor command to move left now produces visual input consistent with moving right, and the reafference system misinterprets the input. Parallel work by Roger Sperry in the 1950s in vertebrates converged on the same principle. The modern computational formulation emerged in the 1990s through Daniel Wolpert and Mitsuo Kawato's work on internal models: the brain maintains a forward model that maps motor commands to predicted sensory consequences, and an inverse model that maps desired sensory outcomes back to required motor commands. Forward models enable prediction; inverse models enable planning. The cerebellum is the primary substrate for both, with the basal ganglia and motor cortex contributing complementary functions.

The cerebellum: predictor and calibrator

The cerebellum has a deeply regular microcircuit architecture: a uniform grid of granule cells providing contextual input to Purkinje cells via parallel fibres, with climbing fibres from the inferior olive carrying error signals. Masao Ito's theoretical and experimental work from the 1970s onward established that cerebellum implements a kind of supervised-learning algorithm — Purkinje cells receive a rich contextual representation, issue a predicted output, and receive an error signal from the climbing fibre indicating how far off the prediction was. Over minutes to hours of motor practice, the synaptic weights between parallel fibres and Purkinje cells are adjusted (long-term depression and potentiation at these synapses) to reduce future prediction error. The cerebellum is therefore not a "motor structure" in the narrow sense; it is a general-purpose prediction-error-minimising engine that happens to operate most obviously on motor-related predictions. Recent work has extended this role to cognitive predictions (Schmahmann, 2019) — cerebellar patients show cognitive affective symptoms consistent with degraded prediction across non-motor domains.

Efference copy in sensory cortex

The consequences of efference-copy prediction are observable at every stage of sensory processing. In somatosensation, touches that are self-produced activate primary somatosensory cortex less than identical externally-produced touches — the difference is explained by prediction-driven attenuation. In audition, the sound of one's own voice produces suppressed auditory-cortex responses via predictions carried from speech motor areas. In vision, the smooth-pursuit eye-movement system predicts the retinal-flow consequences of its own commands and subtracts them from the raw retinal image to produce a stabilised perceptual experience — which is why the world does not appear to jump when you move your eyes. In vestibular perception, self-generated head movements produce predicted vestibular signals that are largely cancelled at the level of the vestibular nuclei; vestibular input that survives cancellation is signal from external perturbation, which is why unexpected movement is so disorienting. The efference-copy mechanism is thus a general principle across modalities, not a specialised feature of any one.

Evidence: what is well-established

The vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) is the best-characterised MSI circuit: head movement produces eye counter-movement at a gain of approximately 1.0, with a latency of roughly 10-15 ms, and the gain is continuously recalibrated by the cerebellar flocculus in response to visual slip. Adjusting the gain experimentally — with magnifying prisms, for example — produces measurable cerebellar plasticity over minutes. Efference-copy suppression of self-generated sensation is replicated across species and modalities; its breakdown in schizophrenia is among the most reliable neural findings in that disorder, and helps explain several positive symptoms including auditory hallucinations (Frith, 1987; updated in recent reviews). The dissociation between movement execution and movement calibration in cerebellar patients is a mainstay of neurology. Tool-use plasticity in body schema — when you pick up a hammer, the hammer becomes incorporated into your body representation — is an MSI phenomenon (Iriki et al., 1996) with direct single-neuron evidence in macaque parietal cortex.

Where it is contested

Two live debates. The model-based vs. model-free debate concerns how much of motor behaviour really runs on explicit internal models versus on model-free reinforcement-learning processes in basal ganglia. Both are certainly operating, but their relative weights in different tasks remain unsettled. Second, the scope of cerebellar prediction debate concerns whether cerebellum really implements a generic prediction-error-minimising function across cognitive domains or whether the cognitive-affective cerebellar findings reflect a narrower class of motor-adjacent predictions. Third, at the computational level, how the forward and inverse models are actually encoded in neural populations remains a frontier.

Clinical and rehabilitation relevance

Motor-sensory integration dysfunction presents across a spectrum of clinical conditions, each illuminating a different part of the architecture. Cerebellar ataxia — whether from degeneration, stroke, or genetic disorder — produces characteristic errors in motor calibration: patients can initiate movement but cannot refine it through sensory feedback, producing the overshoot and correction pattern clinicians test for. Parkinson's disease involves basal-ganglia circuits that complement cerebellar ones; patients lose not the ability to generate movement plans but the ability to initiate them at the right moment. Schizophrenia involves a specific failure of efference-copy suppression: the inner-speech self-generation signal is not properly cancelled, and the resulting uncanceled "sensation" of speech is experienced as external voice — a neurocognitive substrate for auditory hallucinations that has held up across decades of work. In rehabilitation, constraint-induced movement therapy after stroke and mirror therapy for phantom-limb pain both exploit the MSI architecture by providing consistent visuomotor feedback that rebuilds or remaps internal models. VR rehabilitation extends this by delivering customisable sensory feedback that can be graded to the patient's current capacity, accelerating recovery of motor function in ways that pure motor practice cannot match.

How this connects to the other T1 frameworks

MSI is the motor-side implementation of Predictive Processing (PP): forward models are exactly the top-down predictions PP describes, and efference-copy comparison is exactly the prediction-error computation. Embodied Cognition (EC) depends on MSI for body schema and affordance perception — the feeling that one could reach a shelf is a forward-model simulation of reaching it. Multisensory Integration (MS) contributes the cross-modal comparison that MSI uses during movement: visual, vestibular, and proprioceptive signals must all be combined to check predictions. Salience Networks (SN) act on the residual unexpected reafference that survives MSI cancellation — an unexpected vestibular input that the MSI loop failed to predict is maximally salience-recruiting. Neuromodulatory Systems (NM) particularly involve cerebellar learning signals and dopaminergic reward-prediction-error signals that shape motor learning.

Implications for environmental cognition

For architecture and landscape, MSI matters because the built environment provides the sensory feedback on which the motor system depends. Four implications. Vestibular coherence. A stable horizon, a level floor, predictable walking surfaces, and unambiguous gravitational cues let the MSI loop run smoothly. Violations — sloped floors in buildings that look level, reflective surfaces that confuse floor from wall, visually ambiguous thresholds — force the MSI system to resolve disagreement, at cognitive cost. Acoustic spatial coherence. The apparent distance and direction of sound sources should match their visible positions; reverberation should be consistent with the visible room geometry. Walking through a space whose auditory signature contradicts its visual one generates persistent MSI prediction error. Scale consistency. Affordance perception is a forward-model simulation of acting on the space; when the apparent reach of shelves or doors is inconsistent with their actual reach, the simulation and the execution diverge and the space feels "off." Gait-friendly surfaces. Floor materials that produce predictable haptic and acoustic feedback support automatic gait; materials with unpredictable feedback demand conscious attention. All four are directly testable in occupied space via gait analysis, postural-sway instrumentation, or reaction-time probes during locomotion.
