Answer: Natural Light and Cognitive Performance

Controlled exposure to natural or naturalistic daylight (windows, daylight-spectrum fixtures) is associated with improvements in alertness, working memory performance, and sustained attention in office workers, particularly during afternoon hours. Effect sizes are moderate (d ≈ 0.35–0.55) and replicate across laboratory and field settings. Underlying mechanisms are plausibly linked to circadian regulation via ipRGC-mediated melanopsin pathways, though direct mechanistic evidence in office populations remains limited.

✅Strong support — Alertness and sustained attention benefits replicate across 4+ independent studies
⚠️Mixed evidence — Working memory effects vary by task type and time-of-day; not always significant
🔵Theory-linked inference — Circadian mechanism plausible but not directly tested in these populations

Viola et al. (2008) — Loughborough University field study → Science Summary
Office workers with daylight-optimized fixtures showed significantly higher alertness ratings and better performance on the Psychomotor Vigilance Task [Psychomotor Vigilance Task] vs. standard fluorescent condition. Randomized crossover, n = 94.
RCT · Field d = 0.48 ✅ Strong support
Source: Viola et al. (2008) — Ergonomics 51(7)
"Participants in the daylight-spectrum condition reported significantly higher vitality scores (p < .01) and showed faster median RT on PVT blocks 3 and 4."
Method: Randomized crossover, 4-week washout Caveat: Single workplace; possible seasonal confound
Edwards & Torcellini (2002) — NREL [National Renewable Energy Laboratory] review, 13 daylighting studies → Science Summary
Narrative review of workplace daylighting interventions; 10/13 studies reported positive productivity or wellbeing outcomes. Direct cognitive performance measures available in only 4 studies.
Review n = 13 studies ⚠️ Mixed evidence
⚡ Limited evidence — Long-term (>6 month) cognitive outcomes unstudied; most trials run 1–4 weeks
