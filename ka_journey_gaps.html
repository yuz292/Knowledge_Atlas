Gaps in the evidence base

Roles served researcher, admin, 160_student Current status naive Complexity tier Medium — absence is harder to display than presence

◧What this page displaysthe information on offer

The gaps page displays the cells of the topic × outcome crosswalk where the evidence base is thin, absent, or contested. It is the Atlas's answer to the research-planning question "where should the next study be?" and to the admin's question "where is our coverage worst?"

Gaps are a negative claim: "there is no evidence on X." Displaying negative claims honestly is harder than displaying positive ones because absence and under-indexing look the same from outside. The page must distinguish a genuine gap in the field's literature from a gap in the Atlas's coverage.

Every cell on the gap map carries a confidence score about the gap itself: "we are highly confident the field has not studied this" is a different claim from "we have not indexed papers on this, but our coverage is patchy here."

  • ResearcherTo find low-hanging research targets — cells where a single good study would materially advance the field.
  • PractitionerTo recognise when a design decision is being made in an evidential vacuum.
  • AdministratorTo prioritise contributor outreach and index expansion. The Atlas's coverage holes drive its T2 scraping queue.

△Why this page is hard to designthe challenges

  1. Two kinds of gap. Field gap (no one studied this) vs. coverage gap (field studied it, we haven't indexed it). The page must separate them visibly.
  2. Absence display. Zero-value cells are invisible in many charts (e.g., a bar chart). The page must use a representation where zero is as visible as high density — typically a heatmap or a "stars vs. dots" encoding.
  3. False precision. A cell with "3 studies" may be more or less studied than a cell with "5 studies" once study quality is weighted. The page must weight rather than count.
  4. Actionability. A raw gap list is depressing; an actionable one is scoped to what a reader could plausibly study.
  5. Update cadence. Gaps move as the corpus grows. The page must show its freshness and warn when it is stale.

✗The simple naive solution in place nowwhat you'll actually see

naive — the current implementation

The current page renders the topic × outcome crosswalk as a plain HTML table with raw study counts per cell, sorted by topic alphabetically. Zero-count cells are blank. There is no distinction between field and coverage gaps, no quality-weighting, no freshness marker, and no action affordance.

Why this is inadequate. Blank cells read as "nothing here to see" rather than "possible research opportunity"; raw counts mislead by treating a single high-quality meta-analysis and a single opportunistic survey as equivalent. The admin who uses this page to plan outreach cannot tell a field gap from a coverage gap, which is the distinction the page most needs to make.
