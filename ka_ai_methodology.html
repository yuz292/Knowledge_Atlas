COGS 160 · AI-Directed Development

AI-Directed Development:
The KA Methodology

In this course you use AI as a directed instrument in a production pipeline — not as a chatbot. The goal is not "getting good output." It is producing output that meets a formal specification, passes defined tests, and integrates correctly with the larger system. That requires a specific methodology.

CORE

The central distinction: chatbot vs. directed instrument

Why the standard way of using AI fails in production systems

When you use an AI chatbot casually, the implicit contract is: "give me something useful and I will decide whether to use it." This works for one-off tasks where you can evaluate quality by reading the output. It fails completely when the output must satisfy a formal specification, integrate with other code, and be correct enough that downstream processes do not need to check every claim.

In Knowledge Atlas, an AI extracting claims from a scientific paper is part of a 12-stage pipeline. If it invents a citation that does not exist, that fabrication propagates through the epistemic network, inflates credence scores, and corrupts the Bayesian network downstream. By the time a human notices, ten derivative claims may have been generated from the fabricated source. There is no casual recovery.

The methodology in this document treats AI as a directed instrument: you specify exactly what role it plays, what its output format must be, what it is forbidden to do, and what context it needs. You write tests that the output must pass. You verify outputs cross-AI when stakes are high. This is how production AI development works, and this is what you are learning.

The key shift in mental model. A chatbot user asks "can you help me with X?" and evaluates the response impressionistically. A directed-instrument user writes a specification for what X must look like, provides context and constraints, specifies the output schema, and then runs automated tests on the output. The AI is not the evaluator. You are.

PRODUCTION PROMPT STRUCTURE (annotated)
ROLEYou are a bibliometrician specializing in systematic review methodology for cognitive science of the built environment. You have deep familiarity with CNFA construct taxonomy and the difference between empirical, theoretical, and review-type papers. TASKGiven the abstract below, extract every quantitative finding (effect size, p-value, or confidence interval). For each finding, identify: the independent variable, the dependent variable, the sample size, and the study design type. CONSTRAINTSDO NOT invent effect sizes not present in the abstract. DO NOT infer or estimate values — only extract explicit numbers. DO NOT classify the study design if it is ambiguous — use "UNCLEAR" instead. DO NOT output anything outside the JSON schema below. If the abstract contains no quantitative findings, return {"findings": []}. CONTEXTStudy design taxonomy: RCT, QE (quasi-experimental), CLE (controlled lab), FE (field experiment), COR (correlational), META (meta-analysis), REVIEW. CNFA constructs include: spatial_cognition, affective_response, attention_restoration, thermal_comfort, luminous_comfort, acoustic_comfort, biophilic_response, social_density, physical_activity. FORMATReturn ONLY valid JSON matching this schema exactly: { "findings": [ { "iv": "string — independent variable name", "dv": "string — dependent variable name", "effect_size": "number or null", "effect_type": "d | r | eta2 | OR | null", "p_value": "number or null", "n": "integer or null", "design": "RCT | QE | CLE | FE | COR | META | REVIEW | UNCLEAR", "verbatim_quote": "string — exact text from abstract supporting this finding" } ] }

Component-by-component guidance

1 · Role Assignment

Who the AI is pretending to be. Specific enough to activate domain knowledge. "A bibliometrician who specializes in systematic review methodology" is correct. "A helpful assistant" is wrong — it provides no domain constraint and activates no specific expertise. The role should be specific to the task, not a generic capability claim.

2 · Task Definition

What the AI must produce, defined in terms of output format — not intent. "Extract every quantitative finding" is correct. "Summarize the paper's contributions" is wrong — "summarize" does not specify a format, a level of detail, or what counts as a contribution. The task definition must be testable: after the AI responds, can you write a function that checks whether the task was completed?

3 · Constraints & Prohibitions

The "DO NOT" pattern. AIs have systematic failure modes — they confabulate, fill in gaps, average over ambiguity, and add editorial flourishes. Each "DO NOT" clause closes one failure mode. Constraints must be explicit because AIs will otherwise default to seemingly helpful behaviours that violate your requirements. The most common required constraint in KA work: DO NOT invent information not present in the source text.

4 · Context Loading

Vocabulary sheets, canonical term lists, and reference documents the AI needs to do the task correctly. The AI does not know your domain vocabulary — if you do not define "CNFA construct," it will map it to whatever seems closest in its training data. Provide taxonomy tables, enum values, and term definitions inline rather than assuming shared knowledge. Keep context loading concise: a 200-word taxonomy table is better than linking to a 50-page document the AI cannot read.

5 · Output Format Specification

The exact structure of the response — a JSON schema with required fields, not free text. This is the contract between the AI's output and the next step in your pipeline. If your pipeline expects a JSON array and the AI returns a prose summary, the pipeline breaks. Define the schema first (before writing the task), then write constraints and context that enforce it. Include an example of a valid response if the schema is complex.

EXAMPLES

Track-specific prompt examples

The gap between a vague request and a production prompt
❌ Vague — will not work in production
"Look at this image and tell me what CNFA constructs are present."
No output format. "CNFA constructs" is undefined. The AI will guess what that means, produce a prose description, and the result cannot be ingested by the tagger system.
✅ Production prompt
You are a cognitive-science trained image annotator specializing in the CNFA (Cognitive Neuroscience of the Functional Architecture) taxonomy. TASK: Given the image, score each of the 5 algorithm bundles on a 0–2 scale (0=absent, 1=partial, 2=present) based on the Phase 0 reference table provided. DO NOT assign a score greater than 0 if the evidence is ambiguous. DO NOT describe the image narrative — only score the bundles. DO NOT add bundles not in the Phase 0 table. BUNDLES (from Phase 0 reference table): A: Spatial-cognitive — enclosure, prospect/refuge, legibility B: Temporal-cognitive — change over time, rhythmic features, sequence C: Social-cognitive — density, crowding, interpersonal space markers D: Affective-cognitive — valence indicators, arousal cues, safety signals E: Executive-cognitive — complexity, attention demands, task-facilitating affordances OUTPUT FORMAT — return ONLY valid JSON: {"A": 0|1|2, "B": 0|1|2, "C": 0|1|2, "D": 0|1|2, "E": 0|1|2, "rationale": {"A": "string", "B": "string", "C": "string", "D": "string", "E": "string"}}
Specific role. Testable task (parse JSON and check values are 0/1/2). Explicit prohibitions. Context loaded (bundle definitions). Schema defined. This output can be ingested directly.
❌ Vague
"Generate some search queries for research on noise and cognition for Google Scholar."
No specificity on gap target. "Some" is undefined. No format. No instructions on query quality or how to match the KA corpus gaps. Output cannot be logged in the Query Diary schema.
✅ Production prompt
You are a systematic review search specialist with expertise in building academic database queries for cognitive science research. TASK: Generate exactly 5 search queries for the gap target below, optimized for Google AI citation search (conversational natural language, not Boolean operators). GAP TARGET: "acoustic → cognitive performance" — research front with 25 claims, 11 papers, VOI 0.755. Currently missing paradigms: Computational Feature Analysis (CFA), Photo/Video Surrogacy (PVS). Key theories: Neuromodulatory Systems, Arousal Theory, Cognitive Load Theory. DO NOT generate Boolean operator queries (no AND, OR, NOT). DO NOT generate queries so broad they return canonical papers already in most databases. DO NOT repeat the same conceptual angle across multiple queries. Each query must target a different aspect of the gap: mechanism, context, population, method, or intervention type. OUTPUT FORMAT — return ONLY valid JSON: {"queries": [ {"query_text": "string", "angle": "mechanism|context|population|method|intervention", "gap_target_id": "acoustic_cognitive_performance", "rationale": "string (≤30 words)"} ]}
Role is specific. Task is precisely bounded (5 queries, natural language, against a specific gap target). Constraints close the main failure modes. Gap context loaded explicitly. Output schema matches Query Diary format exactly.
❌ Vague
"Evaluate the KA topics page and tell me what UX problems you see."
No user type specified. "UX problems" is undefined. No severity scale. No output format. The response will be impressionistic prose that cannot be compared across pages or tracked over time.
✅ Production prompt
You are a UX evaluator conducting a structured heuristic walkthrough of the Knowledge Atlas topic browser page, evaluated from the perspective of a Track 2 student (Article Finding Pipeline) in their first week. USER TYPE — Track 2 first-week student: Goal: Identify which research topics have the most knowledge gaps and should be prioritized for article searches. Technical level: Moderate — familiar with browser but not with VOI scores or CNFA taxonomy. Mental model: Expects something like a content directory; does not yet understand the epistemic framework. Time constraint: Must decide on a gap target within a 20-minute session. TASK: Walk through the ka_topics.html page as this user. For each friction point you identify, record it using the schema below. DO NOT evaluate the aesthetic design — evaluate functional affordances only. DO NOT flag issues that are only problems for experts who understand the KA framework. DO NOT assign a severity higher than 2 unless the issue would cause the user to abandon the task. SEVERITY SCALE: 1=minor inconvenience, 2=slows task, 3=blocks task completion OUTPUT FORMAT — return ONLY valid JSON: {"friction_points": [ { "page_element": "string — what the user interacted with", "user_action": "string — what they tried to do", "observed_problem": "string", "severity": 1|2|3, "suggested_fix": "string (≤40 words)", "acceptance_criterion": "string — how we'd know the fix worked" } ]}
Role includes specific user type with goals, technical level, mental model, and time constraint — this is what makes the evaluation ecologically valid rather than hypothetical. Output is structured enough to track fixes over sprints.
METHOD 2

The "DO NOT" Pattern: five AI failure modes

Every constraint you omit is a failure mode you accept

These five failure modes account for the majority of errors in AI-generated output in knowledge-system work. For each task you design a prompt for, ask: which of these apply? Add a "DO NOT" clause for each.

F1
Confabulation
The AI invents information not present in the source — a citation, an effect size, an author's name — because it sounds plausible. It does not flag the invention.
DO NOT invent information not explicitly present in the source text. If a value is missing, return null.
F2
Summarizing instead of extracting
When asked to extract, the AI paraphrases instead. This loses precision: effect sizes get rounded, qualifications get dropped, p-values disappear.
DO NOT paraphrase or summarize when asked to extract. Provide verbatim quotes from the source where a string is requested.
F3
Format drift
The AI returns prose, markdown, or a different JSON structure than specified — often adding "helpful" fields or wrapping the output in explanation text.
DO NOT add any text outside the specified output schema. Return ONLY valid JSON. Do not explain your output.
F4
Ambiguity averaging
When the source is ambiguous — a result could be classified multiple ways — the AI picks one arbitrarily and presents it as definitive, without flagging the ambiguity.
DO NOT resolve ambiguity silently. Use the UNCLEAR or null values specified in the schema and flag the specific ambiguity in a "notes" field.
F5
Scope creep
The AI goes beyond the task boundary — adding commentary, additional findings, related topics, or unsolicited recommendations — because it is optimizing for "helpfulness."
DO NOT add commentary, caveats, or information outside the task scope. If you cannot complete a task step, leave that field null.
+
Check your prompt against all five
Before running any prompt in a KA pipeline, review it against F1–F5. If a failure mode could plausibly occur and you have no constraint blocking it, add one.
Template: "DO NOT [failure-mode behaviour]. If [edge case], [safe fallback]."
METHOD 3

Contracts and success conditions

If you cannot write a test for it, it is not a success condition

A contract is a formal document that specifies a component's inputs, outputs, invariants, and measurable thresholds. A contract turns "the AI should produce good output" into "the AI must return a JSON object containing fields X, Y, Z; X must be a float between 0 and 1; Z must be a non-empty string with fewer than 200 characters; and the result must pass validate_extraction(output) without raising an exception."

Every Track 1 and Track 2 deliverable in KA has a contract in contracts/. Before writing a prompt, read the contract for your track's output type. Before opening a PR, run the validation script against your output. If it fails, revise the prompt or the output.

contracts/ARTICLE_EXTRACTION_CONTRACT.md (example excerpt)
input PDF abstract text (string, 50–500 words)
output JSON object conforming to schemas/finding.json
invariant effect_size must be null or a float in [-100, 100]; never a string
invariant verbatim_quote must be a substring of the input abstract (verified by string search)
threshold ≥ 90% of findings on a 20-paper human-verified sample must match human annotation on IV, DV, and design type
failure mode If abstract contains no quantitative findings, return {"findings": []} — never fabricate findings
validation script python3 tests/validate_extraction.py --input output.json --schema schemas/finding.json
"It should work well" is not a success condition. Success conditions must be: specific (what field, what value), measurable (can be checked by a script), and bounded (has a threshold at which the output is accepted or rejected). If you are writing a sprint deliverable and cannot articulate a testable success condition, ask the instructor before proceeding — the spec is incomplete.
METHOD 4

Verification and the Trust Calibration Framework

How much checking does each task type require?

Not all AI output requires the same level of verification. Running a script to rename 200 files requires less oversight than extracting a numerical claim that will propagate into a Bayesian network. The trust calibration framework assigns a verification requirement to each task type based on three factors: reversibility, downstream reach, and fabrication risk.

  • L0 · Auto No human review needed. The task is fully reversible, has no downstream dependencies, and has no fabrication risk. Example: reformatting a JSON file, renaming variables, adding blank lines. Run automated tests; if they pass, accept.
  • L1 · Spot Spot-check 10%. Low fabrication risk; downstream impact is local. Example: generating search queries from gap targets, writing boilerplate code from a clear spec. Spot-check 10% of outputs manually; check automated test pass rate.
  • L2 · Sample Verify a random 20-paper / 20-output sample. Medium fabrication risk; output will be stored and later read. Example: abstract relevance screening, article metadata extraction, tag assignments. Human verifies 20 random samples against ground truth; report inter-rater agreement.
  • L3 · Cross-AI Run the same task on a second AI and compare outputs. High fabrication risk or high downstream reach. Example: effect size extraction, causal claim extraction, CNFA construct classification. Discrepancies between the two AIs flag items for human review. Only the agreed items proceed.
  • L4 · Human Full human review before commit. Irreversible or high-consequence output. Example: writing new warrant type definitions, modifying credence calibration parameters, any output that changes the schema. All outputs reviewed by the instructor before merging.
  • L5 · Panel Expert panel deliberation. Architectural decisions, epistemic parameter choices, or any change that affects the theoretical foundations. Convene a panel of domain roles, run structured deliberation, document reasoning before committing.
Default for most Track 2 work: L2 (20-sample verification). You are extracting claims from papers and logging them to a database. Run a 20-paper random sample from your output against the source PDFs. Document your inter-rater agreement with yourself (or a partner) in your PR description. This is not optional — it is the acceptance criterion.
METHOD 5

Ruthless prompts and the escalation ladder

What to do when the AI keeps failing

When an AI consistently violates a constraint — fabricating citations despite an explicit DO NOT, drifting format despite a schema — the default response is to escalate. A ruthless prompt makes the failure explicit, explains why it matters, demands the fix, and forbids the pattern using language that signals the seriousness of the constraint.

1
Standard constraint Add a clear DO NOT clause. This handles most failure modes on the first or second run.
2
Named failure + consequence If violation continues: name the failure pattern explicitly and state what happens if it occurs. "You fabricated the citation 'Smith et al. 2019.' This is the specific failure mode we are preventing. A fabricated citation will corrupt the knowledge base. DO NOT add any citation not present verbatim in the source text."
3
Worked negative example Show the AI exactly the wrong output it produced and explain what is wrong about each element. This forces it to process the failure mode concretely, not abstractly.
4
Cross-AI verification Run the same prompt on a different AI. If the second AI produces correct output, use that output and document the failure. If both fail, escalate to the instructor — the constraint may be under-specified or the task may exceed current AI capability for this type of content.
5
Task decomposition Break the task into smaller steps where each step is within reliable AI capability. A task that asks for extraction + classification + verification in one prompt can often be split into three separate prompts each of which is more reliable.
METHOD 6

The Expert Panel Pattern

Structured deliberation for decisions that require domain reasoning

When a decision requires domain expertise — choosing between competing definitions, adjudicating a contested classification, or selecting a threshold — the expert panel pattern assigns the AI multiple named roles and asks each role to deliberate on the question independently before a synthetic conclusion is drawn.

The KA system uses expert panels for: warrant type calibration (epistemologists), effect-size threshold setting (statisticians), VR scene ecological validity (cognitive scientists), and GUI evaluation rubric design (UX researchers). The panel pattern prevents the AI from collapsing to the most salient answer and instead forces it to represent genuine epistemic tension. 

# Panel prompt structure (abbreviated)

ROLES: You will sequentially play three expert panelists:
- PANEL_STATS: A biostatistician specializing in effect size interpretation
- PANEL_EPIST: A philosopher of science specializing in evidence and inference
- PANEL_DOMAIN: A cognitive scientist studying workplace environments

QUESTION: Should we classify a study that reports only p < 0.05 with no effect size
as EVIDENCE_PRESENT or EVIDENCE_AMBIGUOUS in the KA credence system?

INSTRUCTIONS:
1. Respond as PANEL_STATS with a 3-5 sentence position and its strongest objection.
2. Respond as PANEL_EPIST with a different 3-5 sentence position.
3. Respond as PANEL_DOMAIN with practical implications for the KA knowledge base.
4. Synthesize: what classification does the panel recommend, and what dissent remains?

DO NOT have panelists agree prematurely. Dissent is informative. Preserve it.
Panel echo chambers. If all panelists agree immediately, the prompt is not forcing genuine deliberation — the AI is defaulting to the most salient answer and writing three versions of it. Add constraints that require each panelist to identify the strongest objection to their own position. Disagreement is the signal that the panel is working.
METHOD 7

Never trust a single AI — cross-check with a second

On overconfidence, blind spots, and what one AI cannot see in its own work

One of the most consistent findings in working with large language models at scale is that a single AI system has systematic blind spots with respect to its own output. When you ask a model to critique something it has just produced, it will find errors — but it tends to find the same category of errors it was already alert to, and miss the structural or conceptual failures it was not primed to look for. This is not a failure of honesty; it is an architectural feature. The model that generated the output and the model that critiques it are, in many important respects, the same instrument running in the same direction.

There is also an overconfidence problem that long sessions compound. After a model has worked through an extended reasoning chain — especially one it generated itself — it becomes increasingly anchored to the conclusions embedded in that chain. Asking it to critique its own work at the end of a long session tends to produce mild, partial critique that misses the most important failures. The model defends what it built. A different model, starting fresh, without the accumulated context of the session, will reliably surface different and often far more serious problems. This is not a hypothesis — it is the consistent experience of anyone who has used both Claude and Gemini on the same artifact, or GPT-4 and Claude on the same argument.

The practical rule: Any output that will be committed to the KA system, published, or used as the basis for a consequential decision should be independently evaluated by a second AI system before you accept it. This is especially true for: classification schemes, threshold values, evidence quality ratings, statistical interpretations, and any argument whose conclusion is not independently verifiable from the sources cited.

When single-AI critique most reliably fails

✗
Long-session anchoring
After 30+ turns of iterative development, the model is deeply invested in the architecture it helped build. Its self-critique will protect the core structure and surface only peripheral issues.
Fix: Start a fresh session with the second AI. Give it only the artifact — no session history, no context about how it was built.
✗
Category blindness
The model that wrote a classification scheme will critique it using the same categorical assumptions embedded in the scheme. It cannot easily see what its own categories exclude.
Fix: Ask the second AI to propose an alternative taxonomy before showing it yours. Compare the two structures.
✗
Statistical overconfidence
Models are inconsistently trained on statistical reasoning. One model may accept a confused interpretation of a confidence interval that a second model immediately recognizes as wrong.
Fix: Send the bare claim to a second AI with no framing: "Is this statistically correct?" No context, no explanation.
✗
Prompt-absorbed assumptions
If your original prompt contained an implicit assumption, the model absorbed it as fact. Its critique will not challenge that assumption because it was part of the framing it was given.
Fix: Ask the second AI: "What assumptions does this output appear to be making? Which of them are most questionable?"

The five-step cross-check protocol

1
Produce with your primary AI in your working session
Use your full context, your session history, your ruthless prompt — the methods in the preceding sections. Produce the strongest version of the artifact you can. Do not shortcut this step; the cross-check is not a substitute for rigorous primary production, it is a check on it.
2
Open a fresh session with a different AI — no context transfer
Do not carry over the session history. Give the second AI only the artifact and the evaluation task. Use a different model family when possible — Claude → Gemini, or GPT-4 → Claude — because different training regimes produce different blind spots. The freshness matters as much as the model switch.
3
Ask for structural critique, not polish
Do not ask "is this good?" Ask: "What are the most serious conceptual or structural problems with this? What is this missing that it should have? What assumptions is it making that you would challenge?" Explicitly forbid praise. Ask it to state its single most damaging objection before providing anything else. Do not let it hedge with "overall this is quite strong."
4
Do not explain or defend — record the objections
When the second AI raises a problem, the instinct is to explain why it is not actually a problem. Resist this entirely. Note the objection and move on. Your explanations are rationalizations for your investment in the artifact. Collect all the objections first, then decide which require action — not during the cross-check session.
5
Return to the primary AI with the strongest objections
Bring the most serious objections back to your working session: "A second AI raised the following three objections about this output. Please assess whether each is valid, and if so, revise accordingly." This closes the loop while preserving the accumulated context from your primary session. Document what changed and why.
In practice for this course. You will use Claude as your primary instrument. For cross-checks, use Gemini via Google AI Studio (free with any Google account). For any deliverable that goes into the KA system — a tagged claim, a scored article, a GUI evaluation report — paste the output into Gemini and ask: "What are the most serious errors or questionable assumptions in this output? State your strongest objection first." Before you submit, note what the cross-check found. Your submission should include a one-paragraph cross-check note: what Gemini flagged, whether you agreed, and what you changed.
METHOD 8
📗

Keep a master document — the canonical memory that outlives every session

Every AI session starts cold. The model you are talking to has no memory of the reasoning behind last week's decisions, no record of which theory you consulted when you chose one implementation over another, no account of how a subsystem evolved from its first draft to its current form. If that reasoning lives only in chat logs, it is lost in practice — chat logs are not re-read, not indexed, and not available to the next AI worker you bring in.

A master document is the solution to this problem. It is not a journal, not a progress report, and not a chat export. It is a living architecture document — the canonical source of truth for everything the system knows, how it works, and why it was built the way it was. At its best, it functions as a substrate from which publishable papers, grant proposals, course materials, and design manuals can be extracted, rather than written from scratch.

What belongs in a master document

Design Decisions

Every significant architectural choice, with the rationale and the alternatives rejected. Not just what was decided — why this, and not that, and what would have to change for you to revisit it.

Supporting Theories

The scientific and scholarly foundations behind each choice. If you consulted Quinean coherentism to justify a pipeline design, that connection belongs here — with references — so a future reader can evaluate the reasoning, not merely observe the result.

Pipeline Revisions

How each subsystem evolved from its first version to its current form, and what triggered each revision. Systems that do not record their revision history are opaque to audit and resistant to principled improvement.

Essay and Paper Summaries

Précis of documents being written about the system — arguments, findings, open questions. When a paper is in progress across many sessions, the master doc is what prevents it from being reconstructed from scratch each time.

Cross-Reference Index

Traceability from concept → code file → test → contract → panel deliberation. Without this web of references, the relationship between theory and implementation is invisible to anyone who was not present when it was built.

What Does Not Belong

Progress updates, task lists, session logs, raw chat exports, or anything ephemeral. Those belong in TASKS.md, session notes, or the coordination system. The master doc contains only what will still matter in six months.

How to maintain it

The critical discipline is writing to the master document immediately after significant work — not at the end of the week, not in a future cleanup session that never happens. The operative test: if a decision or insight would matter to a future AI worker trying to understand why the system is the way it is, it belongs in the master doc now.

When writing a major new section is impractical mid-session, write a Master Doc Brief instead — a structured note (a page or two) recording the decision, its rationale, its theoretical grounding, and a pointer to the relevant code. Briefs can be integrated into the main document in a later session without loss of information. What cannot be recovered is the reasoning that was never written down at all.

The operational test for whether something belongs in the master doc: "Would a skilled researcher reading this document six months from now — with no access to our chat logs — be able to reconstruct not just what was built, but why it was built that way, and what the alternatives were?" If the answer is no, the master doc is incomplete.

For COGS 160 students

You are working on a project, not just completing assignments. That distinction matters. A project has a history that accumulates across weeks. At minimum, each team should maintain a short design log recording: what was decided this session, why, what alternatives were considered, and what open questions remain. This log is your team's master doc in miniature — and it is what allows you, your AI collaborator, and your instructor to evaluate whether you have actually understood what you built, or merely produced output.
